\n May 5, 1805\n

\n
\n \n

[Lewis]

\n \n
\n Sunday May 5th 1805\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nA fine morning \u00a0\u00a0 I walked on shore untill 8 A M when we halted for breakfast and in the course of my walk killed a deer which I carried about a mile and a half to the river, it was in good order. \u00a0\u00a0 soon after seting out the rudder irons of the white perogue were broken by her runing fowl on a sawyer, she was however refitted in a few minutes with some tugs of raw hide and nales. \u00a0\u00a0 as usual saw a great quantity of game today; Buffaloe Elk and goats or Antelopes feeding in every direction; we kill whatever we wish, the buffaloe furnish us with fine veal and fat beef, we also have venison and beaver tales when we wish them; the flesh of the Elk and goat are less esteemed, and certainly are inferior. \u00a0\u00a0 we have not been able to take any fish for some time past. The country is as yesterday beatifull in the extreme.\u2014

\n

\nsaw the carcases of many Buffaloe lying dead along the shore partially devoured by the wolves and bear. \u00a0\u00a0 saw a great number of white brant also the common brown brant, geese of the common kind and a small species of geese which differ considerably from the common canadian goose; their neck head and beak are considerably thicker shorter and larger than the other in proportion to it's size, they are also more than a third smaller, and their note more like that of the brant or a young goose which has not perfectly acquired his notes, in all other rispects they are the same colour habits and the number of feathers in the tale, they frequently also ascociate with the large geese when in flocks, but never saw them pared off with the large or common goose. [1] The white brant [2] ascociate in very large flocks, they do not appear to be mated or pared off as if they intended to raise their young in this quarter, I therefore doubt whether they reside here during the summer for that purpose. this bird is about the size of the common brown brant or two thirds of the common goose, it is not so long by six inches from point to point of the wings when extended as the other; the beak head and neck are also larger and stronger; their beak \u2329and\u232a legs and feet are of a redish or fleshcoloured white. \u00a0\u00a0 the eye is of moderate size, the puple of a deep sea green incircled with a ring of yellowish brown. \u00a0\u00a0 it has sixteen feathers of equal length in the tale; their note differs but little from the common brant, their flesh much the same, and in my opinion preferable to the goose, the flesh is dark. \u00a0\u00a0 they are entirely of a beatifull pure white except the large feathers of the 1st and second joints of the wings which are jut black. \u00a0\u00a0 form and habits are the same with the other brant; they sometimes ascociate and form one common flock. Capt [X: we] Clark found a den of young wolves in the course of his walk today and also saw a great number of those anamals; they are very abundant in this quarter, and are of two species [3] \u00a0\u00a0 the small woolf or burrowing dog of the praries are the inhabitants almost invariably of the open plains; they usually ascociate in bands of ten or twelve sometimes more and burrow near some pass or place much frequented by game; not being able alone to take deer or goat they are rarely ever found alone but hunt in bands; they frequently watch and seize their prey near their burrows; in these burrows they raise their young and to them they also resort when pursued; when a person approaches them they frequently bark, their note being precisely that of the small dog. \u00a0\u00a0 they are of an intermediate size between that of the fox and dog, very active fleet and delicately formed; the \u2329years\u232a ears large erect and pointed the head long and pointed more like that of the fox; tale long \u2329and bushey\u232a; the hair and fur also resembles the fox tho' is much coarser and inferior. \u00a0\u00a0 they are of a pale redish brown colour. \u00a0\u00a0 the eye of a deep sea green colour small and piercing. \u00a0\u00a0 their tallons are reather longer than those of the ordinary wolf or that common to the atlantic states, none of which are to be found in this quarter, nor I believe above the river Plat.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 The large woolf found here is not as large as those of the atlantic states. \u00a0\u00a0 they were lower and \u2329heaver\u232a thicker made shorter leged. \u00a0\u00a0 their colour which is not effected by the seasons, is a grey or blackish brown and every intermediate shade from that to a creen [cream] coloured white; these wolves resort the woodlands and are also found in the plains, but never take refuge in the ground or burrow so far as I have been able to inform myself. \u00a0\u00a0 we scarcely see a gang of buffaloe without observing a parsel of those faithfull shepherds on their skirts in readiness to take care of the mamed & wounded. \u00a0\u00a0 the large wolf never barks, but howls as those of the atlantic states do. Capt. Clark and Drewyer killed the largest \u00a0\u00a0 brown bear this evening which we have yet seen. \u00a0\u00a0 it was a most tremendious looking anamal, and extreemly hard to kill notwithstanding he had five balls through his lungs and five others in various parts he swam more than half the distance across the river to a sandbar & it was at least twenty minutes before he died; he did not attempt to attact, but fled and made the most tremendous roaring from the moment he was shot. We had no means of weighing this monster; Capt. Clark thought he would weigh 500 lbs. \u00a0\u00a0 for my own part I think the estimate too small by 100 lbs. \u00a0\u00a0 he measured 8 Feet 7\u00bd Inches from the nose to the extremety of the hind feet, 5 F. 10\u00bd Inch arround the breast, 1 F. 11 I. arround the middle of the arm, & 3 F. 11 I. arround the neck; his tallons which were five in number on each foot were 4\u215c Inches in length. \u00a0\u00a0 he was in good order, we therefore divided him among the party and made them boil the oil and put it in a cask for future uce; the oil is as hard as hogs lard when cool, much more so than that of the black bear. \u00a0\u00a0 this bear differs from the common black bear in several respects; it's tallons are much longer and more blont, it's tale shorter, it's hair which is of a redish or bey brown, is longer thicker and finer than that of the black bear; his liver lungs and heart are much larger even in proportion with his size; the heart particularly was as large as that of a large Ox. \u00a0\u00a0 his maw was also ten times the size of black bear, and was filled with flesh and fish. \u00a0\u00a0 his testicles were pendant from the belly and placed four inches assunder in seperate bags or pouches.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 this animal also feeds on roots and almost every species of wild fruit.

\n

\nThe party killed two Elk and a Buffaloe today, and my dog caught a goat, which he overtook by superior fleetness, the goat it must be understood was with young and extreemly poor. \u00a0\u00a0 a great number of these goats are devowered by the wolves and bear at this season when they are poor and passing the river from S. W. to N. E. \u00a0\u00a0 they are very inactive and easily taken in the water, a man can out swim them with great ease; the Indians take them in great numbers in the river at this season and in autumn when they repass to the S. W.\u2014

\n \n Point of observation No. 9.\n \n

\n

\nOn the Lard. shore near the fourth course of this day, observed meridian Altitude of the \u2609's L. L. with Octant by the back observation to be 68\u00b0 47'. \u00a0\u00a0 the latitude deduced from which is 45\u00b0 46' 5.6\"\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 I do think this observation can be depended on as it was reather late before I could commence it, the sun was about to decline or perhaps had declined a few minutes.\u2014

[Clark]

\n \n
\n 5th of May Sunday 1805\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nWe Set out verry early and had not proceeded far before the rudder Irons of one of the Perogus broke which detained us a Short time \u00a0\u00a0 Capt Lewis walked on Shore this morning and killed a Deer, after brackfast I walked on Shore Saw great numbers of Buffalow & Elk \u00a0\u00a0 Saw also a Den of young wolves, and a number of Grown wolves in every direction, the white & Grey Brant is in this part of the Missouri \u00a0\u00a0 I shot at the white brant but at So great a distance I did not kill, The Countrey on both sides is as yesterday, handsom & fertile\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 The river rising & Current Strong & in the evening we Saw a Brown or Grisley beare on a Sand beech, I went out with one man Geo. Drewyer & Killed the bear, which was verry large and a turrible looking animal, which we found verry hard to kill \u00a0\u00a0 we Shot ten Balls into him before we killed him, & 5 of those Balls through his lights \u2329before\u232a \u00a0\u00a0 This animal is the largest of the Carnivorous kind I ever Saw \u00a0\u00a0 we had nothing that could way him, I think his weight may be Stated at 500 [600?] pounds, he measured 8 feet 7\u00bd In. from his nose to the extremity of the Toe, 5 feet 10\u00bd in. arround the breast, 1 feet 11 Ins: around the middle of the arm, 3 feet 11 Ins. arround the neck \u00a0\u00a0 his tallents was 4 Inches & \u215c long, he was good order, and appeared verry different from the Common black bear in as much as his \u2329tallents\u232a? [X: talons or nails] were blunt, his tail Short, his liver & lights much larger, his maw ten times as large and Contained meat or flesh & fish only\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 we had him Skined and divided, the oile tried up & put in Kegs for use. \u00a0\u00a0 we Camped on the Stard Side, [5] our men killed three Elk and a Buffalow to day, and our Dog Cought an antilope a fair race, this animal appeared verry pore & with young.

[Ordway]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Sunday 5th May 1805. Clear and pleasant. \u00a0\u00a0 we Set off eairly \u00a0\u00a0 proceeded on. \u00a0\u00a0 one hunter who [s]tay on the S. Shore all night came to us at breakfast time. \u00a0\u00a0 had killed two buffaloe Calfs which we took on board. \u00a0\u00a0 we proceeded on. \u00a0\u00a0 passed bottoms of timber on each Side. \u00a0\u00a0 passed a large handsom plains on the N. S. where we saw a great nomber of buffaloe and white geese. [6] \u00a0\u00a0 we halted for to dine about 2 o.C. on the S. S. at a bottom of timber where we Saw buffaloe and Goats \u00a0\u00a0 our officers gave the party a half a Gill of ardent Spirits. Jo. Fields who was taken Sick yesterday is some worse to day. \u00a0\u00a0 jest as I went Set off with the canoe the bank fell in & all most filled it. \u00a0\u00a0 we directly took out the Sand & bailed out the water and proceeded on \u00a0\u00a0 towards evening Capt. Clark and Several more of the party killed a verry large bair which the natives and the french tradors call white but all of the kind that we have seen is of a light brown only owing to the climate as we suppose. \u00a0\u00a0 we shot him as he was Swimming the River. \u00a0\u00a0 the place where he dyed was Shallow or perhaps he would have Sunk to the bottom. \u00a0\u00a0 with the assistance of Several men was got on board a perogue and took him to the Shore on N. S. and dressed it after taking the measure of him. \u00a0\u00a0 he was verry old \u00a0\u00a0 the tushes most wore out as well as his claws. \u00a0\u00a0 the measure of the brown bair is as follows \u00a0\u00a0 round the head is 3 feet 5 Inches. \u00a0\u00a0 do the neck 3 feet 11 Inches \u00a0\u00a0 do the breast 5 feet 10\u00bd Inches. \u00a0\u00a0 do the middle of the arm 1 foot 11 Inches. \u00a0\u00a0 the length from the nose to the extremity of the hind toe is 8 feet 7\u00bd Inches. \u00a0\u00a0 the length of tallons better than four feet [inches]. \u00a0\u00a0 we found a Cat fish in him which he had Eat. \u00a0\u00a0 we Camped and rendered out about 6 gallons of the greese of the brown bair. \u00a0\u00a0 he was judged to weigh about 4 hundred after dressed. \u00a0\u00a0 one of the party went out and killed an Elk, and Saw another brown bair. \u00a0\u00a0 we Came 16 miles to day before we killed the brown bair. \u00a0\u00a0 we Sailed considerable in the course of the day with an East wind.\n\n\n

[Gass]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Sunday 5th. \u00a0\u00a0 The morning was fine with some white frost. During this day the country appeared beautiful on both sides of the river. We went sixteen miles and encamped on the North side. The sick man has become better. Here \nwe killed a very large brown bear, which measured three feet five inches round the head; three feet eleven inches around the neck; round the breast five feet 10\u00bd inches; the length eight feet 7\u00bd inches; round the middle of the fore leg 23 inches; and his talons four inches and three eights of an inch.\n\n\n

[Whitehouse]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Sunday 5th May 1805. \u00a0\u00a0 Clear and pleasant. \u00a0\u00a0 we Set off eairly. \u00a0\u00a0 one of the hunters lay on the S. S. last night, joined us at breakfast time \u00a0\u00a0 had killed two buffaloe Calfs. \u00a0\u00a0 we proceeded on. \u00a0\u00a0 Saw buffaloe Elk Deer & goats on each side of the River. \u00a0\u00a0 passed bottoms and plains on each side. \u00a0\u00a0 at 12 oC. we Saw 4 bair on a Sand beach on S. S. \u00a0\u00a0 passed a handsom large plain on the N. S. \u00a0\u00a0 we halted to dine on the South Side at a bottom of timber. \u00a0\u00a0 our officers Gave out to the party a half a Gill of ardent Spirits. \u00a0\u00a0 we Saw buffaloe and flocks of Goats. \u00a0\u00a0 Jo. Fields verry Sick. [7] \u00a0\u00a0 we proceeded on. \u00a0\u00a0 towards evening, we killed a verry large bair in the River. \u00a0\u00a0 the Natives call it white but it is of a light brown coulour \u00a0\u00a0 the measuer of the brown bair is as follows. \u00a0\u00a0 round the head is three-feet 5 Inches, do the neck 3 feet 11 Inches, do the breast 5 feet 10\u00bd Inches. \u00a0\u00a0 also round the middle of the arm one foot 11 inches. \u00a0\u00a0 the length from the nose to the extremity of the hind toe is 8 feet 7\u00bd Inches. \u00a0\u00a0 the length of tallons 4 feet \u00be Inches. \u00a0\u00a0 his teeth or tuskes were allmost worn out. \u00a0\u00a0 the toe nales ware worn Short. \u00a0\u00a0 when we got him to Shore we halted for the night on the N. S. dressed the sd. brown bair found a fish in him, which he had caught & eat. \u00a0\u00a0 we rendred out about 6 Gallons \nof Greese and did not render only a part of it \u00a0\u00a0 he was not fat but reckened to be about 600 weight as he was killed. \u00a0\u00a0 one of the hunters went out and killed an elk & Saw another bair nearly of the Same discription. \u00a0\u00a0 Came \n16 miles this day.

\n

\nSunday May 5th \u00a0\u00a0 We had a Clear and pleasant weather, We set out Early this morning, and proceeded on our Voyage; we stoped at 8 o'Clock in Order to break fast on the South side of the River, where we were join'd by one of our hunters who had laid out on that shore all night, he had killed two buffalo calves which were brought to us\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 At 9 oClock A. M we set out again and proceeded on; and saw Buffalo, Elk, & deer in gangs in the Priaries, & Goats in large flocks on the Hills on both sides of the River, and some bottom lands, likewise lying on both sides of the River, At 12 o'Clock A. M. we saw 4 Bear on a Sand beach which lay on the South side of the River, and passed some handsome plains lying on the North side, At one o'Clock P. M the party halted to dine, in a bottom of Timber'd land lying on the South shore, here our Officers gave each Man of the party, half a \nGill of Ardent Spirits, at this place we saw flocks of Goats & Gangs of Buffalo in abundance on both sides of the River.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 One of our party named \u2329Shield\u232a John Fields, was taken very Sick at this place.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 At 3 oClock P. M. we got under way, and proceeded on 'till towards Evening, and saw a very large bear in the River.\u2014

\n

\nThe Men who belonged to the party, that was on board the foremost Pettyauger, shot at; and killed this bear; and got him near the shore on the North side of the River, he was one of those called by the Natives, the white bear, altho' his Colour was a light brown.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 As he was by far the largest of the kind, we had seen, Our officers on their arrival; had his dimensions taken which were as follows.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 Round the head three feet 5 Inches, Round the neck, Three feet 11 Inches; Round the breast five feet 10\u00bd Inches, Round the middle of the Arm (or fore foot) One foot 11 Inches, The length of this animal from the Nose to the extremity of his hind Claws 8 ft. 7\u00bd Inches \u00a0\u00a0 His Talons 5 Inches, his Tushes were all nearly worn off, and his \nNails on the fore feet, worn short, The Officers order'd the Pettyaugers to put too at this place and we encamp'd in Order to Skin and cut up this bear & get it on the Shore, it nearly took our whole party to hawl him up \u2329which lay\u232a on the North side of the River, in a bottom covered with Timber,\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 We dressed this huge animal & found in him a large fish which was fresh & which we supposed he had caught & eat at the time our Men shot him, We got from part of the fat taken out of this Bear, Six Gallons of oil, when melted, he was not so very fat, but at the nearest calculation weigh'd Six hundred pounds Nett weight\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 One of our hunters went out a hunting, and killed an Elk, which was brought to our Camp, he mention'd that he had seen on his Route, another Bear, of the same kind, as the One we had killed, and full as large.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 The distance that we come this day was 16 Miles.\u2014\n\n\n

1. \n Lewis notices snow geese and brant. His common goose is the Canada goose, used for comparing what was probably the lesser Canada, or tundra, goose, Branta canadensis leucopareia [AOU, 172.1], the cackling goose, B. c. minima [AOU, 172.2], or Hutchins's goose B. c. hutchinsii [AOU, 172.3]. All are now grouped as a single species. Clark copied this passage verbatim in his entry for May 26, 1805. Lewis gives a bit more detail on March 6, 1806. Burroughs, 195\u201396; Holmgren, 30; Cutright (LCPN), 430. Someone drew vertical lines through this and the following natural history passage in this entry, perhaps in red. (back)
\n
2. The snow goose, noted in detail on March 8, 1806. Lewis was correct about their breeding habits; they breed in northern Alaska and arctic Canada. (back)
\n
3. The coyote and gray wolf. The latter probably Canis lupus nubilis and now extinct. Both may be Lewis and Clark discoveries. Burroughs, 84\u201389; Cutright (LCPN), 87, 440. (back)
\n
4. Also given on Atlas map 36, in both captains' hands, with an incorrect total of 16\u00bc. (back)
\n
5. In McCone County, Montana, southeast of the present town of Wolf Point. Due to shifts in the river the camp is now apparently on the opposite side from Lewis and Clark's time and a mile or so from the river. The legend \"Encamped 5th May 1805\" is misplaced on Atlas map 57, but the conventional campsite symbol is in the right place. Atlas maps 36, 49; MRC map 63. (back)
\n
6. Probably the snow goose, Chen caerulescens. (back)
\n
7. See Lewis's entry for May 4 for Field's symptoms and treatment. (back)
