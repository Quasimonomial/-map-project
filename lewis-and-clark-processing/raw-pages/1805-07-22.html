\n July 22, 1805\n

\n
\n \n

[Lewis]

\n \n
\n Monday July 22cd 1805.\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nWe set out early as usual. The river being divided into such a number of channels by both large and small Island that I found it impossible to lay it down correctly following one channel only in a canoe and therefore walked on shore took the general courses of the river and from the rising grounds took a view of the Islands and it's different channels which I laid don in conformity thereto on my chart. \u00a0\u00a0 there being but little timber to obstruct my view I could see it's various meanders very satisfactorily. I passed though a large island which I found a beautifull level and fertile plain about 10 feet above the surface of the water and never overflown. \u00a0\u00a0 on this Island I [NB: We] met with great quantities of a smal onion [1] about the size of a musquit ball and some even larger; they were white crisp and well flavored \u00a0\u00a0 I geathered about half a bushel of them before the canoes arrived. I halted the party for breakfast and the men also geathered considerable quantities of those onions. \u00a0\u00a0 it's seed had just arrived to maturity and I gathered a good quantity of it. This appears to be a valuable plant inasmuch as it produces a large quantity to the squar foot and bears with ease the rigor of this climate, and withall I think it as pleasantly flavored as any species of that root I ever tasted. I called this beatifull and fertile island after this plant Onion Island. \u00a0\u00a0 here I passed over to the stard. shore where the country was higher and ascended the river to the entrance of a large creek which discharges itself into the Missouri on the Stard. side. \u00a0\u00a0 it is composed of three pretty considerable creeks which unite in a beautifull and extensive vally a few miles before it discharges itself into the river. [2] \u00a0\u00a0 while wateing for the canoes to arrive I killed an otter which sunk to the bottom on being shot, a circumstance unusual with that anamal. \u00a0\u00a0 the water was about 8 feet deep yet so clear that I could see it at the bottom; I swam in and obtained it by diving. I halted the party here for dinner; the canoes had taken different channels through these islands and it was sometime before they all came up. I placed my thermometer in a good shade as was my custom about 4 P. M. and after dinner set out without it and had proceeded near a mile before I recollected it \u00a0\u00a0 I sent Sergt. Ordway back for it, he found it and brought it on. \u00a0\u00a0 the murcury stood at 80 a. 0 \u00a0\u00a0 this is the warmest day except one which we have experienced this summer. The Indian woman recognizes the country and assures us that this is the river on which her relations live, and that the three forks are at no great distance. \u00a0\u00a0 this peice of information has cheered the sperits of the party who now begin to console themselves with the anticipation of shortly seeing the head of the missouri yet unknown to the civilized world. \u00a0\u00a0 the large creek which we passed on Stard. 15 yds. we call white Earth Creek \u2329in honour of Sergt. Nathaniel pryor who is a steady valuable and usefull member of our party\u232a from the circumstance of the natives procuring a white paint on this crek.\u2014 [3] \u00a0\u00a0 Saw many gees, crains, and small birds common to the plains, also a few phesants and a species of small curlooe or plover [4] of a brown colour which I first met with near the entrance of Smith's river but they are so shy and watchfull there is no possibility of geting a shoot at them \u00a0\u00a0 it is a different kind from any heretofore discribed and is about the size of the yellow leged plover or jack Curlooe. \u00a0\u00a0 both species of the willow that of the broad leaf and narrow leaf still continue, the sweet willow is very scarce. \u00a0\u00a0 the rose bush, small honesuckle, the pulpy leafed thorn, southernwood, sage [5] Box alder narrow leafed cottonwood, red wod, a species of sumac are all found in abundance as well as the red and black goosberries, service berries, choke cherries and the currants of four distinct colours of black, yellow, red and perple. [6] \u00a0\u00a0 the cherries are not yet ripe. \u00a0\u00a0 the bear appear to feed much on the currants. \u00a0\u00a0 late this evening we arrived at Capt. Carks camp on the stard. side of the river; we took them on board with the meat they had collected and proceeded a short distance and encamped on an Island [7] \u00a0\u00a0 Capt. Clark's party had killed a deer and an Elk today and ourselves one deer and an Antelope only. \u00a0\u00a0 altho' Capt C. was much fatiegued his feet yet blistered and soar he insisted [NB: deturmined] on pursuing his rout in the morning nor weould he consent willingly to my releiving him at that time by taking a tour of the same kind. \u00a0\u00a0 finding him anxious I readily consented to remain with the canoes; he ordered Frazier and Jo. & Reubin Filds to hold themselves in readiness to accompany him in the morning. Sharbono was anxious to accompany him and was accordingly permitted. \u00a0\u00a0 the musquetoes and knats more than usually troublesome to us this evening.\u2014

[Clark]

\n \n
\n July 22ct Monday 1805\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\t\na fine morning wind from the S. E. \u00a0\u00a0 the last night verry cold, my blanket being Small I lay on the grass & Covered with it. I opened the bruses & blisters of my feet which caused them to be painfull \u00a0\u00a0 dispatched all the men to hunt in the bottom for Deer, deturmined my Self to lay by & nurs my feet. \u00a0\u00a0 haveing nothing to eat but venison and Currents, I find my Self much weaker than when I left the Canoes and more inclined to rest & repose to day. These men were not Suckcessfull in hunting killed only one Deer \u00a0\u00a0 Capt Lewis & the Party arvd. at 4 oClock & we all proceeded on a Short distance and Camped on an Island \u00a0\u00a0 the Musquitors verry troublesom this evening \u00a0\u00a0 G Drewyer not knowing the place we Camped Continued on up the river. I deturmined to proceed on in pursute of the Snake Indians on tomorrow and directed Jo\n Rubin Fields\n Frasure to get ready to accompany me. Shabono, our interpreter requested to go, which was granted &c. In my absence the hunters had killed Some Deer & a Elk, one fusee found &c. &c.\n\n

[Ordway]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n July 22nd Monday 1805. \u00a0\u00a0 a clear morning. \u00a0\u00a0 we Set out as usal and proceeded on \u00a0\u00a0 passed large Islands mostly covered with grass \u00a0\u00a0 Some fiew trees. \u00a0\u00a0 the currents [currants] Still abound. Some of the canoes I being one went about 5 miles behind an Island through a verry narrow crooked channel. \u00a0\u00a0 these large Islands are mostly level Smooth plain. \u00a0\u00a0 one of the hunters killed a deer. \u00a0\u00a0 about 2 oC. P. m. we halted to dine on the N or Stard. Side then went on \u00a0\u00a0 Capt. Lewis forgot his Thurmometer where we dined \u00a0\u00a0 I went back for it. \u00a0\u00a0 it Stood in the heat of the day at 80 degrees abo. 0, which\nhas only been up to that point but once before this Season as yet. \u00a0\u00a0 we went on \u00a0\u00a0 took a narrow channel behind Some Islands and at Camping time I came out a head of the party nearly a mile, then went down to Camp. [8] \u00a0\u00a0 Capt. Clark had joined them, and his men who were with him. \u00a0\u00a0 they had Seen a great deal of Indian sign \u00a0\u00a0 the fire we saw was made by the natives. \u00a0\u00a0 perhaps they were alarmed by our Shooting So at the game and moved off. \u00a0\u00a0 our Intrepters wife tells us that She knows the country along the River up to hir nation, or the 3 forks. [9] \u00a0\u00a0 we are now 166 miles from the falls of the M. \u00a0\u00a0 Came 17 miles of it to day.\u2014\n\n\n

[Gass]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Monday 22nd. \u00a0\u00a0 We embarked early, the weather being pleasant: passed some fine springs on the southern shore, and a large island near the northern: On the south side the country is level to a good distance, but on the north the hills come close to the river. At breakfast our squaw informed us she had been at this place before when small. Here we got a quantity of wild onions. At half past 9, we proceeded on again; passed a large island at noon; and in the afternoon, more islands: and came to a place where Captain Clarke and his party were encamped. They told us they had seen the same smoke, which we had discovered a few days ago, and found it had been made by the natives, who they supposed had seen some of us, and had fled, taking us for enemies. We went 17 miles and an half and encamped on an island; where we found the musquitoes very bad. We saw to day several banks of snow on a mountain west of us. [10]\n

[Whitehouse]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Monday 22nd July 1805. \u00a0\u00a0 a clear pleasant morning. \u00a0\u00a0 We Set out as usal and proceeded on \u00a0\u00a0 passed verry large Islands covered with grass a fiew trees. \u00a0\u00a0 a great many currents. \u00a0\u00a0 we took a narrow channel behind an Isd. which was about 5 miles through. \u00a0\u00a0 we began to think that we had taken an other River, but proved to the contrary. \u00a0\u00a0 Some ceeder on Some of these Islands. \u00a0\u00a0 passd. 2 large Islds which level and all prarie or plain. \u00a0\u00a0 one of the men killed a Deer in a plain on N. S. \u00a0\u00a0 about 2 oClock we halted to dine on the N. S. then went on \u00a0\u00a0 Capt. Lewis forgot his Thurmometer which he had hung in a Shade. \u00a0\u00a0 it Stood today at 80 degrees above 0. \u00a0\u00a0 I went back and got it then went on after the party. [11] \u00a0\u00a0 passed Several Islands covered with cotton & ceeder timber. \u00a0\u00a0 the River divides in many Channels. \u00a0\u00a0 I took a near cut and at night came out ahead of the party, and went down to the Camp. \u00a0\u00a0 Capt. Clark had joined them & his party. \u00a0\u00a0 they were all well and had Seen a great deal of Indian Sign along the River and a fire which was burning. \u00a0\u00a0 we expect the Snake Indians or a party of them are near this. \u00a0\u00a0 perhaps they are \nalarmed at our firing at the game &c. \u00a0\u00a0 we Came 17\u00bd miles this day thro a verry rapid current and a pleasant country. \u00a0\u00a0 a pleanty of ripe currents &c. along the Shores. \u00a0\u00a0 our Intrepters wife knows the country along the River up to hir nation at the 3 forks. \u00a0\u00a0 we are now 166 miles from the falls of the Missourie.\u2014

\n

\nMonday July 22nd \u00a0\u00a0 A clear pleasant morning, we set out Early, and proceeded on, and passed a very large Island, which was covered with grass & had a few trees growing on it, We found growing along the shores of the River, a great quantity of Currants; We took into a narrow Channell, of the River which run behind an Island, which was about 5 Miles through, Our party began to think before we had passed through this Channel, that we had taken the wrong River, For The whole of these 5 Miles, \u2329had\u232a we saw a \nnumber of Islands lying a small distance from each other, 2 of which was very large. \u00a0\u00a0 They lay very level and were all Priaries.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 We were all well pleased when we passed the last of these Islands, and found that we entered into the \nRiver, which appear'd the same as when we took this Channel, One of our party killed a Deer on the North side of the River, which we took on board our Canoe,\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 Captain Lewis had forgot his Thermometer which he had hung in a Shade, It stood this day, at 80 degrees above 0. \u00a0\u00a0 I was sent back for it, and got it, I then proceeded on after our party, I passed several Islands, which were cover'd with Cotton wood & Cedar timber, the River divided in a number of Channels,\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 I took a near cut to the River, and at Night got to it, but was ahead of our party, I went down the River to where they were encamped.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 Captain Clark, and a party of our Men, had during my absence arrived, and were all well,\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 They mentioned that they had seen a great deal of Indian sign along the River,\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 and a fire which from its burning, appeared to have been lately left, our party expect that fire was made by a party of the Snake Indians, who they suppose is near this place, and that they are alarmed at our party firing at Game &ca. \u00a0\u00a0 We came 17\u00bd Miles this day \u2329and\u232a through a rapid Current.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 We passed this day through a very pleasant Country abounding with Currants &ca.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 which grows along the Shores on both sides\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 Our Interpreters Wife (the Indian woman) informed us, that she knows this Country, along the Shores of the River, up to her nation, (which are the Snake Indians) who she says lives at the 3 forks of this River.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 We are now 166 Miles distant from the great falls of the Mesouri River.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 These Islands we passed this day we named Whitehouses Islands.\n\n\n

1. Possibly Allium cernuum Roth, nodding onion, or A. geyeri S. Wats., Geyer's onion, or A. canadense L. Hahn, Allium maps; Cutright (LCPN), 401: Dorn, 161. It was probably Biddle who drew a red vertical line through this passage about the onion. \"Onion Isd.\" appears on Atlas map 63. (back)
\n
2. \n Beaver Creek, Broadwater County, Montana (see above, July 20, 1805). The streams are present Antelope, Staubach, and Beaver creeks. Atlas map 63; MRC map 81. (back)
\n
3. \n Beaver Creek, Broadwater County. The crossed out portion concerning the name is undoubtedly related in some way to Clark's mislabeling this stream \"Pryors Vally R or C\" on Atlas map 63. The words \"white Earth\" preceding the deletion were written over an erasure. (back)
\n
4. Probably the same bird mentioned briefly on July 14, 1805. It may be either the mountain plover, Charadrius montanus [AOU, 281], or the upland sandpiper (also called upland plover or Bartram's sandpiper), Bartramia longicauda [AOU, 261]. Burroughs, 226, 228; Holmgren, 33. The \"jack Curlooe\" mentioned for comparison may be the greater yellowlegs. Cf. May 9, 1805. It was probably Biddle who drew a red vertical line through the passages about natural history here, to the words \"the currants.\" (back)
\n
5. It is unclear whether two different species of Artemisia are being identified here or just the big sagebrush, A. tridentata, named earlier as hyssop sage, or wild hyssop. If two species then the southernwood may be A. cana, silver sagebrush. See Lewis's earlier description of April 14, 1805. (back)
\n
6. \n Lewis is probably seeing three currants: Ribes americanum (black), R. cereum (red), and R. aureum (yellow and purple). See entries of April 30 and June 18, 1805. (back)
\n
7. \n Atlas map 63 appears to place this camp on the larboard side of the river, near an island. In any case the site was in Broadwater County, Montana, a few miles upstream from Beaver (White Earth) Creek, and is now under Canyon Ferry Lake. MRC map 81. (back)
\n
8. In Broadwater County, Montana, a few miles upstream from Beaver (the party's White Earth) Creek, on a site now under Canyon Ferry Lake. (back)
\n
9. Meaning the Three Forks of the Missouri River, Gallatin County, Montana. (back)
\n
10. \n Elkhorn Mountains. (back)
\n
11. \n Lewis and Ordway say it was Ordway who recovered the thermometer. (back)
