\n
\n \n

[Clark]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n 5th of October Friday 1804 \u00a0\u00a0 Frost this morning, Set out early \u00a0\u00a0 passed a Small Creek on the L. S. \u00a0\u00a0 saw 3 Tetons on the S. S. \u00a0\u00a0 they beged Some Tobacco, we proceed on \u00a0\u00a0 passed a Creek on the S. S. [1] \u00a0\u00a0 I Saw a white brant in a gangue on the Sand bar [2] \u00a0\u00a0 Saw a large herd of Cabra or antelopes Swiming the River, we Killed four of them \u00a0\u00a0 passed a Small Island on the L. S. \u00a0\u00a0 a large Creek on the L. S. at the head of the Island White Brant Creek, [3] I walked on the Island which is covered with wild rye, [4] I Killed a Buck & a Small wolf this evening, Clear pleasant evening, Camped on a mud bar S. S. [5] \u00a0\u00a0 refreshd the men with whiskey.

[Clark]

\n \n
5th of October Friday 1804
\u00a0
\n \n

\nFrost this morning, we Set out early and proceeded on \u00a0\u00a0 (1) passed a Small Creek on the L. S. \u00a0\u00a0 at 7 oclock heard Some yels \u00a0\u00a0 proceeded on Saw 3 Indians of the Teton band, they called to us to Come on Shore, beged Some Tobacco, we answd. them as usial and proceeded on, \u00a0\u00a0 passed (2) a Creek on the S. S. \u00a0\u00a0 at \u2329the\u232a 3 mes. abov the mouth we Saw one white \u2329goose\u232a Brant in a gang of about 30, the others all as dark as usial, a Discription of this kind of Gees or Brant Shall be given here after \u00a0\u00a0 Saw a Gang of Goats Swiming across the river out of which we killed four \u00a0\u00a0 they were not fatt. \u00a0\u00a0 in the evening passed a Small (3) Island Situated Close to the L. Side, at the head of this Isd. a large Creek coms in on the L. S. \u00a0\u00a0 Saw white \u2329Gees\u232a or Brants, we Call this Creek white \u2329gees\u232a Brant Creek\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 I walked on the Isd. \u00a0\u00a0 found it Covered with wild rye, I Shot a Buck, Saw a large gang of Goat on the hills opposit, one Buck killed, also a Prarie wolf this evening, the high Land not So high as below, river about the Same width, the Sand bars as noumerous, the earth Black and many of the Bluffs have the appearance of being on fire, [6] we Came too and Camped on a mud bar makeing from the L. S. \u00a0\u00a0 The evening is Calm and pleasant, refreshed the men with a glass of whiskey\u2014

[Ordway]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Friday 5th Oct. 1804. \u00a0\u00a0 a white frost this morning. Clear & Cool. \u00a0\u00a0 we Set off eairly. \u00a0\u00a0 passed Some wood in a bottom S. S. See Several Indians on the Shore on N. S. \u00a0\u00a0 at 11 oClock we Saw a flock of Goats Swimming the River towards the South Shore. \u00a0\u00a0 one of our hunters ran up the Shore & killed 4 of them \u00a0\u00a0 we took them on board the Boat & pearogues. \u00a0\u00a0 passed a Creek on N. S. called white Goat Creek. [7] \u00a0\u00a0 passd black Bluffs on S. S. \u00a0\u00a0 we halted took dinner at a Timbered bottom S. S. below an Island. \u00a0\u00a0 dressed & took care of our Goat meat as we had no other fresh meat on hand. \u00a0\u00a0 found it to be verry Sweet Good meat. \u00a0\u00a0 proceeded on passing the Island \u00a0\u00a0 we killed a Small prarie woolf Swimming the River. \u00a0\u00a0 passed high Black Bluff on N. S. & a large Bottom covered with Timber. Capt. Clark & 2 of the hunters went out hunting\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 we passed the Bottom & Camped on N. S. Capt Clark & the\nrest of the hunters returned. \u00a0\u00a0 had killed & brought in a Deer.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 had killed or wounded 2 more but did not get them.\n\n\n

[Gass]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Friday 5th. This morning there was a white frost; the day clear and pleasant. About 11 we saw some goats swimming the river, when one of our hunters ran up the shore and killed four of them, and we took them into the boat and periogues as they floated down. We passed a creek on the north side, called Hidden creek, and high black bluffs on the south side. [8] Some of our hunters having gone on an island to hunt scared a prairie wolf into the river, which we killed. We passed a creek on the south side called White Goat \ncreek, and encamped on the north side.\n\n\n

[Whitehouse]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nFriday 5th Oct. 1804. [9] \u00a0\u00a0 Set off eairly \u00a0\u00a0 Some whight frost last night. \u00a0\u00a0 the day clear and pleasant. \u00a0\u00a0 about 11 oClock we Saw Some Goats Swimming the river. \u00a0\u00a0 one of our hunters Shot 4 of them. \u00a0\u00a0 passed a creek on the N. S. \u00a0\u00a0 \ncalled hidden Creek. \u00a0\u00a0 we killed a prarie wolf Swimming in the river \u00a0\u00a0 passed a creek on the S. S. called whight Goat creek. [10] \u00a0\u00a0 Camped on the S. Side.

\n

\nFriday October 5th \u00a0\u00a0 This morning we had a White frost, we set out early, the day being clear & pleasant; we proceeded on about 11 o'Clock A. M. we saw some Goats swimming the River, one of our Hunters pursued them in a Pettyauger & killed four of them, which was brought on board\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 We continued on, and passed a Creek, lying on the North side of the River called hidden creek \u00a0\u00a0 Shortly after we saw a Priari Wolf swimming across the River, which One of the Men Shot & we got on board\u2014

\n

\nWe proceeded on, and passed a Creek, lying on the South side of the River, called white Goat Creek, and encamped in the Evening on the South side of the River\u2014\n\n\n

\n
1. Now the Little Cheyenne River, otherwise Cheyenne Creek, reaching the Missouri in Potter County, South Dakota. It appears as \"Hidden Creek\" on Atlas map 24. MRC map 43; MRY map 92. (back)
\n
2. The captains' references to \"brant\" are often obscure, especially since brant and geese often migrate in mixed flocks. The white brant here may be the snow goose, Chen caerulescens [AOU, 169]. The darker birds mentioned in Codex C, below, may be the brant, Branta bernicla [AOU, 173). The darker birds may not be brant, however, since they are uncommon in South Dakota. It is not clear what bird Clark saw, but it may have been a \"blue\" phase snow goose. Coues (HLC), 1:154 n. 22; Burroughs, 192\u201393. (back)
\n
3. This creek, in Dewey County, South Dakota, retained the same name late in the nineteenth century, according to MRC map 43; more recently it was Swift Bird Creek. Atlas map 24; MRY map 93. (back)
\n
4. \n Elymus canadensis L., Canada wild rye. Barkley, 489. (back)
\n
5. In Potter County, in an area now inundated by Oahe Reservoir. Atlas map 24; Mattison (OR), 71\u201372; MRC map 43. (back)
\n
6. Pierre Shale (see entries of August 24 and September 14, 1804, for a discussion of \"fire\"). (back)
\n
7. \n Clark's\n White Brant Creek; it is today's Swift Bird Creek, Dewey County, South Dakota. (back)
\n
8. \n McKeehan's note: \"To prevent mistakes, owing to the very winding course of the river, Starboard side and Larboard side were made use of in the original journal, instead of north side and south side; during the remainder of the voyage up the Missouri; but have been changed to north side and south side, as being better understood, and sufficiently representing the general course of the river.\" (back)
\n
9. The \"4\" in 1804 is written over either a \"5\" or \"6.\" (back)
\n
10. If this is Whitehouse's hand, and it appears no different from other writing of the journalist designated No. 1, he has misspelled \"white.\" Clark called it White Brant Creek, a name it retained until late in the nineteenth century; it is now Swift Bird Creek, Dewey County, South Dakota. (back)
