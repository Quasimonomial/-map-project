\n March 29, 1806\n

\n
\n \n

[Lewis]

\n \n
\n Saturday March 29th 1806.\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nWe set out early this morning and proceeded along the side of Deer Island; halted at 10 A. M. near its upper point and breakfasted. \u00a0\u00a0 here we were joined by three men of the \n Clan-nah-min-na-mun\n nation. [1] \u00a0\u00a0 the upper point of this Island may be esteemed the lower side or commencement of the Columbian valley. \u00a0\u00a0 after breakfast we proceeded on and at the distance of 14 miles from our encampment of the last evening we passed a large inlet 300 yds in width. [2] \u00a0\u00a0 this inlet or arm of the river extends itself to the South 10 or 12 M. to the hills on that side of the river and receives the waters of a small creek [3] which heads with killamucks river, and that of a bayau which passes out of the Columbia about 20 miles above, the large Island thus formed we call wappetoe island. [4] \u00a0\u00a0 on this inlet and Island the following nations reside, (viz) Clan-nah-min-na-mun, Clacks-star, [5]\n Cath-lah-cum-up, Cl\u00e2h-in-na-ta, Cath-lah-nah-qui-ah, and Cath-lah-cam-mah-tup. [6] \u00a0\u00a0 the two first reside on the inlet and the others on the bayau and island.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 observed a speceies of small wild onion [7] growing among the moss on the rocks, they resemble the shives of our gardens and grow remarkably close together forming a perfect turf; they are quite as agreeably flavoured as the shives. \u00a0\u00a0 on the North side of the columbia a little above the entrance of this inlet a considerable river discharges itself. \u00a0\u00a0 this stream the natives call the \n Cah-wah-na-hi-ooks. \u00a0\u00a0 it is 150 yards wide and at present discharges a large body of water, tho' from the information of the same people it is not navigable but a short distance in consequence of falls and rappids a tribe called the Hul-lu-et-tell reside on this river above it's entr.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 at the distance of three miles above the entrance of the inlet on the N. side behind the lower point of an island we arrived at the village of the Cath [X: Quath]-lah-poh-tle wich consists of 14 large wooden houses. [8] \u00a0\u00a0 here we arrived at 3 P. M. \u00a0\u00a0 the language of these people as well as those on the inlet and wappetoe Island differs in some measure from the nations on the lower part of the river. \u00a0\u00a0 tho' many of their words are the same, and a great many others with the difference only of accent. \u00a0\u00a0 the form of their houses and dress of the men, manner of living habits customs &c as far as we could discover are the same. \u00a0\u00a0 their women wear their ornaments robes and hair as those do below tho' [NB: Indian women on Wappato Island & in that Valey] [9] here their hair is more frequently braded in two tresses and hang over each ear in front of the body. \u00a0\u00a0 in stead of the tissue of bark woarn by the women below, they wear a kind of leather breech clout about the width of a common pocket handkerchief and reather longer. \u00a0\u00a0 the two corners of this at one of the narrow ends are confined in front just above the hips; the other end is then brought between the legs, compressed into a narrow foalding bundel is drawn tight and the corners a little spread in front and tucked at the groin over and arround the part first confind about the waist. \u00a0\u00a0 the small robe which dose not reach the waist is their usual and only garment commonly woarn be side that just mentioned. \u00a0\u00a0 when the weather is a litte warm this robe is thrown aside and the leather truss or breech-clout constitutes the whole of their apparel. \u00a0\u00a0 this is a much more indecent article than the tissue of bark, and bearly covers the mons venes, [10] to which it is drawn so close that the whole shape is plainly perceived. \u00a0\u00a0 the floors of most of their houses are on a level with the surface of the earth tho' some of them are sunk two or 3 feet beneath. \u00a0\u00a0 the internal arrangement of their houses is the same with those of the nations below. \u00a0\u00a0 they are also fond of sculpture. \u00a0\u00a0 various figures are carved and painted on the peices which support the center of the roof, about their doors and beads. \u00a0\u00a0 they had large quantities of dryed Anchovies strung on small sticks by the gills and others which had been first dryed in this manner, were now arranged in large sheets with strings of bark and hung suspended by poles in the roofs of their houses; [11] they had also an abundance of sturgeon and wappetoe; the latter they take in great quantities from the neighbouring bonds, which are numerous and extensive in the river bottoms and islands. \u00a0\u00a0 the wappetoe furnishes the principal article of traffic with these people which they dispose of to the nations below in exchange for beads cloth and various articles. \u00a0\u00a0 the natives of the Sea coast and lower part of the river will dispose of their most valuable articles to obtain this root. \u00a0\u00a0 they have a number of large symeters [12] of Iron from 3 to 4 feet long which hang by the heads of their beads; the blade of this weapon is thickest in the center tho' thin even there. \u00a0\u00a0 all it's edges are sharp and it's greatest width which is about 9 inches from the point is about 4 inches. \u00a0\u00a0 the form is thus. [13] \u00a0\u00a0 this is a formidable weapon. \u00a0\u00a0 they have heavy bludegeons of wood made in the same form nearly which I presume they used for the same purpose before they obtained metal. [14] we purchased a considerable quantity of wappetoe, 12 dogs, and 2 Sea otter skins of these people. \u00a0\u00a0 they were very hospitable and gave us anchovies and wappetoe to eat. \u00a0\u00a0 notwithstanding their hospitality if it deserves that appellation, they are great begers, for we had scarcely finished our repast on the wappetoe and Anchovies which they voluntarily set before us before they began to beg. \u00a0\u00a0 we gave them some small articles as is our custom on those occasions with which they seemed perfectly satisfyed. \u00a0\u00a0 we gave the 1st Cheif a small medal, which he soon transfered to his wife. \u00a0\u00a0 after remaining at this place 2 hours we set out & continued our rout between this island, which we now call Cath-lah-poh-tle after the nation, and the Lard shore. \u00a0\u00a0 at the distance of 2 miles we encamped in a small prarie on the main shore, having traveled 19 miles by estimate. [15] the river rising fast. \u00a0\u00a0 great numbers of both the large and small swans, gees and ducks seen today. \u00a0\u00a0 the former are very abundant in the ponds where the wappetoe is found, they feed much on this bulb. \u00a0\u00a0 the female of the duck [16] which was described yesterday is of a uniform dark brown with some yellowish brown intermixed in small specks on the back neck and breast. \u00a0\u00a0 the garter snakes [17] are innumerable, & are seen entwined arround each other in large bundles of forty of fifty lying about in different directions through the praries. \u00a0\u00a0 the frogs [18] are croaking in the swams and marhes; their notes do not differ from those of the Atlantic States; they are not found in the salt marshes near the entrance of the river. \u00a0\u00a0 heared a large hooting owl [19] hollowing this evening. \u00a0\u00a0 saw several of the crested fishers [20] and some of the large and small black-birds.\u2014 [21]\n

[Clark]

\n \n
\n Saturday March 29th 1806\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nwe Set out very early this morning and proceeded to the head of deer island and took brackfast. \u00a0\u00a0 the morning was very cold wind Sharp and keen off the rainge of Mountains to the East Covered with snow. \u00a0\u00a0 the river is now riseing very fast and retards our progress very much as we are compelled to keep out at Some distance in the Curent to clear the bushes, and fallin trees and drift logs makeing out from the Shore. \u00a0\u00a0 dureing the time we were at Brackfast a Canoe with three Indians of the \n Clan-nar-min-na-mon\n Nation came down, one of those men was dressed in a Salors Jacket & hat & the other two had a blanket each, those people differ but little either in their dress manners & Language from the Clatsops & Chinnooks \u00a0\u00a0 they reside on Wappato Inlet which is on the S W. side about 12 miles above our encampment of the last night and is about 2 miles from the lower point, four other Tribes also reside on the inlet and Sluce which passes on the South W. Side of the Island, the first tribe from the lower point is the Clannarminamon, on the Island, the Clackster Nation on the main S. W. Shore. \u00a0\u00a0 the next \n Cath-lah-cum-up, Cl\u00e2h-in-na-ta, Cath-lah-nah-qui-ah\n and at Some distance further up is a tribe called \n Cath-lah-com-mah-up\n Those tribes all occupie Single Villages. we proceeded on to the lower point of the Said island accompanied by the 3 Indians, & were met by 2 canoes of nativs of the quath-lah-pah-tal who informed us that the chanel to the N E of the Island was the proper one. \u00a0\u00a0 we prosued their advice and Crossed into the mouth of the Chah-wah-na-hi-ooks River which is about 200 yards wide and a great portion of water into the columbia at this time it being high. The indians inform us that this river is crouded with rapids after Some distance up it. Several tribes of the \n Hul-lu-et-tell\n Nation reside on this river. \u00a0\u00a0 at 3 oClock P. M. we arived at the Quath lah pah tle Village of 14 Houses on main Shore to the N E. Side of a large island. \u00a0\u00a0 those people in their habits manners Customs and language differ but little from those of the Clatsops and others below. \u00a0\u00a0 here we exchanged our deer Skins killed yesterday for dogs, and purchased others to the Number of 12 for provisions for the party, as the deer flesh is too poore for the Men to Subsist on and work as hard as is necessary. I also purchased a Sea Otter robe. \u00a0\u00a0 we purchased wappatoe and Some pashaquar roots. \u00a0\u00a0 gave a Medal of the Small Size to the principal Chief, and at 5 oClock reembarked and proceeded up on the N E. of an Island to an inlet about 1 mile above the village and encamped on a butifull grassy plac, where the nativs make a portage of their Canoes and Wappato roots to and from a large pond at a Short distance. \u00a0\u00a0 in this pond the nativs inform us they Collect great quantities of pappato, which the womin collect by getting into the water, Sometimes to their necks holding by a Small canoe and with their feet loosen the wappato or bulb of the root from the bottom from the Fibers, and it imedeately rises to the top of the water, they Collect & throw them into the Canoe, those deep roots are the largest and best roots. Great numbers of the whistling Swan, Gees and Ducks in the Ponds. Soon after we landed 3 of the nativs came up with Wappato to Sell a part of which we purchased. \u00a0\u00a0 they Continued but a Short time. \u00a0\u00a0 our men are recoverey fast. Willard quit well & Bratten much Stronger. \u00a0\u00a0 we made 15 miles to day only.\n\n

[Ordway]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nSaturday 29th March 1806. \u00a0\u00a0 we Set out early and proceed. on. Saw blossom and wild onions [22] along the South Side \u00a0\u00a0 in the afternoon we passd the mo. of [blank] River [23] which came in on N. Side which is high and puts in with a Swift current. \u00a0\u00a0 little above we arived at the village of the [blank] nation [24] which is a large village and the most of their huts join. \u00a0\u00a0 this village is more decent than any I have Seen below. \u00a0\u00a0 we delayed at this village about 3 hours. Capt Clark bought a Robe which was made of 2 Sea otter Skins from the princepal man who he made a chief \u00a0\u00a0 Gave him a meddle. \u00a0\u00a0 he put it on his wife. Capt. Clark Gave the chief a blue blanket edged with red & Small also an old flag, which he was Satisfied with. \u00a0\u00a0 we bought Several fat dogs and some wa pa toes from the natives. \u00a0\u00a0 towards evening we proceed. on a Short distance further and Camped [25] on a handsom Green where had once been a village. Some of the natives visited us. \u00a0\u00a0 thier women instead of wearing the Straw & bark Short peticoats wear a Soft leather breach cloth \u00a0\u00a0 all above this Side of the Mountains are dressed in this way & nothing more to cover the most part of them, but those below on the coast wear the Short pticoats. Some among them all have a kind of a fur garment beaver &C. dressed and cut in narrow stripes & sewed together &C\u2014\n\n\n

[Gass]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Saturday 29th. \u00a0\u00a0 The morning was pleasant with some white frost and we proceeded on early; passed some old Indian lodges, and in the afternoon came to a large village, where we were received with great kindness, and got fish and wapto roots to eat. Here we bought some dogs and waptos, and then went on again, about a mile and encamped. One of the sick men is quite recovered and the other two are getting better. [26]\n

[Whitehouse]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nSaturday March 29th \u00a0\u00a0 We set out early this morning & proceeded on, the Columbia River being very high & the current running very swift. \u00a0\u00a0 in the afternoon we passed the Mouth of a River, which was large & the Water in it very high, and makes into the Columbia River with a greadt deal of rapidity; This River lies a small distance below a large Indian Village of the Kalute Tribe \u2329Nation\u232a & called the Kalute River & at which place we halted & purchased from the Natives 10 fat dogs. \u00a0\u00a0 Captain Clarke also purchased from these Indians a Sea Otter Skin robe, for which he gave, a small piece of blue Cloth, & part of an old flag. \u00a0\u00a0 he also purchased from them some dried fish, Wapatoes &ca \u00a0\u00a0 These Indians, are a much decenter looking sett of Natives, than those who reside on, or near the Sea Coast. \u00a0\u00a0 The women among them wore a soft leather Breech Cloth, which they drawed tight about their breech, & is tied with a belt, & comes up forward in the manner that a breech Cloth does, & those on the Coast & near it, wore a short pettycoat, made out of Straw. \u00a0\u00a0 The remainder of their bodies being exposed to the Weather.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 Our Officers made a Chief, of one of those Indians, and gave him a medal; which he gave to his wife. \u00a0\u00a0 These Kalute tribe of Indians, are a part of the flatt head Nation. \u00a0\u00a0 Towards evening we proceeded on our Voyage, & went a short distance & encamped at a handsome green where their had formerly been an old Indian Village; laying on the So side of the River. \u00a0\u00a0 We saw on the River this day, Swan & Geese in great plenty & the Indians that we were among kill plenty of deer, if we are allowed to judge from the quantities of Vension & Skins which we saw among them. [27]\n

\n \n \n
\n
1. The Katlaminimin, or Kathlaminimin, an Upper Chinookan-language tribe then living on the northwest side of Sauvie (Lewis and Clark's Wapato) Island, Multnomah County, Oregon. See below, n. 6. \"Clan-nah-min-na-mun\" is a Chinookan ethnonym g-a-l/\u00e1-namn + namn, \"ones who have whole(ness) of something.\" (back)
\n
2. Perhaps the part of the Columbia River coming around the back side of Sauvie Island with Scappoose Bay a part of the inlet. (back)
\n
3. Perhaps McCarthy Creek, in Multnomah County, nameless on Atlas map 80. \"Kill-amucks\" is present Tillamook River on the coast; the two streams' heads do not meet. (back)
\n
4. \n Sauvie Island, in Multnomah County. Atlas map 80. (back)
\n
5. The term may not refer to a tribal designation but to the Chinookan word [t]l/a'aq\u0161taq, a derisive name meaning \"round heads.\" They have been considered Clat-skanie Indians. See January 1, 1806. Atlas map 80; figs. 4, 6. (back)
\n
6. These four, with the Katlaminimin noted above, were all Upper Chinookan-language groups living on or around Sauvie Island. Archaeological investigations on Sauvie Island and the adjacent Oregon shore are discussed at November 4, 1805. Atlas maps 79, 80; figs. 4, 6. \"Cath-lah-cum-up\" is g-a-l/\u00e1-q\u0313map, \"ones who have the mound\"; \"Cl\u00e2h-in-nata\" is unidentified; \"Cath-lah-nah-qui-ah\" is g-a-l/\u00e1-nak\u0313\n waix, \"those of Nakwaix\"; \"Cath-lah-cam-mah-tup\" may be g-a-l/\u00e1-q\u0313max\u0323tup, without clear meaning. Silverstein, 534. (back)
\n
7. The wild chive, Allium schoenoprasum L. var. laurentianum Fern. This is the same species as the cultivated chives of Europe. The North American plants are found in wet meadows on rocky or gravelly streambanks and formerly between low and high water levels along the Columbia River from Canada to Umatilla, Oregon, but perhaps now are largely exterminated by impounded water behind dams. Lewis's citation extends the historic range of the species to a lower point on the Columbia. Hitchcock et al., 1:755. A red vertical line runs through this passage, perhaps penned by Biddle. (back)
\n
8. In Clark County, Washington, just above the mouth of Lewis River and behind Bachelor Island. Atlas maps 79, 80; figs. 4, 6. The Cathlapotle village Nahpooitle is discussed at November 5, 1805. (back)
\n
9. \n Biddle wrote the words in red and added a vertical line through several lines of the entry. (back)
\n
10. The Latin words mons veneris, referring to the female pubic area. (back)
\n
11. Another red vertical line running through several lines of writing. (back)
\n
12. Scimitar, after the curves, Oriental sword. (back)
\n
13. A sketch of the sword (fig. 1) in Lewis's Codex K, p. 14. A similar drawing appears in Codex Q, p. 88, apparently by an unknown copyist. See Moulton (SJ), 196\u201398. (back)
\n
14. Cressman provides further information on these distinctive weapons. (back)
\n
15. Behind Bachelor Island, in Clark County, in the vicinity of present Ridgefield. Atlas maps 79, 80. The camp may have been at an archaeological site upstream from the main Cathlapotle village. The site was a series of seasonal camps; the area has been extensively tested. Minor & Toepel, 79\u201380. See November 5, 1805. (back)
\n
16. The ring-necked duck of March 28, 1806. (back)
\n
17. \n Lewis may have been seeing several species of garter snakes: Thamnophis sirtalis, T. elegans, and T. ordinoides. Benson (HLCE), 90. (back)
\n
18. Probably the Pacific tree frog, Hyla regilla. Ibid., 88. Cf. Cutright (LCPN), 428. (back)
\n
19. Probably the great horned owl, Bubo virginianus [AOU, 375]. (back)
\n
20. The belted kingfisher, Ceryle alcyon [AOU, 390]. Burroughs, 237\u201338; Holmgren, 31. (back)
\n
21. The large blackbird is probably the common grackle, Quisculus quiscula [AOU, 511]; the small, probably Brewer's blackbird, Euphagus cyanocephalus [AOU, 510]. Burroughs, 255\u201356; Holmgren, 28. Cf. March 3, 1806. (back)
\n
22. Wild chive, Allium schoenoprasum L. var. laurentianum Fern. (back)
\n
23. \n Lewis River, Cowlitz and Clark counties, Washington. (back)
\n
24. \n Nahpooitle, a village of the Cathlapotle, an Upper Chinookan-language people, Clark \nCounty, Washington, just above the mouth of Lewis River and behind Bachelor Island. The village is described in Clark's entry of November 5, 1805. (back)
\n
25. Behind Bachelor Island, Clark County. (back)
\n
26. \n Clark notes that Willard was well and Bratton stronger. (back)
\n
27. The last portion of this sentence, from \"if we are\" on, is crowded between entries. (back)
