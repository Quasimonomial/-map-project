\n August 10, 1805\n

\n
\n \n

[Lewis]

\n \n
\n Saturday August 10th 1805.\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nWe set out very early this morning and continued our rout through the wide bottom on the Lard. side of the river \u00a0\u00a0 after passing a large creek [1] at about 5 miles we fel in with a plain Indian road which led towards the point that the river entered the mountain we therefore pursued the road I sent Drewyer to the wright to kill a deer which we saw feeding and halted on the river under an immencely high perpendicular clift of rocks [2] where it entered the mountain \u00a0\u00a0 here we kindled a fire and waited for Drewyer. \u00a0\u00a0 he arrived in about an hour and a half or at noon with three deer skins and the flesh of one of the best of them, we cooked and eat a haisty meal and departed, returning a shot distance to the Indian road which led us the best way over the mountains, which are not very high but ar ruggid and approach the river closely on both sides just below these mountains \u00a0\u00a0 I saw several bald Eagles and two large white headed fishinghawks [3] \u00a0\u00a0 boath these birds were the same common to our country. from the number of rattle snakes about the Clifts at which we halted we called them the rattle snake clifts. \u00a0\u00a0 this serpent is the same before discribed with oval spots of yellowish brown. [4] \u00a0\u00a0 the river below the mountains is rapid rocky, very crooked, much divided by islands and withal shallow. \u00a0\u00a0 after it enters the mountains it's bends are not so circuetous and it's general course more direct, but it is equally shallow les divided more rocky and rapid. \u00a0\u00a0 we continued our rout along the Indian road which led us sometimes over the hills and again in the narrow bottoms of the river till at the distance of fifteen Ms. from the rattle snake Clifts we arrived in a hadsome open and leavel vally where the river divided itself nearly into two equal branches; [5] here I halted and examined those streams and readily discovered from their size that it would be vain to attempt the navigation of either any further. \u00a0\u00a0 here also the road forked one leading up the vally of each of these streams. I therefore sent Drewer on one and Shields on the other to examine these roads for a short distance and to return and compare their information with respect to the size and apparent plainness of the roads as I was now determined to pursue that which appeared to have been the most traveled this spring. \u00a0\u00a0 in the mean time I wrote a note to Capt. Clark informing him of the occurrence which had taken place, recommending it to him to halt at this place untill my return and informing him of the rout I had taken which from the information of the men on their return seemed to be in favour of the S W or Left hand fork which is reather the smallest. \u00a0\u00a0 accordingly I put up my note on a dry willow pole at the forks, and set out up the S. E. fork, after proceeding about 1\u00bd miles I discovered that the road became so blind that it could not be that which we had followed to the forks of Jefferson's river, neither could I find the tracks of the horses which had passed early in the spring along the other; I therefore determined to return and examine the other myself, which I did, and found that the same horses had passed up the West fork which was reather largest, and more in the direction that I wished to pursue; I therefore did not hesitate about changing my rout but determined to take the western road. I now wrote a second note to Capt C. informing him of this change and sent Drewyer to put it with the other at the forks and waited untill he returned. \u00a0\u00a0 there is scarcely any timber on the river above the R. Snake Clifts, nor is there anything larger than willow brush in sight of these forks. \u00a0\u00a0 immediately in the level plain between the forks and about \u00bd a mile distance from them stands a high rocky mountain, the base of which is surrounded by the level plain; it has a singular appearance. \u00a0\u00a0 the mountains do not appear very high in any direction tho' the tops of some of them are partially covered with snow. \u00a0\u00a0 this convinces me that we have ascended to a great hight since we have entered the rocky Mountains, yet the ascent has been so gradual along the vallies that it was scarcely perceptable by land. I do not beleive that the world can furnish an example of a river runing to the extent which the Missouri and Jefferson's rivers do through such a mountainous country and at the same time so navigable as they are. \u00a0\u00a0 if the Columbia furnishes us such another example, a communication aross the continent by water will be practicable and safe. \u00a0\u00a0 but this I can scarcely hope from a knowledge of its having in it comparitively short course to the ocean the same number of feet to decend which the Missouri and Mississippi have from this point to the Gulph of Mexico.\u2014

\n

\nThe valley of the west fork through which we passed for four miles boar a little to N of West and was about 1 mile wide hemned in on either side by rough mountain and steep Clifts of rock \u00a0\u00a0 at 4\u00bd miles this stream enters a beatifull and extensive plain about ten miles long and from 5 to six in width. \u00a0\u00a0 this plain is surrounded on all sides by a country of roling or high wavy plains through which several little rivulets extend their wide vallies quite to the Mountains which surround the whole in an apparent Circular manner; forming one of the handsomest coves [EC: Shoshone\n ] I ever saw, of about 16 or 18 miles in diameter. [6] \u00a0\u00a0 just after entering this cove the river bends to the N. W. and runs close under the Stard. hills. \u00a0\u00a0 here we killed a deer and encamped on the Stard. side [7] and made our fire of dry willow brush, the only fuel which the country produces. there are not more than three or four cottonwood trees in this extensive cove and they are but small. \u00a0\u00a0 the uplands are covered with prickly pears and twisted or bearded grass and are but poor; some parts of the bottom lands are covered with grass and tolerably fertile; but much the greater proportion is covered with prickly pears sedge twisted grass the pulpy leafed thorn southernwood wild sage &c and like the uplands is very inferior in point of soil. [8] \u00a0\u00a0 we traveled by estimate 30 Ms. today, that is 10 to the Rattle snake Clift, 15 to the forks of Jefferson's river and 5 to our camp in the cove. \u00a0\u00a0 at the apparent extremity of the bottom above us two perpendicular clifts of considerable hight stand on either side of the river and appers [NB: appears] at this distance like a gate, it is about 10 M. due West.

\n

\n Capt Clark set out at sunrise this morning and pursued his rout; found the river not rapid but shallow also very crooked. \u00a0\u00a0 they were obliged to drag the canoes over many riffles in the course of the day. \u00a0\u00a0 they passed the point which the natives call the beaver's head. \u00a0\u00a0 it is a steep rocky clift of 150 feet high near the Stard. side of the river, opposite to it at the distance of 300 yards is a low clift of about 50 feet which is the extremity of a spur of the mountains about 4 miles distant on Lard. \u00a0\u00a0 at 4 P.M. they experienced a heavy shower of rain attended with hail thunder and Lightning which continued about an hour. \u00a0\u00a0 the men defended themselves from the hail by means of the willow bushes but all the party got perfectly wet. \u00a0\u00a0 after the shower was over they pursued their march and encamped on the stard side. [9] \u00a0\u00a0 only one deer killed by their hunters today. \u00a0\u00a0 tho' they took up another by the way which had been killed three days before by Jos. Fields and hung up near the river.

[Clark]

\n \n
\n August 10th Satturday 1805\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\t\t\nSome rain this morning at Sun rise and Cloudy \u00a0\u00a0 we proceeded on passed a remarkable Clift point on the Stard. Side about 150 feet high, this Clift the Indians Call the \n Beavers head, opposit at 300 yards is a low clift of 50 feet which is a Spur from the Mountain on the Lard. about 4 miles, the river verry Crooked, at 4 oClock a hard rain from the S W accompanied with hail Continued half an hour, all wet, the men Sheltered themselves from the hail with bushes \u00a0\u00a0 We Encamped on the Stard Side near a Bluff, only one Deer killed to day, the one killed Jo Fields 3 Days past & hung up we made use of \u00a0\u00a0 river narrow, & Sholey but not rapid.\n

[Ordway]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Saturday 10th August 1805. \u00a0\u00a0 a clear pleasant morning. \u00a0\u00a0 we Set out as usal. Several men out hunting. \u00a0\u00a0 we have now to live on poor venison & goat or antelopes which goes hard with us as the fatigues is hard. \u00a0\u00a0 the clifts and high land begin to make near the River. \u00a0\u00a0 passed a high bank along L. S. \u00a0\u00a0 took on board a deer which one of the hunters killed 2 days past. \u00a0\u00a0 passed a high clift of rocks on Stard. Side [10] \u00a0\u00a0 proceeded on the valley wider & covered with high Grass. \u00a0\u00a0 the hills make further from the River. \u00a0\u00a0 the River Small & amazeing crooked, our Commanding officers thought proper that the Missourie Should loose its name at the 2 3 forks we passed Some time ago, where we expected to have found the Snake nation of Indians. So they named the North fork \n JeffersonsRiver, the west or middle fork\n \n Maddisons River\n , the South fork\n \n Gallitine River, on which is a large Spring 2 miles from its Junction with the Missourie. \u00a0\u00a0 the Small River which falls in to Jeffersons River above the forks on L. S. they call Phillossofy River. So we Still keep on Jeffersons River \u00a0\u00a0 the last three forks we passed a fiew days past. \u00a0\u00a0 they call the North fork Wisdom River the \u2329west or\u232a South fork they call Philandrophy, and the west or middle Still retains the name of Jeffersons River, which we are yet on. \u00a0\u00a0 it is now gitting a Small Stream verry crooked and Shole in places, So that we have to wade and hall the canoes over. \u00a0\u00a0 about one oClock\nwe halted to dine. \u00a0\u00a0 a hard Thunder Show arose of rain and large hail which lasted nearly an hour. \u00a0\u00a0 we then proceeded on \u00a0\u00a0 the prarie low & Smooth as usal. \u00a0\u00a0 the beaver abound in this valley which is large and extensive & pleasant &C \u00a0\u00a0 our hunters killed only one Deer this day. \u00a0\u00a0 we Came 13 miles this day and camped on the Stard. Side.\u2014\n\n\n

[Gass]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Saturday 10th. \u00a0\u00a0 We set out early in a fine morning, and proceeded on through the valley, until breakfast time, when we came to a place where the river passes through a mountain. [11] This narrow passage is not more than a quarter of a mile in length. At the upper end another valley commences, but not so wide as the one below. There is no timber in the lower end of this valley; and the river very crooked, narrow, and in some places \nso shallow, that we were obliged to get into the water and drag the canoes along. At 1 o'clock we halted to dine, when a shower of rain came on with thunder and lightening, and continued an hour, during which some hail fell. Two hunters were out to day and killed but one deer. We came 13 miles and encamped on the North side. Here the valley begins to be more extensive.\n\n\n

[Whitehouse]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Saturday 10th August 1805. \u00a0\u00a0 a clear pleasant morning. \u00a0\u00a0 we Set out as usal. \u00a0\u00a0 Several hunters out on Shore. \u00a0\u00a0 we now begin to live on fresh meat & that poor venson & goat meat at this time. \u00a0\u00a0 as our fatigues hard we find that poor meat alone is not Strong diet, but we are content with what we can git. \u00a0\u00a0 the high land make near the River on each Side. \u00a0\u00a0 passed a high clifts of rocks on S. Side. [12] \u00a0\u00a0 proceeded on the valley gits wider and the hills make further from the River \u00a0\u00a0 our officers thought proper that the Missourie Should loose its name at the 2nd forks we passed Some time ago where we expected to have found the Snake nation of Indians. \u00a0\u00a0 So they named the North fork\n \n Jeffersons\n River, the west or middle fork\n \n Maddison River, the South fork\n \n Gallitine River, on which is a most beautiful Spring abt 2 mls. from its mouth. \u00a0\u00a0 the Small River that puts in above the forks to Jeffersons River they call phillosify River. \u00a0\u00a0 So Jeffersons River is the one which we Still keep on. \u00a0\u00a0 the last 3 forks they call the North fork, Wisdom R. the South Philandrophey and the west or middle fork Still retains its name Jeffersons River \u00a0\u00a0 it is now gitting Small crooked & Shole in places So that we have to waid and hall the canoes over. \u00a0\u00a0 about one oClock we halted to dine. \u00a0\u00a0 had a hard Thunder Shower of large hail and rain \u00a0\u00a0 thin proceeded on the bottom and river as usal. \u00a0\u00a0 the hunters killed only one deer this day. \u00a0\u00a0 Came 13 miles this day and Camped on the Stard. Side.\u2014

\n

\nSaturday August 10th \u00a0\u00a0 This morning clear & pleasant, several of our Hunters went out early to hunt, & we set out as usual, We now have nothing to live on, but fresh meat, & that poor Venison & Goats flesh, and our men \nseem much fataigued; and find that meat only, is too weak a diet, for men undergoing so much fataigue; but they seem all content with what we can get. \u00a0\u00a0 The high land makes in near to the River on both sides of it, We passed \na high clift of Rocks, which lay on the South side of the River, and Valleys, which seem wider, than those which we passed Yesterday, and the hills lies off farther from the River, Our officers were of oppinion (before Captain Lewis left us) that the Mesouri River should lose its name, at the place where the Second fork enter'd this River; which we passed some days past, and where we expected to have found, the Snake Nation of Indians. \u00a0\u00a0 they named the North fork\n Jefferson River, The West or middle fork\n Maddison River, and the South fork\n Gallatin, River, on which lies a most beautiful spring of Water, about 2 Miles from its mouth; the small River, that puts in above those three forks, to Jefferson River, they named Philosophy river, so that the River that we are now on, is Jefferson River, they also named the last three forks \u00a0\u00a0 the North fork they called Wisdom River, & the South fork, Philanthropy River, the middle fork still retaining the name of Jefferson River (and its course runs near West). \u00a0\u00a0 The River at this place is narrow, crooked, and very shallow; and in many places, we had to go into the water, and hawl our Canoes along the Shore.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 About 1 o'Clock P. M. we halted to dine, and soon after we had a hard thunder Shower, and large hail.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 At 3 o'Clock P. M. we proceeded on, and passed some bottom land, lying along the River, which were as usual rich Soil. \u00a0\u00a0 In the Evening we encamped in a \nbottom of timber'd land, lying on the River, on the North side having come 13 Miles this day.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 Our hunters returned to us here having killed One deer, which they brought with them [13]\n

\n
1. \n Blacktail Deer Creek, \"McNeal's Creek,\" for Hugh McNeal of the party, on Atlas map 66, meeting the Beaverhead River at Dillon, Beaverhead County, Montana. (back)
\n
2. \n Rattlesnake Cliffs, found about ten miles southwest of Dillon, in Beaverhead County, near Barretts siding on Interstate Highway 15. The cliffs are composed of early Tertiary, extrusive volcanic rocks (largely rhyolite). The rock is often badly fractured which allows it to form numerous little hollows when it weathers. These little hollows make excellent dens for snakes. The cliff west of the river rises to a height of about six hundred feet above the floodplain. (back)
\n
3. The bald eagle, Haliaeetus leucocephalus [AOU, 352], and the osprey, Pandion haliaetus [AOU, 364]. (back)
\n
4. Prairie rattlesnake, Crotalus viridus viridus. Perhaps it was Biddle who drew a red vertical line through this passage. (back)
\n
5. The point in Beaverhead County, where Red Rock River, from the east, unite to form the Beaverhead River. Lewis considered Horse Prairie Creek the ultimate headwaters of the Missouri, but modern geographers allot this role to Red Rock River. The immediate area is now inundated by Clark Canyon Dam and Reservoir. Atlas map 66. (back)
\n
6. \"Snake Indian Cove\" on Atlas map 67, otherwise Shoshone Cove in the journals; \"cove\" here means a narrow mountain valley. Today the village of Grant, Beaverhead County, lies in the middle of it. (back)
\n
7. Apparently about six miles east of Grant and the junction of Montana Route 324 with the road from Bannack to the north. Lewis is about one and one-half miles above Clark Canyon Reservoir on Horse Prairie Creek. The camp is not shown on Atlas map 67. (back)
\n
8. This ecological observation describes how the uplands and drier areas adjacent to the bottomlands are dominated by plains prickly pear, needle and thread grass, sedge (probably thread-leaved sedge), greasewood, and big sagebrush, Artemisia tridentata Nutt. (Lewis's \"wild sage\"). The vegetation is similar to that described by Lewis on July 31, 1805, but now with the addition of sagebrush which appears with increasing elevation. Booth & Wright, 252; Mueggler & Stewart, 50\u201351. It was probably Biddle who drew a red vertical line through this passage. (back)
\n
9. Near the Madison-Beaverhead county line and above Beaverhead Rock. The mountains to larboard (east) are the Ruby Range. Atlas map 67. (back)
\n
10. \n Beaverhead Rock, near theBeaverhead County line, Montana. (back)
\n
11. At Beaverhead Rock, Madison County, Montana, near theBeaverhead County line, along Montana Highway 41, about twelve miles southwest of Twin Bridges and fourteen miles northeast of Dillon. See note to Lewis's entry of August 8 and Clark's description of August 10. (back)
\n
12. If we take \"S.\" to mean starboard, unlike the fair copy which makes it \"South,\" then this can be a reference to Beaverhead Rock. The rock lies in Madison County, Montana, near the Beaverhead Countyline, along Montana Highway 41, about twelve miles southwest of Twin Bridges and fourteen miles northeast of Dillon. See Lewis's entry of August 8 and Clark's description on August 10. (back)
\n
13. Between this entry and the next in the fair copy is a pointing hand; its purpose is \nunknown. (back)
