\nSaturday 8th Sept 1804. \u00a0\u00a0 a pleasant morning. \u00a0\u00a0 we Set off eairly \u00a0\u00a0 the wind from the S. E. \u00a0\u00a0 we proceeded on under a gentle breeze \u00a0\u00a0 passed the mouth of a Small creek on S. S. Some thin Timber close along each Side of the river. \u00a0\u00a0 the hills are near the river on boath Sides & are verry Broken &.C.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 we passed an Island N. S. \u00a0\u00a0 halted on S. S. to dine. G. Drewyer joined us who went the evening before, had killed a Buck Elk & a faun Elk & a faun Deer & caught 2 large Beaver,\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 likewise one prarie dog. Capt. Clark went out this morning to walk on N. S. \u00a0\u00a0 we passed a Trading house [7] piched in on the Same Side abo. where the capt. went out in a handsome Timbered Bottom,\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 which had been built in 1796.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 we proceeded on past Several Islands, late in the afternoon we Saw Several Buffelow Swimming the river. Capt. Lewis went out with Some of the party on S. S. in a grove of Timber & killed 2 Buffelow \u00a0\u00a0 Shot at one of them Several times in the river \u00a0\u00a0 he being wounded Swam a Shore again & they Shot him down at the edge of the water.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 we proceeded on about 1\u00bd miles & camped on the lower point of a handsome Timbered Island. \u00a0\u00a0 on which we Saw large gangue of Buffalow.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 Capt. Clark joined us after dark. \u00a0\u00a0 had killed 1 faun Deer 3 turkies & a Squirril.\u2014 [8] \u00a0\u00a0 he informed us that he Travelled over a riged and mountanious Country without water & riseing 5 or 600 feet, where these hills had been lately burned over by the natives.\u2014\n\n

[Gass]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Saturday 8th. \u00a0\u00a0 We proceeded early on our voyage, and had a clear day and fair wind from the S. E. Passed the bed of a creek [9] without water. At 9 I went \nout with one of our men, who had killed a buffaloe and left his hat to keep off the vermin and beasts of prey; but when we came to the place, we found the wolves had devoured the carcase and carried off the hat. Here we found \na white wolf dead, supposed to have been killed in a contest for the buffaloe. We passed high bluffs on the south side and burnt prairie on the north. We encamped on an island covered with timber; and having a number of buffaloe on it. Captain Lewis who had been out with some of the men hunting informed us he had passed a trading house, built in 1796. This day we killed two buffaloe, a large and a small elk, a deer and two beaver.\n\n\n

Drewyer joined with the horses had killed 2 Elk a faun Deer and caught 2 large beaver \u00a0\u00a0 we passed an old Trading house on N. S. \u00a0\u00a0 capt. \n

\nSaturday September 8th \u00a0\u00a0 This morning we had fine pleasant weather, One of our hunters came in, and brought the horses, he had with him & 2 Elk, & four deer which he had killed, and two large Beaver which he caught in a Trap. \u00a0\u00a0 We set off early, and passed an old trading house, lying on the North side of the River. \u00a0\u00a0 Captain Clark, who had went out a hunting this Morning, returned in a short time, with the other hunters having kill'd 2 Buffalo, and One Deer, We passed several small Creeks, and encamped on the South side of the River in the Evening.\u2014\n\n\n

1. \n Lewis appears to have written the second entry in the course material to \"in length.\" (back)
\n
2. \n Jean Baptiste Truteau came to St. Louis from Canada in 1774 and served as schoolmaster there for over half a century. In 1795 he stated that he had been making trips into the Indian country for twenty-six years. He could converse in the languages of several of the river tribes. In 1794 he headed the expedition of the Company of Explorers of the Upper Missouri in an attempt to reach the Mandan villages, having been appointed to the position by the Spanish lieutenant-governor, Zenon Trudeau, with whom his name has been confused. One would assume that the captains consulted him while in St. Louis, but records do not indicate this. Lewis and Clark apparently had portions of his journal of that trip with them. The Sioux blocked Truteau from reaching the Mandans, so he wintered at the post mentioned here. Clark's \"Pania House\" (or \"Pawnee House\") is more accurately named \"Ponca House,\" being in the neighborhood of that tribe. It was located some thirty-four miles above the mouth of the Niobrara, in Charles Mix County, S. Dak., South Dakota. Truteau evidently wintered there in 1794\u201395, so Clark may have been in error in writing \"96.\" It could be either \"Panca\" or \"Pania\" on Evans's map 1 (Atlas map 7). April 1810], Jackson (LLC), 1:136\u201339, 2:528, 739 n. 3; Abel (TD); Diller (MMR); Diller (PH); Allen, 68, 138\u201339. (back)
