S. \u00a0\u00a0 passed Willow Creek\n [1] and Some \u2329Stone\u232a rock \u2329above\u232a below the mouth of the Seouex river\n [2] on the Starboard Side \u00a0\u00a0 those Clifts are about 170 fee[t] high, this river heads with the St. peters\n [3] and is navagable 75 Leagues (by the act. of Mr. Durien [Dorion]) to a fall of near 200 for [from], 2 large & Som Small Pitchs [4] \u00a0\u00a0 below the falls on the \u2329left\u232a right a Creek coms in on which \u2329all\u232a the red pipe Stone is percured, & in the praries about, a place of Peace with all nations. \t \nWe Set out verry early this morning and proceeded on under a gentle Breeze from the S. E. \u00a0\u00a0 passed willow creek Small on the S. S. below a Bluff of about 170 feet high and one \u00bd mes. above Floyds river \u00a0\u00a0 at 1 \u00bd miles higher & above the Bluff passed the \n Soues River\n S. S. \u00a0\u00a0 this River is about the Size of Grand river and as Mr. Durrien\n our Soues intptr. says \"navagable to the falls 70 or 80 Leagues and above these falls Still further, those falls are 200 feet or there abouts & has two princapal pitches, and heads with the St. peters\n passing the head of the Demoien, on the right below the falls a Creek Coms in which passed thro Clifts of red rock which the Indians make pipes of, and when the different nations Meet at [X: a Sort of asylum for all nations, no fightg there] those queries all is piece, passed a place in a Prarie on the L. S. where the Mahars had a Village formerly. [6] the Countrey above the Platt R has a great Similarity. \u00a0\u00a0 Campd. on the L. Side. [7] \u00a0\u00a0 Clouds appear to rise in the west & threten wind. I found a verry excellent froot resembling the read Current, the Scrub on which it grows resembles Privey & about the Common hight of a wild plumb\u2014 [8]\n

[Ordway]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Tuesday Augt. 21st \u00a0\u00a0 we Set off eairly this morning under a hard Breeze from the S. \u00a0\u00a0 we proceded on verry well \u00a0\u00a0 passed a large Sand bar on N. S. \u00a0\u00a0 the wind blew so hard that we were oblidged to take a reefe in our Sail & the Sand blew So thick from the Sand bars that we could not see the channel far ahead & it filled the air before us about a mile. \u00a0\u00a0 we Saw Several woolves on Sd. Sand beach \u00a0\u00a0 we passed the Mouth of the Grand River de Souix close above a high clay Bluff below Sd. Sand bar on the N. S. \u00a0\u00a0 the white pearogue could hardly Sail for want of Ballass, we put in Several kegs of pork &.C. Shannon went out to hunt on N. S. \u00a0\u00a0 we proceeded on 20 odd miles to day & Camped on S. S. Shannon joined us late. But killed nothing. \u00a0\u00a0 the man with the horses did not join us yet,\n\n\n

[Gass]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Tuesday 21st. \u00a0\u00a0 We set out early; passed handsome pale coloured bluffs, willow creek\n [11] and the Sioux river on the north side: and having come upwards of 20 miles, encamped on the south side.\n\n\n

[Whitehouse]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nTuesday August 21st 1804. \u00a0\u00a0 we Set out eairly this morning under a hard Breeze from the South. \u00a0\u00a0 we passed the mouth of the Grand River Souix close abo. a high Bluff on N. S. \u00a0\u00a0 we came 20 odd miles & camped [12] on S. S.\u2014

\n

\nTuesday August 21st \u00a0\u00a0 We set out early this morning with a stiff breeze from the South, and pass'd the Mouth of the Grand River Souix; which is close to a high bluff, which lays on the North side of the River, we saild 21 Miles this day and encamped on the South side of the River.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 The Country here is very Rich Priari land, having very high Grass on it; and at the farther side from the River, it has some Trees growing on it & affords a pleasant view.\u2014\n\n\n

\n
1. \n Perry Creek, which meets the Missouri at Sioux City, Woodbury County, Iowa. Atlas map 16; MRC map 27; MRR map 76. (back)
\n
2. The Big Sioux River reaches the Missouri after skirting the western edge of Sioux City. It forms the South Dakota\u2013Iowa boundary, Union County, South Dakota lying to the west. Atlas maps 16 and 17; MRC map 27; MRR map 76. (back)
\n
3. The St. Peters River is the present Minnesota River. The source of the Big Sioux in northeast South Dakota is fairly close to that of the Minnesota River on the Minnesota\u2013South Dakota border, in the area called Coteau des Praries. Petersen, 230\u201333. (back)
\n
4. \"Pitch\" is frequently used by the captains in reference to a waterfall. The term was used in Virginia for a slope or a descent. McJimsey, 103. (back)
\n
5. The red stone is catlinite, named for the artist George Catlin, who brought it to the attention of mineralogists. It is found at the quarries along Pipestone Creek, Pipestone County, in southwestern Minnesota. The area is now a national monument and only Indians are allowed to extract the stone. Tradition holds that the quarries were neutral ground where all tribes met in peace. Hodge, 1:217\u201319; Catlin, 2:164\u201371; Woolworth. (back)
\n
6. The Omahas are said to have had a village here before founding Tonwontonga; it was called Ti ta\u00f1ga ji\u00f1ga. Dorsey notes that the lodges here were made of wood, that is, they were bark-covered \"wig-wams,\" not earth lodges or tipis. Dorsey, 213. This village was probably located several miles west of present Dakota City, Dakota County, Nebraska, on the west side of the Missouri River. Atlas map 16; MRC map 27; MRR maps 76, 78. (back)
\n
7. In Union County, south of present Jefferson, and probably on the north side of Lake Goodenough, which appears to have been the 1804 bed of the Missouri. Atlas map 17; MRC map 28; MRR map 78. (back)
\n
8. \n Shepherdia argentea (Pursh) Nutt., buffaloberry, was then new to science. The privey, or privet, used for comparison is Ligustrum vulgare L., a native of Europe. Barkley, 203. (back)
\n
9. \n Reubin Field was with the horses on August 23, but it is not clear that he was one of the two mentioned here. (back)
\n
10. \n Lewis's observation from Codex O. (back)
\n
11. \n Perry Creek, at Sioux City, Woodbury County, Iowa. (back)
\n
12. In Union County, South Dakota, south of present Jefferson, and probably on the north side of Lake Goodenough, the apparent 1804 bed of the Missouri. (back)
