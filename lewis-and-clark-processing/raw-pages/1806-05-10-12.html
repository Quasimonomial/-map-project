\n May 10, 1806\n

\n
\n \n

[Lewis]

\n \n
\n Saturday May 10th 1806.\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nThis morning the snow continued falling \u00bd after 6 A. M. when it ceased, the air keen and cold, the snow 8 inches deep on the plain; we collected our horses and after taking a scant breakfast of roots we set out for the village of Tunnachemootoolt; our rout lay through an open plain course S. 35 E. and distance 16 ms. \u00a0\u00a0 the road was slippery and the snow clogged to the horses feet, and caused them to trip frequently. \u00a0\u00a0 the mud at the sources of the little ravines was deep black [1] and well supplyed with quawmash. Drewyer turned off to the left of the road in order to hunt and did not join us this evening. \u00a0\u00a0 at 4 in the afternoon we decended the hills to Commearp Creek and arrived at the Village of Tunnachemootoolt, [2] the cheeif at whos lodge we had left a flag last fall. \u00a0\u00a0 this flag was now displayed on a staff placed at no great distance from the lodge. underneath the flag the Cheif met my friend Capt. C. who was in front and conducted him about 80 yds. to a place on the bank of the creek where he requested we should encamp; I came up in a few minutes and we collected the Cheifs and men of consideration smoked with them and stated our situation with rispect to provision. \u00a0\u00a0 the Cheif spoke to his people and they produced us about 2 bushels of the Quawmas roots dryed, four cakes of the bread of cows and a dryed salmon trout. [3] We thanked them for this store of provision but informed them that our men not being accustomed to live on roots alone we feared it would make them sick, to obviate which we proposed exchangeing a good horse in reather low order for a young horse in tolerable order with a view to kill. \u00a0\u00a0 the hospitality of the cheif revolted at the aydea of an exchange, he told us that his young men had a great abundance of young horses and if we wished to eat them we should by furnished with as many as we wanted. \u00a0\u00a0 accordingly they soon produced us two fat young horses one of which we killed, the other we informed them we would pospone killing untill we had consumed the one already killed. This is a much greater act of hospitality than we have witnessed from any nation or tribe since we have passed the Rocky mountains. \u00a0\u00a0 in short be it spoken to their immortal honor it is the only act which deserves the appellation of hospitallity which we have witnessed in this quarter. [4] \u00a0\u00a0 we informed these people that we were hungry and fatiegued at this moment, that when we had eaten and refreshed ourselves we would inform them who we were, from whence we had come and the objects of our resurches. \u00a0\u00a0 a principal Cheif by name Ho-h\u00e2st,-ill-pilp\n [5] arrived with a party of fifty men mounted on eligant horses. \u00a0\u00a0 he had come on a visit to us from his village which is situated about six miles distant near the river. \u00a0\u00a0 we invited this man into our circle and smoked with him, his retinue continued on horseback at a little distance. \u00a0\u00a0 after we had eaten a few roots we spoke to them as we had promised; and gave Tinnachemootoolt and Hoh\u00e2stillpilp each a medal; the former one of the small size with the likeness of Mr. Jefferson and the latter one of the sewing medals struck in the presidency of Washington, [6] we explained to them the desighn and the importance of medals in the estimation of the whites as well as the red men who had been taught their value. The Cheif had a large conic lodge of leather [7] erected for our reception and a parsel of wood collected and laid at the door after which he invited Capt. C. and myself to make that lodge our home while we remained with him. \u00a0\u00a0 we had a fire lighted in this lodge and retired to it accompanyed by the Cheifs and as many of the considerate men as could croud in a circcle within it. \u00a0\u00a0 here after we had taken a repast on some horsebeef we resumed our council with the indians which together with smoking the pipe occupyed the ballance of the evening. I was surprised to find on decending the hills of Commearp Cr. to find that there had been no snow in the bottoms of that stream. \u00a0\u00a0 it seems that the snow melted in falling and decended here in rain while it snowed on the plains. \u00a0\u00a0 the hills are about six hundred feet high about one fourth of which distance the snow had decended and still lay on the sides of the hills. \u00a0\u00a0 as these people had been liberal with is with rispect to provision I directed the men not to croud their lodge surch of food in the manner hunger has compelled them to do at most lodges we have passed, and which the Twisted hair had informed me was disgreeable to the natives. \u00a0\u00a0 but their previous want of hospitality had induced us to consult their enclinations but little and suffer our men to obtain provision from them on the best terms they could. The village of the broken arm as I have heretofore termed it consists of one house only which is 150 feet in length built in the usual form of sticks matts and dry grass. \u00a0\u00a0 it contains twenty four fires and about double that number of families. \u00a0\u00a0 from appearances I presume they could raise 100 fighting men. \u00a0\u00a0 the noise of their women pounding roots reminds me of a nail factory. The indians seem well pleased, and I am confident that they are not more so than our men who have their somachs once more well filled with horsebeef and mush of the bread of cows.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 the house of coventry is also seen here.\u2014 [8]\n

[Clark]

\n \n
\n Saturday 10th of May 1806\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nThis morning the Snow continued falling untill \u00bd past 6 A M when it Seased. \u00a0\u00a0 the air keen and Cold the Snow 8 inches deep on the plain. \u00a0\u00a0 we Collected our horses and after takeing a Scanty brackfast of roots, we Set out for the Village of the Chief with a flag, and proceeded on through an open plain. \u00a0\u00a0 the road was Slipry and the Snow Cloged and caused the horses to trip very frequently. \u00a0\u00a0 the mud at heads of the Streams which we passed was deep and well Supplied with the Car mash.\n [9]\n Drewyer turned off the road to hunt near the river to our lef and did not join us today. \u00a0\u00a0 at 4 P M we arrived at the Village of \n Tin nach-e-moo-toolt\n \n [10] the Chief whome We had left a flag. \u00a0\u00a0 this flag was hoisted on a pole \u00a0\u00a0 unde the flag the Chief met me and Conducted me to a Spot near a Small run about 80 paces from his Lodges here he requested me to halt which I did. Soon after Cap Lewis who was in the rear Came up and we Smoked with and told this Chief our Situation in respect to provisions. \u00a0\u00a0 they brought foward about 2 bushels of quawmash 4 Cakes of bread made of roots and a dried fish. \u00a0\u00a0 we informed the Chief that our Party was not accustomed to eate roots without flesh & proposed to exchange Some of our oald horses for young ones to eate. \u00a0\u00a0 they Said that they would not exchange horses, but would furnish us with Such as we wished, and produced 2 one of which we killed and informd. them that we did not wish to kill the other at this time. \u00a0\u00a0 we gave Medals to the broken arm or \n Tin-nach-e-moo tolt\n and \n Hoh-h\u00e2st-ill-pitp\n \n [11] two principal Chiefs of the Chopunnish Natn. and was informed that there was one other Great Chief (in all 4) who had but one eye. \u00a0\u00a0 he would be here tomorrow. \u00a0\u00a0 a large Lodge of Leather was pitched and Capt. Lewis and my Self was envited into it. \u00a0\u00a0 we entered and the Chief and principal men came into the lodge and formed a Circle \u00a0\u00a0 a parcel of wood was Collected and laid at the dore and a fire made in this Conic lodge before we entered it. \u00a0\u00a0 the Chief requested that we might make the Lodge our homes while we remained with him. \u00a0\u00a0 here after we had taken a repast on roots & horse beef we resumed our Council with the indians which together with Smokeing took up the ballance of the evening. I was Supprised to find decending the hill to \n Commearp Creek to find that there had been no snow in the bottoms of that Stream. \u00a0\u00a0 it seams that the Snow melted in falling and decended here in rain while it snowed in the plain. \u00a0\u00a0 the hills are about Eight hundred feet high about \u00bc of which distance the Snow had decended and Still lay on the Sides of the hill. \u00a0\u00a0 as those people had been liberal I directed the men not to croud their Lodge in serch of food the manner hunger has Compelled them to do, at most lodges we have passed, and which the \n Twisted Hair\n had informed us was disagreeble to the nativs. \u00a0\u00a0 but their previous want of hospitality had enduced us to consult their enclinations but little and Suffer our men to obtain provisions from them on the best terms they could.

\n

\nThe Village of the \n broken Arm\n consists of one house or Lodge only which is 150 feet in length built in the usial form of Sticks, Mats and dry grass. \u00a0\u00a0 it contains 24 fires and about double that number of families. \u00a0\u00a0 from appearance I prosume they could raise 100 fighting men. \u00a0\u00a0 the noise of their women pounding the cows roots remind me of a nail factory. The Indians appear well pleased, and I am Confident that they are not more so than our men who have their Stomach once more well filled with horse beef and the bread of cows. Those people has Shewn much greater acts of hospitallity than we have witnessed from any nation or tribe Since we have passed the rocky Mountains. \u00a0\u00a0 in Short be it Spoken to their immortal honor it is the only act which diserves the appelation of hospitallity which we have witnessed in this quarter.\n\n

[Ordway]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nSaturday 10th of May 1806. \u00a0\u00a0 the wind fell and the rain turned to Snow Some time last night and the Snow fell 6 Inches deep & continues chilly & cold this morning, & we had not any thing to eat. \u00a0\u00a0 got up our horses & Set out & proced. on over a high Smooth plain no timber. The Snow melts a little but the air cold. \u00a0\u00a0 wrode about 20 miles \u00a0\u00a0 descended a Steep hill down in a valley and bottom in which a creek runs through, and Camped [12] near a village of the head chiefs of abt. 15 lodges. \u00a0\u00a0 they had their flag hoisted and appeared glad to See us. \u00a0\u00a0 gave us Some commass roots which had been Swetted last fall. Some Shapealell and a little dry fish, but have but little \u00a0\u00a0 the natives have great numbers of horses \u00a0\u00a0 gave us two to kill. \u00a0\u00a0 we killed one to eat. Some of the women pitched a leather lodge [13] and brought wood & made a fire in it and chiefs invited our officers to Stay in it, and talked together \u00a0\u00a0 our officers told them our business &C. \u00a0\u00a0 in the evening we played the fiddle and danced a while \u00a0\u00a0 a number of Indians came from other villages to See us \u00a0\u00a0 the Snow is gone in this bottom but lyes on the high plains & hills \u00a0\u00a0 considerable of cottonwood and wild or choke cherry along this creek & Scattering pine on the edges of the hills, &C. \u00a0\u00a0 we are now as near the Mountains as we can git untill Such times as the Snow is nearly gone of[f] the mountains as we are too eairly to cross. \u00a0\u00a0 one of the party purchased a dog this eveng. but the most of their dogs are too poor to eat\n\n\n

[Gass]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Saturday 10th. \u00a0\u00a0 At dark last night the weather became cloudy and it rained about an hour, when the rain turned to snow, and it continued snowing all night. In the morning the weather became clear. Where we are lying in the plains the snow is about five inches deep; and amidst snow and frost we have nothing whatever to eat. Without breakfast we started to go to a village of the natives, who live on a branch of the river, about a south course from this place. We travelled through the snow about 12 miles, and then went down a long steep descent to the branch where the village is situated. When we were about half way down the hill there was not a particle of snow nor the least appearance of it. It was about 3 o'clock when we arrived at the village, [14] and the Commanding Officers held a conversation with the natives, who informed them that they had not more provisions and roots, than they wanted for themselves. They, however, divided their stock with us; and told us what they had given was all they could spare; but drove up some horses and told us to shoot one, which we did. They then offered another, but that was reserved for another time, and we dressed the one we had killed; and in our situation find it very good eating. We remained here all night. One of the hunters who had gone on before the party did not join us yet. [15]\n

1. Snow meltwater or spring discharge of groundwater from snowmelt when mixed with the rich volcanic soil of the area would form a black sandy or silty mud. (back)
\n
2. In Lewis County, Idaho, on Lawyer (Commearp) Creek, southwest of present Kamiah; not shown on Atlas map 71. \"Commearp\" is Nez Perce\n q\u00e9myex\u0323p, the designation for Lawyer Creek. They remained here until May 13. Space, 27; Peebles (RLC), 20; Peebles (LT), map. If the party traveled along the historic Nez Perce trail in this area, they followed Suzie Creek to its junction with Lawyer Creek about five miles above its mouth. Peebles places the party approximately one mile farther east, but he is uncertain about this portion of the route. Peebles (LT), map. From the journal description of May 13, 1806, the campsite and village was located about three and one-half miles up Lawyer Creek. There were a number of historic Nez Perce villages along this portion of Lawyer Creek. Several of these have been confirmed as archaeological sites but none have yet been excavated or dated. Shawley, 40; Stapp, Bryan, & Rigg, 43, 67\u201368. (back)
\n
3. Steelhead trout, Oncorhynchus mykiss (formerly Salmo gairdneri). Kendall. See March 14, 1806, for Lewis's full description. (back)
\n
4. \n Biddle has a comment, perhaps given by Clark in their conversations in 1810, that those who had had no contact with whites were much more hospitable than those who had, such as the coastal Indians. Biddle Notes [ca. April 1810], Jackson (LLC), 2:543\u201344. (back)
\n
5. Somewhat more correctly, Hohots Ilppilp. His name referred to a red, or bleeding, grizzly bear, his spiritual animal helper or guardian. From this, or from his many battle scars, later whites called him \"The Bloody Chief.\" The Nez Perce word is \n x\u0323\u00e1x\u0323a\u00b7c 'ilp\u00edlp, \"red grizzly.\" He was still alive in the early 1840s, when he claimed to be the oldest chief of the Nez Perces, and spoke to whites of having met Lewis and Clark. He was friendly to the missionaries and his grandson, Ellis, having received an education in English, was designated \"head chief\" by U.S. authorities. Nez Perce legend asserts that the sister of Red Grizzly Bear bore a son by William Clark. This man, who had light hair, was proud of his ancestry and would proclaim \"Me Clark!\" He was photographed at least once, in his old age. He was with the famous Nez Perce flight in 1877, and with this group was deported to Indian Territory, where he died. His descendents were known by the name Clark. Reportedly a black child was also born after the expedition's passing, but did not live to maturity. Wheeler, 2:267; Josephy (NP), 11, 14, 165, 228, 231, 238; Space, 29; [Toole]; Ronda (LCAI), 233. (back)
\n
6. A medal showing a man sowing grain, one of a series of three depicting the white man's way of life, to show Indians the attractions of \"civilization.\" They were discontinued because most Indians preferred a likeness of the \"Great Father\"\u2014the president. See also October 29, 1804. Prucha (IPM), 89\u201390. (back)
\n
7. A tipi, showing the assimilation of some elements of plains culture. (back)
\n
8. Again the lodge for menstruating women. This sentence may have been crowded in at the end of the entry later. (back)
\n
9. Or quamash, which is camas. (back)
\n
10. His name may have been added to a blank space. (back)
\n
11. The two names may have been added to blank spaces. (back)
\n
12. On Lawyer Creek, Lewis County, Idaho, southwest of Kamiah and near Broken Arm's village; the party remained here until May 13. (back)
\n
13. A tipi, showing the influence of Plains Indian culture. (back)
\n
14. In Lewis County, Idaho, southwest of Kamiah, on Lawyer Creek, which they called Commearp Creek, from its Nez Perce name. They remained here until May 13. (back)
\n
15. Apparently Drouillard, who came in the next day. (back)
