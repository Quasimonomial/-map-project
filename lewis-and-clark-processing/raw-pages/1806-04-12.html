\n April 12, 1806\n

\n
\n \n

[Lewis]

\n \n
\n Saturday April 12th 1806.\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nIt rained the greater part of last night and still continued to rain this morning. \u00a0\u00a0 I therefore determined to take up the remaining perogue this morning for which purpose I took with me every man that could be of any service. \u00a0\u00a0 a small distance above our camp there is one of the most difficult parts of the rapid. \u00a0\u00a0 at this place the current sets with great violence against a projecting rock. \u00a0\u00a0 in hawling the perogue arround this point the bow unfortunately took the current at too great a distance from the rock, she turned her side to the stream and the utmost exertions of all the party were unable to resist the forse with which she was driven by the current, they were compelled to let loose the cord and of course both perogue and cord went a drift with the stream. \u00a0\u00a0 the loss of this perogue will I fear compell us to purchase one or more canoes of the indians at an extravegant price. \u00a0\u00a0 after breakfast all hands were employed in taking our baggage over the portage. \u00a0\u00a0 we caused all the men who had short rifles to carry them, [1] in order to be prepared for the natives should they make any attempts to rob or injure them. I went up to the head of the rapids and left Capt. C. below. \u00a0\u00a0 during the day I obtained a vocabulary of the language of the War-clel-lars &c. I found that their numbers were precisely those of the Chinnooks but the other parts of their language essentially different. [2] \u00a0\u00a0 by 5 P. M. we had brought up all our baggage and Capt. C. joined me from the lower camp with the Clahclellah cheif. there is an old village [3] situated about halfway on the portage road \u2329there is an old village\u232a; the fraim of the houses, which are remarkably large one 160 by 45 feet, remain almost entire. \u00a0\u00a0 the covering of the houses appears to have been sunk in a pond back of the village. \u00a0\u00a0 this the chief informed us was the residence occasionally of his tribe. \u00a0\u00a0 these houses are fraimed in the usual manner but consist of a double set as if oune house had been built within the other. \u00a0\u00a0 the floors are on a level with the ground. \u00a0\u00a0 the naives did not croud about us in such numbers today as yesterday, and behaved themselves much better; no doubt the precautions which they observed us take had a good effect. I employed sergt. Pryor the greater part of the day in reparing and corking the perogue and canoes. \u00a0\u00a0 it continued to rain by showers all day. \u00a0\u00a0 about 20 of the Y-eh-huhs remained with me the greater part of the day and departed in the evening. \u00a0\u00a0 they conducted themselves with much propryety and contemned the conduct of their relations towards us. We purchased one sheepskin for which we gave the skin of an Elk and one of a deer. \u00a0\u00a0 this animal was killed by the man who sold us the skin near this place; he informed us that they were abundant among the mountains and usually resorted the rocky parts. \u00a0\u00a0 the big horned animal [4] is also an inhabitant of these mountains. I saw several robes of their skins among the natives.\u2014 as the evening was rainy cold and far advanced and ourselves wet we determined to remain all night. [5] \u00a0\u00a0 the mountains are high steep and rocky. the rock is principally black. [6] \u00a0\u00a0 they are covered with fir of several speceis and the white cedar. [7] \u00a0\u00a0 near the river we find the Cottonwood, sweet willow, broad leafed ash, a species of maple, [8] the purple haw, [9] a small speceis of cherry; [10] purple currant, [11] goosberry, [12] red willow, vining and white burry honeysuckle, [13] huckkle burry, sacacommis, two speceis of mountain holley, [14] & common ash. [15] \u00a0\u00a0 for the three last days this inclusive we have made only 7 miles.\u2014\n

\n \n
\n
\n \n

[Clark]

\n \n
\n Saturday April 12th 1806.\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nrained the greater part of the last night and this morning untile 10 A. M. \u00a0\u00a0 we employed all hands in attempting to take up the lost Canoe. in attempting to pass by a rock against which the Current run with emence force, the bow unfortunately took the Current at too great a distance from the rock, She turned broad Side to the Stream, and the exertions of every man was not Sufficient to hold her. \u00a0\u00a0 the men were Compelled to let go the rope and both the Canoe and rope went with the Stream. \u00a0\u00a0 the loss of this Canoe will I fear Compell us to purchase another at an extravigent price. \u00a0\u00a0 after brakfast all hands who were employed in Carrying the baggage over the portage 1\u00bd miles which they performed by 4 P. M. \u00a0\u00a0 the nativs did not visit us in Such Crouds to day as yesterday. \u00a0\u00a0 we Caused all the men of the party who ha Short guns to carry them on the portage for fear of Some attempt on the part of the nativs to rob the party. The rain Continued at intervales all day. \u00a0\u00a0 in the evening after everry thing was taken from the lower Camp I Set out myself accompanied by the Cheif of the Clah-clal lars to the head of the portage. \u00a0\u00a0 as we passed the remains of an old Village about half way the portage, this Cheif informed me that this old Village had been the residence of his Tribe dureing the last Salmon Season. \u00a0\u00a0 this village I mentiond in decending this river, but did not know the Tribes that had inhabited it that time. Capt. Lewis took a vocabulary of the languge of those people whilst I had all the baggage taken across the portage & we formed a Camp at the place we had encamped on our way down.

\n

\nat my arival at the head of the portage found about 20 of the natives of the \n Wy ach hich\n tribe who reside above the rapids, with Capt Lewis. those people appeared much better disposed towards us than either the \n Clahclallah\n or \n Wahclellah\n and Condemn their Conduct much. Those tribes I believe to be all the Same Nation their Language habits manners dress &c. are presisely alike and differ but little from those below the Great \u2329Falls\u232a Narrows of this river. I observed a woman with a Sheep Skin robe on which I purchased for one Elk and one deer Skin. \u00a0\u00a0 the father of this woman informed me that he had killed the animal off of which he had taken this Skin on the mountains imediately above his village, and that on those mountains great numbers of those animals were to be found in large flocks among the Steep rocks. I also purchased 2 pieces of Chapellell and Some roots of those people. \u00a0\u00a0 as the evening was rainey and ourselves and party wet we Concluded to delay untill the morning and dry our selves. The Indians left us about 6 P M and returned to their Village on the opposit Side. \u00a0\u00a0 mountains are high on each Side and Covered with Snow for about \u2153 of the way down. \u00a0\u00a0 the growth [16] is principally fir and White Cedar. \u00a0\u00a0 the bottoms and low Situations is Covered with a variety Such as Cotton, large leafed ash, Sweet willow a Species of beech, alder, white thorn, cherry of a Small Speces, Servis berry bushes, Huckleberries bushes, a Speces of Lorel &c. &c. I saw a turkey buzzard which is the 3rd which I have Seen west of the rocky mountains. \u00a0\u00a0 the 1st was on the 7 inst. above quick Sand river. \u00a0\u00a0 for the three last days this inclusive we have made 7 miles only.\n

\n \n

[Ordway]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nSaturday 12th of April 1806. \u00a0\u00a0 a rainy wet morning. \u00a0\u00a0 all the party except a guard went with Capt. Lewis to take up the other large canoe. \u00a0\u00a0 we got it under way verry well but She took a Swing on us and broke away and rid the high waves down the rapids. \u00a0\u00a0 then all hands went at packing the baggage\npast the portage which is 1\u00bd miles. \u00a0\u00a0 carried it all up at 4 loads a peace towards evening to everry thing to the head of the portage and Campd. for the night. \u00a0\u00a0 our officers finding that the natives do not incline to Sell their canoes So they divided the baggage & men among the 4 canoes. \u00a0\u00a0 a number of the natives visited us. \u00a0\u00a0 one of the Squaws told us in the Clatsop tongue that She had Slept with the white tradors &c.\n\n\n

\n \n
\n
\n \n

[Gass]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Saturday 12th. \u00a0\u00a0 This morning was wet. We all set out to take the other canoe over; but after we had fastened the rope to her she swung out into the current, which was so strong, that it pulled the rope out of the men's hands and went down the river.\u2014 We then went to carry our baggage across the \nportage, which was a very fatiguing business; but about sunset, we got all over. It rained at intervals all day; and upon the very high mountains on the south side of the river, [17] snow fell and continued on the trees and rocks during the whole of the day. We had a number of the natives about us in the day \ntime; but they left us at night. We encamped, all excessively fatigued, at the upper end of the portage.\n\n\n

1. The U.S. Model 1803 rifle (see May 10, 1804); if they were to be carried by men transporting they were probably equipped with slings. Russell (GEF), 176\u201382; Russell (FTT), 38\u201341. (back)
\n
2. The Watlalas (Wahclellahs) spoke a Chinookan dialect. See November 2, 1805. (back)
\n
3. Presumably the \"old village of verry large houses\" on Atlas maps 78 and 79, in Skamania County, Washington, in the vicinity of the Bridge of the Gods, noted by Clark on October 30, 1805. The village corresponds to the Ice House Lake archaeological site. Archaeological testing indicates that occupation at this locality began about 500 years ago and continued at least untill 1855, being abandoned after an Indian uprising in 1856. The village was called Wahlala (\"their lake\"). With the decline in the native population and subsequent amalgamation into fewer groups, the identity of this and other Chinookan bands (Wy-ach-hich, Clahclellah) was gradually lost, with the survivors becoming known simply as the Cascades Indians. Minor (IHL); Spier & Sapir, 167, 172. (back)
\n
4. Bighorn sheep, Ovis canadensis; see May 25, 1805. (back)
\n
5. Evidently at the camp of October 30-31, 1805, on a small island just above the narrows, in Skamania County. See Clark's entry of this day. Atlas maps 78, 79. (back)
\n
6. The rocks in this area are dark brown to black basalts of the Grande Ronde Basalt of lower-middle Miocene age and basalts of a slightly different age. Both types of rock belong to the Columbia River Basalt Group. From this to the end of the entry runs a red vertical line, perhaps set down by Biddle. (back)
\n
7. The dominant species of this upland forest are, indeed, Douglas fir, western hemlock, and western redcedar. (back)
\n
8. \n Lewis describes the dominant and understory, woody, riparian species of this region of the Columbia. The maple may be vine maple, Acer circinatum Pursh. Hitchcock et al., 3:411\u201312; Little (MWH), 8. (back)
\n
9. Black hawthorn, Crataegus douglasii Lindl., an expedition discovery. Little (MWH), 59-NW; Hitchcock et al., 3:101; Cutright (LCPN), 288\u201389, 407. (back)
\n
10. The bitter cherry, Prunus emarginata Dougl., is the most common cherry west of the Cascades, along with the choke cherry, P. virginiana L., which is found mostly east of the Cascades within the Columbia Gorge region. Lewis's detailed discussion of the bitter cherry is at June 7, 1806. Little (MWH), 122-W, 127-NW; Hitchcock et al., 3:158\u201359. (back)
\n
11. Probably the red flowering currant. The present common name derives from the color of the flower, while Lewis's name may refer to the fruit color. (back)
\n
12. Again, either canyon gooseberry or straggly gooseberry; see March 25, 1806. (back)
\n
13. The vining honeysuckle is either the western trumpet, or orange, honeysuckle, Lonicera ciliosa (Pursh) DC., or the California honeysuckle, L. hispidula (Lindl.) Dougl. ex T. & G. The white berry honeysuckle is probably snowberry. Hitchcock et al., 4:458\u201359. (back)
\n
14. Oregon and dull Oregon grape. (back)
\n
15. Again, the Oregon ash. (back)
\n
16. \n Clark's listing of plants is similar to Lewis's of this day, but includes several species not noted by Lewis: beech is probably red alder; alder is possibly white alder; white thorn (Lewis's \"purple haw\") is black hawthorn, Clark's name deriving from the clusters of white flowers in the spring; serviceberry; huckleberry is possibly evergreen huckleberry or some other species of Vaccinium; and laurel possibly refers to the California rhododendron, Rhododendron macrophyllum G. Don (see November 6, 1805) or it may be salal, Gaultheria shallon Pursh. Hitchcock et al., 4:27. (back)
\n
17. Part of the Cascade Range. (back)
