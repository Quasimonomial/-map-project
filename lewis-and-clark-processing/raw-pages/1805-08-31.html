\n August 31, 1805\n

\n
\n \n

[Clark]

\n \n
\n August 31st 1805 Satturday\n [1]\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nA fine morning \u00a0\u00a0 Set out before Sun rise, as we passed the lodges at which place I had encamped for thre nights and left 2 men, Those 2 men joined us and we proceeded on in the Same rout I decended the 21st Instant, halted 3 hours on Sammon Creek to Let our horses graze \u00a0\u00a0 the wind hard from the S. W. I met an Indian on horse back who fled with great Speed to Some lodges below & informed them that the Enemis were Coming down, armd with guns &c. \u00a0\u00a0 the inhabitents of the Lodges indisceved him, we proceeded on the road on which I had decended as far as the 1st run [EC: Tower Cr\n ] below & left the road & Proceeded up the Run [NB: run] in a tolerable road 4 miles & Encamped in Some old lodjes at the place the road leaves the Creek and assends the high Countrey [2] \u00a0\u00a0 Six Indians followed us four of them the Sons of our guide; our hunters killed one Deer a goose & Prarie fowl. This day warm and Sultrey, Praries or open Valies on fire in Several places\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 The Countrey is Set on fire for the purpose of Collecting the different bands, and a Band of the \n Flatheads\n to go to the Missouri where They intend passing the winter near the Buffalow \u00a0\u00a0 Proceeded on 22 miles to Day, 4 miles of which up a run\n

\n \n
\n
\n \n

[Ordway]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Saturday 31st August 1805. \u00a0\u00a0 a fare morning. \u00a0\u00a0 we Set out eairly and proceeded on 2 miles and passd. Several lodges of the Snake nation of Indians who Stay here to fish. \u00a0\u00a0 they catch Sammon in their pots and wires [weirs] which they have made of willows across the River and have more or less in them everry morng. \u00a0\u00a0 we bough a nomber of fine large Sammon of them and proceeded on. \u00a0\u00a0 one hunter on a head. \u00a0\u00a0 one strange Indian seen which is Supposed to be one of the flat head nation. [3] \u00a0\u00a0 he ran off, and the Indians could not find him. \u00a0\u00a0 we then proceeded on over rough high hills. Some deep Gullies of white earth. Several of the natives followed us. \u00a0\u00a0 went about eight miles without water and halted at a large Spring branch [4] to let our horses feed and dine ourselves. Some pitch pine on the mountains which make near the River on each Side. \u00a0\u00a0 the River bottoms narrow and verry much dryed up. \u00a0\u00a0 the Soil verry indifferent. \u00a0\u00a0 we proceeded on over a level Smooth plain abt. 7 miles \u00a0\u00a0 then passed the end of a mountain near the\nRiver where the Stone lay one on an other & holes So that the horses could Scarsely git along without breaking their legs. \u00a0\u00a0 we then proceeded on to a large Creek [5] which falls in on the East Side of the River. \u00a0\u00a0 we took a path up Sd. Creek and proceeded on \u00a0\u00a0 found wild or choke cherrys along the\nbranch. \u00a0\u00a0 also Servis berrys which were ripe. \u00a0\u00a0 we Came [blank] miles this day and Camped at Some Indian lodges near the Creek. \u00a0\u00a0 one of the hunters killed a deer at dusk and brought it to Camp after dark.\u2014\n\n\n

\n \n
\n
\n \n

[Gass]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Saturday 31st. \u00a0\u00a0 They all came down to our camp, and we proceeded on with 27 horses and one mule. Our old guide after consulting with the rest of the Indians, thought it was better to go along the north side of the Columbia, than on the south side. [6] We therefore proceeded down, the same way Captain Clarke had been before, 30 miles, and then turned up a creek [7] that comes in from the north, and encamped [8] on it about 3 miles and an half from the mouth. Two hunters had gone on ahead this morning, and at night joined us, having killed one deer. The first cost of the articles, which had been given for each horse, did not amount to more than from three to five dollars; so that the whole of them only cost about one hundred dollars.\n\n\n

\n
\n \n

[Whitehouse]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Saturday 31st August 1805. [9] \u00a0\u00a0 a fine morning. \u00a0\u00a0 we Set out eairly and proceeded on 2 miles \u00a0\u00a0 passed Several Indian lodges where we bought a nomber of fine Salmon. \u00a0\u00a0 the natives have wires [weirs] fixed across the River in which they catch more or less every night. \u00a0\u00a0 a Strange Indian came in Site of these lodges who they expected to be one of the nation called the flat heads. \u00a0\u00a0 he ran as Soon as he Saw us \u00a0\u00a0 Several of these natives followed after him. \u00a0\u00a0 we went on a Short distance further crossed the River and halted for breakfast. \u00a0\u00a0 one man out a hunting. \u00a0\u00a0 2 men Sent to Some other Indian Camps, with Some articles to buy Some more Sammon, but they had moved their Camps and we got none. \u00a0\u00a0 we then proceeded on over rough hills Some of them high & Steep, deep Gullies and white earth which had been washed down by rains &c. \u00a0\u00a0 went about eight miles and halted to bate our horses and dine. \u00a0\u00a0 4 or five of the natives follow us. \u00a0\u00a0 Some pitch pine on the Mountains which make near the River on each Side. \u00a0\u00a0 we proceeded on over a Smooth plain about 7 miles and passed along [th]e Side of a mountain near the River, [10] where the Stone lay one [up]on an other and full of holes, So that it is allmost impossable for horses to pass without breaking their leggs. \u00a0\u00a0 we then passed along the end of this mountain. \u00a0\u00a0 Come to a large creek which falls in on the East Side. \u00a0\u00a0 we followed up this creek or Spring branch. \u00a0\u00a0 found pleanty of wild or choke cherries and Servis berrys. \u00a0\u00a0 one of the hunters killed a Deer at the edge of the evening. \u00a0\u00a0 we Came [blank] Miles this day and Camped [11] at the Creek where we had Several good Indian lodges to Sleep in this night.

\n

\nSaturday August 31st \u00a0\u00a0 We had a fine morning, & set out early, and proceeded on about 2 miles, and passed several Indian lodges, and met with some Snake Indians, from whom we purchased for trifles a number of fine Salmon, the Natives have Wares fixed across the Columbia River, in which they catch more or less of them every night\u2014. \u00a0\u00a0 A strange Indian came in sight of the lodges, which we past; which the Snake Indians thought was one of the Nation of Indians, which they called the flatt head nation:\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 he ran \nas soon as he saw us; and several of the Snake Indians followed him, We proceeded on a short distance, and crossed the Columbia River, where we halted to break fast. \u00a0\u00a0 One of our party was sent out this morning a hunting, and two others of our party was sent to the Indian Camp with some articles to purchase some Salmon,\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 but the Indians had removed their Camps, & they got none. \u00a0\u00a0 We proceeded on our way, and crossed over some rough hills, some of them very steep, & passed through some deep Vallies, which had white earth in them, which was occasioned by the Earth being washed down from the Hills of that Collour.\u2014

\n

\nWe went about eight miles & halted to rest our horses & to dine, where some of the Natives followed us. \u00a0\u00a0 We saw some Pitch pine trees which grew on the Mountains which make near to the River on each side of it. \u00a0\u00a0 We proceeded on & passed through a smooth plain about 7 Miles & along the side of a mountain; which lay near the River, where the Stones lay very plenty, & the place full of holes, & where we found it almost impossible, for our horses to pass, without breaking their legs. \u00a0\u00a0 We passed along the Edge of this mountain with great difficulty, & came to a large Creek, which falls in, on the East side of the Columbia River; we proceeded up this Creek, & found plenty of wild or choak cherries and service berries. \u00a0\u00a0 One of our hunters killed a Deer on the Edge of the Mountain, which we had passed & which he brought to us in the evening; We came 15 Miles this day, & encamped on the Creek, where we found several Indian lodges, which we slept in\n\n\n

\n
1. \n Clark's table of courses for August 31\u2013September 4 are found with his entry of September 2, 1805. (back)
\n
2. The route appears as a dotted line on Atlas map 67; at some points it is hard to distinguish from Clark's earlier route (marked \"William Clark's route\") on his Salmon River reconnaissance. Having crossed to the east side of the Lemhi, they proceeded down that river to the Salmon, raveled down that river and headed up Tower Creek. The campsite was, as Clark notes, some four miles up the creek, in Lemhi County, Idaho. Peebles (RW), 15, and fig. 13. (back)
\n
3. Also called Salish Indians, see September 4. (back)
\n
4. Probably Carmen Creek, Lemhi County, Idaho. (back)
\n
5. \n Tower Creek, flowing into the Salmon River, Lemhi County, Idaho. (back)
\n
6. Although this passage is obscure, the \"south side\" may refer to a proposed route along the Snake River in southern Idaho, with the \"north side\" referring to the route they actually took, up the North Fork Salmon River, down the Bitterroot River, and along the Lolo Trail to the Clearwater River. See Gass's entry of August 24, 1805, and also Lewis's entry of June 20, 1806. Possibly Gass, or McKeehan, is referring to the Lemhi and the Salmon rivers as the Columbia, and their route along the \"north\" (actually the east) side on this day. (back)
\n
7. \n Tower Creek, flowing into the Salmon River, Lemhi County, Idaho. (back)
\n
8. A few miles above the mouth of Tower Creek, Lemhi County. (back)
\n
9. This is the last entry in this section of Whitehouse's original journal and ends near the top one-third of the page. The remainder of the page is filled with random scribbles, most of them Whitehouse's signature. (back)
\n
10. The Salmon River, which the party considered to be the same as the the Lemhi, calling both Lewis's River. (back)
\n
11. A few miles above the mouth of Tower Creek, Lemhi County. (back)
