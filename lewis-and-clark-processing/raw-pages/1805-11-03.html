[Clark]

\n November 3rd Sunday 1805\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nThe fog So thick this morning we did not think it prudent to Set out untill \u2329it Cleared away at\u232a 10 oClock we Set out and proceeded on verry well, [1] accompanied by our Indian friends\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 This morning \n Labich\n killed 3 Geese flying Collins killed a Buck\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 The water rose \u23292\u232a Inches last night the effects of tide. The Countrey has a handsom appearance in advance no mountains extensive bottoms\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 the water Shallow for a great distance from Shore\u2014. \u00a0\u00a0 The fog continued thick untill 12 oClock, we Coasted, and halted at the mouth of a large river on the Lard Side, [2] This river throws out emence quanty of \u2329quick\u232a Sand and is verry Shallow, th narrowest part 200 yards wide bold Current, much resembling the river Plat, Several Islands about 1 mile up and has a Sandbar of 3 miles in extend imedately in its mouth, discharging it waters by 2 mouths, and Crowding its Corse Sands So as to throw the Columbia waters on its Nothern banks, [3] & confdg it to \u00bd ms. in width Passed a Small Prarie on the Stard. Side above, a large Creek [4] opposit qk Sand River on the Stard. Side, extensive bottoms and low hilley Countrey on each Side (good wintering Place) a high peaked mountain Suppose to be Mt. Hood is on the Lard Side S. 85\u00b0 E. 40 miles distant from the mouth of quick Sand river.\u2014

[Clark]

\n \n
\n November 3rd Sunday 1805\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\t\nThe Fog So thick this morning that we could not See a man 50 Steps off, this fog detained us untill 10 oClock at which time we Set out, accompanied by our Indian friends who are from a village near the great falls, previous to our Setting out Collins killed a large Buck, and Labiech killed 3 Geese flying. I walked on the Sand beech Lard. Side, opposit the canoes as they passed allong. The under groth rushes, vines &c. in the bottoms too thick to pass through, at 3 miles I arrived at the enterance of a river which appeared to Scatter over a Sand bar, the bottom of which I could See quite across and did not appear to be 4 Inches deep in any part; I attempted to wade this Stream and to my astonishment found the bottom a quick Sand, and impassable\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 I called to the Canoes to put to Shore, I got into the Canoe and landed below the mouth, & Capt Lewis and my Self walked up this river about 1\u00bd miles to examine this river which we found to be a verry Considerable Stream Dischargeing its waters through 2 Chanels which forms an Island of about 3 miles in length on the river and 1\u00bd miles wide, composed of Corse Sand which is thrown out of this quick Sand river Compressing the waters of the Columbia and throwing the whole Current of its waters against its Northern banks, within a Chanel of \u00bd a mile wide, Several Small Islands 1 mile up this river, This Stream has much the appearance of the \n River Platt: roleing its quick Sands into the bottoms with great velocity after which it is divided into 2 Chanels by a large Sand bar before mentioned, the narrowest part of this River is 120 yards\u2014on the Opposit Side of the Columbia a \u2329large Creek\u232a [NB: Small river called Seal river\n ] falls in above this Creek \u00a0\u00a0 on the Same Side is a Small prarie. \u00a0\u00a0 extensive low country on each Side thickly timbered.

\n

\nThe Quick Sand river appears to pass through the low countrey at the foot of those high range of mountains in a Southerly direction,\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 The large Creeks which fall into the Columbia on the Stard. Side rise in the Same range of mountains to the N. N. E. and pass through Some ridgey land\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 A Mountain which we Suppose to be Mt. Hood is S. 85\u00b0 E about 47 miles distant from the mouth of quick sand river This mtn. is Covered with Snow and in the range of mountains which we have passed through and is of a Conical form but rugid\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 after takeing dinner at the mouth of this river we proceeded on \u00a0\u00a0 passed the head of a Island near the lard Side back of which on the Same Side and near the head a large Creek falls in, and nearly opposit & 3 miles below the upper mouth of quick Sand river is the lower mouth, [for?] This Island is 3\u00bd miles long, has rocks at the upper point, Some timber on the borders of this Island in the middle open and ponney [NB: pondy]. Some rugid rocks in the middle of the Stream opposit this Island. \u00a0\u00a0 \u2329proceeded in\u232a [NB: proceeded on] to Center of a large Island in the middle of the river which we call Dimond Isld. from its appearance, here we met 15 Indn men in 2 canoes from below, they informed us they Saw 3 vestles below &c. &c. \u00a0\u00a0 we landed on the North Side of this Dimond Island and Encamped, [6]\n Capt. L walked out with his gun on the Island, Sent out hunters & fowlers\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 below quick Sand River the Countrey is low rich and thickly timbered on each Side of the river, the Islands open & Some ponds \u00a0\u00a0 river wide and emence numbers of fowls flying in every direction Such as Swan, geese, Brants, Cranes, Stalks [NB: Storks], white guls, comerants & plevers &c. [7] \u00a0\u00a0 also great numbers of Sea Otter in the river\u2014 [8] \u00a0\u00a0 a Canoe arrived from the village below the last rapid with a man his wife and 3 children, and a woman whome had been taken prisoner from the Snake Inds. [NB: on a river from the South which we found to be Mulknoma\n ] on Clarks River\n [9] I Sent the Interpreters wife who is a \n So So ne\n or Snake Indian of the Missouri, to Speake to this Squar, they Could not understand each other Sufficiently to Converse. This familey and the Inds. we met from below continued with us Capt Lewis borrowed a Small Canoe of those Indians & 4 men took her across to a Small lake in the Isld. Cap L. and 3 men Set out after night in this Canoe in Serch of the Swans, Brants Ducks &c. &c. which appeared in great numbers in the Lake, he Killed a Swan and Several Ducks which made our number of fowls this evening 3 Swan, 8 brant and 5 Ducks, on which we made a Sumptious Supper. We gave the Indian\u2014who lent the Canoe a brant, and Some meat to the others. \u00a0\u00a0 one of those Indians, the man from the village near the lower Rapids has a gun with a brass barrel & Cock of which he prises highly\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 note the mountain we Saw from near the forks proves to be Mount Hood\n \n

[Ordway]

\nSunday 3rd Nov. 1805. \u00a0\u00a0 a foggy morning. \u00a0\u00a0 the geese verry pleanty on a marshey green below the Camp \u00a0\u00a0 one of our hunters Shot Several flying. Several Savages Stayed with us last night \u00a0\u00a0 they have 2 canoes with them. \u00a0\u00a0 we perceive the tide rise and fall a little at this place. \u00a0\u00a0 one of the hunters [10] went out a Short distance to hunt and killed a large Deer. \u00a0\u00a0 about 9 oClock \nwe set out and proceeded on \u00a0\u00a0 the fog So thick that we could not See across the River. \u00a0\u00a0 passd. Several Sand Islands \u00a0\u00a0 about noon we halted to dine at the mouth of a River which is filled with quick Sand and is wide and Shallow \u00a0\u00a0 our officers name this River Quick Sand River\n [11] on the Lard. Side. \u00a0\u00a0 here we perceive the tide water. \u00a0\u00a0 we Saw the round mountain some distance a head which we expect is the Same which was discovred by Lieut. Hood and is called Hoods Mountain. [12] \u00a0\u00a0 the after part of the day pleasant \u00a0\u00a0 the River is better than a mile wide in general. \u00a0\u00a0 towards evening we met several Indians in canoes \u00a0\u00a0 they Signed to us that their was Some white people and vessells &C. \u00a0\u00a0 passed Several bottoms covred with cotten and oak pine &C. \u00a0\u00a0 the Country not so Mountaineous as above. \u00a0\u00a0 we came 13 miles this day and Camped [13] on a verry large Island which is mostly prarie and large ponds, which is full of Swan Geese brants and ducks &C. Several Indians Camped with us. \u00a0\u00a0 at Sunset we got a small canoe and carried in the pond \u00a0\u00a0 Several Swan geese and brants killed by the party to day and this evening.\u2014\n\n\n

[Gass]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Sunday 3rd. \u00a0\u00a0 The morning was foggy: one of the men went out and killed a fine buck. At 9 we proceeded on, but could not see the country we were passing, on account of the fog, which was very thick till noon when it disappeared, and we had a beautiful day. We at that time came to the mouth of a river on the south side, a quarter of a mile broad, but not more than 6 or 8 inches deep, running over a bar of quicksand. At this place we dined on venison and goose; and from which we can see the high point of a mountain covered with snow, in about a southeast direction from us. Our Commanding Officers are of opinion that it is Mount Hood, discovered by a Lieutenant of Vancoover, who was up this river 75 miles. The river that falls in here has two mouths, through which it drives out a considerable quantity of sand into the Columbia. Opposite the lower mouth there is a handsome island. At 2 o'clock we proceeded on, and passed another island. The country on both sides appears level and closely timbered: on the river the timber is cotton wood, maple [14] and some ash; and back from it mostly spruce pine. We made 13 miles and encamped on a large island, in which is a large pond full of swans, geese and ducks. On our way and here we killed some of each kind. At night, Captain Lewis had a small canoe carried over to the pond in order to hunt by moon light; but the party did not happen to have good luck, having killed only a swan and three ducks.\n\n\n

[Whitehouse]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Sunday 3rd Nov. 1805. \u00a0\u00a0 a foggy morning. \u00a0\u00a0 we delayd. untill abt. 9 oClock. \u00a0\u00a0 Several men went out a Short distance to hunt. \u00a0\u00a0 we Shot Several geese flying over our Camp this morning. \u00a0\u00a0 one canoe belonging to the Savages and Several Indians camped with us last night. \u00a0\u00a0 a handsom Spring run came in near our Camp. \u00a0\u00a0 the fog So thick this morning that we cannot See more than one hundred yards distance. \u00a0\u00a0 agreeable to all calculations it cannot be more than two hundred miles from this to the ocean. \u00a0\u00a0 one of the men killd. a large buck Deer \u00a0\u00a0 we then Set out abt. 9 oC. and proceeded on \u00a0\u00a0 the fog continued So thick that we could Scarsely See the Shores or Islands \u00a0\u00a0 passed Several Isld. \u00a0\u00a0 abt. noon we halted to dine at the mo. of a River which came in on the Lard. Side, the mouth of which was filled with quick Sand So that we could run a pole 6 or 8 feet in it, and it emptyd. in at Several places thro a verry large Sand bar which lay at the mo \u00a0\u00a0 we then proceeded on \u00a0\u00a0 passd. the mo. of a Small River [15] on Stard. \u00a0\u00a0 the after part of the day clear and pleas[ant] \u00a0\u00a0 we Saw a high round mountain on the Lard Side which we expect is the Same we Saw abo. the great falls and the Same that Lieut. Hood gave an account off. \u00a0\u00a0 (it is nearly covd. with Snow) \u00a0\u00a0 we proceeded on the River verry wide better than a mile in general. \u00a0\u00a0 a nomber of large Islands &c. \u00a0\u00a0 towards evening we met Several Indians in a canoe who were going up the River. \u00a0\u00a0 they Signed to us that in two Sleeps we Should See [the Ocean vessels and?] white people &c. &c. \u00a0\u00a0 the Country lower and not So mountanous \u00a0\u00a0 the River more handsome the current verry gentle. \u00a0\u00a0 Some bottoms covered with cotton and pine &c. \u00a0\u00a0 passd. a nomber of large Islands. \u00a0\u00a0 went about 13 miles and Camped on a verry large & long Island which was mostly prarie and handsome. \u00a0\u00a0 a large lake on it. \u00a0\u00a0 we have Seen a great many Sea otter in the River ever Since we came to the Big falls. \u00a0\u00a0 the geese and Swan ducks and brants &c. \u00a0\u00a0 we killed Several Swan geese and brants &c. this day. \u00a0\u00a0 a nomber of the Savages Camped with us. \u00a0\u00a0 at Sunset we got a Small canoe from them and carried it out to the pond or lake and killed a nomber of large Swan and geese.

\n

\nSunday Novemr. 3d \u00a0\u00a0 A foggy morning & we delayed setting out till about 9 oClock A. M. \u00a0\u00a0 Several of our men went out a short distance for to hunt.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 The party that remained at Camp, shot this morning several Geese as they were flying over our Camp. \u00a0\u00a0 One of the Canoes that we saw at the large rapid stopped at the place we encamped, & the Indians that were in her, & several others of the same Nation that came by land staid with us all night. \u00a0\u00a0 The fog got so thick that we could not see 100 Yards distance from the Camp. \u00a0\u00a0 We find agreeable to the best calculation we can make that we are not more than 200 Miles from the Ocean. \u00a0\u00a0 One of the party that went out hunting this morning returned to the Camp and brought with him a large buck Deer which he had killed. \u00a0\u00a0 The remainder of the hunters also returned, & had killed a number of Geese & Ducks. \u00a0\u00a0 We then set out on our Voyage, the fog continuing so thick, that we could scarcely see the Shores or Islands as we passed along \u00a0\u00a0 We saw about 12 o'Clock A. M. several Islands & halted to dine at the Mouth of a River, which came into the Columbia River on the South side. \u00a0\u00a0 The mouths or entrance of this River is filled with a quick sand, which we run a pole 8 feet down, & had no solid bottom and it emptied itself by several Mouths

\n

\nAt 2 o'Clock P. M. we passed also the Mouth of a small river, lying on the North side of the Columbia River. \u00a0\u00a0 The weather now got clear & pleasant. \u00a0\u00a0 We continued on, and saw a high round Mountain, lying on the South side \nof the River, which we supposed to be the same Mountain, that we saw above the great falls of this River, and believe it to be the same Mountain; that Lieutenant Hood gave an account of when on a Voyage round the World with Captain Cook, This Mountain appeared nearly covered with Snow. \u00a0\u00a0 We proceeded on, the River being nearly One Mile & a quarter wide in general, & a number of large Islands in it, laying on both sides of it.\u2014

\n

\nTowards evening we met with several Indians, who were in a Canoe, & who were going up the River. \u00a0\u00a0 The Indians made signs to us, that in 2 Sleeps, (meaning 2 days,) that we should come to a place, where we should see two Vessells, white people &ca. \u00a0\u00a0 The country during this day, appears to lay lower than it had been for some time past, & but few mountains to be seen. \u00a0\u00a0 The River had a handsomer appearance & the Current of it very gentle. \u00a0\u00a0 We passed by some bottom land, lying along the River, which were cover'd with Cotton wood & Pine Trees, and a number of Islands. \u00a0\u00a0 We encamped on a very large long Island by us called Swan Island, which was chiefly a Priari, and had a large lake in it, having gone about 13 Miles this day. \u00a0\u00a0 We saw in the River ever since we came through the great falls, a great many Sea Otters, Geese, Swans, brants, ducks & other water fowl.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 Our party killed several Geese Swans & Ducks \u2329of them\u232a this day. \u00a0\u00a0 A number of Indians came to the Island that we were encamped on, in Canoes, & encamped with us. \u00a0\u00a0 Some of our party borrowed a small Canoe from those Indians, & carried it out to the lake, where they killed a further quantity of Swans & Geese.\u2014\n\n\n\n

\n
1. Between the camp of November 2 and the mouth of Sandy (Quicksand) River they passed the highest point on the Columbia reached by Lieutenant William Broughton of George Vancouver's expedition in 1792. This was on a large sand island, today's Reed Island, much of which had disappeared by 1805. Thus they returned, for the first time since April 1805, to country previously explored by whites. They also passed the point Broughton named Point Vancouver, having sighted it from the island; the point is about four miles east of Washougal, Clark County, Washington, and across the river from Oregon's\n Rooster Rock State Park. Barry (BOC); Anderson (SSGV), 116\u201317; Meany, 264\u201365; Atlas maps 79, 88. (back)
\n
2. \n Sandy River, \"Quick Sand River\" on Atlas map 79, in Multnomah County, Oregon. (back)
\n
3. The sand deposited by Sandy River is derived by erosion of mudflows from Mt. Hood containing abundant volcanic ash and volcaniclastic fragments. The mudflow is the last of many and may have occurred less than two hundred years ago\u2014probably just a few years before Lewis and Clark passed through here. Clark was correct about the impact of the Sandy on the Columbia, as its delta has diverted the main stream's channel two miles to the north. Allen (MG), 82. (back)
\n
4. \n Washougal River, meeting the Columbia near present Washougal; \"Seal R\" on Atlas map 79. (back)
\n
5. \"White Brant Isld\" on Atlas map 79, and \"Fowl I\" on Atlas map 88; apparently later Lady Island. Archaeological work here, although largely unpublished, is noteworthy for the discovery of prehistoric ceramic artifacts associated with occupation of a site on the island approximately 2,000\u20132,500 years ago. Woodward (ECT). (back)
\n
6. \n Diamond Island is later Government and McGuire islands, opposite Portland, Multnomah County. The samp was on the island, about three miles west of present Camas, Clark County, Washington. Atlas map 79. (back)
\n
7. Some of these birds may be identified as the sandhill crane, Grus canadensi [AOU, 206], and the double-crested cormorant, Phalacrocorax auritus [AOU, 120]. Although the wood stork, Mycteria americana [AOU, 188], was once considered to be one of the bird's identified, this information may not be valid since its range is well south of Lewis and Clark's path. The swans (noted as the \"large & Small kind\" in the first entry) would be the trumpeter swan, Cygnus buccinator [AOU, 181], and the tundra, or whistling, swan, C. columbianus [AOU, 180]. See below, March 9, 1806. The other references are too obscure for precise identification. Holmgren, 33; Burroughs, 199\u2013200. (back)
\n
8. The harbor seal. (back)
\n
9. The \"Snake\" woman was probably a Paiute, but perhaps a Shoshonean whose language was unfamiliar to Sacagawea. Biddle added the red-inked note about the \"Mulknoma\" knowing the party's discovery of this river on their return journey in April 1806; it is the present Willamette River, meeting the Columbia at Portland. Actually their original supposition that these Indians were living on the \"Clark's\" River (present Deschutes) was probably correct. Ray & Lurie, 365; Atlas maps 77, 79. (back)
\n
10. \n Collins, according to Clark. (back)
\n
11. \n Sandy River, Multnomah County, Oregon. (back)
\n
12. \n Mt. Hood, Hood River County, Oregon, was named for British Admiral Sir Samuel Hood by Lieutenant William Robert Broughton of George Vancouver's seaborne exploring \nexpedition in 1792. They passed his farthest point on the river this day. (back)
\n
13. On either Government or McGuire islands, opposite and upstream from Portland, \nMultnomah County. (back)
\n
14. Probably bigleaf maple, Acer macrophyllum Pursh. (back)
\n
15. \n Washougal River, joining the Columbia River near Washougal, Clark County, \nWashington. (back)
