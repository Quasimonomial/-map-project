\n July 19, 1804\n

\n
\n \n

[Clark]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n of no consequence 3 [1] \u00a0\u00a0 July 19th \u00a0\u00a0 afte[r] breakfast which was on a rosted Ribs of a Deer a little and a little Coffee I walked on Shore intending only to Keep up with the Boat, Soon after I got on Shore, Saw Some fresh elk Sign, which I was induced to prosue those animals by their track to the hills \u00a0\u00a0 after assending and passing thro a narrow Strip of wood Land, Came Suddenly into an open and bound less Prarie, I Say bound less because I could not See the extent of the plain in any Derection, the timber appeared to be confined to the River Creeks & Small branches, this Prarie was Covered with grass about 18 Inches or 2 feat high and contained little of any thing else, except as before mentioned on the River Creeks &c, This prospect was So Sudden & entertaining that I forgot the object of my prosute and turned my attention to the Variety which presented themselves to my view \u00a0\u00a0 after continueing on the rise for Some minits, I deturmined to make my course to a line of woods to S: E. \u00a0\u00a0 I found in this wood a butifull Streem of running water, in prosuing it down Several others Joined it and at 3 miles fell into the river between 2 clifts, [2] I went up & under one clift of dark rich Clay for \u00bd me. \u00a0\u00a0 above this a Clay bank which had sliped in \u00a0\u00a0 her[e] I found Sand Stone Containing Iron ore, this ore appears to be inbeded under the Clay just above the water [3]\n

\n July 19th Thursday 1804\n\n

\n

\nSet out early \u00a0\u00a0 pass between 2 Islands one in mid: & the other L. S. opsd. wher Prarie aproaches the river S. S. This place is called the Bakers oven or in french Four le Tour tere \u00a0\u00a0 passd. Some high lands 4\u00bd ms. above the Isds. on the L. S. forming a Clift to the river of yellow earth, on the top a Prarie, passd. many a bad Sand bar in this distance, & the river wide & Shallow, above this Clift 2 Small butiffull runs Come from the Plains & fall into the river, a Deer lick on the first, above those two Creeks, I found in my walk on Shore Some ore in a bank which had Sliped in to the river \u00be me. above the Creeks, I took a cerequite around & found that those two runs mentioned contained a good proposion of wood Surrounded by a plain, with grass about 18 Inchs. high, [(]Capt Lewis walked on Shore after Dinner) \u00a0\u00a0 in the first bind to the right above those Runs passed a Small Island opsd. is a Sand bar \u00a0\u00a0 I call this Island Butter Island, as at this place we mad use of the last of our butter, as we approach this Great River \n Platt\n the Sand bars are much more noumerous than they were, and the quick & roleing Sands much more danjerous, where the Praries aproach the river it is verry wide, the banks of those Plains being much easier to undermine and fall than the wood land \u00a0\u00a0 passed (4) a willow Island Situated near the middle of the river, a Sand bar on the S. S. and a Deep bend to the L S. \u00a0\u00a0 camped on the right Side of the Willow Island\u2014 [5] \u00a0\u00a0 W. Bratten hunting on the L. S Swam to the Island. Hunters Drewyer killed 2 Deer, Saw great numbers of young gees. The river Still falling a little \u00a0\u00a0 Sand bars thick \u00a0\u00a0always in view.

[Clark]

\n July 19th, Thursday 1804\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nSet out early \u00a0\u00a0 passed between two Small Islands, one in the middle of the river, the other Close on the L S. opposit a prarie S. S. Called (1) by the french Four le tourtre,\n [6] The Bakers oven Islands, passed (2) Some high Clifts 4\u00bd miles above the Islands on the L. S. \u2329forming\u232a of yellow earth \u00a0\u00a0 passed Several \u2329bad\u232a Sand bars that were wide and at one place verry Shallow (two Small butifull runs falls into the river near each other at this Clift, a Deer Lick 200 yards up the Lowest of those runs[)] [7] \u00a0\u00a0 Those runs head at no great distance in the plains and pass thro: [NB: Skirts] or timber to the river. In my walk on Shore I found Some ore in the bank above those runs which I take to be Iron ore (3) \u00a0\u00a0 at this place the Side of the hill has Sliped about half way into the river for \u00be of a Mile forming a Clift from the top of the hill above. In the first bend to the right passed a Small Island a Sand bar opposit,\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 worthey of remark as we approach this great River Plate the Sand bars much more numerous and the quick or moveing Sands much worst than they were below \u00a0\u00a0 at the places where Praries approach the river it is verry wide those places being much easier to wash & under Mine than the wood Land's. \u00a0\u00a0 (4) passed a Willow Isd. Situated near the Middle of the river and a large Sand makeing out from the S. S. \u00a0\u00a0 a Deep bend to the L S. \u00a0\u00a0 we Camped at the head of this Island on the Starboard Side of it, Hunters Killed Two Deer. Saw great numbers of young Gees \u00a0\u00a0 River falling a little.

[Ordway]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nThursday July 19th 1804. \u00a0\u00a0 we Set out eairly. \u00a0\u00a0 between 2 Islands passed a place called Bakers oven & an Island called Bakers Island. [9] \u00a0\u00a0 we passed the mouth of a Small creek [10] in a bend above high clifts on the South Side \u00a0\u00a0 passed high land along the South Side forming clifts to the river of yallow earth. \u00a0\u00a0 praries on the top. \u00a0\u00a0 we Saw Some Signs of Elk. \u00a0\u00a0 we gethered a quantity of cherries [11] at noon time & put in to the Whisky barrel \u00a0\u00a0 we proceded on \u00a0\u00a0 passed Several Sand bars & Islands and we camped on the north Side of an a willow Island which is in a round bend of the river. G. Drewyer Joined us with 2 Deer this evening. Bratton also. \u00a0\u00a0 he found Callimous [12] opposite where we camped & a large quantity. \u00a0\u00a0 (Sweet flag we call it)\n\n

\nThursday July 19th \u00a0\u00a0 we Set out errly this morning \u00a0\u00a0 prosed on \u00a0\u00a0 passed a Run on the South Side \u00a0\u00a0 Has no name we Called Cherry Run\n [13] \u00a0\u00a0 the Land is High Cliefts and \u2329bore\u232a pore whare a Grate nomber of thos Cherres \u00a0\u00a0 \u2329frute groe\u232a thay Gro on Low Bushes about as High as a mans hed \u00a0\u00a0 Came 9 miles \u00a0\u00a0 past Several Isd. \u00a0\u00a0 water Strong \u00a0\u00a0 Campt on the South Side on a Small willow Isd. near the South Side \u00a0\u00a0 the Land on the N. is Low Land \u00a0\u00a0 that on the South is High prarie Land\n\n\n

[Gass]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Thursday 19th. \u00a0\u00a0 At sunrise we renewed our voyage, and passed a number of sand bars, and high land on the south side. Where we halted for dinner we found a great quantity of cherries, called by some choak-cherries. We encamped for the night on an island of Willows.\n\n

[Whitehouse]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nThursday 19th \u00a0\u00a0 Got on under way \u00a0\u00a0 the Morning was Clear \u00a0\u00a0 passd. 4 Islands to our Stabourd \u00a0\u00a0 as we came along Shore there was two large Cat fish had hold of Each other Could not get off \u00a0\u00a0 one of the french men Shot the two the first Shot. [14] \u00a0\u00a0 On the W. Shore at Butter run, [15] the men pulld a Great Quantity of wild Cherrys \u00a0\u00a0 put them in the Barrel of whisky. \u00a0\u00a0 Roed. 12 Miles \u00a0\u00a0 Campd. on an Island neer the River Calld. the Crying Water\u2014

\n

\nThursday July 19th \u00a0\u00a0 We got under Way this morning, having clear weather, and passed 4 Islands on our way, laying on the No. East Side of the River, as we passed along the Shore we espy'd two of the largest siz'd catfish, which had hold of each other, One of the Canadian french Men, shot at and kill'd one of them on the So. Western side of the River & wounded the other, which we got also, we landed & found a great quantity of Wild cherries, we gather'd a Quantity of them, which we put into a barrell of Whiskey, we proceeded on at 3oClock P.M. \u2329we proceeded on\u232a & encamped on an Island called the crying Island, & near a River call'd the crying Water, distance come this day 12 miles.\u2014\n\n\n

1. These words occur immediately in front of the date in the Field Notes (document 32). There is no apparent connection to the July 18 entry above or to anything else. Since there is a second, separate Field Notes entry for this date, Clark may have intended to indicate that there was nothing important in this first one. There is a course overwritten to one side of this entry: N 54\u00b0 W. (back)
\n
2. In his Codex A entry and the second Field Notes entry, below, Clark indicates that there were two creeks. They are probably North Table Creek and South Table Creek, which reach the Missouri River at Nebraska City, Otoe County, Nebraska. MRC map 21; MRR maps 59, 60. (back)
\n
3. See July 18, 1804, n. 3. (back)
\n
4. This is a second entry for July 19, or a continuation of the first, on a separate sheet (document 33) of the Field Notes. Biddle's notation at the top of this sheet reads, \"July 19 to 21.\" Clark divided the text around the course material. Here the text is brought together for ease of reading. (back)
\n
5. Probably in Fremont County, Iowa, two to three miles upstream and opposite present Nebraska City. MRC map 21; MRR map 60. (back)
\n
6. \n Biddle apparently placed brackets around the phrase \"by the french Four le tourtre\" and then crossed it out, all in red ink. (back)
\n
7. \n South Table Creek. (back)
\n
8. \n Lewis's observation from Codex O. (back)
\n
9. In the vicinity of Nebraska City, Otoe County, Nebraska; see Clark's entry for July 18. (back)
\n
10. Either North Table Creek or South Table Creek, both of which reach the Missouri River at Nebraska City. (back)
\n
11. The enlisted men all mention the cherries, perhaps because they added them to their whiskey barrel. It is probably choke cherry, Prunus virginiana L., as Gass indicates. (back)
\n
12. \n Ordway is the only writer to mention William Bratton's observation of sweet flag, Acorus calamus L. The final, parenthetical phrase may not be in Ordway's hand. (back)
\n
13. \n Floyd is the only person to apply this name to either North Table Creek or South Table Creek. (back)
\n
14. Only Whitehouse mentions this incident; perhaps the fish were channel catfish, Ictalurus punctatus. (back)
\n
15. Yet another name mentioned only by Whitehouse; probably either North Table Creek or South Table Creek, both entering the Missouri at Nebraska City, Otoe County, Nebraska. (back)
