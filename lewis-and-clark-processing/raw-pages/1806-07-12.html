\n July 12, 1806\n

\n
\n \n

[Lewis]

\n \n
\n July 12th 1806.\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nwe arrose early and resumed our operations in compleating our canoes which we completed by 10 A. M. \u00a0\u00a0 about this time two of the men whom I had dispatched this morning in quest of the horses returned with seven of them only. \u00a0\u00a0 the \u2329othe\u232a remaining ten of our best horses were absent and not to be found. I fear that they \u2329have\u232a are stolen. I dispatch two men on horseback in surch of them. \u00a0\u00a0 the wind blew so violently that I did not think it prudent to attempt passing the river.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 at \u23293 PM\u232a Noon Werner returned having found three others of the horses near Fort Mountain. Sergt. Gass did not return untill 3 P. M. not having found the horses. \u00a0\u00a0 he had been about 8 ms. up medecine river. I now dispatched Joseph Fields and Drewyer in quest of them. \u00a0\u00a0 the former returned at dark unsuccessfull and the latter continued absent all night. \u00a0\u00a0 at 5 P. M. the wind abated and we transported our baggage and meat to the opposite shore in our canoes which we found answered even beyond our expectations. \u00a0\u00a0 we swam our horses over also and encamped at sunset. [1] \u00a0\u00a0 quetoes extreemly troublesome. I think the river is somewhat higher than when we were here last \u2329spring\u232a summer. \u00a0\u00a0 the present season has been much more moist than the preceeding one. \u00a0\u00a0 the grass and weeds are much more luxouriant than they were when I left this place on the 13th of July 1805.\u2014 saw the brown thrush, [2] pigeons, [3] doves &c.\u2014

\n

\nthe yellow Currants begining to ripen. [4]\n

\n \n
\n
\n \n

[Clark]

\n \n
\n Saturday 12th July 1806\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Sergt. Pryor did not join me last night he has proceeded on down. the beaver was flacking [5] [NB: flapping their tails] in the river about us all the last night. \u00a0\u00a0 this Morning I was detained untill 7 A M makeing paddles and drawing the nails of the Canoe to be left at this place and the one we had before left here. \u00a0\u00a0 after completing the paddles &c and takeing Some Brackfast I set out \u00a0\u00a0 the Current I find much Stronger below the forks than above and the river tolerably streight as low as panther Creek\n [6] when it became much more Crooked \u00a0\u00a0 the Wind rose and blew hard off the Snowey mountains to the N. W. [7] and renderd it very difficuelt to keep the canoes from running against the Shore \u00a0\u00a0 at 2 P. M. the Canoe in which I was in was driven by a Suden puff of wind under a log which projected over the water from the bank, and the man in the Stern Howard was Caught in between the Canoe and the log and a little hurt after disingaging our selves from this log the canoe was driven imediately under a drift which projected over and a little abov the Water, here the Canoe was very near turning over \u00a0\u00a0 we with much exertion after takeing out Some of the baggage hauled her out, and proceeded on without receving any damage. \u00a0\u00a0 the men in the other Canoes Seeing our Situation landed and come with as much Speed as possible through the briers and thick brush to our assistance. \u00a0\u00a0 but from the thickness of the brush did not get up to our assistance untill we had got Clear. \u00a0\u00a0 at 3 P M we halted at the enterance of Fields Creek and dined here Willard and Collins over took us with two deer which they had killd. this morning and by takeing a different Side of an Island from which we Came, we had passed them. after dinner I proceeded on and Encamped a little below our encampmt. of the 31st of July last. [8] \u00a0\u00a0 the Musquetoes very troublesome this evening \u00a0\u00a0 Some old buffalow Signs. I killed 4 young gees and Collins killed 2 bever this evening.\n\n\n\n

\n \n
\n
\n \n

[Ordway]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nSaturday 12th July 1806. \u00a0\u00a0 a clear morning. \u00a0\u00a0 the canoe we left here last year we Split up this morning for paddles &C. Set out as usal and proceed. on down the river fast \u00a0\u00a0 the canoe Capt. Clark was in got drove to Shore by the wind under Some tops of trees and was near being filled with water. Capt.\nClark fired 2 guns as a Signal for help \u00a0\u00a0 I and the other canoes which was a head halted and went to their assistance. \u00a0\u00a0 they Soon got him Safe off. \u00a0\u00a0 about 2 P. M. we halted to dine at the mouth of R. Fields Creek\n [9] \u00a0\u00a0 the hunters killed one deer and one beaver. \u00a0\u00a0 we dined and proceeded on down the little gape of the mount. and Camped [10] about Sunset \u00a0\u00a0 Collins killed two beaver this evening.\u2014\n\n\n

\n \n
\n
\n \n

[Gass]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Saturday 12th. \u00a0\u00a0 Again a fine morning. We went out to collect our horses and found that ten of them were missing. I then set out to look for them, went seven miles up Medicine river, where I found three of them and returned to camp. Two more [11] went to hunt for them, and the rest of us crossed the river in our new craft, [12] which we find answer the purpose very well. At night one of the men [13] returned without finding the lost horses.\n\n\n

\n \n
\n \n \n
\n
1. This camp was on the east bank of the Missouri, in Cascade County, Montana, somewhat below the old White Bear Islands camp and south of the city of Great Falls. The area, but not the camp, appears on Atlas maps 42, 54, 61. (back)
\n
2. Brown thrasher, Toxostoma rufum [AOU, 705]. (back)
\n
3. Perhaps passenger pigeons, Ectopistes migratorius [AOU, 315]. (back)
\n
4. Probably golden currant, Ribes aureum Pursh. Booth & Wright, 107. (back)
\n
5. Apparently an obsolete dialect form of \"flapping.\" Criswell, 39. (back)
\n
6. Present Big Pipestone Creek, reaching the Jefferson River near Whitehall, in Jefferson County, Montana. See August 3, 1805. Atlas map 65. (back)
\n
7. \n Highland Mountains, in Jefferson and Madison counties. (back)
\n
8. \n Clark does not indicate on which side of the Jefferson River this camp was. It would be in either Jefferson or Madison County, some two miles below the mouth of Antelope Creek and the camp of July 31, 1805, near where U.S. Highway 287 crosses the Jefferson. Atlas map 65. (back)
\n
9. \n Boulder River meets the Jefferson River in Jefferson County, Montana. (back)
\n
10. About two miles below the mouth of Antelope Creek in either Jefferson or Madison County, Montana, near where U.S. Highway 287 crosses the Jefferson. Ordway's \"little gape of the mount.\" is the area the captains called the third gap in the mountains on the outbound journey, near their encampment of July 31, 1805, and near where they now camp. (back)
\n
11. \n Joseph Field and Drouillard, says Lewis. (back)
\n
12. They camped on the east bank of the Missouri, Cascade County, Montana, somewhat \nbelow the old White Bear Islands camp and south of the city of Great Falls. (back)
\n
13. \n Joseph Field, Lewis says. (back)
