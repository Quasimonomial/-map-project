\n April 23, 1806\n

\n
\n \n

[Lewis]

\n \n
\n Wednesday April 23rd 1806.\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nAt day light this morning we were informed that the two horses of our Interpreter Charbono were absent; on enquiry it appeared that he had neglected to confine them to picquts as had been directed last evening. \u00a0\u00a0 we immediately dispatched Reubin Feilds and Labuish to assist Charbono in recovering his horses. \u00a0\u00a0 one of them was found at no great distance and the other was given over as lost. \u00a0\u00a0 at 8 A. M. Reuben Feilds and Sergt. Gass proceeded in the canoe. \u00a0\u00a0 at 10 Labuish and Charbono returned unsuccessfull, they had gone back on the road nearly to the last village and suched the plains on either hand to a considerable distance. \u00a0\u00a0 our remaining longer would have prevented our making a timely stage which in our situation is all important; we therefore determined to proceed immediately to the next village which from the information of our guide will occupy the greater part of the day to reach \u00a0\u00a0 at eleven OCk. we loaded our horses and set out. \u00a0\u00a0 during the time we were detained this morning we had two packsaddles made. \u00a0\u00a0 we continued our march along a narrow rocky bottom on the N. side of the river about 12 miles to the Wah-how-pum Village [1] of 12 temperary mat lodges near the Rock rapid. \u00a0\u00a0 these people appeared much pleased to see us, sold us 4 dogs and some wood for our small articles which we had previously prepared as our only resource to obtain fuel and food through those plains. \u00a0\u00a0 these articles conisted of pewter buttons, strips of tin iron and brass, twisted wire &c. \u00a0\u00a0 we also obtained some shap-le-lell newly made from these people. \u00a0\u00a0 here we met with a Chopunnish man on his return up the river with his family and about 13 head of horses most of them young and unbroken.\u00a0\u00a0 he offered to hire us some of them to pack as far a his nation, but we prefer bying as by hireing his horses we shal have the whole of his family most probably to mentain. \u00a0\u00a0 at a little distance below this village we passed five lodges of the same people who like those were waiting the arrival of the salmon. \u00a0\u00a0 after we had arranged our camp we caused all the old and brave men to set arround and smoke with us. \u00a0\u00a0 we had the violin played and some of the men danced; after which the natives entertained us with a dance after their method. [2] \u00a0\u00a0 this dance differed from any I have yet seen. \u00a0\u00a0 they formed a circle and all sung as well the spectators as the dancers who performed within the circle. these placed their sholders together with their robes tightly drawn about them and danced in a line from side to side, several parties of from 4 to seven will be performing within the circle at the same time. \u00a0\u00a0 the whole concluded with a premiscuous dance in which most of them sung and danced. \u00a0\u00a0 these people speak a language very similar to the Chopunnish whome they also resemble in their dress \u00a0\u00a0 their women wear long legings mockersons shirts and robes. \u00a0\u00a0 their men also dress with legings shirts robes and mockersons. \u00a0\u00a0 after the dance was ended the indians retired at our request and we retired to rest. \u00a0\u00a0 we had all our horses side hubbled and turned out to graize; at this village, a large creek [3] falls in on the N. side which we did not observe as we decended the river. \u00a0\u00a0 the river is by no means as rapid as when we decended or at least not obstructed with those dangerous rapids \u00a0\u00a0 the water at present covers most of the rocks in the bed of the river. \u00a0\u00a0 the natives promised to barter their horses with us in the morning we therefore entertained a hope that we shall be enabled to proceede by land from hence with the whole of our party and baggage. \u00a0\u00a0 came 12 miles by land. \u00a0\u00a0 the sands made the march fatieguing.\u2014\n

\n \n

[Clark]

\n \n
\n Wednesday 23rd 1806\n [4]\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nat day light this morning we were informed that the two horses of our interpreter Shabono were missing \u00a0\u00a0 on enquirey we were informed that he had neglected to tie up his horses as derected last evening. \u00a0\u00a0 we imedeately dispatch him, R. Fields & Labiech in Serch of the horses, one of them were \u2329purch\u232a found at no great distnace. \u00a0\u00a0 the other was not found. R. Fields retd. without finding the horse Set out with Sergt Gass in the Small Canoe at about 8 A M. \u00a0\u00a0 at 10 Shabono and Labiech returned also unsucksessfull \u00a0\u00a0 they had went on the back trail nearly to the last Village and took a circle around on the hills. \u00a0\u00a0 as our Situation was Such that we Could not detain for a horse, which would prevent our makeing a timely Stage which is a great object with us in those open plains, we Concluded to give up the horse and proceed on to the next village which we were informed was at Some distance and would take us the greater part of the day. \u00a0\u00a0 at 11 A. M. we packed up and Set out and proceeded up on the N. Side of the Columbia on a high narrow bottom and rockey for 12 miles to the \n Wah-how-pum\n village near the rock rapid of 12 temporary mat Lodges, those people appeared pleased to See us. \u00a0\u00a0 they Sold us 4 dogs Some Shapollell and wood for our Small articles Such as awls pieces of Tin and brass. \u00a0\u00a0 we passed Several Lodges on the bank of the river where they were fixed waiting for the Salmon. I over took a Choponish man whome I had Seen at the long, [5] and who had found a bag of our powder and brought it to me at that place. \u00a0\u00a0 this man had his family on the [blank] and about 3 head of horses which appeared young and unbroke. \u00a0\u00a0 his spous as also that of the other gave me a Cake of Chapellell and proceeded on with me to the Wah howpum Village and formed his Camp near us. \u00a0\u00a0 we Caused all the old & brave men to Set around and Smoke with us. \u00a0\u00a0 we Caused the fiddle to be played and Some of the men danced. \u00a0\u00a0 after them the nativs danced. \u00a0\u00a0 they dance different from any Indians I have Seen. \u00a0\u00a0 they dance with their Sholders together and pass from Side to Side, defferent parties passing each other, from 2 to 7. \u00a0\u00a0 and 4 parties danceing at the Same time and Concluding the dance by passing promiscuisly throu & beetween each other. \u00a0\u00a0 after which we Sent of the Indians and retired to bed. Those people Speak a language verry Similal to the Chopunish and with a very inconsiderable difference. \u00a0\u00a0 their dress and appearance to the Chopunish and with a very inconsiderable difference. \u00a0\u00a0 their dress and appearance is more like those of the Great falls of the Columbia. \u00a0\u00a0 we had all our horses Side hobbled and let out to feed. \u00a0\u00a0 at this village a large Creek falls in on the N. Side which I had not observed as I decended the river. \u00a0\u00a0 the river is by no means as rapid as it was at the time we decended. The nativs promised to give is a horse for one of our Canoes. and offer to Sell us another for a Scarlet robe which we have not at present. Shabono made a bargin with one of the Indian men going with us, for a horse for Which he gave his Shirt. \u00a0\u00a0 and two of the leather Sutes of his wife. The Sand through which we walked to day is So light that renders the march verry fatigueing. \u00a0\u00a0 made 12 miles by land.\n\n

[Ordway]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nWednesday 23rd of April 1806. \u00a0\u00a0 one of our horses broke loose and Strayed away last night \u00a0\u00a0 we delayed to hunt for him & made two pack Saddles. \u00a0\u00a0 we could not find our lost horse. \u00a0\u00a0 about 10 A. M. Set out \u00a0\u00a0 proced on through high plains and banks of Sand along the river. \u00a0\u00a0 the day warm. \u00a0\u00a0 towards evening we arived at a large village at the mouth of a creek where we Camped [6] \u00a0\u00a0 our canoes came up. \u00a0\u00a0 we purchased 4 dogs and a considerable\nquantity of Chappalell &C. \u00a0\u00a0 the day warm. \u00a0\u00a0 these Savages have lately mooved here & have a great number of horses. \u00a0\u00a0 our horses are troublesome as the most of them are Studs. \u00a0\u00a0 but the feed is good the prarie covred with flowrs \u00a0\u00a0 the natives are numerous at this place. \u00a0\u00a0 they had a dance [7] at our fire this evening. \u00a0\u00a0 nearly the Same manner & way as those on the Missourie. \u00a0\u00a0 we played the fiddle and danced &C. Several of the flat heads continue on with us & assist us as much as lyes in their power.\u2014\n\n\n

\n
\n \n

[Gass]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Wednesday 23rd. \u00a0\u00a0 We had a cloudy morning. I went also by water to day, and we had very laborious work in getting along. In the evening we met the party at a large village of the Wal-la-waltz nation, [8] on the north side of the river; where the other canoe had also arrived. Here we halted, unloaded the canoes and encamped. A horse had got away last night and could not be found.\n\n\n\n

1. A Shahaptian-speaking group who lived around the mouth of Rock Creek in Klickitat County, Washington. See October 21, 1805; Altas map 77. Lewis's \"Wah-how-pum\" may come from the tribe's self-designation, h\u01cehau'p\u01d4m, \"willow people.\" However, Spier equates the term with Waiy\u01ce'mp\u01cem, \"those of Waiy\u01ce'm,\" the Shahaptian name for Celilo Falls. Spier also suggests that the name Waiy\u01ce'mp\u01cem may not refer to a specific group but collectively to the people who congregated in this area on both sides of the Columbia River. Another possibility is Shahaptian \n x\u0323\n wal/\n x\u0323\n waypam, representing a Shahaptian-speaking group within the Klickitat River drainage. These people under discussion are now generally subsumed under the name Teninos, including others in this general area. Archaeological investigations in this vicinity at the Wildcat Canyon site on the Oregon shore encountered evidence of occupation beginning around 8,000 years ago, with a major village documented at this locality between 2,500 and 1,000 year ago. Hodge, 2:890; Spier (TDW), 19; Ray et al.; Dumond & Minor. The day's camp was at a village above Rock Creek. Atlas map 77. See also figure. (back)
\n
2. While not necessarily religious in form, the circle dance here described suggests an early version of the Prophet Dance, a nativistic movement that later spread widely throughout the Columbia Plateau. Spier (PD). (back)
\n
3. \n Rock Creek, in Klickitat County, nameless on Atlas map 77; it reaches the Columbia above the mouth of John Day River (River La Page) on the opposite shore. (back)
\n
4. Near this date in Clark's Voorhis No. 3 is the word \"Wah-how-pum.\" (back)
\n
5. The \"Long Narrows\" of the Columbia, at The Dalles. Atlas map 78. (back)
\n
6. At Rock Creek, Klickitat County, Washington, at a village of Tenino Indians. (back)
\n
7. \n Lewis and Clark describe the dance more fully in their entries for this day. (back)
\n
8. A \"Wah-how-pum\" village at the mouth of Rock Creek, Klickitat County, Washington. \nThese Shahaptian-language people are now generally classified as Teninos, with others in the vicinity. The party camped here, somewhat above the mouth of John Day River, their River Lapage, after party member Jean Baptiste Lepage, opposite. (back)
