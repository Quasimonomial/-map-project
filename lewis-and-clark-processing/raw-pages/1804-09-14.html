S. \u00a0\u00a0 passed a round Island on the \u2329L\u232a S S.\u2014 [2] \u00a0\u00a0 Caught 3 beaver last night, Some drizzeley rain \u00a0\u00a0 Cloudy & Disagreeable and Som hard Showers, I walked on Shore with a view to find an old Volcano Said to be in this neghbourhood by \n Mr. McKey\n \n [3] \u00a0\u00a0 I was Some distance out \u00a0\u00a0 Could not See any Signs of a Volcanoe, I killed a Goat, [4] which is peculier to this Countrey about the hite of a Grown Deer Shorter, its horns Coms out immediately abov its eyes \u00a0\u00a0 broad 1 Short prong the other arched & Soft \u00a0\u00a0 the color is a light gray with black behind its ears, white round its neck, no beard, his Sides & belly white, and around its taile which is Small & white and Down its hams, actively made \u00a0\u00a0 his brains on the back of its head, his noisterals large, his eyes like a Sheep \u00a0\u00a0 only 2 hoofs on each foot no a[n]telrs (more like the antelope or gazella of Africa than any other Specis of Goat). Shields Killed a Hare weighing 6\u00bd lb: verry pore, the head narrow and its ears 3 Inches wide and 6 long, from the fore to the end of the hind foot; is 2 feet 11 Inch. hite 1 foot 1\u00be its tail long & thick white, clearly the mountain Hare of Europe, [5] a rainy evening \u00a0\u00a0 all wett \u00a0\u00a0 The Soil of those Plains washes down into the flats, with the Smallest rain & disolves & mixes with the water. [6] \u00a0\u00a0 we See back from the river high hills in a leavel plain, evidently the remains of mountains, what mud washed into the river within those few days has made it verry mudy, passed two Small Creeks on the L. S. & Camped below a 3rd on the L. S. [7] \u00a0\u00a0 rained all evening

\n 14th Septr. Friday 1804. \u00a0\u00a0 Set out early proceeded on \u00a0\u00a0 passed Several Sand bars \u00a0\u00a0 the river wide and Shallow \u00a0\u00a0 3 beaver Caught last night, Drizeley rain in the forepart of this day, cloudy and disagreeable, I walked on Shore with a view to find an old Vulcanio, Said to be in this neighbourhood by Mr. J. McKey of St. Charles. I walked on Shore the whole day without Seeing any appearance of the Villcanoe, in my walk I Killed a Buck Goat of this Countrey, about the hight of the Grown Deer, its body Shorter, the Horns which is not very hard and forks \u2154 up \u00a0\u00a0 one prong Short the other round & Sharp arched, and is imediately above its Eyes \u00a0\u00a0 the Colour is a light gray with black behind its ears down its neck, and its Jaw white round its neck, its Sides and its rump round its tail which is Short & white \u00a0\u00a0 verry actively made, has only a pair of hoofs to each foot. \u00a0\u00a0 his brains on the back of his head, his Norstral large, his eyes like a Sheep\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 he is more like the Antilope or Gazella of Africa than any other Species of Goat. Shields Killed a Hare like the mountain hare of Europe, waighing 6\u00bc pounds (altho pore) his head narrow, its ears large i, e, 6 Inches long & 3 Inchs wide one half of each white, the other & out part a lead grey \u00a0\u00a0 from the toe of the hind foot to toe of the for foot is 2 feet 11 Inches, the hith is 1 foot 1 Inche & \u00be, his tail long thick & white.

\n

\nThe rain Continued the Greater part of the day \u00a0\u00a0 in My ramble I observed, that all those parts of the hills which was Clear of Grass easily disolved and washed into the river and bottoms, and those hils under which the river run, Sliped into it and disolves and mixes with the water of the river, the bottoms of the river was covered with the water and mud frome the hills about three Inches deep\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 those bottoms under the hils which is Covered with Grass also [receves?] a great quantity of mud.

\n

\nPassed 2 Small Creeks on the L. S and Camped below the third, (the place that Shannon the man who went a head lived on grapes) \u00a0\u00a0 Some heavy Showers of rain all wet, had the Goat & rabit Stufed \u00a0\u00a0 rained all night\n

\n Friday 14th Sept. 1804. \u00a0\u00a0 we Set off eairly. \u00a0\u00a0 a foggy morning. Cloudy.\nGeorge Drewyer caught 3 Beaver last night. I Sergt. Pryor & Shannon returned to the Boat at 8 o.C. where they were at breakfast, near a grove of Timber N. S. \u00a0\u00a0 the water Shallow. \u00a0\u00a0 all hands out in water Several times to drag the Boat over the Sand bars &.C. \u00a0\u00a0 passd a black Bluff on S. S. Capt. Clark &.C. one man went out hunting on S. S. John Shields who went on Shore with the horse killed a verry large white rabbit or haire it was as big as a Ureopian hare.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 nearly all white\u2014& of a different discription of any one ever yet Seen in the States.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 passed a creek on S. S. & a grove of Timber \u00a0\u00a0 Some rain \u00a0\u00a0 we Camped [10] on the South Side in a Stripe of woods. Capt. Clark joined us had killed a curious annamil resembling a Goat \u00a0\u00a0 Willard brought it on board. \u00a0\u00a0 it was 3 feet high \u00a0\u00a0 resembles a Deer in some parts. \u00a0\u00a0 the legs like a Deer. \u00a0\u00a0 feet like a Goat. \u00a0\u00a0 horns like a Goat only forked \u00a0\u00a0 Turn back \u00a0\u00a0 picked hair thick & of a white a dark redish coullour. Such an anamil was never yet known in U. S. States.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 The Capt had the Skins of the hair & Goat Stuffed in order to Send back to the city of Washington. \u00a0\u00a0 the bones and all.\u2014\n\n

[Gass]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Friday 14th. \u00a0\u00a0 We proceeded as yesterday, and with the same kind of weather. Had considerable difficulty in getting along, on account of the shallowness of the river; all hands in the water dragging the boat. At 8 we halted for breakfast, and the men who went to hunt yesterday came in, and had only killed a porcupine. Three beaver were caught last night. The musketoes are as troublesome as they have been any time in summer. We passed black bluffs on the south side, and an island with timber on it. Passed a creek on the same side and encamped on it. The man who had gone by land with the horse came to us here; had killed a hare. Captain Clarke killed a goat or antelope.\n\n

\n Friday 14th Sept. 1804. \u00a0\u00a0 a foggy morning, a cloudy day and Some rain \u00a0\u00a0 the water is So Shallow that we had to waid & hall the barge over the Sand bars. \u00a0\u00a0 at 8 oClock we halted for to take breakfast. \u00a0\u00a0 the 3 men who went a hunting yesterday joined us here. \u00a0\u00a0 the hills and praries are pleasant but barron. \u00a0\u00a0 G. Drewyer caught 3 beaver last night \u00a0\u00a0 the Musquitoes are troublesom. \u00a0\u00a0 passd. black bluffs on the S. Side and an Island which had Som timber on it. \u00a0\u00a0 passed a creek on South Side. \u00a0\u00a0 the hunters killed a goat and a hare. [11] \u00a0\u00a0 the Goat was killed by Capt Clark & the first that was Seen by the party on the Missourie\u2014

\n

\nFriday September 14th \u00a0\u00a0 This morning we had a great fog, on its going off, it was Cloudy & we had some Rain, We set off, and found the Water so shallow, that we had to go into it\u2014and to haul the Boat over the Sand Barrs, at 8 oClock A. M. we stopped the Boat to breakfast; Captain Clark & the two Men who went a hunting, returned to us here.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 The Country here, is hilly and Priaries.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 which appear to be barren land; but is tolerably pleasant, One of our hunters caught during last night 3 Beavers in his traps, We proceeded on, and passed some black Bluffs lying on the South side of the River, and two Islands lying on the same side, which were cover'd with Timber, we also passed a Creek which lay on the same side of the River; Captain Clark shot a Goat and a hare, which they brought to us.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 and all our hunters join'd us in the Evening, this was the first Goat that was seen by any of our party since we enter'd the Mesouri \u00a0\u00a0 We encamped on the South side of the River at dark.\u2014\n\n

1. \n Biddle added \"& 16\" after the date to indicate that document 54 of the Field Notes goes through September 16. (back)
\n
2. Probably the later Dry Island. Atlas maps 20, 21, 22; MRC map 36. (back)
\n
3. There is no evidence of volcanic action in the area. Burning deposits of coal or lignite may have led Mackay to this belief, which would have been based on the testimony of Evans, since Mackay was never on this part of the Missouri. It is probably the same phenomenon as the Ionia Volcano of Nebraska (see August 24, 1804), that is, a zone where the oxidation of pyritic material yields sulfuric acid which, with the addition of water, produces a reaction so hot that the bituminous shale is ignited. Mackay's reference was probably to the \"Burning Bluff\" in Gregory County, South Dakota, which they had passed several days earlier. Robinson, 555; South Dakota Guide, 403. (back)
\n
4. The first scientific description of the pronghorn, often incorrectly called an antelope. Lewis and Clark continued to refer to it as a \"goat,\" as do many persons on the plains; they also used the term \"cabra.\" Cutright (LCPN), 81. (back)
\n
5. The first description of the white-tailed jackrabbit, Lepus townsendii, previously unknown to science. See Lewis's natural history notes for this day for more information. Ibid., 82\u201383; Jones et al., 114\u201316. (back)
\n
6. The soil is probably the dark, clayey soil of the grasslands which would make the water blackish. The White River, which they pass the next day, derives its name from white clay of western South Dakota. (back)
\n
7. As Clark notes in the Codex B entry, this third creek was where Shannon remained for some days while lost (see Atlas maps 20, 21, 22); they called it Shannon's River. Someone, perhaps Maximilian, has penciled additional names on Atlas map 21. It is apparently the stream later known as Ball or Bull Creek, in Lyman County, South Dakota. The September 14 camp was just below the mouth of the creek, in an area now inundated by Fort Randall Reservoir. Of the other two creeks, nameless on the Atlas maps, the second was Waterhole Creek. Mattes, 514\u201315; Nicollet (MMR), 412; MRC map 36; MRY map 41. (back)
\n
8. \n Lewis's natural history notes from Codex Q. The goat is the pronghorn, also described by Clark this day; see n. 4. (back)
\n
9. \n Lewis's second entry of natural history notes from Codex Q for this date. The white-tailed jackrabbit is also described by Clark this day; see n. 5. (back)
\n
10. Near the mouth of Bull Creek, Lyman County, South Dakota. (back)
\n
11. Pronghorn, Antilocapra americana, were seen as early as September 3 but were not fully described by the captains until this day. The hare is a white-tailed jackrabbit, Lepus townsendii. (back)
