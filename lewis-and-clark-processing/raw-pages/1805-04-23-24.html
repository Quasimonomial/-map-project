\n April 23, 1805\n

\n
\n \n

[Lewis]

\n \n
\n Tuesday April 23rd\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nSet out at an early hour this morning. \u00a0\u00a0 about nine A. M. the wind arose, and shortly after became so violent that we were unabled to proceed, in short it was with much difficulty and some risk that I was enabled to get the canoes and perogues into a place of tolerable safety, there being no timber on either side of the river at this place. \u00a0\u00a0 some of the canoes shiped water, and wet several parsels of their lading, which I directed to be opened and aired \u00a0\u00a0 we remained untill five in the evening when the wind abating in some measure, we reloaded, and proceeded. \u00a0\u00a0 shortly after we were joined by Capt. Clark who had walked on shore this morning, and passing through the bottom lands had fallen on the river some miles above \u2329us\u232a, and concluding that the wind had detained us, came down the river in surch of us. \u00a0\u00a0 he had killed three blacktaled, or mule deer, [1] and a buffaloe Calf, in the course of his ramble. \u00a0\u00a0 these hard winds, being so frequently repeated, become a serious source of detention to us.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 incamped on the Stard. side.\u2014 [2]\n

[Clark]

\n \n
23rd of April 1805\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\na cold morning \u00a0\u00a0 at about 9 oClock the wind as usial rose from the N W and continued to blow verry hard untill late in the evening \u00a0\u00a0 I walked on Shore after brackfast \u00a0\u00a0 in my walk on the S side passed through extensive bottoms of timber intersperced with glades & low open plains, I killed 3 mule or black tail Deer, which was in tolerable order, Saw Several others, I also killed a Buffalow Calf which was verry fine, I Struck the river above the Perogus which had Come too in a bend to the L. S. to Shelter from the wind which had become violently hard, I joined Capt Lewis in the evening & after the winds falling which was late in the evening we proceeded on & encamped on the S. S. The winds of this Countrey which blow with Some violence almost every day, has become a Serious obstruction in our progression onward, as we Cant move when the wind is high with[out] great risque, and [if] there was no risque the winds is generally a head and often too violent to proceed

[Ordway]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Tuesday 23rd April 1805. \u00a0\u00a0 a clear and pleasant morning. \u00a0\u00a0 not quite as cold as it has been for Several morning. \u00a0\u00a0 we Set off as usal \u00a0\u00a0 proceeded on. Some of the men caught two beaver last night \u00a0\u00a0 passed high bluffs on the S. S. and a bottom in a bend on the N. S. \u00a0\u00a0 the river verry crooked \u00a0\u00a0 halted and took breakfast on a sand beach S. S. \u00a0\u00a0 one man Shot a beaver in the willows. \u00a0\u00a0 another Shot a Goose in the river, proceeded on \u00a0\u00a0 the wind blew So hard that the large perogues Sailed in a bend where the wind came fair verry high the Small canoes took in Some water. \u00a0\u00a0 the large perogues Sailed verry fast. \u00a0\u00a0 a Short distanc we were obledged to halt the first Safe place untill the wind abated which was about 3 hours. \u00a0\u00a0 dryed the articles which was wet. \u00a0\u00a0 towards evening the wind abated and we proceeded on round a point and Camped in a bottom covered with c. w. timber on N. S. \u00a0\u00a0 came 14\u00bd miles to day. Capt. Clark killed to day one buffaloe Calf, and three black taild deer.\u2014\n

\n \n

[Gass]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Tuesday 23rd. \u00a0\u00a0 We set our early and had a fine day; but the wind was ahead, and we were obliged to lie to about three hours. We went 15 miles and encamped on the North side. Captain Clarke killed 3 blacktailed deer \nand a buffaloe calf.\n\n\n

\n \n
\n
\n \n

[Whitehouse]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nTuesday April 23rd [4] \u00a0\u00a0 This morning, we had Clear weather and set off at the usual hour,\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 (Early) \u00a0\u00a0 We proceeded on about 3 Miles, when the Wind blew so fresh, that we had to come too, it being a head Wind from the North west.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 We stopped for about two hours, & Captain Clark, and some of our Men went out hunting; We then proceeded \u2329then\u232a on our Voyage, & passed the finest thickets of Wood & level priaries, that we had seen, since we left the Mandan Nation, In the Evening, we came too, and Encamped on the North side of the Mesouri River, having come 15 Miles this day.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 Captain Clark & the party that went with him hunting, joined us some short time after, having kill'd that day 3 large Male Deer, and a Buffalo Calf, which they brought to the Camp\u2014\n\n\n

1. Evidently the first written use of the term \"mule deer\" for Odocoileus hemionus, an expedition discovery. See above, September 16, 1804. Cutright (LCPN), 83\u201384. Other references to deer in this chapter are to O. virginianus, white-tailed deer. (back)
\n
2. The campside, where they remained until April 25, in Williams County, North Dakota, appears on Atlas map 56 as that for April 24 only. Atlas maps 34, 47; MRC map 59. (back)
\n
3. Also given on Atlas map 35, in both captains' hands. (back)
\n
4. When compared with other journals, this appears to be the true entry for April 23. (back)
