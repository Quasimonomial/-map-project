E by S. \u00a0\u00a0 at 3 miles passed the head of the Island on L. S. called by the French Chauve or bald pate (1) opsd. the middle of this Island the Creek on L. S. is within 300 yds. of the river. \u00a0\u00a0 back of this Island the lower point of (2) another Island in the bend to the L. S. \u00a0\u00a0 passed large Sand bar making out from each point with many channels passing through them, \"Current runs 50 fathm. in 41 Seconds\" but little timber on either Side of the river, except the Isds. & points which are low wet & Covered with lofty trees, Cotton wood Mulberry Elm &c. &c. \u00a0\u00a0 passed the head of a long Island in high water \u00a0\u00a0 at this time no water passes thro: the Channel (3) opposit the Lower point of a Island on the L. S. \u00a0\u00a0 pass the Island and opsd. the point (4) above & on the L. S. the hills come to the river, This Hill has Sliped into the river for about \u00be of a mile, and leaves a Bluff of considerable hight back of it \u00a0\u00a0 this Hill is about 200 foot high compsd. of Sand Stone inter mingled with Iron ore of an inferior quallity on a bed of Soft Slate Stone. E. by S. passing over the Prarie, at about 3 Miles we passed the head of the Island L. S. Called by the French \n Chaube\n or \n Bald pate\n \u00a0\u00a0 opposit the middle of (1) This Island the Creek on the S. S. is nearest the river, In high water an Island is formed in the bind above the last (2)\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 Measured the Current and found that in forty one Seconds it run 50 fathoms \u00a0\u00a0 but little timber is to be Seen except in the Low points on Islands & on Creeks, the Groth of timber is generally cotton Mulberry Elm Sycomore &c &c. \u00a0\u00a0 passed a Island on the 2d point to the S. S. oposite the water (3) whin high passes out in the Plain \u00a0\u00a0 oppsid this Island on the L. S. the hills jut to the river (4) \u00a0\u00a0 this Hill has Sliped from the top which forms a Bluff above & 200 foot above the water, about \u00be of a mile in length & about 200 feet in Depth has Sliped into the river \u00a0\u00a0 it is Composed of Sand Stone intermixed with an indiffert. Iron ore \u00a0\u00a0 near the bottom or next to the water is a Soft Slate Stone, Som pebble is also intermixt, we passed a verry bad Sand bar and incamped on the L. S. at the lower point of the oven Islands & opposit the Prarie Calld. \u2329by the french Four le Tourtue\u232a [NB: Baker's oven] [5] \u00a0\u00a0 Saw a Dog nearly Starved on the bank, gave him Som meet, he would not follow, our hunters killed 2 Deer to day

[Ordway]

\nWednesday July 18th 1804. \u00a0\u00a0 we Set out at Sun rise under a gentle Breeze from the S. E. by S. \u00a0\u00a0 a fair morning, we proceded on along this prarie \u00a0\u00a0 passed Several Islands, the current of the River Runs 50' fathom in 41 Seconds, their is but little timber on either Side of the River, except the Islands and points which are low wet & covered with lofty trees Cotton wood Mulbery Elm [6] & C.\u2014&C.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 we passed hill praries, and a place in a high bank where Some appearance of Iron oar where the Bank Sliped in to the River about 200 feet high. \u00a0\u00a0 we camped on the South Side of the Missouris. \u00a0\u00a0 towards night we Saw an Indian dog on the Bank of the River, which appeared to have been lost. Drewyer joined us with 2 Deer this evening late.

[Gass]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Wednesday 18th. \u00a0\u00a0 Early this morning we prosecuted our voyage with a fair wind and pleasant weather. This is the most open country I ever beheld, almost one continued prairie. Two of our hunters went by land with the horses as usual. On the south side we passed high handsome banks or bluffs of red and blue strata; [7] found some iron ore here, and encamped on the south side, where one of the hunters [8] brought us two deer.\n\n\n\n

\n

\nWednesday July 18th \u00a0\u00a0 This morning being clear, we got under way at day light, with a fair wind, and sailed 13 Miles before Noon, we passed a Mine of Iron Ore, laying on the bank of the River on the So. West side. \u00a0\u00a0 The hunters brought us in this evening 2 Deer which they kill'd. \u00a0\u00a0 We encamped on the bank of the River, having Sail'd this day 22 Miles\u2014\n\n

3. Rocks along the bluffs of the Missouri River near Nebraska City have been mapped as Pennsylvanian Wabaunsee Group. The lower part of the Otoe shale is a red shale, which has stained the very thin limestone beneath it and may have been mistaken for \"Iron ore of an inferior quallity.\" A sandstone bed overlies the Otoe, and it is underlain by a bluish gray shale.
