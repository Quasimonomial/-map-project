\n April 11, 1805\n

\n
\n \n

[Lewis]

\n \n
Thursday April 11th
\u00a0
\n \n

\nSet out at an early hour; I proceeded with the party and Capt Clark with George Drewyer walked on shore in order to procure some fresh meat if possible. \u00a0\u00a0 we proceeded on abot five miles, and halted for breakfast, when Capt Clark and Drewyer joined us; the latter had killed, and brought with him a deer which was at this moment excepable as we had had no fresh meat for several days. \u00a0\u00a0 the country from fort Mandan to this place is so constantly hunted by the Minetaries that there is but little game we halted at two P. M. and made a comfortable dinner on a venison stake and beavers tales with the bisquit which got wet on the 8th inst. by the accidant of the canoe filling with water before mentioned. \u00a0\u00a0 the powder which got wet by the same accedent, and which we had spread to dry on the baggage of the large perogue, was now examined and put up; it appears to be almost restored, and our loss is therefore not so great as we had at first apprehended.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 the country much the same as yesterday. \u00a0\u00a0 on the sides of the hills and even the banks of the rivers and sandbars, there is a white substance t[h]at appears in considerable quantities on the surface of the earth, which tastes like a mixture of common salt and glauber salts. \u00a0\u00a0 many of the springs which flow from the base of the river hills are so strongly impregnated with this substance that the water is extreemly unpelasant to the taste and has a purgative effect.\u2014 [1] \u00a0\u00a0 saw some large white cranes pass up the river\u2014 [2] \u00a0\u00a0 these are the largest bird of that genus common to the country through which the Missouri and Mississippi pass. they are perfectly white except the large feathers of the two first joints of the wing which are black. \u00a0\u00a0 we encamped this evening on the Stard. shore just above the point of woodland which formed to extremity of the last course of this day. [3] \u00a0\u00a0 there is a high bluff opposite to us, under which we saw some Indians, but the river is here so wide that we could not speake to them; suppose them to be a hunting party of Minetares.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 we killed two gees today.

[Clark]

\n \n
\n 11th of April Thursday 1805\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\t\t\t\t\t \nSet out verry early \u00a0\u00a0 I walked on Shore, Saw fresh bear tracks, one deer & 2 beaver killed this morning \u00a0\u00a0 in the after part of the day killed two gees; Saw great numbers of Gees Brant & Mallard Some White Cranes Swan & guls, [5] the plains begin to have a green appearance, the hills on either side are from 5 to 7 miles asunder and in maney places have been burnt, appearing at a distance of a redish brown choler, containing Pumic Stone & lava, Some of which rolin down to the base of those hills\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 In maney of those hills forming bluffs to the river we procieve Several Stratums of bituminious Substance which resembles Coal;\n [6] thoug Some of the pieces appear to be excellent Coal it resists the fire for Some[time], and consumes without emiting much flaim.

\n

\nThe plains are high and rich \u00a0\u00a0 Some of them are Sandy Containing Small pebble, and on Some of the hill Sides large Stones are to be Seen\u2014 [7] \u00a0\u00a0 In the evening late we observed a party of \n Me ne tar ras\n on the L. S. with horses and dogs loaded going down, those are a part of the Menetarras who camped a little above this with the Ossinniboins at the mouth of the little Missouri all the latter part of the winter \u00a0\u00a0 we Camped on the S. S. below a falling in bank. \u00a0\u00a0 the river raise a little.

[Ordway]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Thursday 11th April 1805. Clear and pleasant. \u00a0\u00a0 we Set off at light. \u00a0\u00a0 one of the party caught a beaver in a trap which he Set last night. \u00a0\u00a0 the frenchmen killed a Goos & caught one beaver. \u00a0\u00a0 proceeded on. \u00a0\u00a0 one of the party went on Shore in the bottom. N. S. \u00a0\u00a0 came to the crafts at breakfast. \u00a0\u00a0 had killed & brought in a deer \u00a0\u00a0 we passed bare barron hills on S. S. & a bottom covered with timber on the N. S. \u00a0\u00a0 one of the party killed 2 Geese. \u00a0\u00a0 we halted and dined about 2 oClock at a bottom on N. S. \u00a0\u00a0 we had a Gentle breeze from the South So that the large perogues Sailed verry well. \u00a0\u00a0 the day verry warm. Some of the men worked naked. \u00a0\u00a0 only a breech cloth. \u00a0\u00a0 the River being low we have to waid at Some places. \u00a0\u00a0 proceeded on verry well \u00a0\u00a0 about Sunset we Saw a nomber of Indians on S. S. \u00a0\u00a0 they had Some horses. \u00a0\u00a0 \nwe Supposed them to be Grossvantares who had been up the River to Some other nation after corn. \u00a0\u00a0 we camped on handsome bottom covered with Strait tall cottonwood timber N. S. \u00a0\u00a0 came this day 21 Miles.\n\n\n

\n \n

[Gass]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Thursday 11th. \u00a0\u00a0 We got under way early, had a fine clear pleasant day, and went on very well. We saw some Indians on the South side, but did not speak with them. We came about twenty-one miles and encamped on the North side.\n\n\n

[Whitehouse]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\t\t\t\nThursday April 11th \u00a0\u00a0 We set off at day light, this morning, the weather being Cool, about 10 oClock A. M, We passed another party of Gross Vaunters or Big belly Indians a hunting also, on the South side of the River, we proceeded on, and Encamped in the Evening on the North side of the River having rowed 20 Miles this day.\u2014\n\n\t \n\n\n
