\n June 19th Tuesday\n

\nrain last night \u00a0\u00a0 after fixing the new Oars and makeing all necessary arrangements, we Set out under a jentle breese from the S. E. and proceeded on \u00a0\u00a0 passed two large Islands on the S. S. [2] leaving J. Shields and one man [3] to go by land with the horses \u00a0\u00a0 Some verry hard water, passed Several Islands & Sand bars to day \u00a0\u00a0 at the head of one we were obliged to cleare away Driftwood to pass, passed a Creek on the L. Side Called \u2329Tabboe\u232a [NB: Tabo] [4] 15 yds. wide \u00a0\u00a0 passed a large Creek at the head of an Island Called Tiger River\n [5] [NB: 25 yds] on the S. S. The Island below this \u2329river\u232a Isd. is large and Called the Isle Of Pant[h]ers, formed on the S. S. by a narrow Channel, I observed on the Shore Goose & Rasp berries [6] in abundance \u00a0\u00a0 in passing Some hard water round a Point of rocks on the L. S. we were obliged to take out the roape & Draw up the Boat for \u00bd a mile, we Came too on the L. S. near a Lake of the Sircumfrance of Several miles Situated on the L. S. about two miles from the river [7] \u00a0\u00a0 this Lake is Said to abound in all kinds of fowls, great quanties of Deer frequent this Lake dureing Summer Season, and feed on the hows [haws] [8] &c. &c. they find on the edgers \u00a0\u00a0 the Lands on the North Side of the river is rich and Sufficiently high to afford Settlements, the Lds. on the South Side assends Gradually from the river \u00a0\u00a0 not So rich, but of a good quallity and appear well watered

[Ordway]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nTuesday June 19th 1804. \u00a0\u00a0 we Set out at 9 o.C. with a fair wind. \u00a0\u00a0 we\npassed a beautiful large prarie on the North Side, high Rich Bottom on South Side \u00a0\u00a0 we passed Tabbo Creek on South Side \u00a0\u00a0 we Saw pleanty of goos Berries & Ras Berries [9] on the banks, we passed a bad place of Rocks. \u00a0\u00a0 the water So Swift that we were obledged to hole the Boat by a Rope, we Camped [10] on South Side opposite a pond, which was near to where we camped \u00a0\u00a0 The Musquetoes [11] are verry troublesome. \u00a0\u00a0 we Got Musquetoes bears [12] from Capt Lewis to sleep in,\n\n

[Whitehouse]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\t\nTusday 19 \u00a0\u00a0 Got on Our way at the Roap walk Camp perarie \u00a0\u00a0 the day was Clear \u00a0\u00a0 a Sharp wind Arose \u00a0\u00a0 Saild. 12 Miles \u00a0\u00a0 Campd. at neer the River Taboe, it Running N. E. the Breadth of it at the Mouth is 50 Yds. at the Mouth

\n

\nTuesday June 19 \u00a0\u00a0 This morning we started early, from the Rope walk camp Priari, the weather being fine & clear, about 9 oClock A. M. a good Brees sprung up from the South East, We set sail, and in the evening encamped near the River Taboe, This River runs North East to its head the breadth of it at its Mouth is 50 Yards, The distance we rowed and Sailed this day being 12 Miles.\u2014\n\n

\n \n
\n \n \n
\n
1. \"Rushes\" refers to Equisetum sp., scouring rush, horsetail. This is probably the common horsetail, E. arvense L. Steyermark, 11. (back)
\n
2. In the neighborhood of what was later called Baltimore Bar. MRC map 12. (back)
\n
3. \n Whitehouse reveals that the other man was John Collins. (back)
\n
4. \n Tabo Creek, in Lafayette County, Missouri, perhaps named for Pierre-Antoine Tabeau, a fur trader whom the captains would meet among the Arikaras on October 10, 1804. It is Nicollet's \"Tabeau River.\" McDermott (WCS), 148; Nicollet (MMR), 364; MRC map 12. (back)
\n
5. The Tiger River and Island of Panthers are named for the cougar, or mountain lion, Felis concolor. The creek may be Crooked River in Ray County, Missouri. However, it appears that they did not actually pass it until the next day; see below, June 20, 1804. Hall, 2:1039\u201343; MRC map 12. (back)
\n
6. Gooseberries are a species of Ribes, here possibly Missouri gooseberries. (back)
\n
7. In Lafayette County, a few miles east of present Lexington. The lake appears as Nicollet's \"Marais de [Apakwa?],\" a few miles east of present Lexington. The point passed just before they camped may have been what was later called Sheep Nose. Nicollet (MMR), 364; MRC map 12. (back)
\n
8. Probably one or more of the many species of Crataegus, hawthorn, red haw, known to occur in the area. These are C. crus-galli L., cockspur thorn; C. mollis (T. & G.) Scheele, summer haw, turkey apple; and C. calpodendron (Ehrh.) Medic., urn-trees. Less probably Viburnum prunifolium L., black haw, or V. rufidulum Raf., southern black haw. Steyermark, 810\u201322, 1414\u201315. (back)
\n
9. Perhaps Missouri gooseberry, Ribes missouriense Nutt., and black raspberry, Rubus occidentalis L. (back)
\n
10. A few miles below Lexington, Lafayette County. (back)
\n
11. Probably Aedes vexans. (back)
\n
12. A bier or mosquito netting. (back)
\n
13. Not mentioned by any other journal keeper; perhaps a very minor nameless watercourse in Carroll County, Missouri, nearly opposite Tabo Creek. The lake was a few miles east of Lexington, Lafayette County. (back)
