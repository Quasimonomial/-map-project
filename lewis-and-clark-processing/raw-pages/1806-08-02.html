\n August 2, 1806\n

\n
\n \n

[Lewis]

\n \n
\n Saturday August 2cd 1806.\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nThe morning proved fair and I determined to remain all day and dry the baggage and give the men an opportunity to dry and air their skins and furr. \u00a0\u00a0 had the powder parched meal and every article which wanted drying exposed to the sun. \u00a0\u00a0 the day proved warm fair and favourable for our purpose. I permitted the Fieldses to go on a few miles to hunt. by evening we had dryed our baggage and repacked it in readiness to load and set out early in the morning. \u00a0\u00a0 the river fell 18 inches since yesterday evening. \u00a0\u00a0 the hunters killed several deer in the course of the day. \u00a0\u00a0 nothing remarkable took place today. \u00a0\u00a0 we are all extreemly anxious to reach the entrance of the Yellowstone river where we expect to join Capt. Clark and party.\n

[Clark]

\n \n
\n Monday August 2nd 1806.\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nMusquetors very troublesom this morning \u00a0\u00a0 I Set out early \u00a0\u00a0 river wide and very much divided by islands and Sand and Mud bars. \u00a0\u00a0 the bottoms more extencive and contain more timber Such as Cotton wood ash willow &c. The Country on the N W. Side rises to a low plain and extends leavel for great extent. Some high rugid hills in the forepart of this day on the S E. Side on which I saw the big horns but could not get near them. Saw emence numbers of Elk Buffalow and wolves to day. \u00a0\u00a0 the wolves do catch the elk. I saw 2 wolves in pursute of doe Elk which I beleive they Cought they very near her when She entered a Small wood in which I expect they cought her as She did not pass out of the small wood during my remaining in view of it which was 15 or 20 minits &c. \u00a0\u00a0 passed the enterance of Several brooks on each Side, [2] a Small river 30 yds wide with Steep banks on the Stard. Side, which I call \n Ibex River\n [3] \u00a0\u00a0 the river in this days decent is less rapid crouded with Islds and muddy bars and is generally about one mile in wedth. \u00a0\u00a0 as the islands and bars frequently hide the enterance of Brooks &c. from me as I pass'd maney of them I have not noticed. \u00a0\u00a0 about 8 A. M this morning a Bear of the large vicious [4] Species being on a Sand bar raised himself up on his hind feet and looked at us as we passed down near the middle of the river. \u00a0\u00a0 he plunged into the water and Swam towards us, either from a disposition to attack't or from the Cent of the meat which was in the Canoes. \u00a0\u00a0 we Shot him with three balls and he returned to Shore badly wounded. \u00a0\u00a0 in the evening I saw a very large Bear take the water above us. I ordered the boat to land on the opposit Side with a view to attack't him when he Came within Shot of the Shore. \u00a0\u00a0 \u2329I let swim\u232a when the bear was in a fiew paces of the Shore I Shot it in the head. \u00a0\u00a0 the men hauled her on Shore and proved to be an old Shee which was so old that her tuskes had worn Smooth, and Much the largest feemale bear I ever Saw. \u00a0\u00a0 after taking off her Skin, I proceeded on and encampd [5] a little above the enterance of Jo: Feilds Creek on Stard. Side in a high bottom Covered with low Ash and elm. \u00a0\u00a0 the Musquetors excessively troublesom.

\n

\nI have noticed a great preportion Buck Elks on this lower part of the river, and but very few above. \u00a0\u00a0 those above which are emencely noumerous are females Generally. Shields killed a Deer this morning dureing the time we were at Brackfast. \u00a0\u00a0 we were very near being detained by the Buffalow today which were Crossing the river we got through the line between 2 gangues.

\nSaturday 2nd August 1806. \u00a0\u00a0 a fair morning. \u00a0\u00a0 the two Fields Sent on a head to hunt. \u00a0\u00a0 we delayed to dry our baggage. \u00a0\u00a0 the day warm. Some of the men dressed deer Skins, &C.\n

\n \n
\n
\n \n

[Gass]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Saturday 2nd. \u00a0\u00a0 This was a fine clear morning, and Captain Lewis thought it best to stay here to day also, and dry all our baggage, as it was become damp and wet. Two hunters [10] were sent on in a canoe to hunt; and in the course of the day we got every thing dry and ready to set out the next morning.\n\n\n

\n
3. \"Ibex\" may have been written in later; the name refers to the bighorn sheep. On Atlas map 112 it seems likely that the name \"Ibex river\" was also written in later, the earlier name being \"Jo. F. Creek \u00a0\u00a0 River.\" In the draft courses \"Jo Feilds\" is crossed out. Clark must at first have thought the stream to be the Joseph Fields Creek of Atlas maps 48 and 56 (see April 26, 1805), present Charbonneau Creek in McKenzie County, North Dakota, indicating that he thought himself much nearer the mouth of the Yellowstone than was the case. Ibex River is apparently Smith Creek in Richland County, Montana, reaching the Yellowstone near present Savage on the opposite side. Atlas map 122. (back)
\n
4. Apparently the \"v\" was written over an \"f,\" \"ferocioius\" being the word Clark perhaps first intended. There is no doubt that the animal was a grizzly. (back)
\n
5. \n Clark camped just above the mouth of Charbonneau Creek in McKenzie County; the site is shown on Atlas map 56. (back)

6. There is some red underlining of words in this table; it is not repeated here. (back)

7. \n Burns Creek in Richland County, meeting the Yellowstone from the west about a mile north of the Wibaux County line. This is the \"Samuels Creek\" of Atlas maps 112 and 122. \"Samuel\" may have been the same person for whom Point Samuel in Oregon was named on November 26, 1805. Coues (HLC), 2:721 n. 3, speculates, without evidence, that he may have been Samuel Lewis, copyist for Clark's map of 1814 (Atlas map 126), whom he further theorizes to have been a relative of Meriwether Lewis. (back)
