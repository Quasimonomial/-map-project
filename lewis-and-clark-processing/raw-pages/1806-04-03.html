\n April 3, 1806\n

\n
\n \n

[Lewis]

\n \n
\n Thursday April 3rd 1806.\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nEarly this morning Joseph Fields came over and informed me that Reubin Feilds\n Drewyer and himself had killed four Elk. \u00a0\u00a0 as the party with me were now but weak and the Indians constantly crouding about our camp, I thought it best to send a few men to dry the meat on the other side of the river; accordingly Sergt Pryor and two men returned with Jos. Fields for that purpose. \u00a0\u00a0 the hunters were ordered to continue the chase; while the others were employed in drying the meat. I have had no account as yet from the party below the entrance of Quicksand river. The Indians continued to visit us today in considerable numbers \u00a0\u00a0 most of them were decending the river with their families. \u00a0\u00a0 these poor people appeared to be almost starved, they picked up the bones and little peices of refuse meat which had been thrown away by the party. \u00a0\u00a0 they confirm the report of the scarcity of provision among the natives above. I observe some of the men among them who wear a girdle arround the waist between which and the body in front they confine a small skin of the mink or polecat [1] which in some measure conceals the parts of generation, they also frequently wear a cap formed of the skin of the deer's head with the ears left on it, they have some collars of leather wrought with porcupine [2] quills after the method of the Shoshonees. From this place Mount Hood bears S. 85\u00b0 E. distant 40 miles. This evening we completed drying the flesh of the Elk which had been brought to camp. \u00a0\u00a0 at 6 P. M. Capt. Clark returned, having completely succeeded in his expedition. \u00a0\u00a0 he found the entrance of the large river of which the Indians had informed us, just at the upper part of wappetoe Island. \u00a0\u00a0 the following is a sketch of the rivers furnished Capt C. by an old and inteligent Indian man.\u2014 [3]\n

[Clark]

\n \n
\n Thursday April 3rd 1806\n [4]\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nThe water had fallen in the course of last night five inches. I Set out and proceeded up a Short distance [5] and attempted a Second time to fathom the river with my cord of 5 fathom but could find no bottom. \u00a0\u00a0 the mist was So thick that I could See but a Short distance up this river. \u00a0\u00a0 where I left it, it was binding to the East of S. E. \u00a0\u00a0 being perfectly Satisfyed of the Size and magnitude of this great river which must Water that vast tract of Country betwen the Western range of mountains and those on the Sea coast and as far S. as the Waters of Callifornia about Latd. 37\u00b0 North I deturmined to return. \u00a0\u00a0 at 7 oClock A. M. Set out on my return. \u00a0\u00a0 the men exirted themselves and we arived at the Ne er cho ki oo house in which the nativs were So illy disposed yesterday at 11 A. M. I entered the house with a view to Smoke with those people who Consisted of about 8 families, finding my presence alarmed them So much that the children hid themselves, womin got behind their men, and the men hung their heads, I detained but a fiew minits and returnd on board the canoe. My pilot who Continued in the Canoe informed me on my return that those people as well as their relations were very illy disposed and bad people. I proceeded on along the South Side met five canoes of the \n Shah-ha-la\n Nation from the Great rapids with their wives and Children decending the Columbia into this fertile Vally in pursute of provisions. \u00a0\u00a0 my Pilot informed me in a low voice that those people were not good, and I did not Suffer them to come along Side of my Canoe which they appeared anxious to do. \u00a0\u00a0 their numbers in those canoes who appeard anxious to come along Side was 21 men and 3 boys. \u00a0\u00a0 at 3 P M. we arived at the residence of our Pilot [6] which consists of one long house with Seven appartments or rooms in Square form about 30 feet each room opening into a passage which is quit through the house \u00a0\u00a0 those passages are about 4 feet in width and formed of Wide boads Set on end in the ground and reaching to the Ruff which Serves also as divisions to the rooms. The ground plot is in this form [7] \u00a0\u00a0 1 1 1 1 is the passages. \u00a0\u00a0 2 2 &c. is the apartments about 30 feet square. \u00a0\u00a0 this house is built of bark of the White Cedar Supported on long Stiff poles resting on the ends of broad boads which form the rooms &c. \u00a0\u00a0 back of this house I observe the wreck of 5 houses remaining of a very large Village, the houses of which had been built in the form of those we first Saw at the long narrows of the \n E-lute Nation with whome those people are connected. [8] I indeavored to obtain from \u2329them\u232a those people of the Situation of their nation, if scattered or what had become of the nativs who must have peopled this great town. an old man who appeared of Some note among them and father to my guide brought foward a woman who was badly marked with the Small Pox and made Signs that they all died with the disorder which marked her face, [9] and which She was verry near dieing with when a Girl. \u00a0\u00a0 from the age of this woman this Distructive disorder I judge must have been about 28 or 30 years past, and about the time the Clatsops inform us that this disorder raged in their towns and distroyed their nation. Those people Speak a different language from those below tho' in their dress habits and manners &c. they differ but little from the Quathlahpohtles. \u00a0\u00a0 theire women ware the truss as those do of all the nations risideing from the quathlahpohtle to the enterance of Lewis's river and on the Columbia above for Some distance. \u00a0\u00a0 those people have Some words the Same with those below but the air of their language is entirely different, their men are Stouter and much better made, and their womin ware larger & longer robes than those do below; those are most commonly made of Deer Skins dressed with the hair of them. \u00a0\u00a0 they pay great attention to their aged Severall men and women whom I observed in this village had arived at a great age, and appeared to be helthy tho' blind. I provailed on an old man to draw me a Sketch of the Multnomar River ang give me the names of the nations resideing on it which he readily done, See draft on the other Side [10] \u00a0\u00a0 and gave me the names of 4 nations who reside on this river two of them very noumerous. The first is Clark a-mus nation reside on a Small river which takes its rise in Mount Jefferson and falls into the Moltnomar about 40 miles up. \u00a0\u00a0 this nation is noumerous and inhabit 11 Towns. \u00a0\u00a0 the 2d is the \n Cush-hooks\n who reside on the N E. Side below the falls, the 3rd is the \n Char-cowah\n \n [11] who reside above the Falls on the S W. Side \u00a0\u00a0 neether of those two are noumerous. The fourth Nation is the \n Cal-lar-po-e-wah\n \n [12] which is very noumerous & inhabit the Country on each Side of the Multnomar from its falls as far up as the knowledge of those people extend. they inform me also that a high mountain passes the Multnomar at the falls, [13] and above the Country is an open plain of great extent.

\nI purchased 5 dogs of those people for the use of their Oil in the Plains, and at 4 P M left the Village and proceeded on to Camp where I joind Capt. Lewis\n [14]\n

\n

\nThe enterance of Multnomah river is 142 miles up the Columbia river from its enterance into the Pacific Ocean\u2014. \u00a0\u00a0 in my absence and Soon after I left camp Several Canoes of men women and Children came to the camp. \u00a0\u00a0 and at one time there was about 37 of those people in Camp Capt Lewis fired his Air gun which astonished them in Such a manner that they were orderly and kept at a proper distance dureing the time they Continued with him\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 as maney as 10 Canoes arrived at Camp in the Course of this day. \u00a0\u00a0 they all Seem to give the Same account of the Scercity of Provisions above. \u00a0\u00a0 one family Continued all night and behaved themselves in a very orderly manner.

\n

\non the 3rd Joseph Field returned from the woods and informed the Drewyer\n Rubin & himself had killed four Elk. Capt L. Sent Sergt. Pryor and two men with Joseph Field to dry the flesh of the Elk in the woods on Scaffolds with fire. \u00a0\u00a0 the party bilow quick Sand river did not return to day. The Indians continue to vist our Camp in Considerable number from above with their families. \u00a0\u00a0 these pore people appeared half Starved. \u00a0\u00a0 they picked up the bones and little refuse meat which had been thrown away by the party. Capt L had the flesh of the 4 Elk which was killed on the 1st inst. dried\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 Some of the men of the nativs who visited Capt Lewis wore a girdle, with a Small Skin in front and a Cap of the Skin of the deers head &c.\n

[Ordway]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nThursday 3rd of April 1806. \u00a0\u00a0 a foggey morning. \u00a0\u00a0 one hunter [16] came across the River & informed us that the hunters had killed 4 Elk 2 of which\nis about 3 miles from the River so Sergt. pryor and 3 more men were Sent over to help jourk the meat \u00a0\u00a0 Slight Showers of rain in the course of the day. \u00a0\u00a0 the Savages who Stayed with us last night were of five different nations and had Several prisoners among them \u00a0\u00a0 these Savages tells us that they are going down the River after wapatoes &C &C. \u00a0\u00a0 in the evening Capt. Clark & party returnd. to Camp and informed us that they had been and took a view of the River which the Indians informed us of, and that it is a verry large River & is 500 yd. wide and is Supposed to head with the waters of the California. \u00a0\u00a0 they went 7 miles up Sd. River. \u00a0\u00a0 their guide informed them that a large nation lived up the fork of this River by the name of Clack-a-mus Nation\n [17] of 30 Towns, and that another Nation lives along distance up this River where it gits Small by the name of Callah-no-wah Nation [18] who are verry numerous. \u00a0\u00a0 on their return they halted at a new discovred village behind Swan Island\n [19] where they bought 5 dogs, a fiew Commass roots and some wa-pa-toes &C\n\n\n

\n \n
\n
\n \n

[Gass]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Thursday 3rd. \u00a0\u00a0 We went out and killed some deer; and then to bring in the meat of the bear and dry that of the elk; but it rained so hard we could not dry the meat; and therefore brought in the carcase of the bear. On our way, we saw 3 small cubs in a den, but the old bear was not with them. In the evening we returned to our camp, and remained there all night.\n\n

1. The mink is Mustela vison; the polecat or skunk is Mephitis mephitis.\n (back)
\n
2. Porcupine, Erethizon dorsatum.\n (back)
\n
3. See fig. 4, from Lewis's Codex K, pp. 28\u201329. (back)
\n
4. This is the last daily entry in Clark's Voorhis No. 2. The remainder of the journal consists of weather diaries for February and March 1806, and Clark's table of \"Longitude to each Degree of Latitude from the Equator to the pole,\" written backwards to other writing in the notebook and printed here as the next entry under this date. See Appendix C. (back)
\n
5. Estimated by Jones (WI), 32, 34, to be somewhat above the present St. John's Bridge, Portland, Multnomah County, Oregon, on the Willamette River. Atlas map 79. (back)
\n
6. The single-house \"Nech-e-co kee\" village on Atlas map 79, in northeast Portland; see April 2, 1806. (back)
\n
7. A sketch of the house in Clark's Voorhis No. 2 (fig. 5). (back)
\n
8. \n Clark must mean his Echelute Indians, who were Wishram Chinookans , the term being a self designation i-\u010d-xl\u00fait, \"I am a Wishram[-Wasco] Indian.\" See October 24, 1805. On that day Clark noted that these Indians constructed \"the first wooden houses in which Indians have lived Since we left those in the vicinity of the Illinois.\" (back)
\n
9. The woman was probably a survivor of the earlier of two smallpox epidemics known to have occurred among Indians of the lower Columbian Valley before the arrival of Lewis and Clark. The first, during the 1770s, was probably especially devastating, as it presumably took hold upon populations previously unaffected by this disease. Smallpox was then reintroduced in 1800\u20131801, as indicated by the captains on February 7, 1806. For the effects of the disease on Northwest Coast peoples, see Boyd. (back)
\n
10. See fig. 6. (back)
\n
11. Probably a part of the Upper Chinookan-speaking Clowewallas, living on the Willamette River above the falls near present Oregon City, Clackamas County. Berreman, 17; Hajda (SOLC), 117; figs. 4, 6. The term \"Char-cowah\" may be derived from \n \u010dagwaix, \"summertime,\" and has been misunderstood by Lewis and Clark as an ethnonym. (back)
\n
12. The Kalapuyas, or Calapooyas, were part of the Kalapuyan-language family. The territory of the Kalapuyan speakers extended south from Willamette Falls at Oregon City to the Umpqua River Basin and from the Coast Range east to the foothills of the Cascade Range. Berreman, 20\u201324; Hajda (SOLC), 118; Hodge, 1:187\u201388; figs. 4, 6. The term is probably from Clackamas\n k\u00e1lapuyuway.\n (back)
\n
13. At the Willamette Falls, Oregon City. (back)
\n
14. The camp of the main party near Washougal, Clark County, Washington, above the Washougal River. Atlas map 79. Between this paragraph and the next appears a map of the region in Clark's Voorhis No. 2 (fig. 6). The word \"joind\" appears to have been written over \"found.\" (back)
\n
15. This table appears as the final page in Voorhis No. 2, written backwards to other writing in the notebook (see n. 4). It is placed here with the last dated entry in the journal. To the side Clark has also written, \"\n Co-\u00e2 litch ke\n is the 1st river to the right\" and \"69\u00bd Miles a Degree of Latitude.\" The Co-\u00e2litchke River may be the Cowlitz, in Cowlitz County, Washington. \"Cow e lik kee\" and the like is the usual version of the name. (back)
\n
16. \n Drouillard, says Lewis. (back)
\n
17. The Clackamas were a Chinookan-language people living on the Clackamas River in Clackamas County, Oregon. (back)
\n
18. \n Ordway may mean the Indians Clark gave as \"Cal-lar-po-e-wah.\" They are the Kalapuyas, or Calapooyas, of the Kalapuyan-language family living in the vicinity of the Willamette\nFalls at Oregon City, Clackamas County, and beyond. (back)
\n
19. Apparently \"Nech-e-co kee\" village behind Government and McGuire islands, east of Portland, Multnomah County, Oregon. Lewis and Clark called the islands Diamond Island,\nbut neither used the term \"Swan Island.\" Whitehouse, who was with Clark, used that designation in his journal, so it may have been from him that Ordway obtained his information. (back)
