\n April 13, 1805\n

\n
\n \n

[Lewis]

\n \n
\n Saturday April 13th\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nBeing disappointed in my observations of yesterday for Longitude, I was unwilling to remain at the entrance of the river another day for that purpose, and therefore determined to set out early this morning; which we did accordingly; the wind was in our favour after 9 A. M. and continued favourable untill three 3 P. M. \u00a0\u00a0 we therefore hoisted both the sails in the White Perogue, consisting of a small squar sail, and spritsail, which carried her at a pretty good gate, untill about 2 in the afternoon when a suddon squall of wind struck us and turned the perogue so much on the side as to allarm Sharbono who was steering at the time, in this state of alarm he threw the perogue with her side to the wind, when the spritsail gibing was as near overseting the perogue as it was possible to have missed. \u00a0\u00a0 the wind however abating for an instant I ordered Drewyer to the helm and the sails to be taken in, which was instant executed and the perogue being steered before the wind was agin plased in a state of security. \u00a0\u00a0 this accedent was very near costing us dearly. \u00a0\u00a0 beleiving this vessell to be the most steady and safe, we had embarked on board of it our instruments, Papers, medicine and the most valuable part of the merchandize which we had still in reserve as presents for the Indians. \u00a0\u00a0 we had also embarked on board ourselves, with three men who could not swim and the squaw with the young child, all of whom, had the perogue overset, would most probably have perished, as the waves were high, and the perogue upwards of 200 yards from the nearest shore; however we fortunately escaped and pursued our journey under the square sail, which shortly after the accident I directed to be again hoisted. \u00a0\u00a0 our party caught three beaver last evening; and the French hunters 7. \u00a0\u00a0 as there was much appearance of beaver just above the entrance of the little Missouri these hunters concluded to remain some days; we therefore left them without the expectation of seeing them again.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 just above the entrance of the Little Missouri the great Missouri is upwards of a mile in width, tho' immediately at the entrance of the former it is not more than 200 yards wide and so shallow that the canoes passed it with seting poles. \u00a0\u00a0 at the distance of nine miles passed the mouth of a creek on the Stard. side which we called onion creek\n [1] from the quantity of wild onions which grow in the plains on it's borders. Capt. Clark who was on shore informed me that this creek was 16 yards wide a mile & a half above it's entrance, discharges more water than creeks of it's size usually do in this open country, and that there was not a stick of timber of any discription to be seen on it's borders, or the level plain country through which it passes. \u00a0\u00a0 at the disetance of 10 miles further we passed the mouth of a large creek; [2] discharging itself in the center of a deep bend. \u00a0\u00a0 of this creek and the neighbouring country, Capt Clark who was on shore gave me the following discription \u00a0\u00a0 \"This creek I took to be a small river from it's size, and the quantity of water which it discharged. I ascended it 1\u00bd miles, and found it the discharge of a pond or small lake, which had the appearance of having formerly been the bed of the Missouri. \u00a0\u00a0 several small streems discharge themselves into this lake. \u00a0\u00a0 the country on both sides consists of beautifull level and elivated plains; asscending as they recede from the Missouri; there were a great number of Swan and gees in this lake and near it's borders I saw the remains of 43 temperary Indian lodges, which I presume were those of the Assinniboins who are now in the neighbourhood of the British establishments on the Assinniboin river\u2014\" \u00a0\u00a0 This lake and it's discharge we call \n goos Egg from the circumstance of Capt Clark shooting a goose while on her nest in the top of a lofty cotton wood tree, from which we afterwards took one egg. \u00a0\u00a0 the wild gees frequently build their nests in this manner, at least we have already found several in trees, nor have we as yet seen any on the ground, or sand bars where I had supposed from previous information that they most commonly \u2329lay\u232a deposited their eggs.\u2014 [3] \u00a0\u00a0 saw some Buffaloe and Elk at a distance today but killed none of them. \u00a0\u00a0 we found a number of carcases of the Buffaloe lying along shore, which had been drowned by falling through the ice in winter and lodged on shore by the high water when the river broke up about the first of this month. \u00a0\u00a0 we saw also many tracks of the white bear of enormous size, [4] along the river shore and about the carcases of the Buffaloe, on which I presume they feed. \u00a0\u00a0 we have not as yet seen one of these anamals, tho' their tracks are so abundant and recent. \u00a0\u00a0 the men as well as ourselves are anxious to meet with some of these bear. \u00a0\u00a0 the Indians give a very formidable account of the strengh and ferocity of this anamal, which they never dare to attack but in parties of six eight or ten persons; and are even then frequently defeated with the loss of one or more of their party. \u00a0\u00a0 the savages attack this anamal with their bows and arrows and the indifferent guns with which the traders furnish them, with these they shoot with such uncertainty and at so short a distance, [NB: unless shot thro' head or heart wound not mortal] that they frequently mis their aim & fall a sacrefice to the bear. \u00a0\u00a0 two Minetaries were killed during the last winter in an attack on a white bear. \u00a0\u00a0 this anamall is said more frequently to attack a man on meeting with him, than to flee from him. When the Indians are about to go in quest of the white bear, previous to their departure, they paint themselves and perform all those supersticious rights commonly observed when they are about to make war uppon a neighbouring nation. Oserved more bald eagles on this part of the Missouri than we have previously seen. [5] \u00a0\u00a0 saw the small hawk, frequently called the sparrow hawk, which is common to most parts of the U States. [6] \u00a0\u00a0 great quantities of gees are seen feeding in the praries. \u00a0\u00a0 saw a large flock of white brant or gees with black wings pass up the river; there were a number of gray brant with them; from their flight I presume they proceed much further still to the N. W.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 we have never been enabled yet to shoot one of these birds, and cannot therefore determine whether the gray brant found with the white are their brude of the last year or whether they are the same with the grey brant common to the Mississippi and lower part of the Missouri.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 we killed 2 Antelopes today which we found swiming from the S. to the N. side of the river; they were very poor.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 We encamped this evening on the Stard. shore in a beautifull plain. elivated about 30 feet above the river. [7]\n

[Clark]

\n \n
13th of April Satturday 1805
\u00a0
\n \n

\nSet out this morning at 6 oClock, the Missouri above the mouth of Little Missouri widens to nearly a mile containing a number of Sand bars \u00a0\u00a0 this width &c. of the River Continues Generally as high as the Rochejhone River.

\n

\nCought 3 beaver this morning, at 9 miles passd. the mouth of a Creek on the S. S. on the banks of which there is an imence quantity of wild onions or garlick, I was up this Creek \u00bd a m. and could not See one Stick of timber of any kind on its borders, this creek is 16 yds wide \u00bd a mile up it and discharges more water than is common for Creeks of its Size. \u00a0\u00a0 at about 10 miles higher we pass a Creek about 30 yards wide in a deep bend to the N W. This creek I took to be a Small river from its Size & the quantity of water which it discharged, I assended it 1\u00bd mes and found it the discharge of a pond or Small Lake which has appearance of haveing been once the bead of the river, Some Small Streams discharge themselves into this Lake. \u00a0\u00a0 the Countery on both Side is butifull elevated plains assending in Some parts to a great distance \u00a0\u00a0 near the aforesaid Lake (which we call Goose egg L from a Circumstance of my Shooting a goose on her neast on Some Sticks in the top of a high Cotton wood tree in which there was one egg) We Saw 8 buffalow at a distance, We also Saw Several herds of Elk at a distance which were verry wild, I Saw near the Lake the remains of 43 lodges, which has latterly been abandond \u00a0\u00a0 I Suppose them to have been Ossinniboins and now near the british establishments on the Ossinniboin River tradeing. \u00a0\u00a0 we camped on the S. S. in a butifull Plain. I observe more bald Eagles on this part of the Missouri than usial also a Small Hawk \u00a0\u00a0 Killed 2 Antelopes in the river to day

[Ordway]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Saturday 13th April 1805. \u00a0\u00a0 clear pleasant & warm \u00a0\u00a0 Som of our men caught 2 beaver and one fish last night \u00a0\u00a0 at Sun rise we Set off and proceeded on \u00a0\u00a0 over took the frenchmen who came on yesterday trapping. \u00a0\u00a0 they had caught Seven beaver last night. \u00a0\u00a0 a handsom timbred bottom on the South Side \u00a0\u00a0 passed a creek on the N. S. \u00a0\u00a0 proceeded on under a fine breeze of wind from the South. \u00a0\u00a0 in the afternoon we Saw three Goats under a Steep bank on N. S. \u00a0\u00a0 the attempted to git up the bank Several times &.c. \u00a0\u00a0 some of the men Shot at them \u00a0\u00a0 they then took in to the River & Swam across. Some of the men on S. S. Shot 2 of them. \u00a0\u00a0 proceeded on \u00a0\u00a0 passed a high Stoney bank on the S. S. Saw a gang of Elk on a plain near a bottom of\nwood on the N. S.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 Camped the N. S. at a handsome plain. \u00a0\u00a0 came 22\u00bd miles to day.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 Saw a Goose nest on a tree \u00a0\u00a0 one man clumb it \u00a0\u00a0 found only 1 Egg.\u2014\n\n\n

\n \n

[Gass]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Saturday 13th. \u00a0\u00a0 We had a pleasant day and a fair wind; but our small canoes could not bear the sail. Some of the party caught some beaver, and some Frenchmen who were out trapping [9] caught 7 of them. We passed a large creek on the South side, called Onion creek. [10] We came 23 miles and encamped on the North side, where we found a wild goose [11] nest on a tree about 60 feet high. \nOne of the men climbed the tree and found one egg in the nest.\n\n\n

\n
\n \n

[Whitehouse]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nSaturday April 13th \u00a0\u00a0 We set out at day light this morning, having a fair wind from the Eastward, we sailed the greater part of this day, The land, being all Priaries that we passed. \u00a0\u00a0 in the Evening we encamped on the North side of the Mesouri having come 24\u00bd Miles this day.\u2014\n\n\n

1. Former Lucky Mound Creek (a corruption of the French \n L'eau qui monte, \"water that rises\"), in McLean County, North Dakota, also known as Rising Water or Pride Creek; it is today's Deepwater Creek. Mattison (GR), 44; Atlas maps 33, 46, 55; MRC map 55. (back)
\n
2. \n Shell Creek, now inundated, in Mountrail County, North Dakota; the actual source is much higher than the captains thought. The lake, \"Goose Egg Lake\" on Atlas maps 33, 46, 55, is also under Garrison Reservoir. Mattison (GR), 45; MRC map 55. (back)
\n
3. Nesting in trees is contrary to the habits of the Canada goose in the East. Nineteenth-century ornithologists challenged the captains' statement, but Coues confirmed this behavior, which provided protection from such predators as wolves and badgers, from observations in the same region. See also May 3, 1805. Coues (HLC), 1:269\u201370 n. 27; Cutright (LCPN), 128. Someone drew a vertical line through this passage, apparently in red. (back)
\n
4. The bear is the grizzly, Ursus horribilis; a specimen was taken on April 29, 1805. (back)
\n
5. Bald eagles, Haliaeetus leucocephalus [AOU, 352]. (back)
\n
6. The sparrow hawk, otherwise the American kestrel, Falco sparverius [AOU, 360]. Holmgren, 30. (back)
\n
7. In Mountrail County, North Dakota, in what was later called Fort Maneury Bend, now under Garrison Reservoir. Mattison (GR), 45; Atlas maps 33, 46, 55; MRC map 55. (back)
\n
8. Also given on Atlas map 33, in both captains' hands. (back)
\n
9. They encountered these three French trappers on April 10. (back)
\n
10. \n Deepwater Creek, McLean County, North Dakota. (back)
\n
11. Canada goose, Branta canadensis. See Clark's entry for a discussion of the bird nesting in trees. (back)
