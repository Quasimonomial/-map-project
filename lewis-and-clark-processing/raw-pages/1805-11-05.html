\nRained all the after part of last night, rain continues this morning, I [s]lept but verry little last night for the noise Kept dureing the whole of the night by the Swans, Geese, white & Grey Brant Ducks &c. on a Small Sand Island close under the Lard. Side; they were emensely noumerous, and their noise horid\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 we Set out \u2329at about Sun rise\u232a early \u00a0\u00a0 here the river is not more than \u00be of a mile in width, passed a Small Prarie on the Stard. Side \u00a0\u00a0 passed 2 houses about \u00bd a mile from each other on the Lard. Side \u00a0\u00a0 a Canoe came from the upper house, with 3 men in its mearly to view us, passed an Isld. Covered with tall trees & green briers Seperated from the Stard. Shore by a narrow Chanel [5] at 9 [8?] miles \u00a0\u00a0 I observed on the Chanel which passes on the Stard Side of this Island a Short distance above its lower point is Situated a large village, the front of which occupies nearly \u00bc of a mile fronting the Chanel, and closely Connected, I counted 14 houses [NB: Quathlapotle nation] [6] in front \u00a0\u00a0 here the river widens to about 1\u00bd miles. Seven canoes of Indians came out from this large village to view and trade with us, they appeared orderly and well disposed, they accompanied us a fiew miles and returned back. \u00a0\u00a0 about 1\u00bd miles below this village on the Lard Side behind a rockey Sharp point, we passed a Chanel \u00bc of a mile wide, [7] which I take to be the one the Indian Canoe entered yesterday from the lower point of \n Immage Canoe Island [X: So named] a Some low clifts of rocks below this Chanel, a large Island Close under the Stard Side opposit, [8] and 2 Small Islands, below, here we met 2 canoes from below,\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 below those Islands a range of high hills form the Stard. Bank of the river, the Shore bold and rockey, Covered with a thick groth of Pine \u00a0\u00a0 an extensive low Island, [9] Seperated from the Lard side by a narrow Chanel, on this Island we Stoped to Dine I walked out found it open & covered with \u2329Small\u232a grass interspersed with Small ponds, in which was great numbr. of foul, the remains of an old village on the lower part of this Island, I saw Several deer \u00a0\u00a0 our hunters killed on this Island a Swan, 4 white 6 Grey brant & 2 Ducks all of them were divided, below the lower point of this Island, I saw Several deer our hunters killed on this Island a Swan, 4 white 6 Grey brant & 2 Ducks all of them were divided, below the lower point of this Island a range of high hills [10] which runs S. E. forms the Lard. bank of the river \u00a0\u00a0 the Shores bold and rockey & hills Covered with pine, The high hills leave the river on the Stard. Side a high bottom between the hill & river. We met 4 Canoes of Indians from below, in which there is 26 Indians, one of those Canoes is large, and ornimented with Images on the bow & Stern. That in the Bow the likeness of a Bear, and in Stern the picture of a man\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 we landed on the Lard. Side & camped a little below the mouth of a creek [11] on the Stard. Side a little below the mouth of which is an Old Village which is now abandaned\u2014; [12] here the river is about one and a half miles wide. and deep, The high Hills which run in a N W. & S E. derection form both banks of the river the Shore boald and rockey, the hills rise gradually & are Covered with a thick groth of pine &c. The valley which is from above the mouth of Quick Sand River to this place may be computed at 60 miles wide on a Derect line, & extends a great Distanc to the right & left rich thickly Covered with tall timber, with a fiew Small Praries bordering on the river and on the Islands; Some fiew Standing Ponds & Several Small Streams of running water on either Side of the river; This is certainly a fertill and a handsom valley, at this time Crouded with Indians. The day proved Cloudy with rain the greater part of it, we are all wet cold and disagreeable\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 I saw but little appearance of frost in this valley which we call \u2329Wap-pa-too\n Columbia\u232a [NB: Columbian Valley] [13] from the root or plants growing Spontaniously in this valley only In my walk of to Day I saw 17 Striped Snakes \u00a0\u00a0 I killed a grouse which was verry fat, and larger than Common. This is the first night which we have been entirely clear of Indians Since our arrival on the waters of the Columbia River. We made 32 miles to day by estimation\u2014\n\n

[Gass]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Tuesday 5th. \u00a0\u00a0 We embarked very early. Some rain fell last night about 2 o'clock, and the morning was cloudy. We passed several handsome islands, generally near the shore, on the one side or the other of the river. The county on both sides is somewhat higher than what we passed yesterday, and closely covered with spruce timber. The bottoms are large, covered with cotton wood, maple, and the like kinds of wood. We passed a great many Indian camps, their lodges made chiefly of poles and cedar bark. At noon we stopped about an hour at an island, and some of the men went out and killed nine brants and a swan. Three of the brants [15] were quite white except the points of their wings, which were black. We proceeded on in the afternoon, during which some rain and a little hail fell; went 31 miles and encamped on the north side. Here the tide rises and falls 4 feet.\n\n\n

Islands as usal [16] \u00a0\u00a0 abt. 10 miles [page torn] the largest village we have [page torn] the S. Side. \u00a0\u00a0 the cabbens [page torn] they had a great no[mber] [page torn] \u00a0\u00a0 Some of them got in their [canoes and] came out in the River to See us. \u00a0\u00a0 they wanted to trade us Elk Skins for muskets. \u00a0\u00a0 we proceeded on 20 miles Since we Started this morng. and halted about 1 oC. to dine on an Isl. [17] \u00a0\u00a0 Several men went out a Short time to hunt, and killed one Swan and Several brants. \u00a0\u00a0 we then proceeded on. \u00a0\u00a0 passed one or 2 more villages \u00a0\u00a0 had Several Small Showers of rain \u00a0\u00a0 the \nIsld. continues all the way covd. with cotton timber. \u00a0\u00a0 the bottoms cont. as usal. \u00a0\u00a0 we went 31 miles to day and Camped on the Lard. Side where the pine hills make close to the River \u00a0\u00a0 and Some clifts of rocks on L. Shore. \u00a0\u00a0 the River about a mile wide \u00a0\u00a0 this evening rainy.\u2014

\n

\nTuesday Novemr. 5th \u00a0\u00a0 It began to rain about one o'Clock last night, and continued till day light & This morning was Cloudy. \u00a0\u00a0 We set out early on our Voyage, & found the River run very strait, & grew wider. \u00a0\u00a0 We passed a number of large Islands for the distance of 10 Miles, and then went by the largest Indian Village that we had yet seen on this River; This village lay on the South side of the River. \u00a0\u00a0 The Cabbins in this Village all joined, and the Indians belonging to this Village, had a great number of Canoes. \u00a0\u00a0 Some of the Indians from this Village, came out with their Canoes in the River to us; & wanted to Trade us Elk skins, for Muskets, or Guns of any kind, but our \nOfficers refused, we having not more Rifles than what we wanted. \u00a0\u00a0 We proceeded on, and halted about 1 o'Clock to dine on an Island, where several of our Men went out & killed a swan & several Brants.\u2014

\n

\nWe continued on our Voyage at 2 o'Clock P. M, & passed 2 More Indian Villages, lying on the South side of the River. \u00a0\u00a0 The Islands still continued as we passed down the River all the way, we went this day; & we had frequently small showers of rain. \u00a0\u00a0 The Islands I have last mentioned, were covered with Cotton wood, & other timber. \u00a0\u00a0 The bottom land along the shore, continue the same as those I last mentioned. \u00a0\u00a0 We came about 31 Miles this day, & encamped on the North side of the River, where hills covered with pine Trees made close into the River, & some Clifts of Rocks. \u00a0\u00a0 This evening continued Rainey. \u00a0\u00a0 the River was about 1 Mile wide at this place.\n\n\n

6. The Cathlapotles were an Upper Chinookan-language group living on the Columbia and lower Lewis rivers in Clark County. Hodge, 1:217; Spier, 21; Swanton, 414\u201315; Atlas maps 80, 89. The name is Chinookan gal\\\u00e1pui\u019bx, \"(those of) Lewis River,\" from n\u00e1p\u0313ui\u019bx, \"Lewis River.\" Silverstein. The Cathlapotle village at the mouth of Lewis River was called Nahpooitle (Hodge 2:217); it is shown on Atlas map 80 on the Washington shore across from the lower of end of Sauvie (\"Wapp\u00e2to\") Island. There are two archaeological sites in this vicinity. The principal Cathlapotle village has not been formally excavated, but a large artifact collection from this area has been described. Minor (CAC). The other site, consisting of a series of seasonal camps located along Lake River upstream from the village, has been extensively tested. The occupation of these camps spans the last 2,000 years. Abramowitz; Minor & Toepel. Biddle wrote the interlineation in red ink. (back)

7. The channel behind Sauie (\"Wapp\u00e2to\") Island, in Washington and Columbia counties, Oregon. Atlas map 80. (back)
