\n August 19, 1806\n

\n
\n \n

[Clark]

\n \n
\n Tuesday 19th of August 1806\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nSome rain last night and this morning the wind rose and blew with great Violence untill 4 P. M and as our camp was on a Sand bar we were very much distressed with the blows of Sand. I directed the hunters to proceed on down the bottom and kill and butcher Some meat and if the wind Should lie that I should proceed on down to their Camp &c. Capt. Lewis'es wounds are heeling very fast, I am much in hope of his being able to walk in 8 or 10 days\u2014. \u00a0\u00a0 at 4 P. M the wind Seased to blow with that violence which it had done all day we Set out and proceeded on down. \u00a0\u00a0 the hunters which was Sent out this morning killed 4 Elk & 12 deer near the river we came too and brought in the most of the flesh and proceeded on to a Sand on the N E Side and Encamped. [1] \u00a0\u00a0 the wind rose and become very Strong from the S. E. and a great appearance of rain. Jessomme the Interpreter let me have a piece of a lodge and the Squars pitched or Stretched it over Some Sticks, under this piece of leather I Slept \u2329under\u232a dry, it is the only covering which I have had Suffecient to keep off the rain Since I left the Columbia. \u00a0\u00a0 it began to rain moderately Soon after night. The indians appear well Satisfyed with the party and mode of proceedure. \u00a0\u00a0 we decended only 10 miles to day \u00a0\u00a0 Saw Some Elk and buffalow on the Shore near where we Encamped. \u00a0\u00a0 the Elk beginning to run. the Buffalow are done running & the bulls are pore.\n

\n \n
\n
\n \n

[Ordway]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nTuesday 19th August 1806. \u00a0\u00a0 a Showery morning. Thunder and high wind So it detained us. \u00a0\u00a0 the hunters went out & killed 5 Elk 10 deer. \u00a0\u00a0 about 4 P. M. the wind fell a little and we procd. on \u00a0\u00a0 took on board the best of the meat which was below and Campd. at dark. \u00a0\u00a0 windy & cold.\u2014\n\n\n

\n \n
\n
\n \n

[Gass]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Tuesday 19th. \u00a0\u00a0 This was a cloudy windy morning; and the water so rough, that our small canoes could not safely ride the waves: so we remained here and several of the men went out to hunt. We do not go on so rapidly as we did higher up the river: but having lashed our small canoes together, we go on very safe and can make 50 or 60 miles a day Captain Lewis is getting much better and we are all in good spirits. At 3 o'clock [2] in the afternoon the wind ceased, and we proceeded on, and met with our hunters on the bank, who had killed six elk and eleven deer. We took the meat on board, proceeded on, and encamped on a sand-beach.\n\n\n

\n \n
\n \n \n
\n
1. As Clark notes, their camp was some ten miles below the previous night's camp, in Burleigh County, North Dakota, probably near the camp of October 19, 1804, on the opposite side. The site is probably now inundated by Oahe Reservoir. Atlas map 28; MRC map 49. (back)
\n
2. Four o'clock, Clark says. (back)
