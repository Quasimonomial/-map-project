\n July 23, 1805\n

\n
\n \n

[Lewis]

\n \n
\n Tuesday July 23rd 1805.\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nSet out early as usual; Capt. Clark left us with his little party of 4 men and continued his rout on the Stard. side of the river. \u00a0\u00a0 about 10 OCk. A M. we came up with Drewyer who had seperated from us yesterday evening and lay out all night not being able to find where we had encamped. \u00a0\u00a0 he had killed 5 deer which we took on board and continued our rout. \u00a0\u00a0 the river is still divided by a great number of islands, it channels sometimes seperating to the distance of 3 miles; the current very rapid with a number of riffles; the bed gravel and smooth stones; the banks low and of rich loam in the bottoms; some low bluffs of yellow and red clay with a hard red slate stone intermixed. [1] \u00a0\u00a0 the bottoms are wide and but scantily timbered; the underbrush very thick consisting of the narrow & broad leafed willow rose and Currant bushes principally. \u00a0\u00a0 high plains succeeds the river bottoms and extend back on either side to the base of the mountains which are from 8 to 12 miles assunder, high, rocky, some small pine and Cedar on them and ly parallel with the river. \u00a0\u00a0 passed a large creek on Lard. side 20 yds. wide which after meandering through a beautifull and extensive bottom for several miles nearly parallel with the river discharges itself opposite to a large cluster of islands which from their number I called the 10 islands and the creek Whitehous's Creek, [2] after Josph. Whitehouse one of the party. \u00a0\u00a0 saw a great abundance of the common thistles; [3] also a number of the wild onions of which we collected a further supply. \u00a0\u00a0 there is a species of garlic [4] also which grows on the high lands with a flat leaf now green and in bloe but is strong tough and disagreeable. \u00a0\u00a0 found some seed of the wild flax ripe which I preserved; this plant grows in great abundance in these bottoms. I halted rearther early for dinner today than usual in order to dry some articles which had gotten wet in several of the canoes. I ordered the canoes to hoist their small flags in order that should the indians see us they might discover that we were not Indians, nor their enemies. \u00a0\u00a0 we made great uce of our seting poles and cords the uce of both which the river and banks favored. \u00a0\u00a0 most of our small sockets were lost, and the stones were so smooth that the points of their poles sliped in such manner that it increased the labour of navigating the canoes very considerably, I recollected a parsel of giggs which I had brought on, and made the men each atatch one of these to the lower ends of their poles with strong wire, which answered the desired purpose. \u00a0\u00a0 we saw Antelopes Crain gees ducks beaver and Otter. \u00a0\u00a0 we took up four deer which Capt. Clark & party had killed and left near the river. \u00a0\u00a0 he pursued his rout untill late in the evening and encamped on the bank of the river 25 ms. above our encampment of the last evening; [5] he followed an old indian road which lyes along the river on the stard side Capt. saw a number of Antelopes, and one herd of Elk. \u00a0\u00a0 also much sign of the indians but all of ancient date. I saw the bull rush and Cattail flag today.\u2014 [6]\n

[Clark]

\n \n
\n July 23rd Tuesday 1805\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\na fair morning wind from the South. I Set out by land [9] at 6 miles overtook G Drewyer who had killed a Deer. \u00a0\u00a0 we killed in the Same bottom 4 deer & a antelope & left them on the river bank for the Canoes \u00a0\u00a0 proceeded on an Indian roade through a wider Vallie which the Missouri Passes about 25 miles & Camped on the bank of the river, High mountains on either Side of the Vallie Containing Scattering Pine & Cedar Some Small Cotton willow willow &c. on the Islands & bank of the river \u00a0\u00a0 I Saw no fresh Sign of Indians to day \u00a0\u00a0 Great number of antelopes Some Deer & a large Gangue of Elk\n

[Ordway]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n July 23rd Tuesday 1805. Capt. Clark and 3 men [10] Set out again in order to go on to the 3 forks of the River expecting to find the Snake nation at that place. \u00a0\u00a0 a little cloudy. \u00a0\u00a0 the Musquetoes verry troublesome. \u00a0\u00a0 we proceeded on as usal. \u00a0\u00a0 passed level Smooth plains on each side. \u00a0\u00a0 passd. a Small creek on L. Side. \u00a0\u00a0 passed a nomber of Islands. \u00a0\u00a0 considerable of good flax now fit to pull in the praries & bottoms. \u00a0\u00a0 the Thissels pleanty. [11] \u00a0\u00a0 the pine continues on the Sides of the hills. \u00a0\u00a0 our hunter who Stayed out last night joined us at noon \u00a0\u00a0 had killed Several deer. \u00a0\u00a0 we dryed Some articles which got wet in the canoes. \u00a0\u00a0 we hoisted up our flags expecting the natives would See them & know the meaning of them. \u00a0\u00a0 the current verry Swift. \u00a0\u00a0 we Came 24 miles this day & Camped on the Larbord Side. \u00a0\u00a0 we find pleanty of wild Inions \nor what Some calls leeks, or garlick. [12] \u00a0\u00a0 we gethered a quantity of them to eat\u2014\n\n\n

[Gass]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Tuesday 23rd. \u00a0\u00a0 A cloudy morning. We embarked early, and at the same time Captain Clarke and four men went on again to endeavour to meet with some of the natives. We had rapid water, and passed a great number of islands. Capt. Clarke and his men killed four deer and a cabre, and left the skins and meat on the shore, where we could easily find them. The course of the river all day was nearly from the south, through a valley of 10 or 12 miles wide. The mountains are not so high nor so rocky, as those we passed. Large \ntimber is not plenty, but there are a great quantity of small shrubs and willows. We passed a small river on the south side, and some banks of very white clay. We encamped on an island, having made 24 miles.\n\n\n

[Whitehouse]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Tuesday 23rd July 1805. \u00a0\u00a0 Capt. Clark and 3 men Set out in order to go on to the 3 forks, expecting to find the Snake nation, near that place. \u00a0\u00a0 Some cloudy. \u00a0\u00a0 the Musquetoes verry troublesome. \u00a0\u00a0 I cannot keep them out of my face at this time. \u00a0\u00a0 the current verry rapid. \u00a0\u00a0 we proceeded on. \u00a0\u00a0 the river Spreads wide, and full of Islands. \u00a0\u00a0 we passed the mouth of a Small River [13] which came in behind an Island on the South Side. \u00a0\u00a0 passed livel Smoth large plains, on each Side. \u00a0\u00a0 high [14] grass in places & fine Short grass in general. \u00a0\u00a0 considerable of good flax now going to Seed. \u00a0\u00a0 the thissels also pleanty & high now in blossom. \u00a0\u00a0 the timber continues on the Island and along the Shores. \u00a0\u00a0 the beaver pleanty. \u00a0\u00a0 the pine timber continues on the \nSides of the hills at Some distance from the River. \u00a0\u00a0 our hunter [15] who Stayed out last night came to us at noon where we delayed to dry the articles wh[ich] is wet in the canoes. \u00a0\u00a0 he had killed Several Deer, and Saved the most of the meat. \u00a0\u00a0 we hoisted up our flags and proceeded on \u00a0\u00a0 the current verry rapid. \u00a0\u00a0 Came 24 miles this day and Camped on the South Side. \u00a0\u00a0 the party in general much fatigued. \u00a0\u00a0 we find pleanty of wild Inions or garlick, in these bottoms & Islands &c. &c.\u2014

\n

\nTuesday July 23rd \u00a0\u00a0 This morning Cloudy, Captain Clark and three of our \nparty, set out in Order to go to the three forks of the Mesouri River, where they expect to find the Snake Nation of Indians residing, at or near that place.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 We set out early, and found the current run very rapid, and the River spreading wide, and full of Islands, we passed the mouth of a small River, which came in behind an Island lying on the South side of the River, & passed level smooth large plains, lying on each side of the River, having fine Grass on it & Flax now going to seed, Thistles high & in bloom, the Timber plenty growing along the Shores, and on the sides of the hills, some distance from the Shores.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 Our hunters that had staid out all last night \ncame to us at noon.\u2014

\n

\nWe delayed at the place that the hunters came to us, to dry the articles that was wet in the Canoes, the hunters had killed several deer & had saved most of the meat,\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 We hoisted our flags on board our Canoes & proceeded on at 2 oClock P. M. the current still running very Rapid, We came 24 Miles this day, and encamped on the South side of the River Mesouri, our Men being very much fataigued, We found here plenty of Wild Onions.\u2014\n\n\n
