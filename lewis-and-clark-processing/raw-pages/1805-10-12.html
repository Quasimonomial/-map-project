[Gass]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Saturday 12th. \u00a0\u00a0 We had a fine morning and proceeded on early. Two of the Flathead chiefs remained on board with us, and two of their men went with the stranger [7] in a small canoe, and acted as pilots or guides. We saw some ducks and a few geese, but did not kill any of them. There is no four-footed game of any kind near this part of the river, that we could discover; \nand we saw no birds of any kind, but a few hawks, eagles and crows. At noon we halted, cooked and eat some fish and then proceeded on. The country and river this day is much the same in appearance as what we passed yesterday. A little before sunset we came to a bad rapid, which we did not wish to pass at night, so we encamped above on the north side, having made 30 miles.

\n

\nSome of the Flathead nation [8] of Indians live all along the river this far down. There are not more than 4 lodges in a place or village, and these small \ncamps or villages are 8 or 10 miles apart: at each camp there are 5 or 6 small canoes. Their summer lodges are made of willows and flags, and their winter lodges of split pine, almost like rails, which they bring down on rafts to this part of the river where there is no timber.\n\n\n

[Whitehouse]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n\u2329Thursday\u232a Saturday 12th Oct. 1805. \u00a0\u00a0 a clear pleasant morning. \u00a0\u00a0 we Set out eairly and proceeded on as usal. \u00a0\u00a0 the country continues the Same as yesterday \u00a0\u00a0 Saw a nomber of old fishing Camps along the Shores. \u00a0\u00a0 the current Swift in Some places, but gentle in general. \u00a0\u00a0 about 12 oClock we halted \nto dine on the Lard. Shore. \u00a0\u00a0 could Scarsely find wood enofe to cook our victules. \u00a0\u00a0 Capt. Lewis took an Meridian observation. \u00a0\u00a0 we then proceeded on verry well \u00a0\u00a0 passed Several more fishing Camps. \u00a0\u00a0 the wind rose hard from the west \u00a0\u00a0 our general course west. \u00a0\u00a0 high clifts of rocks & high prarie on each Side. \u00a0\u00a0 this River is verry handsom and country pleasant but no timber at all. \u00a0\u00a0 we Came 35 miles this day and Camped on the Starbord Side at the head of a bad rockey rapid which we expect is difficult to pass. \u00a0\u00a0 the Indians canoe and our Small pilot canoe went over this evening. \u00a0\u00a0 we expect that we have got past the numerous flat head nation. \u00a0\u00a0 only the guides who are with us they tell us that in 2 days more we will come to another nation at a fork which comes in on the St. Side of the Columbian River.\u2014

\n

\nSaturday October 12th \u00a0\u00a0 We had a clear pleasant morning, & we set out early & proceeded on our Voyage down the River. \u00a0\u00a0 The Country has the same appearance as it had Yesterday. \u00a0\u00a0 we passed a number of old fishing \nCamps, lying along the Shores on both sides of the River, and found the current of the River run very swift in many places. \u00a0\u00a0 About 12 o'Clock A. M. we halted to dine on the South side of the River where we could scarcely find wood enough to Cook our provisions. \u00a0\u00a0 Captain Lewis took at this place a \nMeridian Observation and found this place to lay in Latitude 46\u00b0 29' 21 7/10S. North. \u00a0\u00a0 We continued our Voyage at 2 o'Clock P. M. & passed several more fishing Camps, lying on both sides of the River. \u00a0\u00a0 The wind rose & blew hard from the West. \u00a0\u00a0 We also passed high clifts of Rocks & high Priaries, both lying on each side of the River, & they had a handsome appearance. \u00a0\u00a0 The Country has a pleasant appearance this day, but no kind of timber is to be seen. \u00a0\u00a0 We came about 35 Miles this day, & encamped on the North side of the River, at the head of a bad Rockey rapid, where we expect to meet with difficulty in passing it.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 We got the Indians Canoe & our smallest Canoe over this rapid this evening. \u00a0\u00a0 We expect that we have passed the flatt head Nation, which were very numerous. \u00a0\u00a0 Our Guides who are Indians inform us, that in 2 days more sailing, that we shall come to another Nation of Indians, who reside near a fork of the River Columbia & that this fork lies on the South side of the said River.\u2014\n\n\n\n\n

\n
6. The dark rugged stone is Miocene-age basalt of the Columbia River Basalt Group. These rocks occupy much of the Columbia Plateau and were formed by the cooling of sheets of molten lava that extended for hundreds of miles and were up to several hundred feet thick. (back)

7. Presumably the Indian of \"another nation\" mentioned by Gass the previous day. Clark does not note his presence with the party. (back)
