\n8th of July Sunday \u00a0\u00a0 Set out early this morning, the Sick man [Frazer] much better, Serjt. Oddeway was waiting at a Creek [2] on the S. S. below an Island, passed (1) two Island on the S. S. and came to at the upper point, G Drewyer went out \u00a0\u00a0 R. Fields & Guterich [Goodrich], five men Sick to day with a violent Head ake &c. and Several with Boils, we appoint a Cook to each mess to take Charge of the Provisions. \u00a0\u00a0 in Serjt. Pryor's = \n Collens\n \u00a0\u00a0 in Sjt. Ordway's\n \n Werner\n \u00a0\u00a0 in Sergt. Floyd's\n \n Thompson, \u00a0\u00a0 The french men Killed a young Deer on the Bank, (2) passed up a narrow Channel of about 80 to 100 yds wide about 5 miles to the mouth of Nadawa River\n [3] which coms in to this channel from the N W. and is abt. 70 yards wide at its mouth [blank] feet Deep and has a jentle Current, Perogues can navagate this river near its head, which is between the Missourie & the Grand River, passed up the gut \u00be of a mile to the river at the head of the \u2329river\u232a Island & camped [4] \u00a0\u00a0 opposit the head of this Island is another nearest the \u2329Larboard Shore,\u232a Middle R \u00a0\u00a0 this Island Nadawa is the largest I have Seen, formed by a Channel washing into the Nadawa river.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 \"8 or 10000 acrs\"

\n July the 8th Sunday 1804
\u00a0
\n \n

\nSet out early \u00a0\u00a0 passed a Small Creek on the S. S. and two (1) Small Islands on the S. S. \u00a0\u00a0 five men Sick to day with a violent head ake &c. \u00a0\u00a0 we made Some arrangements as to provisions & Messes, came to for Dinner at the lower point of a very large Island Situated near the S. S. \u00a0\u00a0 after a delay of two hours we passed a narrow channel of 45 to 80 yds wide five miles to the mouth of (3) \n N\u00e1d\u0103w\u0103 River,\n \n [8] This river Coms in from the North and is navagable for Perogues Some distance. \u00a0\u00a0 it is about 70 yards wide a little above the mouth, at the mouth not So side, the mud of the Gut running out of the Missourie is thrown and Settles in the mouth half a mile higher up \u00a0\u00a0 this Channel or gut is the upper point of the Said Island, This Island is Called Nadawa, & is the largest I have Seen in the river, containing 7 or 8000 acres of Land Seldom over flowed \u00a0\u00a0 we Camped at the head of this Island on the S. S. \u00a0\u00a0 opposit the head or our Camp is a Small Island near the middle of the river, river Still falling. \u00a0\u00a0 our flank party did not join us this evening

Detachment Orders
\n Nadawa Island July 8th 1804.\u2014 [9]\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nIn order to insure a prudent and regular use of all provisions issued to the crew of the Batteaux in future, as also to provide for the equal distribution of the same among the individuals of the several messes, The Commanding Officers Do appoint the following persons to recieve, cook, and take charges of the provisions which may from time to time be issued to their respective messes, (viz) John B. Thompson to Sergt. Floyd's mess, William Warner to Sergt. Ordway's mess, and John Collins to Sergt. Pryor's Mess.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 These Superintendants of Provision, are held immediately responsible to the commanding Officers for a judicious consumption of the provision which they recieve; they are to cook the same for their several messes in due time, and in such manner as is most wholesome and best calculated to afford the greatest proportion of nutriment; in their mode of cooking they are to exercise their own judgment; they shall \u2329point\u232a allso point out what part, and what proportion of the mess provisions are to be consumed at each stated meal (i. e.) morning, noon and night; nor is any man at any time to take or consume any part of the mess provisions without the privity, knowledge and consent of the Superintendant. The superintendant is also held responsible for all the cooking eutensels of his mess. \u00a0\u00a0 in consideration of the duties imposed by this order on Thompson, Warner, and Collins, they will in future be exempt from guard duty, tho' they will still be held on the royster for that duty, and their regular tour\u2014shall be performed by some one of their rispective messes; they are exempted also from pitching the tents of the mess, collecting firewood, and forks poles &c. for cooking and drying such fresh meat as may be furnished them; those duties are to be also performed by the other members of the mess.\u2014

\n

\n \n M. Lewis\n
\n Wm. Clark\n \n

\n Sunday 8th. \u00a0\u00a0 We were under way this morning before day light. The river here is crooked and narrow. At one we came to a large island, with only a small stream on the north side which we went up. A large creek called Nadowa flows in from the north; and on this side we encamped.\n\n\n

\nSunday 8th \u00a0\u00a0 the wind rose before we started and blew fair with us \u00a0\u00a0 Saild. Chiefly for the space of Eight hours \u00a0\u00a0 we Came to Small River Calld little Nan doughe,\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 In Indian tounge, Inglish little woody River, [11] it lieing in latude 39D 39M 22S 7/100 \u00a0\u00a0 an lsland to the S.S \u00a0\u00a0 On Our W.S. a bear apeared but Could not be Shot Made his alopement \u00a0\u00a0 we Got to the River Nandouie \u00a0\u00a0 Roed. 15 Miles \u00a0\u00a0 Incamd. at the head of a large Island\u2014

\n

\nSunday July 8th \u00a0\u00a0 This morning we embark'd early with a fair wind, and sail'd for 8 hours, when we came to a small River called Little Nan doughe, in the Indian language, which is in english little wood River, it lying by observation taken by Captains Lewis & Clark in Latitude 39\u00b0 39' 22 7/100 North. \u00a0\u00a0 there is an Island lying on the South side of the River, & on the So. West side, a bear was seen & being pursued by one of our hands, it made its escape, we proceeded on, and arrived at the River Nandoucee, and encamped at the head of a large Island, the distance we came this day being 15 Miles.\u2014\n\n\n

1. This July 8 entry is written over the following address, indicating that the sheet was earlier used as an envelope, probably by Lewis: Captn Wm Clark \u00a0\u00a0 River Dubois \u00a0\u00a0 Pryor. (back)
\n
2. If Nicollet's identification in 1839 is correct, this is apparently Mace Creek, reaching the Missouri near Amazonia, Andrew County, Missouri; Nicollet labels it \"Ordway's Creek (L. & Cl.) or Nadoway [Set?] Creek.\" Ordway says that the captains named it after him, and Biddle also uses his name. No extant expedition map shows that name or the stream itself, which may have appeared on one of the missing route maps of the lower Missouri. Coues (HLC), 1:41 ; Nicollet (MMR), 375; MRC map 18. (back)
\n
3. The Nodaway River today forms the boundary between Holt and Andrew counties, Missouri, on the lower part of its course. Nicollet remarks on the changes in the group of islands above Ordway's Creek, and the mouth of the Nodaway may also have shifted subsequently, altering the shape and location of Nodaway Island and rendering the smaller islands indistinguishable from the bottomlands. The name is apparently an Algonquian term applied to enemy tribes, metaphorically \"snakes.\" Fenton, 320; Coues (HLC), 1:41 n. 88; Stewart (APN), 330; Nicollet (MMR), 375; MRC map 18. (back)
\n
4. Near the present mouth of the Nodaway and the town of Nodaway, in Andrew County. Nodaway Island was still on the map near the end of the century. MRC map 18. (back)
