\n July 5, 1804\n

\n
\n \n

[Clark] [1]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n July the 5th 1804 \u00a0\u00a0 Set out verry early this morning, Swam the horse across the river, proceeded on for two miles under the bank where the old Kansas town formerly stood (Say in 1724) \u00a0\u00a0 The Cause of those people moveing from this place I cannot learn, but naterally conclude that War has reduced their nation & compelled them to retire further into the Plains with a view of defending themselves & opposeing their enemey (more effectually[)] on hors back (I neglected to mention yesterday that the Lake on the S. S. was large \u00a0\u00a0 Say \u00be me. wide & 7 or 8 long \u00a0\u00a0 one creek & Several brooks running into it from the hills, it contains Great quantities of Sun fish & Gosling's from which we gave it the name,) passed Some verry bad Sand bars Situated parrelel to each other, (1) the Boat turned three times once on the [Plat?] of a Drift wood. She recved no \u2329dammage\u232a proceiviable damage, we came to for Dinner at a Beever house, Cap Lewis's Dog\n \n Seamon\n [Scannon] went in & drove them out. \u00a0\u00a0 the high Lands on the L. S. is open, a few trees Scattering \u00a0\u00a0 (2) passed a Small Creek on the L. S. in the ls[1st] bend to the left I call \u2329Roses Creek\u232a yellow oaker [ochre] creek from \u2329the number of rose about &\u232a a bank of that Mineral just above. [2] \u00a0\u00a0 we camped on the L. S. under a high bank [3] \u00a0\u00a0 Latd. 39\u00b0 25' 41\" North

\n July 5th Thursday 1804
\u00a0
\n \n

\nSet out verry early, proceeded on near the bank where the old village Stood for two miles, (Swam the hors found a few day ago) \u00a0\u00a0 passed Some bad Sand bars, The Origan of this old village is uncertain \u00a0\u00a0 M. de Bourgmont\n [4] a French officer who Comdd. a fort near the Town of the Missouris in about the year 1724 and in July of the Same year he visited this Village \u00a0\u00a0 at that time the nation was noumerous & well desposed towards the french \u00a0\u00a0 Mr. Du Pratz must have been badly informed as to the Cane opposd this place \u00a0\u00a0 we have not Seen one Stalk of reed or cane on the Missouries, he States that the \"Indians that accompanied M De Bourgmont Crossed to the Canzes Village on \u2329rafts\u232a floats of Cane\" [5]\n

\n

\nThose people must have been verry noumerous at that time as Mr. De B: was accompanied by 300 Warriers, 500 young people & 300 Dogs of burthen out of this Village

\n

\nThe Cause of Those Indians moveing over to the Kanzis river I have never lernt\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 we passed Some bad Sand bars, Situated parrelel to each other (1) \u00a0\u00a0 The Boat turned twice on the quick Sand & once on \u2329the\u232a a raft of Drift, no procievable damage \u00a0\u00a0 Prarie Contine on the high land on the L. S. \u00a0\u00a0 passsd a Small Creek (2) \u00a0\u00a0 on L. S. in the first bend to the L S. I call Yellow-Oaker Creek from a quantity of that Mineral in a bank a little above

[Ordway]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nThursday July 5th 1804 \u00a0\u00a0 we Set out verry eairly. \u00a0\u00a0 we Swam the white horse a cross this River, proceeded on for two miles under the bank where the old Kansas Town formerly Stood (Say in 1724) \u00a0\u00a0 the cause of those people moveing from this place we cannot learn, but naturly conclude that war has reduced their nation and compelled them to retire further into the plains with a view of Defending themselves. I did not mention on yesterday that the Lake on the north side was large say \u00be of a Mile wide & 7 or 8 miles long \u00a0\u00a0 one Creek & several Creeks running in to it from the hills \u00a0\u00a0 it contains a great quantity of fish and Goslings from which it takes its name, we passed Some verry bad Sand bars \u00a0\u00a0 the Boat turned three times once on a Drift wood, but recived no procevable Damage, we came too at a beaver house for Dinner. \u00a0\u00a0 the high land on the South Side is open \u00a0\u00a0 a fiew trees \u00a0\u00a0 Scattering, we passed a Small creek on the left named yallow Oakey Creek, [6] we Camped on the South Side under a high bank. \u00a0\u00a0 the land on the opposite Side is well timbered Good bottom, fine place for a Range \u00a0\u00a0 verry thick high Rushes for common,\n\n\n

[Floyd]

\n \n

\nThursday July 5th 1804 \u00a0\u00a0 Set out errley this morning \u00a0\u00a0 Swam ouer Stray Horse a Cross the River to Join our other Horses \u00a0\u00a0 prossed on for two miles under the Bank of the old Kansas village formaley Stood in 1724 \u00a0\u00a0 the couse of the Indians moving from this place I cant \u2329tell\u232a Larn but natreley Concluded that war has reduced thair nation and Compelled them to Retir further in to the Plaines with a view of Defending themselves and to operserve their enemey and to Defende them Selves on Horse Back \u00a0\u00a0 encampt on the South Side \n\n\n

[Gass]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Thur. 5th. \u00a0\u00a0 We proceeded on our voyage at five in the morning; and found the land high on the south side. We went through a large bend full of sand-bars where we had some difficulty in passing; and encamped on the south side at high prairie land.\n\n\n

[Whitehouse]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nThursdy 5 \u00a0\u00a0 Got on our way \u00a0\u00a0 Roed. a mile Up the prarie \u00a0\u00a0 Crossd the River with the white horse and left him with the others that the hunters had on the E. Shore \u00a0\u00a0 Roed. 10 Miles \u00a0\u00a0 Incampd at the Rock Prarie\u2014 [7]\n

\n

\nThursday July 5th \u00a0\u00a0 We started early this morning, and rowed one Mile on our way; and took in the white horse and crossed the River with him, and landed him with the other horses on the No. East shore where the hunters had left them, and Encamp'd in the evening at the Rock Priari, distance come this day being 10 Miles\n\t\n

1. \n Biddle's notation at the top of this sheet of the Field Notes (document 28) reads, \"July 5 to.\" (back)
\n
2. One of several small streams in Doniphan County, Kansas. Quaife identifies it as rush Creek. Nicollet shows a \"yellow ocher\" creek at about the right spot. Quaife (MLJO), 92 n. 3; Nicollet (MMR), 374; MRC map 17; MRM map 47. Yellow ocher generally refers to the mineral limonite, used in pigments and, where found on the frontier in large masses, sometimes as a source of iron by blacksmiths. (back)
\n
3. In Doniphan County, some miles northeast of Doniphan. There are a number of high banks in the area. MRC map 17. (back)
\n
4. \n Etienne V\u00e9niard, Sieur de Bourgmont, a Frenchman of \"an adventurous and undisciplined nature,\" was commandant of Detroit in 1705 and first visited the Missouri Indians in 1714. Having deserted, he lived for some time among the Missouris and Osages, marrying an Indian woman; eventually he received a pardon for desertion. He founded Fort Orleans, in Carroll County, Missouri, in 1723 (see above, June 16, 1804). At various times he explored up the Missouri River, possibly as far as the Arikara villages in South Dakota. After various ventures among the Plains tribes, he took a delegation of Indian chiefs to Paris in 1725 and apparently remained in France. Nasatir (BLC), 1:12\u201322; Houck, 1:31, 173, 223, 258\u201368; Barry, 13, 18\u201321, 27, 189. (back)
\n
5. \n Antoine Simon Le Page du Pratz was a French military engineer who came to Louisiana in 1718 and spent sixteen years there, chiefly on the lower Mississippi. Clark is referring to the 1774 English translation of his three-volume Histoire de la Louisiane . . . (Paris, 1758). The passage that puzzled Clark occurs in an account of Bourgmont's 1724 mission to the Indians on the Kansas River. The confusion arises from a faulty translation; the \"canes\" do not appear in the original, which states that the Indians crossed the river in cajeux (rafts) made of unstated materials. Lewis borrowed a copy of the work from Benjamin Smith Barton of Philadelphia in 1803 and returned it to the owner after the expedition. It is now in the custody of the Library Company, in Philadelphia. Nasatir (BLC), 1:13, 17, 21, 56; Jackson (SBLC), 9\u201310; Cutright (LCD); Allen, 91, 96, 156, 178. (back)
\n
6. Any of several small streams in Doniphan County, Kansas, at this point; see Clark's entry. Ordway seems to borrow heavily from Clark's Field Notes for this day's entry, and also for the\nnext. (back)
\n
7. Yet another place-name given only by Whitehouse. The place is in Doniphan County, Kansas, some miles northeast of Doniphan. (back)
