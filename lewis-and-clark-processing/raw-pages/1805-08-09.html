\n August 9, 1805\n

\n
\n \n

[Lewis]

\n \n
\n Friday August 9th 1805.\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nThe morning was fair and fine; we set out at an early hour and proceeded on very well. \u00a0\u00a0 some parts of the river more rapid than yesterday. I walked on shore across the land to a point which I presumed they would reach by 8 A. M. our usual tme of halting. \u00a0\u00a0 by this means I acquired leasure to accomplish some wrightings which I conceived from the nature of my instructions necessary lest any accedent should befall me on the long and reather hazardous rout I was now about to take. [1] \u00a0\u00a0 the party did not arrive and I returned about a mile and met them, here they halted and we breakefasted; I had killed two fine gees on my return. \u00a0\u00a0 while we halted here Shannon arrived, and informed us that having missed the party the day on which he set out he had returned the next morning to the place from whence he had set out or furst left them and not finding that he had supposed that they wer above him; that he then set out and marched one day up wisdom river, by which time he was convinced that they were not above him as the river could not be navigated; he then returned to the forks and had pursued us up this river. \u00a0\u00a0 he brought the skins of three deer which he had killed which he said were in good order. he had lived very plentifully this trip but looked a good deel worried [2] with his march. \u00a0\u00a0 he informed us that Wisdom river still kept it's course obliquely down the Jefferson's river as far as he was up it. \u00a0\u00a0 immediately after breakfast I slung my pack and set out accompanyed by Drewyer\n Shields and McNeal who had been previously directed to hold themselves in readiness for this service. I directed my course across the bottom to the Stard. plain led left the beaver's head about 2 miles to my left and interscepted the river about 8 miles from the point at which I had left it; I then waded it and continued my rout to the point where I could observe that entered the mountain, but not being able to reach that place, changed no direction to the river which I struck some miles below the mountain and encamped for the evening having traveled 16 M. [3] \u00a0\u00a0 we passed a handsom little stream formed by some large spring which rise in this wide bottom on the Lard. side of the river. \u00a0\u00a0 we killed two Antelopes on our way and brought with us as much meat as was necessary for our suppers and breakfast the next morning. \u00a0\u00a0 we found this bottom fertile and covered with taller grass than usual. \u00a0\u00a0 the river very crooked much divided by lands, shallow rocky in many plases and very rapid; insomuch that I have my doubts whether the canoes could get on or not, or if they do it must with great labour.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 Capt. Clark proceeded after I left him as usual found the current of the river increasing in rapidity towards evening his hunters killed 2 antelopes only. \u00a0\u00a0 in the evening it clouded up and experienced a slight rain attended with some thunder and lightning the musquetoes very troublesome this evening. \u00a0\u00a0 there are some soft bogs in these vallies covered with turf. \u00a0\u00a0 the earth of which this mud is composed is white or bluish white and appears to be argillacious. [4]\n

[Clark]

\n \n
\n August 9th Friday 1805\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\na fine morning wind from the N. E \u00a0\u00a0 we proceeded on verry well rapid places more noumerous than below, Shannon the man whome we lost on Wisdom River Joined us, haveing returned to the forks & prosued us up after prosueing Wisdom River one day

\n

\n Capt Lewis and 3 men Set out after brackft. to examine the river above, find a portage if possible, also the Snake Indians. I should have taken this trip had I have been able to march, from the rageing fury of a tumer on my anckle musle, in the evening Clouded up and a fiew drops of rain \u00a0\u00a0 Encamped on the Lard Side near a low bluff, [5] the river to day as yesterday. \u00a0\u00a0 the three hunters Could kill only two antelopes to day, game of every kind Scerce\n

[Ordway]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nFriday 9th August 1805. \u00a0\u00a0 a clear cool morning. Several hunters out eairly. \u00a0\u00a0 we Set out at Sun rise, and proceeded on. \u00a0\u00a0 the wind high from S. E. \u00a0\u00a0 took on board a deer which the hunters killed. \u00a0\u00a0 about 8 oClock we halted for breakfast. George Shannon who had been 3 days lost joined us with 3 buck Skins which he killed & found fat. \u00a0\u00a0 he brought in a little of the meat also. Capt. Lewis, Shields, Drewyer & Mcneal Set out to go on a head a long distance to make discoveries in hopes to find Indians &C. \u00a0\u00a0 we proceeded on \u00a0\u00a0 took on board another Deer which the hunters had killed. \u00a0\u00a0 the Game is generally Scarse along here. \u00a0\u00a0 only a fiew Deer to be Seen. The River & bottoms prarie continues as yesterday \u00a0\u00a0 \u2329back at the 3 forks our Captains named this fork Jeffersons River \u00a0\u00a0 the North we call Sensable River because we were Sensable of it. \u00a0\u00a0 the South fork named\u232a not known for certn. yet \u00a0\u00a0 So I expect the forks ought to be called the head of the Missourie, although we are yet on a branch which we expect to See the head of it in a fiew days. Some thunder \u00a0\u00a0 the Musquetoes troublesome. \u00a0\u00a0 the beaver as pleanty as usal, &C. Saw Snow on the Mountains Some distance a head. \u00a0\u00a0 \nproceeded \u00a0\u00a0 passed the head of the old Channel where the River formerly ran along the high land at the South Side of the prarie. Some timber along the old bed. Some part of the prarie is low and boggy and will Shake for Some distance around a man when he walks on it. \u00a0\u00a0 we expect it would be good turf to burn. Some Showers passed over. \u00a0\u00a0 we Came 18 mls. and Camped at a fiew trees on L. Side.\n\n\n

[Gass]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Friday 9th. \u00a0\u00a0 We set out at sunrise, and had a fine morning with some dew; proceeded on till 9 o'clock when we halted for breakfast. Here one of the hunters [6] came to us who had been out since the morning the canoes went up the north branch by mistake, and who had that morning proceeded them by land. Here also Captain Lewis and three men [7] started to go on ahead; and at 10 we proceeded on with the canoes. The river is narrow and very crooked, and the valley continues about the same breadth. There is some timber on the mountain on the south side, and white earth or rocks appearing through the pines. At noon we halted for dinner, and hauled out one of the canoes, which had sprung a leak and caulked her.

\n

\nThis morning our commanding officers thought proper that the Missouri should lose its name at the confluence of the three branches we had left on the 30th ultimo. [8] The north branch, which we went up, they called Jefferson; the west or middle branch, Madison; the south branch, about 2 miles up which a beautiful spring comes in, Gallatin! and small river above the forks they called \n Philosophy. Of the 3 branches we had just left, they called the north \n Wisdom, the south \n Philanthropy, and the west or middle fork, which we continued our voyage along, retained the name of Jefferson. We went 14 miles and encamped on the south side. Our two hunters killed but one goat.\n\n\n

[Whitehouse]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Friday 9th August 1805. \u00a0\u00a0 a clear cool morning. \u00a0\u00a0 Several hunters out on Shore \u00a0\u00a0 we Set out as usal and proceeded on. \u00a0\u00a0 the wind high from the S. E. \u00a0\u00a0 took on board a goat which one of the hunters had killed. \u00a0\u00a0 we halted abt. 8 oC. for breakfast. \u00a0\u00a0 George Shannon joined us who had been lost 3 days. \u00a0\u00a0 he had killed 3 buck Deer, which was fat. \u00a0\u00a0 he brought in the Skins & a little meat. \u00a0\u00a0 Capt. Lewis\n G. Drewyer\n H. McNeal & John Shields Set out to go on by land a long distance to look out the way for us to go & expect to find the Snake nation of Indians. \u00a0\u00a0 we proceeded on. \u00a0\u00a0 took on bord a deer which one of the hunters killed. \u00a0\u00a0 we Saw no game worth notice except a fiew deer. \u00a0\u00a0 \nthe River and Smooth prarie the Same as yesterday. \u00a0\u00a0 \u2329back at the forks our Captains named this Stream Jeffersons River, the N. fork Sensable River, [9] and the South fork\u232a, not known yet. \u00a0\u00a0 So I expect that ought to be called the head of the Missourie although we are yet on the head branch, which we expect to See the head of it Soon. \u00a0\u00a0 Some Thunder. \u00a0\u00a0 the Musquetoes troublesome. \u00a0\u00a0 the beaver pleanty as usal, &c. \u00a0\u00a0 Saw Snow on the Mountains Some distance a head. \u00a0\u00a0 proceeded on \u00a0\u00a0 passed the old bed of the River where it formerly ran along the high land at South Side of the prarie Some cotton trees along it. \u00a0\u00a0 the prarie low, Some part of which is soft & boggy which we expect is good turf to burn was dug & dryed. \u00a0\u00a0 Thunder Showers passed round or over. \u00a0\u00a0 Came 18 miles and Camped on L. S. near a grove of cotton trees & willows.

\n

\nFriday August 9th \u00a0\u00a0 We had a clear cool morning, several of our Hunters left the camp early to go out a hunting & We set out as usual, and proceeded on our Voyage, the wind blowing high from the South east, We stopped with \none of our Canoes & took on board a Goat, which one of the hunters, that went out this morning had killed, & left on the bank of the River; we proceeded on till about 8 o'Clock A. M. when we halted to take breakfast. \u00a0\u00a0 Here \nwe were joined by George Shannon, one of our party that had been lost, for \nthese 3 days past, he had killed 3 buck Deer, which he said was very fat, he brought with him, some of the Meat and the Skins of them. \u00a0\u00a0 Captain Lewis & 3 of our party \u2329here\u232a left us here & set out to go by land a long distance up the River, in order to look out the best way for us to proceed, & to find out the Snake nation of Indians.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 We then proceeded on a small distance, & took on board one of the Canoes, a Deer which one of the hunters had killed & left on the bank of the River also; We saw no Game, excepting a few deer, which were in the bend of the River. \u00a0\u00a0 We passed some smooth plains much the same as those we passed Yesterday. \u00a0\u00a0 We all expect that we are near the head Waters or source of the Mesouri River, as the River, here is growing much narrower than it was, We had some thunder in the afternoon, and the Musketoes was very toublesome. \u00a0\u00a0 The beaver was very plenty to be seen in the River, & along the shores. \u00a0\u00a0 We saw Snow on the mountains which lay ahead of us.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 We continued on our way, and passed a place, where we supposed the Bed of the River formerly was, and high land, lying on the South side of the Priaries lying back from the River, with some Cotton wood Trees growing on it, The priaries here lay low on both sides of the River, some part of which is soft & boggy, which we expect would make good turf \u00a0\u00a0 In the evening we had some Showers of rain accompanied with thunder, We encamped on the South side of the River, near a Grove of Cotton wood trees, & Willows, having come 18 Miles this day.\u2014\n\n\n\n

1. The nature of these \"wrightings\" is unclear; he may have been bringing his journals up to date or preparing written instructions to Clark in case he did not return. (back)
\n
2. \n Biddle has it \"wearied.\" Coues (HLC), 2:470. (back)
\n
3. \n Lewis's camp was in Beaverhead County, Montana, northeast of present Dillon, by his own estimate five miles below the mouth of Blacktail Deer (McNeal's) Creek. Atlas map 66. Two \"x's\" cross out lines at about this point. Not in red and not concerned with natural history the marks are probably not by Biddle, nor is their purpose clear. (back)
\n
4. Peat or turf bogs develop in swampy or waterlogged areas when vegetation growth is rapid enough to cover the previous year's dead vegetation before it can decompose completely. Tertiary clays are common throughout the Beaverhead Valley and are often exposed along the river banks. When these clays wash down onto the floodplain they can color the mud and silt of the bottoms a whitish color. Nevertheless, Lewis's comments on the color of the mud here are unclear. Their high organic content should make them appear black or dark brown. (back)
\n
5. In Madison County, Montana, a little downstream from the Beaverhead County line and the crossing of Montana Highway 41 over the Beaverhead River. Atlas map 66. (back)
\n
6. \n Shannon, who had been up the Big Hole (Wisdom) River; see Lewis's entry for this day. (back)
\n
7. \n Lewis took Drouillard, Shields, and McNeal. (back)
\n
8. \n Lewis gives the names of the Forks of the Missouri in his entry of July 28, 1805, and names the forks of the Jefferson on August 6. If Gass is correct, Lewis's entries could not have been written until August 9 or later. Both Ordway and Whitehouse wrote that the names were decided on \"back at the 3 forks,\" then both crossed out their material relating to the naming of the rivers. It may be that Gass only learned of their decisions about nomenclature on this date, or that McKeehan made some mistake in putting his version together. (back)
\n
9. Perhaps the party's initial name for their Wisdom River, today's Big Hole River. (back)
