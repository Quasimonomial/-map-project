[Clark]

\n \n
\n Tuesday 26th November 1805\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nCloudy and Some rain this morning from 6 oClock. \u00a0\u00a0 wind from the E. N. E, we Set out out early and crossed a Short distance above the rock out in the river, & between Some low marshey Islands to the South Side of the Columbia at a low bottom about 3 miles below Point Samuel\n [7] and proceeded near the South Side leaveing the Seal Islands to our right and a marshey bottom to the left 5 Miles to the \n Calt-har-mar\n [NB: Cathlahma\n ] Village of 9 large wood houses on a handsom elivated Situation near the foot of a Spur of the high land behind a large low Island Seperated from the Southerly Shore by a Chanel of about 200 yards Wide, This nation appear to differ verry little either in language, Customs dress or appearance from the \n Chin nooks\n & \n War-ci \u00e2 cum\n live principally on fish and pap-pato \u00a0\u00a0 they have also other roots, and Some Elk meat.

\n

\nWe purchased Some green fish, & wap pa to for which we gave Imoderate pricie's. \u00a0\u00a0 after dining on the fresh fish which we purchased, we proceeded on through a Deep bend to the South and encamped under a high hill, where we found much difficuelty in precureing wood to burn, as it was raining hard, as it had been the greater part of the day. \u00a0\u00a0 Soon after we encamped 3 Indians of the last town Came in a Canoe with wap-pato roots to Sell to us \u00a0\u00a0 Some of which we purchased with fish hooks\u2014 from the Village quite around this bend to the West the land is high and thickly timbered with pine balsom &c. \u00a0\u00a0 a Short distance below the \n Calt har mer\n Village on the Island which is Opposit I observed Several Canoes Scaffold in which Contained their dead, as I did not examine this mode of deposing the dead, must refer it to a discription hereafter.\n

[Ordway]

\n \n
\u00a0

\nTuesday 26th Nov. 1805. \u00a0\u00a0 a Cloudy wet morning. \u00a0\u00a0 we Set out eairly \u00a0\u00a0 went about one mile then crossed the River and went down along the South Shore \u00a0\u00a0 passed Several Islands, halted at a village of the Clotsop nation. [8] \u00a0\u00a0 they gave us pleanty to eat and appeared verry friendly. \u00a0\u00a0 we bought a fiew wapatoes roots, &C \u00a0\u00a0 the day rainy and cold. \u00a0\u00a0 we went on \u00a0\u00a0 passd. Several low marshey Islands which was covred with course grass, and willows \u00a0\u00a0 the Shore is high land covred thick with pine timber and under brush. \u00a0\u00a0 we Campd [9] in a thick part of wood\n\n\n

[Gass]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Tuesday 26th. \u00a0\u00a0 The morning of this day was cloudy and wet; but we set out early, west about a mile, and then crossed the river; passing in our way several islands. Immediately after we crossed, we came to a small village of the natives, [10] and procured a few roots, called Wapto, from them, and then proceeded on, coasting down the bay on the south side. The whole of the day was wet and unpleasant, and in the evening, we encamped [11] for the night.\n\n\n

[Whitehouse]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nTuesday Novemr. 26th \u00a0\u00a0 A cloudy wet morning, & we set out early. \u00a0\u00a0 we proceeded about 1 Mile up the River & then crossed it. \u00a0\u00a0 In doing of which we passed through several Islands. \u00a0\u00a0 We proceeded on down the South side of the River, & came to an Inhabited Village of Indians. [12] \u00a0\u00a0 We halted at this place for a short time; where the Indians gave us plenty to eat consisting of Roots not unlike potatoes & behaved friendly to us.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 They also gave us a few of these Roots or wild potatoes to take with us. \u00a0\u00a0 We continued on still down the River; the day being wet, cold and very disagreeable. \u00a0\u00a0 We encamped [13] in a thicket on the South shore. \u00a0\u00a0 Several Indians came to us in a Canoe, with Roots to sell. \u00a0\u00a0 We saw along the shore, a number of Islands that lay very low & marshy. \u00a0\u00a0 The Geese, swan & Ducks are in the greatest plenty at this place, & our Hunters killed a number of them. \u00a0\u00a0 We purchased the Roots the Indians had brought with them in the Canoe, and they left us well pleased.\u2014\n\n\n\n

1. These islands (called \"Seal Islands\" in second entry and on Atlas map 82) in Clatsop County, Oregon, are now within the Lewis and Clark National Wildlife Refuge. Atlas maps 82, 89. (back)
\n
2. For the Cathlamets see above, November 11, 1805. The village of \"9 large houses\" is shown on Atlas maps 82, 92; it was named Hlilusqahih and was located at the present town of Knappa, Clatsop County. Archaeological excavations at this site uncovered house remains and artifacts relating to the Cathlamet occupation in the early historic period. Curtis, 8:182; Minor (ASCR). (back)
\n
3. In Clatsop County, near present Svenson. Atlas maps 82, 92. (back)
\n
4. A page of bearings and distances interrupts the narrative here in the Elkskin-bound Journal; it may have been written first and the day's narrative written around it later. It has now been placed at the end of the text. (back)
\n
5. Apparently the asterisk at the end of this line refers to two courses in the left margin, at right angles to the rest of page. They have been placed after this course. (back)
\n
6. Probably the river on which they built Fort Clatsop, \"Netul River\" in later entries, today's Lewis and Clark River, Clatsop County. Atlas map 84. (back)
\n
7. Present Cathlamet Point, Clatsop County. Coues (HLC), 2:721 n. 3, suggests that the captains may have named it for Samuel Lewis, the copyist of Clark's 1814 map, Atlas map 126, who he conjectures might have been a relative of Meriwether Lewis. In Clark's\n Estimated Distances it seems to be \"Point Samuel g\u2014.\" Atlas maps 81, 82, 92. The word here appears as if it could have ben added to a blank space. (back)
\n
8. The Indians were Cathlamets, living along the south shore of the Columbia River; see Clark's entry for November 11. (back)
\n
9. In Clatsop County, Oregon, near Svenson. (back)
\n
10. The people were Cathlamets, speaking Kathlamet, a Chinookan language, and their village was at Knappa, Clatsop County, Oregon. (back)
\n
11. In Clatsop County, near Svenson. (back)
\n
12. The people were Cathlamets, speaking Kathlamet, a Chinookan language, and their village was at Knappa, Clatsop County, Oregon. (back)
\n
13. In Clatsop County, near Svenson. (back)
