\n March 25, 1806\n

\n
\n \n

[Lewis]

\n \n
\n Tuesday March 25th 1806.\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nThe morning being disagreeably cold we remained and took breakfast. \u00a0\u00a0 at 7 A. M. we set out and continued our rout along the South Coast of the river against the wind and a strong current, our progress was of course but slow. \u00a0\u00a0 at noon we halted and dined. \u00a0\u00a0 here some Clatsops came to us in a canoe loaded with dryed anchovies, which they call Olthen', Wappetoe and Sturgeon. [1] \u00a0\u00a0 they informed us that they had been up on a trading voyage to the Skillutes.\u2014 [2] I observe that the green bryer [3] which I have previously mentioned as being common on this river below tide water retains it's leaves all winter.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 the red willow [4] and seven bark [5] begin to pur fourth their leaves.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 after dinner we passed the river to a large Island [6] 2 and continued our rout allong the side of the same about a mile when we arrived at a Cathlahmah fishing cam of one lodge; here we found 3 men 2 women and a couple of boys, who from appearances had remained here some time for the purpose of taking sturgeon, which they do by trolling. \u00a0\u00a0 they had ten or douzen very fine sturgeon which had not been long taken. [7] \u00a0\u00a0 we offered to purchase some of their fish but they asked us such an extravegant price that we declined purchase. \u00a0\u00a0 one of the men purchased a sea Otterskin at this lodge, for which he gave a dressed Elkskin and an handkercheif. \u00a0\u00a0 near this lodge we met some Cathlahmahs who had been up the river on a fishing excurtion. \u00a0\u00a0 they had a good stock of fish on board, but did not seem disposed to sell them. \u00a0\u00a0 we remained at this place about half an hour and then continued our rout up the Island to it's head and passed to the south side. \u00a0\u00a0 the wind in the evening was very hard. \u00a0\u00a0 it was with some difficulty that we could find a spot proper for an encampment, the shore being a swamp for several miles back; at length late in the evening opposite to the place we had encamped on the 6th of November last; we found the entrance of a small creek which afforded us a safe harbour from the wind and encamped. [8] \u00a0\u00a0 the ground was low and moist tho' we obtained a tolerable encampment. \u00a0\u00a0 here we found another party of Cathlahmahs about 10 in number who had established a temperary residence for the purpose of fishing and taking seal. \u00a0\u00a0 they had taken a fine parcel of sturgeon and some seal. \u00a0\u00a0 they gave us some of the fleese of the seal which I found a great improvement to the poor Elk. \u00a0\u00a0 here we found Drewyer and the Feildses who had been seperated from us since morning; they had passed on the North side of the large Island which was much nearer. \u00a0\u00a0 the bottom lands are covered with cottonwood, the growth with a broad leaf which resembles ash except the leaf. [9] \u00a0\u00a0 the underbrush red willow, broad leafed willow, [10] sevenbark, goosburry, [11] green bryer & the larged leafed thorn; [12] the latter is now in bloom; the natives inform us that it bears a freut about an inch in diameter which is good to eat.\u2014\n

\n \n
\n
\n \n

[Clark]

\n \n
\n Tuesday 25th of March 1806\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nLast night and this morning are cool wend hard a head and tide going out, after an early brackfast we proceeded on about 4 miles and came too on the south side to worm and dry our Selves a little. Soon after we had landed two Indians Came from a War kia cum village on the opposite Side with 2 dogs and a fiew Wappato to Sell neither of which we bought. Som Clatsops passed down in a Canoe loaded with fish and Wappato. \u00a0\u00a0 as the wind was hard a head and tide against us we Concluded to delay untill the return of the tide which we expected at 1 oClock, at which hour we Set out \u00a0\u00a0 met two Canoes of Clatsops loaded with dried anchovies and Sturgion which they had taken and purchased above \u00a0\u00a0 we crossed over to an Island on which was a Cath lahmah fishing Camp of one Lodge; here we found \u2329one\u232a 3 man two woman and a couple of boys who must have for Some time for the purpose of taking Sturgeon which they do by trolling. \u00a0\u00a0 they had 10 or 12 very fine Sturgeon which had not been long taken; we wished to purchase some of their fish but they asked Such extravegent prices that we declined purchaseing. \u00a0\u00a0 one of our Party purchased a Sea otter Skin at this Lodge for which he gave a dressed Elk Skin & a Handkerchief. \u00a0\u00a0 we remained at this place about half an hour and then Continued our rout. \u00a0\u00a0 the winds in the evening was verry hard, it was with Some dificuelty that we Could find a Spot proper for an encampment, the Shore being a Swamp for Several miles back; at length late in the evening opposit to the place we had encamped on the 6th of Novr. last; we fouond the enterance of a Small Creek which offered us a Safe harbour from the Winds and Encamped. \u00a0\u00a0 the Ground was low and moist tho' we obtained a tolerable encampment. \u00a0\u00a0 here we found another party of Cathlahmahs about 10 in number, who had established a temporary residence for the purpose of fishing and takeing Seal. \u00a0\u00a0 they had taken about 12 Sturgeon and Some Seal. \u00a0\u00a0 they gave us Some of the flesh of the Seal which I found a great improvement to the poor Elk. \u00a0\u00a0 here we found Drewyer and the 2 Fields' who had been Seperated from us Since Morning; they had passed on the North Side of the large Island which was much nearest. \u00a0\u00a0 the bottom lands are Covered with a Species of Arspine, the Growth with a broad leaf which resembles ash except the leaf. \u00a0\u00a0 the under brush red willow, broad leafed Willow, Seven bark, Goose berry, Green bryor, and the larged leaf thorn; the latter is Now in blume, the nativs inform us that it bears a \u2329leaf\u232a fruit about an Inch in diamieter which is a good to eate. \u00a0\u00a0 the red willow and 7 bark begin to put foth their leaves. The green bryor which I have before mentioned retains leaves all winter. \u00a0\u00a0 made 15 Miles.\n\n

[Ordway]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nTuesday 25th of March 1806. \u00a0\u00a0 we met a canoe of the Clatsops going down with their canoe loaded with fish and wa pa toes. \u00a0\u00a0 the winds hard a head and tide against us So we delayd untill 1 oClock P. M. at which time we set out \u00a0\u00a0 met 2 canoes of the Clotsops loaded with dried fish and wa pa toes &C & Sturgeon which they had purchased above. \u00a0\u00a0 we crossed over to an Island [13] on which was a fishing Camp of the Cath le mahs. \u00a0\u00a0 they had a vast Site of Sturgeon \u00a0\u00a0 one of the men purchased a Sea otter Skin, the price of which was a dressed Elk Skin and a Silk hankerchief. \u00a0\u00a0 we proceeded on from thence \u00a0\u00a0 the after part of the day the wind rose high \u00a0\u00a0 after dark we arived at another fishing Camp of the Cath le mahs where we Camped [14] for\nthe night.\n\n\n

\n \n
\n
\n \n

[Gass]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Tuesday 25th. \u00a0\u00a0 We set out after breakfast and had a fair morning; proceeded on to 12 o'clock, when we again halted, the wind and tide being both against us. When the tide began to rise we went on again, saw some of the natives [15] in canoes descending the river, and in the afternoon passed an Indian lodge, [16] where one of the men purchased an otter skin.\u2014 At this time the wind rose and blew very hard accompanied with rain; notwithstanding we proceeded on till night, when we came to the mouth of a small creek, [17] which formed a good harbour for our canoes. Here we found several of the natives [18] encamped and catching sturgeon, of which they had taken 14 large ones.\n\n\n

\n
\n \n

[Whitehouse]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nTuesday March 25th \u00a0\u00a0 This morning early a Canoe with some of the Natives of the Clatsop Tribe came to where our Canoes lay, their Canoe was loaded with fish & Wapatoes roots. \u00a0\u00a0 the wind & tide being against us, We had to delay at our encampment, until 1 o'clock P. M. at which time we proceeded on our voyage, and met two Canoes with Indians, who were descending the River. \u00a0\u00a0 We continued on, & crossed over to an Island, on which we found a fishing Camp of the Cath-le-mah Indians, These Indians had a great number of Sturgeon laying tied at the Edge of the water, which were fastened to Stakes drove into the ground. \u00a0\u00a0 One of our party purchased a Sea otter Skin from these Natives. \u00a0\u00a0 the price he gave for it was a dressed Elk skin, & an old silk handkerchief. \u00a0\u00a0 We proceeded on. \u00a0\u00a0 the remainder of the day proved Stormy. \u00a0\u00a0 we continued on till after dark, & came to another Indian fishing Camp, laying on the South side of the River; where we encamped for the night\n\n\n

\n \n \n
\n
1. \"Ol-then',\" is Chinookan \u00fa-l\\\n x\u0323an, \"dried eulachon.\" The \"anchovies\" are actually eulachon, or candle fish, Thaleichthys pacificus. See Lewis's description at February 24, 1806. The sturgeon may be the white sturgeon, Acipenser transmontanus. Lee et al., 42, 126. The Clatsops are discussed at November 21, 1805. (back)
\n
2. \"Skillutes\" is probably from the Chinookan form s(i)k\u0313l\u00fatk, \"look (at him)!\" and not a tribal designation. See November 24, 1805. (back)
\n
3. Probably Pacific blackberry, Rubus ursinus Cham. & Schlecht. See Lewis's description at February 13, 1806. (back)
\n
4. Red osier dogwood, Cornus sericea L. (or C. stolonifera Michx.). Hitchcock et al., 3:588\u201390. (back)
\n
5. Ninebark, Physocarpus capitatus (Pursh) Kuntze. See December 1, 1805. Hitchcock et al., 3:125\u201326. (back)
\n
6. The later Puget Island, in Wahkiakum County, Washington, perhaps \"[Sea] otter Isd.\" on Atlas map 81; see November 6, 1805. (back)
\n
7. A vertical line runs through several lines to this point, perhaps done by Biddle. (back)
\n
8. In Columbia County, Oregon, below one of the mouths of the Clatskanie River, opposite the point called Cape Horn on the Washington shore. The exact position relates to identifying \"Sturgeon Isd.\" on Atlas map 81 as modern Wallace Island; see November 6, 1805. (back)
\n
9. The cottonwood is black cottonwood, Populus trichocarpa T. & G. Curiously, in Clark's entry for this day he calls it a \"Species of Arspine [aspen].\" The tree which resembles an ash is bigleaf maple, Acer macrophyllum Pursh. The confusion between the Oregon ash, Fraxinus latifolia Benth., and the bigleaf maple is clarified at February 10, 1806. See also November 4, 1805. Little (CIH), 153-W, 95-W; Franklin & Dyrness, 72. (back)
\n
10. \n Lewis is probably here using the term broad-leaved willow species with wide leaves, including the area's abundant peach-leaved willow, Salix amygdaloides Anderss., also Pacific willow, S. lasiandra Benth., yellow willow, S. lutea Nutt., and perhaps northwest willow, S. sessilifolia Nutt. The broad-leaved willow of the Missouri River, however, was either peach-leaved willow or diamond willow, S. rigida Muhl. Little (MWH), 173-W, 180; Franklin & Dryness, 125. (back)
\n
11. Possibly canyon gooseberry, Ribes menziesii Pursh, or more probably straggly gooseberry, R. divaricatum Dougl., which is found at lower levels on the west side of the Cascades and up the Columbia Gorge to Klickitat County, Washington. Both were new to science, while the former was a plant collected by the party. Hitchcock et al., 3:71\u201373, 78\u201379; Cutright (LCPN), 289\u201390, 373, 417. (back)
\n
12. The \"thorn\" is likely salmonberry, Rubus spectabilis Pursh, as indicated by fruit size and flowering time. Lewis collected the type specimen two days later near the mouth of the Cowlitz River. However, Lewis may have been seeing some other species of Rubus this day. See also December 1, 1805, and April 8, 1806. Hitchcock et al., 3:182; Cutright (LCPN), 278, 289\u201390, 418. (back)
\n
13. \n Puget Island, Wahkiakum County, Washington. (back)
\n
14. Below the mouth of the Clatskanie River, Columbia County, Oregon; see the captains'\nentries for this day. (back)
\n
15. \n Clatsops, say the captains. (back)
\n
16. A Cathlamet lodge on Puget Island, Wahkiakum County, Washington. (back)
\n
17. In Columbia County, Oregon, below one of the mouths of the Clatskanie River, opposite Cape Horn on the Washington shore; see the captains' entries for the day. (back)
\n
18. \n Cathlamets. (back)
