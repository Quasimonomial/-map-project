\n August 22, 1804\n

\n
\n \n

[Clark]

\n \n
\u00a0

\n22nd of August \u2329Friday\u232a Wendesday 1804 \u00a0\u00a0 Set out early \u00a0\u00a0 wind from the South. G Shannon joined the Boat last night. Course this morning is S 47\u00b0 W. 1\u00bc on the S. point \u00a0\u00a0 West 1\u00bc me. to the Commencement of a Bluff on the L. S. \u00a0\u00a0 the High land near the river for Some distance below. This Bluff contain Pyrites alum, Copperass & a Kind Markesites also a clear Soft Substance which \u2329will mold and become pliant like wax\u232a Capt lewis was near being Poisened by the Smell in pounding this Substance I belv to be arsenic or Cabalt. I observe great Quantity of Cops. ans [copperas] and almin [alum] pure & Straters of white & brown earth of 6 Inch. thick. [1] \u00a0\u00a0 a Creek Coms in above the Bluffs on which there is great quantities of those minerals, This Creek I call \n Roloje\n a \u2329name given me last night in my Sleep,\u232a [2] at those Allom banks Shields joined in with two Deer\u2014

22nd August Friday 1804
\u00a0
\n \n

\t\t\t \nSet out early wind from the South \u00a0\u00a0 at three miles we landed at a Bluff where the two men Sent with the horses were waiting with two Deer, by examonation of this (1) Bluff Contained alum, Copperas, Cobalt, Pyrites; a alum rock Soft & Sand Stone. \u00a0\u00a0 Capt. Lewis in proveing the quality of those minerals was near poisoning himself by the fumes & task of the Cabalt which had the appearance of Soft Isonglass\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 Copperas & alum is verry pure, Above this Bluff a Small Creek Coms in from the L. S. passing under the Clifts for Several miles, this Creek I Call Roloje a name I learned last night in m[y] S[leep]. \u00a0\u00a0 (2) \u2329Eight\u232a Seven miles above is a Clift of Allom Stone of a Dark Brown Colr. Containing also in crusted in the Crevices & Shelves of the rock great qts. of Cabalt, Semented Shels & a red earth. \u00a0\u00a0 from this the (3) river bends to the East and is within 3 or 4 miles of the River Soues at the place where that river Coms from the high land into the Low Prarie & passed under the foot of those Hills to its mouth. [6]\n

\n

\n Capt Lewis took a Dost of salts to work off the effects of the Arsenic, we Camped on the S. S. \u00a0\u00a0 Sailed the greater part of this day with a hard wind from the S. E. \u00a0\u00a0 great deel of Elk Sign, and great appearance of wind from the N.W.

[Ordway]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\t\nWednesday 22d August 1804. \u00a0\u00a0 we Set off eairly \u00a0\u00a0 the current verry Swift. \u00a0\u00a0 the wind hard from the South, we proceded on round a bend & Sand bar N. S. \u00a0\u00a0 the hills make to the River on S. S. \u00a0\u00a0 below the hills their is Some large Timber of cottonwood Elm maple [7] &.C. Some oak & ceeder on the Bluffs. \u00a0\u00a0 we halted under the Bluff & found different kinds of oar which has the appearence of brass, copperas alum &.C. &C \u00a0\u00a0 all of which has a Sulpheras Smell, Broken praries Back. The 2 men who had been with the horses from the Mahars village, joined us below Sd. Bluffs. \u00a0\u00a0 had killed 2 Deer, 2 other men went on with the horses hunting; G. Drewyer caught one Beaver last night.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 a Small creek comes in close above Sd. Bluffs which we call Roloje Cr.\n [8] on S. S. \u00a0\u00a0 we proceeded on under a fine Breeze from the South. \u00a0\u00a0 passed a Red ceeder Bluff on the South Side & little above on the Same Side we passed an Alum Stone clift about 50 feet high & a great nomber of birds nests, near the top of the clift. \u00a0\u00a0 we Sailed on to a prarie where we Camped on N. S. after Sailing 19 Miles. \u00a0\u00a0 back of Sd. prarie is a large pond. \u00a0\u00a0 we See a great deal of Elk Sign,\n\n

[Gass]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Wednesday 22nd. \u00a0\u00a0 We proceeded early upon our voyage; passed bluffs on the south side, where there is copperas, allum and ore of some kind; also passed a creek. The high land on the south side for nine or ten miles runs close to the river, where there are cedar bluffs of various colours. We encamped on the north side.\n\n

[Whitehouse]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\t\nWednesday 22nd Augt. 1804. \u00a0\u00a0 we Set off eairly \u00a0\u00a0 the current Swift. \u00a0\u00a0 the [wind] hard from the South. \u00a0\u00a0 we passed a ceedar Bluff on the South Side in which we found Some kinds of minral Substance, the 2 men who had been with the horses joined us [9] \u00a0\u00a0 had killed 2 Deer we pasd. an allum Stone clift on the S. S. \u00a0\u00a0 we camped at a prarie on the N. S. \u00a0\u00a0 we See a great deal of Elk Sign &c

\n

\nWednesday August 22nd \u00a0\u00a0 This morning we set out early, and found the current running very Strong against us, the Wind blowing from the South, we passed under a bluff lying on the south side of the River, and some of our Men landed from the Pettiauger, they found some kind of mineral substance, but its qualities appear'd to be utterly unknown to us, The Men that had the Horses came to the bank of the River, having killed 2 Deer, which we took on board,\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 we then proceeded on, and passed an Alum stone Clift, laying on the South side of the River, we proceeded on, & in the Evening we encamped at a Priari which lay on the North side, where we saw a great many Tracts & signs of Elk.\u2014\n\n\n\n

\n
1. Pyrite and marcasite are both ferrous sulfide minerals, although they have different crystal forms. Copperas is one name for the mineral melanterite, a hydrous ferrous sulfate that results from the weathering of pyrite and marcasite. Clark's reference to Lewis's being poisoned by substances in this area is unclear, and the mineral is indeterminable. The bluff begins above present Jackson, Dakota County, Nebraska. Atlas maps 16 and 17; MRC map 28; MRR map 76. (back)
\n
2. \n Clark crossed out the words giving the source of the name and cut short his mention of it in his Codex B entry. The creek is probably the present Aowa, or Ayoway, Creek which reaches the Missouri in Dixon County, Nebraska, near present Ponca. The name derives from the Omaha, Max\u00fade waa i te, \"where the Iowa farmed.\" The Iowas moved from this village down to the one noted July 28. Fletcher & La Flesche, 91; Atlas map 17; Osgood (FN), 112 n. 6. (back)
\n
3. Probably in Union County, South Dakota, somewhat south of the present community of Elk Point. MRC map 28; MRR map 79. (back)
\n
4. The purging of the body by a laxative was a favorite method of removing harmful substances. Chuinard, 230\u201331. (back)
\n
5. See the Detachment Order for August 26, 1804. (back)
\n
6. \n Atlas map 17. (back)
\n
7. An unknown species of maple, Acer sp. (back)
\n
8. Probably Aowa, or Ayowa, Creek, Dixon County, Nebraska, near Ponca. (back)
\n
9. Evidently Reubin Field and George Shannon, but Clark refers to Field's return with the horses the next day and says nothing about Shannon's return. (back)
