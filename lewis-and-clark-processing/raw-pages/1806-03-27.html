\n March 27, 1806\n

\n
\n \n

[Lewis]

\n \n
\n Thursday March 27th 1806.\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nWe set out early this morning and were shortly after joined by some of the Skillutes who came along side in a small canoe for the purpose of trading roots and fish. \u00a0\u00a0 at 10 A. M. we arrived at two houses of this nation on the Stard. side where we halted for breakfast. [1] \u00a0\u00a0 here we overtook our hunters, they had killed nothing. \u00a0\u00a0 the natives appeared extreemly hospitable, gave us dryed Anchovies, Sturgeon, wappetoe, quamash, and a speceis of small white tuberous roots about 2 inches in length and as thick as a man's finger; [2] these are eaten raw, are crisp, milkey, and agreeably flavored. \u00a0\u00a0 most of the party were served by the natives with as much as they could eat; they insisted on our remaining all day with them and hunting the Elk and deer which they informed us were very abundant in their neighbourhood. \u00a0\u00a0 but as the weather would not permit us to dry our canoes in order to pitch them we declined their friendly invitation, and resumed our voyage at 12 OCk. \u00a0\u00a0 the principal village of these Skillutes reside on the lower side of the Cow-e-lis'-kee river\n [3] a few miles from it's entrance into the columbia. \u00a0\u00a0 these people are said to be numerous. \u00a0\u00a0 in their dress, habits, manners and language they differ but little from the Clatsops\n Chinnooks &c. \u00a0\u00a0 they have latterly been at war with Chinnooks but peace is said now to be restored between them, but their intercourse is not yet resumed. \u00a0\u00a0 no Chinnooks come above the marshey islands nor do the Skillutes visit the mouth of the Columbia. \u00a0\u00a0 the Clatsops, Cathlahmahs and Wackkiacums are the carriers between these nations being in alliance with both.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 The Coweliskee is 150 yards wide, is deep and from indian Information navigable a very considerable distance for canoes. \u00a0\u00a0 it discharges itself into the Columbia about three miles above a remarkable high rocky nole [4] which is situated on the N. side of the river by which it is washed on the South side and is seperated from the Nothern hills of the river by a wide bottom of several miles to which it is united. I suspect that this river waters the country lying West of the range of mountains [5] which pass the columbia between the great falls and rapids, and north of the same nearly to the low country which commences on the N. W. coast about Latitude [blank] North. \u00a0\u00a0 above the Skillutes on this river another nation by the name of the Hul-loo-et-tell\n [6] reside, who are said also to be numerous. \u00a0\u00a0 at the distance of 2 m. above the village at which we breakfasted we passed the entrance of this river; we saw several fishing camps of the Skillutes on both sides of the Columbia, and were attended all the evening by parties of the natives in their canoes who visited us for the purpose of trading their fish and roots; we purchased as many as we wished on very moderate terms; they seemed perfectly satisfyed with the exchange and behaved themselves in a very orderly manner. \u00a0\u00a0 late in the evening we passed our camp of the 5th of November and encamped about 4\u00bd above at the commencement of the bottom land on stard. below Deer Island. [7] \u00a0\u00a0 we had scarcely landed before we were visited by a large canoe with eight men; from them we obtained a dryed fruit which resembled the raspburry [8] and which I beeive to be the fruit of the large leafed thorn frequently mentioned. \u00a0\u00a0 it is reather ascid tho' pleasently flavored. I preserved a specemine of this fruit I fear that it has been baked in the process of drying and if so the seed will not vegitate. \u00a0\u00a0 saw the Cottonwood, sweet willow, [9] oak, ash and the broad leafed ash, the growth which resembles the beach [10] &c. \u00a0\u00a0 these form the growth of the bottom lands while the hills are covered almost exclusively with the various species of fir heretofore discribed. \u00a0\u00a0 the black Alder [11] appears as well on some parts of the hills as the bottoms. \u00a0\u00a0 before we set out from the Skillute village we sent on Gibson's canoe and Drewyers with orders to proceed as fast as they could to Deer island and there to hunt and wait our arrival. \u00a0\u00a0 we wish to halt at that place to repair our canoes if possible. \u00a0\u00a0 the indians who visited us this evening remained but a short time, they passed the river to the oposite side and encamped. \u00a0\u00a0 the night as well as the day proved cold wet and excessively disagreeable. \u00a0\u00a0 we came 20 miles today.\u2014\n

[Clark]

\n \n
\n Thursday March 27th 1806.\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\na rainey disagreeable night \u00a0\u00a0 rained the greater part of the night \u00a0\u00a0 we Set out this morning verry early and proceeded on to two houses of the Skil-lute Indians on the South Side here we found our hunters who had Seperated from us last evening. \u00a0\u00a0 the wind rose and the rain became very hard Soon after we landed \u00a0\u00a0 here we were very friendly receved by the natives who gave all our party as much fish as they Could eate, they also gave us Wappato and pashaquaw roots to eate prepared in their own way. \u00a0\u00a0 also a Species of Small white tuberous roots about 2 inches in length and as thick as a mans finger, these are eaten raw, or crips, milkey and agreeably flavoured; the nativs insists on our remaining all day with them and hunt the Elk and deer which they informed us was very abundant in this neighbourhood. \u00a0\u00a0 but as the weather would not permit our drying our Canoes in order to pitch them, we declined their friendly invertation, and resumed our voyage at 12 oClock. The principal village of the \n Skil-lutes\n is Situated on the lower Side of the \n Cow-e-lis kee\n river a fiew miles from it's enterance into the Columbia. \u00a0\u00a0 those people are Said to be noumerous, in their dress, habits, manners and Language they differ but little from the Clatsops, Chinnooks &c. \u00a0\u00a0 they have latterly been at war with the Chinnooks, but peace is Said to be now restored between them, but their inter Course is not yet restored. \u00a0\u00a0 \u2329but\u232a no Chinnook Come above the Warkiacums, nor do the Skillutes visit the Mouth of the Columbia. The Clatsops, Cath lahmahs & War kia coms are the Carriers between those nations being in alliance with both\u2014. The Cow e lis kee river is 150 yards wide, is deep and from Indian information navigable a very conslderable distance for canoes. \u00a0\u00a0 it discharges itself into the Columbia about 3 miles above a remarkable knob which is high and rocky and Situated on the North Side of the Columbia, and Seperated from the Northern hills of the river by a Wide bottom of Several Miles, to which it united. I Suspect that this river Waters the Country lying west of a range of Mountains which passes the Columbia between the Great falls and rapids, and North of the Same nearly to the low country which Commences on the N W. Coast about Latitude 4\u00b0 [blank] [12] North. \u00a0\u00a0 above the Skil lutes on this river another nation by the name of the \n Hul-loo-et-tell\n reside who are Said also the be numerous. \u00a0\u00a0 at the distance of 2 miles above the village at which we brackfast we passed the enterance of this river; we Saw Several fishing camps of the Skillutes on both Sides of the Columbia, and also on both Sides of this river. \u00a0\u00a0 we were attended all the evening by parties of the nativs in their Canoes who visited us for the purpose of tradeing their fish and roots; we purchased as maney as we wished on very moderate terms; they Seamed perfectly Satisfied with the exchange and behaved themselves in a very orderly manner. \u00a0\u00a0 late in the evening we passed the place we Camped the 5th of Novr. and Encamped about 4 miles above at the Commencement of the Columbian Vally on the Stard. Side below Deer Island. \u00a0\u00a0 we had Scercily landed before we were visited by a large Canoe with 8 men; from them we obtained a dried fruit which resembled the raspberry and which I beleave is the fruit of the large leafed thorn frequently mentioned. \u00a0\u00a0 it is reather ascide tho' pleasently flavored. Saw Cotton wood, Sweet Willow, w[hite] oake, ash and the broad leafed ash the Growth which resembles the bark &c. \u00a0\u00a0 these form the groth of the bottom lands, whilst the Hills are almost exclusively Covered with the various Species of fir heretofore discribed. \u00a0\u00a0 the black alder appears on Maney parts of the hills Sides as on the bottoms. \u00a0\u00a0 before we Set out from the 2 houses where we brackfast we Sent on two Canoes with the best hunters, with orders to pro ceed as fast as they Could to Deer island and there to hunt and wait our arrival. \u00a0\u00a0 we wish to halt at that place and repare 2 of our Canoes if possible. \u00a0\u00a0 the Indians that visited us this evining remained but a Short time, they passed over to an Island and encamped. \u00a0\u00a0 the night as well as the day proved Cold wet and excessively disagreeable. \u00a0\u00a0 we Came 20 miles in the Course of this day.\n\n

[Ordway]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nThursday 27th March 1806. \u00a0\u00a0 rain commenced this morning and continued thro the day. \u00a0\u00a0 we halted at a village of the Chilutes nation\n [13] \u00a0\u00a0 they treated us in a friendly manner \u00a0\u00a0 Gave us Some wapa toes & anchoves to eat. Several Indians followed after us with Small canoes. \u00a0\u00a0 our officers purchased a large Sturgeon from them \u00a0\u00a0 we proceed on to the mo of a River named Calams River and Camped [14] on the South Side little above Said River\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 Six of our hunters Sent on this afternoon to deer Island\n [15] with the Small canoes in order to hunt.\n\n\n

\n \n

[Gass]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Thursday 27th. \u00a0\u00a0 There was a cloudy wet morning. We embarked early and went about 6 miles, when we came to a small Indian village, [16] where the natives received us very kindly. They belong to the Chil-ook nation, [17] and differ something in their language from the Chin-ooks. We got some Wapto roots and fish from them and then proceeded on, though it rained very hard. Two small canoes went on ahead to Deer island, in order to kill some game by the time we should come up. We passed several Indian lodges where the natives [18] were fishing for sturgeon, and got a large one out of a small canoe; a number of which followed us with 2 Indians in each of them. At night we encamped where we had plenty of good wood, oak and ash. [19]\n

[Whitehouse]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nThursday March 27th \u00a0\u00a0 This morning early it commenced raining, which continued during the whole of this day. \u00a0\u00a0 At 7 o'clock A. M. we proceeded on, & crossed over to an Island, which lay on the North side of the River, where we halted. \u00a0\u00a0 We found on this Island, an Indian Village of the Chilutes Tribe\n [20] it contained 7 Houses.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 These Indians treated us in a friendly manner. \u00a0\u00a0 At 10 o'Clock A. M. we left this Island and continued on & passed several Indian fishing Camps. \u00a0\u00a0 A number of Indians followed us with small Canoes. \u00a0\u00a0 Our Officers purchased from these Indians a large Sturgeon. \u00a0\u00a0 We continued on & passed the Mouth of a River called by the Natives Calamus, [21] & encamped on the South side of the River a small distance above the said River. \u00a0\u00a0 Our officers sent 6 of our hunters in Canoes to go on a head, to an Island called Deer Island, in order to hunt, untill we came up with them\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 These hunters left us this afternoon. \u00a0\u00a0 We have still hard rain this evening. \u00a0\u00a0 We encamped on the South side of the River, where we found plenty of Oak & Ash wood to make our fires with.\u2014\n\n

\n \n \n
\n
1. In Columbia County, Oregon, in the vicinity of present Rainier, and opposite present Longview and Kelso, Cowlitz County, Washington, on the Cowlitz River. Symbols for the \"houses\" are shown near the west edge of Atlas map 80. (back)
\n
2. \"Quamash\" is camas, Camassia quamash (Pursh) Greene. Hitchcock et al., 1:780\u201382. Clark calls it \"pashaquaw\" in his entry for this day. The first term comes from Nez Perce\n q\u00e9'mes, while the second is Shoshone\n pasigoo, both signifying the camas. See September 20, 1805. The identity of the small edible root is unknown. (back)
\n
3. On the upriver trip the captains applied the name Skillute (variously spelled) to two separate groups. One group was here at the entrance of the Cowlitz (\"Cow-e-lis'-kee\") River, in Cowlitz County. Atlas maps 80, 81. The other group they noted on April 16, 1806; see note there. For the Skillutes, a Chinookan people, see November 4, 1805, and March 25, 1806. \"Cow-e-lis'-kee,\" qawilick-i[x], \"where the Cowlitz (people) are,\" was evidently borrowed into the Lower Chinook language from Salish\n k\u00e1wlicq, \"language, people of the Cowlitz River.\" Hajda (SCS), 516. The final \"ee\" of Lewis and Clark probably represents the Chinookan locative suffix \"-i[x]\". (back)
\n
4. \n Mount Coffin, in Cowlitz County; see November 6, 1805. Atlas map 80. (back)
\n
5. The Cascade Range. Atlas map 79. (back)
\n
6. These people were a group of Salish-speaking Cowlitz. Ray (HCI); Hajda (SOLC), 107. The term is a Chinookan ethnonym, \n x\u0323luit \u00edl, \"strange country.\" Silverstein, 545. According to Lewis they lived on the Cowlitz River, but on March 29 both an interlineation and Clark's entry have them living on Lewis River. They were identified incorrectly as Watlala Chinookans in the Estimate of Western Indians. (back)
\n
7. In Columbia County, Oregon, in the vicinity of Goble, and roughly opposite present Kalama, Cowlitz County, Washington. Deer Island, in Columbia County, retains that name today. (back)
\n
8. Probably the party purchased either dried salmonberries or Pacific blackberries. Lewis believed the berries had come from the shrub he called \"large leafed thorn.\" See April 8, 1806, for a discussion of the confusion concerning these species. (back)
\n
9. Probably the Pacific willow, although a number of other willow species are possible. Lewis mentions the sweet willow as an important species in entries of April 2 and 30, 1806. The Pacific willow continues to be an important riparian species along the lower Columbia River. (back)
\n
10. Probably the red, or Oregon, alder, Alnus rubra Bong. See October 30, 1805. Hitchcock et al., 2:74. (back)
\n
11. This alder species is not identifiable with certainty. Apparently, it is a plant separate from \"the growth which resembles the beach.\" It is possible that Lewis's black alder refers to the red alder, and that the \"beach\" may refer to the white alder, Alnus rhombifolia Nutt., which is a riparian species found in the Willamette Valley and along the Columbia River on the east side of the Cascades. Ibid.; Little (MWH), 13; Franklin & Dyrness, 126. (back)
\n
12. A space perhaps for some latitude between 40\u00b0 and 50\u00b0 North. (back)
\n
13. The name is given as Skillute or a variant in Lewis and Clark. See Lewis's and Clark's\nentries for November 4, 1805, March 25 and 27, and April 16, 1806. (back)
\n
14. In the vicinity of Goble, Columbia County, Oregon, with the Kalama River on the opposite side in Cowlitz County, Washington. (back)
\n
15. Still Deer Island, Columbia County. (back)
\n
16. A \"Skillute\" village near Rainier, Columbia County, Oregon. See the captains' entries \nfor March 25 and 27, 1806. (back)
\n
17. That is, they spoke a Lower Chinookan dialect. (back)
\n
18. More \"Skillutes\"; see above. (back)
\n
19. The oak is probably Oregon white oak and the ash may be Oregon ash. (back)
\n
20. A Skillute village, say the captains' entries for March 25 and 27, 1806. The captains say it contained only two houses; the copyist may have misread the numeral in the original. They also place it on the starboard side, near present Rainier, Columbia County, Oregon, which is hard to reconcile with Whitehouse's island on the \"North side.\" (back)
\n
21. The Kalama River in Cowlitz County, Washington. (back)
