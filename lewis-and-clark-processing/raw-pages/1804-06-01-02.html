[Gass]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Friday 1st June, 1804. \u00a0\u00a0 Before daylight we embarked and proceeded on \nour voyage; passed Big Muddy creek on the north side; and on the opposite side saw high banks. Two and an half miles higher up, we passed\nBear creek and at 4 o'clock P. M. arrived at the Osage river; where we remained during the evening and the next day. The Osage river is 197 [8] yards wide at its confluence with the Missouri, which, at this place, is 875 yards broad. The country on the south side is broken, but rich: and the land on the other of a most excellent quality. The two men who went by land with the horses, came to us here: they represented the land they had passed through as the best they had ever seen, and the timber good, consisting chiefly of oak, ash, hickory, and black walnut. [9] They had killed in their way five deer. The periogue left at the mouth of Gaskenade river, came up with the man who had been lost. Here our hunters went out and killed three deer. The Osage nation of Indians live about two hundred miles up this river. They are of a large size and well proportioned, and a very warlike people. Our arms and ammunition were all inspected here and found in good order.\n\n\n

[Whitehouse]

\n Friday 1st June 1804. \u00a0\u00a0 a pleasant morning. \u00a0\u00a0 we Set out eairly and proceeded on \u00a0\u00a0 passed a River on the N. Side called big muddy, the bottoms low. \u00a0\u00a0 passd. a creek called beaver creek\n [10] on the S.Side.

\n

\nFriday June 1st \u00a0\u00a0 We set off early this morning, the weather being pleasant, and passed a River on the North side called Big muddy; the bottom land laying low on the Mesouri, we passed a Creek called beaver creek, lying on the South side of the River, and encamp'd at the Mouth of the Grand Osage River\n
