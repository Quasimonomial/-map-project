\n March 31, 1806\n

\n
\n \n

[Lewis]

\n \n
\n Monday March 31st 1806\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nWe set out early this morning and proceeded until 8 A. M. \u00a0\u00a0 when we Landed on the N. side opposite one large wooden house of the \n Sh\u00e2h-ha-la\n nation [1] and took breakfast. \u00a0\u00a0 when we decended the river in November last there were 24 other lodges formed of Straw and covered with bark near this house; these lodges are now distroyed and the inhabitants as the indians inform us have returned to the great rapids\n [2] of this river which is their permanent residence; the house which remains is inhabited; soon after we landed two canoes came over from this house with 4 men and a woman. \u00a0\u00a0 they informed us that their relations who were with them last fall usuly visit them at that season for the purpose of hunting deer and Elk and collecing wappetoe and that they had lately returned to the rapids I presume to prepare for the fishing season as the Salmon will begin to run shortly.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 this morning we overtook the man who had visited our camp last night he had a fine sturgeon in his canoe which he had just taken. \u00a0\u00a0 the Sagittaria Sagittifolia dose not grow on this river above the Columbian valley.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 These indians of the rapids frequently visit this valley at every season of the year for the purpose of collecting wappetoe which is abundant and appears never to be out of season at any time of the year. \u00a0\u00a0 at 10 A. M. we resumed our march accompanyed by three men in a canoe; one of these fellows appeared to be a man of some note among them; he was dressed in a salor's jacket which was decorated in this own fassion with five rows of large and small buttons in front and some large buttons on the pocket flaps. \u00a0\u00a0 they are remarkably fond of large brass buttons. \u00a0\u00a0 these people speak a different language from those below [3] tho' in their dress habits manners &c they differ but little from the quathlahpohtles. \u00a0\u00a0 their women wear the truss as those do of all the nations residing from the quathlahpohtles to the entrance of Lewis's river. \u00a0\u00a0 they differ in the manner of intering their dead. [4] \u00a0\u00a0 they lay them horizontally on boards and cover them with mats, in a valt formed with boards like the roof of a hose supported by forks and a single pole laid horizontally on those forks. \u00a0\u00a0 many bodies are deposited in the same valt above ground. \u00a0\u00a0 these are frequently laid one on the other, to the hight of three or for corps. \u00a0\u00a0 they deposit with them various articles of which they die possessed, and most esteem while living. \u00a0\u00a0 their canoes are frequently broken up to strengthen the vault.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 these people have a few words the same with those below but the air of the language is intirely different, insomuch, that it may be justly deemed a different language. their women wear longer and larger robes generally, than those below; these are most commonly made of deer skins dressed with the hair on them. \u00a0\u00a0 we continued our rout along the N. side of the river passed diamond Island and whitebrant island\n [5] to the lower point of a handsom prarie opposite to the upper entrance of the Quicksand river; [6] here we encamped having traveled 25 miles today. \u00a0\u00a0 a little below the upper point of the White brant Island\n Seal river discharges itself on the N. side. \u00a0\u00a0 it is about 80 yards wide, and at present discharges a large body of water. the water is very clear. \u00a0\u00a0 the banks are low and near the Columbia overflow and form several large ponds. \u00a0\u00a0 the natives inform us that it is of no great extent and heads in the mountains just above us. \u00a0\u00a0 at the distance of one mile from the entrance of this stream it forks, the two branches being nearly of the same size. \u00a0\u00a0 they are both obstructed with falls and innumerable rappids, insomuch that it cannot be navigated. \u00a0\u00a0 as we could not learn any name of the natives for this stream we called it Seal river from the great abundance of those animals [7] which we saw about it's entrance. we determined to remain at our present encampment a day or two for the several purposes of examining quicksand river [NB: which Capt Clark could not believe to be the river watering the Country to the Sth & for the purpose of] making some Celestial observations, and procuring some meat to serve us as far as the falls or through the Western mountains where we found the game scarce as we decended.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 the three indians who accompanied us last evening encamped a little distance above us and visited our camp where they remained untill 9 P. M. \u00a0\u00a0 in the entrance of Seal river I saw a summer duck or wood duck [8] as they are sometimes called. \u00a0\u00a0 this is the same with those of our country and is the first I have seen since I entered the rocky mountains last summer.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 our hunters who had halted a little below Seal river in consequence of the waves being too high for their small canoe did not join us untill after dark. Drewyer who was out below Seal river informed us that game was very scarce in that quarter, a circumstance which we did not expect.\n

\n \n

[Clark]

\n \n
\n Monday March 31st 1806\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nwe Set out this morning and proceeded untill 8 oClock when we landed on the N. Side opposit one large House of the \n Shah-ha-la\n Nation \u00a0\u00a0 near this house at the time we passed on the 4th of November last was Situated 25 houses, 24 of them were built of Straw & Covered with bark as before mentioned. \u00a0\u00a0 those [of] that description are all distroyed, the one built of wood only remains and is inhabited. \u00a0\u00a0 we overtook the man whome came to our Camp last night and Soon after we landed two canoes Came over from the opposit Side with 5 men & a woman \u00a0\u00a0 those people informed us that their relations who was with them last fall reside at the Great rapids, and were down with them last fall gathering Wappato which did not grow above, and also killing deer, that they Secured the bark of the houses which they then lived in against their return next fall. \u00a0\u00a0 they also inform us that their relations also visit them frequently in the Spring to collect this root which is in great quantities on either Side of the Columbia. \u00a0\u00a0 at 10 A. M we proceeded on accompanied by one Canoe and three men, one of them appeared to be a man of Some note, dressed in a Salors jacket which had 5 rows of large & Small buttons on it. Those people Speak a differant language from those below, with Some fiew Words the Same, the accent entirely different. \u00a0\u00a0 their dress and Manners appear very Similar. \u00a0\u00a0 the women ware the truss or breach clout and Short robes, and men roabs only \u00a0\u00a0 passed up on the N. Side of White brant Island near the upper point of Which a Small river falls in about 80 yards wide and at this time discharges a great quantity of water. \u00a0\u00a0 the nativs inform us that this river is very Short and heads in the range of mountains to the N E of its enterance into the Columbia \u00a0\u00a0 the nativs haveing no name which we could learn for this little river we Call it \n Seal\n \n [9] river from the great number of those Animals which frequents its mouth. \u00a0\u00a0 this river forks into two nearly equal branches about 1 mile up and each branch is crouded with rapids & falls. \u00a0\u00a0 we proceeded on about 2 miles above the enterance of this Seacalf river and imedeately opposit the upper mouth of the quick Sand river we formed a Camp in a Small Prarie on the North Side of the Columbia where we intend to delay one or two days to make Some Selestial observations, to examine quick sand river, and kill Some meat to last us through the Western Mountains which Commences a fiew miles above us and runs in a N. N. W. & S. S. E. derection. The three Indians encamped near us and visited our fire \u00a0\u00a0 we entered into a kind of a Conversation by signs, of the Country and Situation of the rivers. \u00a0\u00a0 they informed us that Seal river headed in the mountains at no great distance. \u00a0\u00a0 quick Sand river was Short only headed in Mt. Hood which is in view and to which he pointed. \u00a0\u00a0 this is a circumstance we did not expect as we had heretofore deemed a comsiderable river. Mount Hood bears East from this place and is distant from this place about 40 miles. \u00a0\u00a0 this information if true will render it necessary to examine the river below on the South Side behind the image canoe and Wappato islands for some river which must water the Country weste of the western mountains to the Waters of California. [10] The Columbia is at present on a Stand and we with dificuelty made 25 miles to day\u2014.\n

\n \n

[Ordway]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nMonday 31st March 1806. \u00a0\u00a0 a clear pleasant morning. \u00a0\u00a0 we Set out eairly and proceed. on \u00a0\u00a0 passd. a village [11] which was a large one when we went down last fall but the Savages are more Scattered along the River in fishing parties &C. \u00a0\u00a0 only 2 cabbins left at this village on the South Shore in a large bottom. \u00a0\u00a0 the wind rose from the Southward. \u00a0\u00a0 a number of the Savages followed us with their canoes. \u00a0\u00a0 one of our hunters [12] killed a deer & Saw a great number more Deer & Elk in these bottoms &c. this morning. \u00a0\u00a0 in the evening we passed the mo of Seal River\n [13] on N. Side \u00a0\u00a0 the waves high. \u00a0\u00a0 we Encamped [14] a Short distance above Sd. River on a handsom high plain of rich land & timber \u00a0\u00a0 near this is opposite the mouth of Quick Sand River\n [15] which puts in on South Side & is high at this time.\n\n\n

\n \n
\n
\n \n

[Gass]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Monday 31st. \u00a0\u00a0 This was a beautiful clear morning, and we proceeded on early. One of the men went along shore, and in a short time killed a deer: the deer are very plentiful on this part of the river.\u2014 We proceeded on, and passed a large village which was full of people as we went down, but is now all deserted except one lodge. [16] In the evening, we came to a small prairie, opposite the mouth of Quicksand river, where we encamped.\n

\n \n

[Whitehouse]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nMonday March 31st \u00a0\u00a0 A clear pleasant morning, We set out early and proceeded on, & passed a Village which lay on the So. side of the River. \u00a0\u00a0 This Village when we descended the River was large, but the greater part of the houses were removed & lay scattering along the Shore, for the convenience of the Inhabitants fishing. \u00a0\u00a0 One of our hunters killed a deer, & mention'd that he had seen a number of them, Elk &ca \u00a0\u00a0 Several of the natives followed us from the last Indian Village, in small Canoes. \u00a0\u00a0 In the Evening we passed the Mouth of a river lying on the North side of the River & encamped a short distance above it, on a handsome high Priari laying on the North Side of the River & Opposite to Quick sand River.\n\n\n\n\n

\n \n
\n \n \n
\n
1. \n Shah-ha-la is from Chinookan \n \u0161\u00e1x\u0323l(a), \"upriver, above,\" and \n \u0161\u00e1x\u0323latk\u0161, \"those upriver.\" For the people and the village see November 2 and 4, 1805. (back)
\n
2. The later Cascades, in the vicinity of present Bonneville Dam. (back)
\n
3. On this portion of the Columbia the inhabitants were of the Upper Chinook division of the Chinookan linguistic family, living below The Dalles. The Lower Chinooks, consisting of Chinooks and Clatsops, lived near the mouth of the river. There were various dialectal differences within the division. (back)
\n
4. This is the farthest downstream occurrence of burial vaults on the Columbia River. (back)
\n
5. A combination of Government and McGuire islands, Multnomah County, Oregon, and Lady (White Brant) Island, Clark County, Washington. Atlas map 79. See also November 3, 1805. (back)
\n
6. \n Sandy River, in Multnomah County, Oregon. The camp, where the main party would remain until April 6, was in Clark County, Washington, above the entrance of the Washougal (Seal) River in the vicinity of present Washougal. Atlas map 79. (back)
\n
7. The harbor seal, Phoca vitulina richardii.\n (back)
\n
8. The wood duck, Aix sponsa [AOU, 144]; see July 29, 1805. Burroughs, 191\u201392; Holmgren, 29. A vertical line runs through this passage, perhaps Biddle's mark. (back)
\n
9. This word appears to have replaced another, erased word. (back)
\n
10. The Willamette River, reaching the Columbia below Portland, Multnomah County; the \"Multnomah R\" of Atlas map 79. It does not drain nearly as great an area as the captains imagined, nor does any western river. They could not, of course, know of the existence of the Great Basin, the huge area west of the Rockies that has no exterior drainage. The concept of this mighty river would persist among geographers until fully dispelled by John C. Fr\u00e9mont's exploration in the 1840s. See Atlas maps 123, 125. Allen (PG), 334\u201336, 394. (back)
\n
11. A village of the Watlala Indians, Portland, Multnomah County, Oregon. See Clark's\nentries of November 2 and 4, 1805. (back)
\n
12. Perhaps Drouillard; see Lewis's entry of this day. (back)
\n
13. \n Washougal River, Clark County, Washington. (back)
\n
14. In Clark County, Washington, above the entrance of Washougal River (the party's Seal River), near Washougal, where the party remained until April 6. (back)
\n
15. \n Quicksand River is present Sandy River, Multnomah County, Oregon. (back)
\n
16. The captains called the people Shahalas; they were probably Watlalas. The village was \nwithin Portland, Multnomah County, Oregon, probably on the site of the airport. (back)
