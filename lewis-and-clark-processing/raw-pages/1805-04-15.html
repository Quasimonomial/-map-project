\n April 15, 1805\n

\n
\n \n

[Lewis]

\n \n
\n Monday April 15th 1805.\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nSet out at an early hour this morning. I walked on shore, and Capt. Clark continued with the party it being an invariable rule with us not to be both absent from our vessels at the same time. I passed through the bottoms of the river on the Stard. side. \u00a0\u00a0 they were partially covered with timber & were extensive, level and beatifull. \u00a0\u00a0 in my walk which was about 6 miles I passed a small rivulet of clear water making down from the hills, which on tasting, I discovered to be in a small degree brackish. \u00a0\u00a0 it possessed less of the glauber salt, or alumn, than those little streams from the hills usually do.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 in a little pond of water fromed by this rivulet where it entered the bottom, I heard the frogs crying for the first time this season; [1] their note was the same with that of the small frogs which are common to the lagoons and swam[p]s of the U States.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 I saw great quantities of gees feeding in the bottoms, of which I shot one. \u00a0\u00a0 saw some deer and Elk, but they were remarkably shy. I also met with great numbers of Grouse or prarie hens\n [2] as they are called by the English traders of the N. W. \u00a0\u00a0 these birds appeared to be mating; the note of the male is kuck, kuck, kuck, coo, coo, coo. \u00a0\u00a0 the first part of the note both male and female use when flying. \u00a0\u00a0 the male also dubbs [EC: drums] [NB: with his wings] something like the pheasant, but by no means as loud. \u00a0\u00a0 after breakfast Capt. Clark walked on the Std. shore, and on his return in the evening gave me the following account of his ramble. [3] \"I assended to the high country, about 9 miles distant from the Missouri. \u00a0\u00a0 the country consists of beatifull, level and fertile plains, destitute of timber \u00a0\u00a0 I saw many little dranes, which took their rise in the river hills, from whence as far as I could see they run to the N. E.\" \u00a0\u00a0 these streams we suppose to be the waters of Mous river a branch of the Assinniboin which the Indians informed us approaches the Missouri very nearly, about this point. \"I passed,\" continued he, [\"]a Creek about 20 yards wide, [4] which falls into the Missouri; the bottoms of this creek are wide level and extreemly fertile, but almost entirely desitute of timber. \u00a0\u00a0 the water of this creek as well as all those creeks and rivulets which we have passed since we left Fort Mandan was so strongly impregnated with salts and other miniral substances that I was incapable of drinking it. I saw the remains of several camps of the Assinniboins; near one of which, in a small ravene, there was a park which they had formed of timber and brush, for the purpose of taking the Cabrie or Antelope. \u00a0\u00a0 it was constructed in the following manner. \u00a0\u00a0 a strong pound was first made of timbers, on one side of which there was a small apparture, sufficiently large to admit an Antelope; from each side of this apparture, a curtain was extended to a considerable distance, widening as they receded from the pound.\u2014\" \u00a0\u00a0 we passed a rock this evening standing in the middle of the river, and the bed of the river was formed principally of gravel. \u00a0\u00a0 we encamped this evening on a sand point on Lard. side. [5] \u00a0\u00a0 a little above our encampment the river was confined to a channel of 80 yards in width.\u2014

[Clark]

\n \n
15th of April Monday 1805\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nSet out at an early hour, Captn Lewis walked on Shore and Killed a goose, passed a Island in a bend to the L. S. \u00a0\u00a0 the wind hard from the S. E. \u00a0\u00a0 after brackfast I walked on Shore and assended to the high Countrey on the S. S. and off from the Missouri about three miles \u00a0\u00a0 the countrey is butifull open fertile plain the dreans take theer rise near the Clifts of the river and run from the river in a N E derection as far as I could See, this is the part of the River which Mouse river the waters of Lake Winnipec approaches within a fiew miles of \u2329the\u232a Missouri, and I believe those dreans lead into that river. \u00a0\u00a0 we passed a creek about 20 yds. wide on the S. S. \u00a0\u00a0 the bottoms of this Creek is extensive & fertile, the water of this as also, all the Streams which head a fiew miles in the hills discharge water which is black & unfit for use (and can Safely Say that I have not Seen one drop of water fit for use above fort Mandan except Knife and the little Missouris Rivers and the Missouri, the other Streams being So much impregnated with mineral as to be verry disagreeble in its present State.[)] \u00a0\u00a0 I saw the remains of Several Camps of ossinniboins, near one of those camps & at no great distance from the mouth of the aforesid Creek, in a hollow, I saw a large Strong pen made for the purpose of Catching the antelope, with wings projecting from it widining from the pen

\n

\nSaw Several gangs of Buffalow and Some elk at a distance, a black bear [7] Seen from the Perogues to day\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 passed a rock in the Middle of the river, Some Smaller rocks from that to the L. Shore, the dog that came to us yesterday morning continues to follow us, we camped on a Sand point to the L. S.

[Ordway]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Monday 15th April 1805. \u00a0\u00a0 a clear pleasant morning. \u00a0\u00a0 we Set off eairly. \u00a0\u00a0 proceeded on. Sailed under a fine breeze from the S. E. \u00a0\u00a0 passed Several runs on each Side of the river and handsome bottoms plains hills & vallies &.c. \u00a0\u00a0 we Saw flocks of Goats on S. S. and Gangs of buffaloe on Sand beach \nS. S. Saw a large black bair and 2 white ones on the N. S. Capt. Clark was near \nShooting one of the white ones. Sailed on \u00a0\u00a0 one man killed a Goose. \u00a0\u00a0 came 22 miles this day. \u00a0\u00a0 the river Shallow \u00a0\u00a0 only about 8 feet deep in some places. \u00a0\u00a0 we poled across in one place with a Small canoe. \u00a0\u00a0 passd. goat pen creek [8] on N. S. & the river is nearly as wide here as it was at St. Charles near its mouth. Camped on a large Sand beach on the South Side of the river.\u2014\n\n\n

\n \n
\n
\n \n

[Gass]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Monday 15th. \u00a0\u00a0 We had a pleasant day and a fair wind; set forward early as usual, and went on very well. Passed a large creek on the North side, called Goat-pen creek. We saw a number of buffaloe and two bears on the bank of \nthe river. After going 23 miles we encamped on the South side.\n\n

\n \n
\n
\n \n

[Whitehouse]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nMonday April 15th \u00a0\u00a0 We set off this Morning, having a fresh breeze from the N-East \u00a0\u00a0 about 8 o'Clock it veered round to the South East, and blew moderately.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 We proceeded on, and passed a small River, and a Creek; [9] lying both on the North side of the River, and encamped in the Evening, on the bank of the River on the North side.\u2014\n\n\n\n\n\n
