\n June 4, 1804\n

\n
\n \n

[Clark] [1]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n June 4th 1804 Monday, a fair Day \u00a0\u00a0 Sent out 3 hunters, our mast broke by the boat running under a tree \u00a0\u00a0 Passed an Islands on Stbd Side on which grow Seede[r] [2] a Creek at [blank] miles on the Starbd Sd. Course N. 30\u00b0 W 4 ms. to pt. on St. Side below 2d Isd. \u00a0\u00a0 passed a Creek on Lbd Side 15 yd. wide, I call \n Nightingale Creek. \u00a0\u00a0 this Bird Sang all last night and is the first of the kind I ever herd, [3] below this Creek and the last \u00a0\u00a0 Passed a Small Isd on the Stbd. N. 25 W. 3 ms. to a pt. on St. Sd. \u00a0\u00a0 passed a Sm. Isd. on St. Sd. and \n Seeder Creek\n [4] on the Same Side 20 yds wide \u00a0\u00a0 passed a Creek on Lbd Sd. 20 yd wide, I call Mast Creek, [5] this is a Short Creek, fine land above & below the mouth. Jentle rise of about 50 foot, Delightfull Timber of Oake ash walnut hickory &c. &c. \u00a0\u00a0 wind from N W. by W. N. 58\u00b0 W. 7 \u00bd ms. passed a Creek Called \n Zoncar\n \n [6] on the Lbd Side, N 75 W 3 me. to a pt. S. Sd. called Batue a De charm,\n [7] a plain on the hill opposit. I got out & walked on the L Sd. thro a Charming Bottom of rich Land about one mile \u00a0\u00a0 then I assended a hill of about 170 foot on the top of which is a Moun [8] and about 100 acres of Land of Dead timber on this hill \u00a0\u00a0 one of the party says he has found Lead ore [9] a verry extensive Cave under this hill next the river, the Land on the top is fine, This is a very bad part of the river \u00a0\u00a0 Seven Deer Killed to day by our hunters\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 one of the horses is Snaged, the other lost his Shous to day \u00a0\u00a0 the Bottom on the St. Side to day is covered with rushes, not verry good [one word illegible] \u00a0\u00a0 the high land Comes to the bank on the Labd Side and good 2d rate land.\n

[Clark]

\n \n
\n June 4th Monday 1804
\u00a0
\n \n

\na fair day \u00a0\u00a0 three men out on the right flank \u00a0\u00a0 passed a large Island on the St. Side Called Seeder Island, this Isd. has a great Deel of Ceedar on it, passed a Small Creek at 1 ms. [NB: 1 mile] 15 yd. Wide which we named \n Nightingale Creek\n from a Bird of that discription which Sang for us all last night, and is the first of the Kind I ever heard. \u00a0\u00a0 passed the mouth of Seeder Creek at 7 ms. on the S. S. abt. 20 yds. Wide above Some Small Isds. \u00a0\u00a0 passed a Creek on the L. S. abt. 15 yds. wide. \n Mast [NB: Mast] Creek,\n here the Sergt. at the helm run under a bending Tree & broke the mast, Some delightfull Land, with a jentle assent about the Creek, well timbered, Oake, Ash, walnut &c. &c. passed, wind N W. by W. \u00a0\u00a0 passed a Small Creek Called Zan Cau C on the L. S: at this last point I got out and walked on the L. Sd. thro a rush [NB: rush] bottom for 1 Miles & a Short Distance thro: Nettles [10] as high as my brest \u00a0\u00a0 assended a hill of about 170 foot to a place where the french report that Lead ore has been found, I saw no mineral of that description, Capt Lewis Camped imediately under this hill, [11] to wate which gave me Some time to examine the hill, on the top is a moun of about 6 foot high and about 100 Acres of land which the large timber is Dead \u00a0\u00a0 in Decending about 50 foot a projecting lime Stone rock under which is a Cave \u00a0\u00a0 at one place in this projecting rocks I went on one which Spured up and hung over the Water \u00a0\u00a0 from the top of this rock I had a prospect of the river for 20 or 30 ms. up, from the Cave which incumposed [encompassed] the hill I decended by a Steep decent to the foot, a verry bad part of the river opposit this hill, the river Continu to fall Slowly, our hunters killed 7 Deer to day \u00a0\u00a0 The land our hunters passed thro: to day on the S. S. was Verry fine the latter part of to day. \u00a0\u00a0 the highland on the S. S: is about 2d rate

[Ordway]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nMonday June 4th 1804. \u00a0\u00a0 a fair day \u00a0\u00a0 3 hunters went out. \u00a0\u00a0 our mast broke by my Stearing the Boat \u2329alon\u232a near the Shore \u00a0\u00a0 the Rope or Stay to the mast got fast in a limb of a Secamore tree & it broke verry Easy. \u00a0\u00a0 passed a Creek on the South Side about 15 yds wide which we name nightingale Creek, this Bird Sung all last night & is the first we heard below on the River \u00a0\u00a0 we passed Seeder Creek on S Side \u00a0\u00a0 line fine land above & below the Creek. Rising land, Delightfull Timber of oak ash, Black walnut hickery &C &C\u2014 [12] \u00a0\u00a0 passed a Creek called on car [13] on the S Side \u00a0\u00a0 \u23293 miles to\u232a \u00a0\u00a0 we encamped on S Side of the River at the Lead mines \u00a0\u00a0 our hunters killed 8 Deers \u00a0\u00a0 it was Jerked this evening &C

[Floyd]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nmonday June 4th \u00a0\u00a0 Set out \u00a0\u00a0 the Clear morning \u00a0\u00a0 2 miles Byouer Sters man [14] Let the Boat Run under a lim and Broke our mast off \u00a0\u00a0 3 miles past a Creek on the South Side Called mast creek \u2329on the S Side \u00a0\u00a0 no name for it but I cal it Rich Land Creek at\u232a \u00a0\u00a0 a Butifull a peas of Land as ever I saw \u00a0\u00a0 walnut shoger tree ash and mulber trees [15] \u00a0\u00a0 Level land on both sides. \u00a0\u00a0 this Creek is Clear watter about 30 yardes wide \u00a0\u00a0 one mile past a River on the N. Side called Sidder River \u00a0\u00a0 the Land is Level and good \u00a0\u00a0 4 miles past Creek Called Zon Cer on the S. Sid at the loer pint of Isld. on the same \u00a0\u00a0 3 miles to a pint on the N Sd Called Batue De charr [16] \u00a0\u00a0 a prarie on the S Sid high \u00a0\u00a0 Cliftes on the South Side \u00a0\u00a0 ouer hunters Kild & Deer \u00a0\u00a0 Strong water \u00a0\u00a0 came 10 miles \u00a0\u00a0 encamt on the South Side under the Cliftes \u00a0\u00a0 \u2329Set out\u232a\n\n\n

\n \n

[Gass]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Monday 4th. \u00a0\u00a0 Three hunters went out this morning. We continued our voyage, and during the day broke our mast by steering too close to the shore. In the evening we encamped on the south side, near lead mines; when our hunters came in with seven deer.\n\n

[Whitehouse]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nMonday 4th June 1804. \u00a0\u00a0 a fair morning. \u00a0\u00a0 we branded Several trees &c. [17] \u00a0\u00a0 in the afternoon we Set out and proceeded about 4 miles and Camped at the [mouth] of a creek on S. Side.

\n

\nMonday June 4th \u00a0\u00a0 We started early this morning; Fair weather, Captains Lewis & Clark had several Trees branded, with their Names and proceeded on about 4 Miles, & encamped at the Mouth of a Creek laying on the South side of the River, The Name of this Creek was unknown to any of our party.\u2014\n\n\n\n\n

1. Under the lowest portion of this entry on this sheet of the Field Notes (reverse of document 17) is a sketch map of the area between the Osage River and Little Tavern Creek (see fig. 10). In some instances it is difficult to distinguish between map and text. See Wood. (back)
\n
2. The island retained the name Cedar Island; it lay nearly opposite Jefferson City at present Cedar City. The \"seeder\" is eastern red cedar. Steyermark, 45; MRC map 6. (back)
\n
3. Either Wears Creek or Coon Creek, both at Jefferson City. MRC map 7; MRM map 17. Coon Creek is no more; Wears Creek and Coon Creek are now one and the same, called Wears Creek, at Jefferson City. Clark's \"\n Nightingale Creek\n \" may be an unnamed stream one mile west of Moreau River, between that stream and Boggs Creek. There is no true nightingale (Luscinia megarhynchos) in America. The cardinal, Cardinalis cardinalis [AOU, 593], was sometimes called the Virginia nightingale, but this bird would have been familiar to the captains. The same objection applies to the mockingbird, Mimus polyglottos [AOU, 703], which has also been suggested. Paul Johnsgard (personal communication) suggests that it might be the whip-poor-will, Caprimulgus vociferus [AOU, 417]. But in the weather remarks on June 11, the \"whiperwill\" is named. Perhaps Lewis recognized the bird and made the weather observation, while Clark was unfamiliar with the species and used the term nightingale; or perhaps nightingale was a common name at the time for the whip-poor-will. One final possibility is the hermit thrush, Catharus guttatus [AOU, 759]. Coues (HLC), 1:14 n. 27; Cutright (LCPN), 55 n. 13; Holmgren, 32. For a discussion of the creeks in question and the possible identification of Clark's \"nightingale,\" see James Wallace, \"The Mystery of Clark's Nightingale,\" We Proceeded On 26 (May 2000): 18\u201325. (back)
\n
4. \n Cedar Creek does not flow into Turkey Creek; they are separate streams. Turkey Creek reaches the Missouri at Cedar City, Callaway County, Missouri. See fig. 14. MRC map 7; MRM map 17. (back)
\n
5. Evidently later Grays Creek, in Cole County. See fig. 14. MRC map 7. (back)
\n
6. Perhaps later Workman Creek, in Cole County, Missouri. MRC map 7. (back)
\n
7. A bature is a sandy beach built up inside a curve in a river. There may be some connection with Jean Marie Ducharme of Cahokia, who traded with the Little Osages and Missouris in 1772\u201373. On the other hand, charme was a French word for the hornbeam or yoke elm, Carpinus caroliniana Walt. Osgood (FN), 50 n. 4; McDermott (WCS), 148; McDermott (GMVF), 21; Steyermark, 524\u201326. (back)
\n
8. Probably the same group of mounds noted on June 2. (back)
\n
9. This area is within the central Missouri lead-zinc region, and galena (lead ore) has been reported not only from the Jefferson City (Ordovician) limestone and all lower formations, but even from some of the coal beds of the area. Hinds, 158\u201359. (back)
\n
10. \n Urtica dioica. L. var. procera (Muhl.) Wedd., tall nettle. Steyermark, 567. (back)
\n
11. In northwestern Cole County, in the vicinity of Sugar Loaf Rock, which is Clark's \"Mine Hill\" of the second entry's course and distance table and between modern Workman Creek and Meadows Creek. Coues (HLC), 1:14 and n. 30; MRC map 7. (back)
\n
12. Only Ordway notes that the walnut is black walnut, Juglans nigra L., otherwise the trees cannot be identified specifically, oak is Quercus sp., ash, Fraxinus sp., and hickory, Carya sp. (back)
\n
13. \n Clark's \"Zoncar,\" Charles Floyd's \"Zon cer.\" Perhaps Meadow Creek, Cole County. (back)
\n
14. Perhaps Cruzatte or Labiche, probably the former, although Clark calls the man a\nsergeant. (back)
\n
15. \n Floyd's list of trees varies somewhat from the tree lists of Clark and Ordway. His trees\nare probably black walnut, the sugar tree may be either sugar maple, Acer saccharum Marsh.,\nor silver maple, A. saccaharinum L., an unknown ash, and probably red mulberry. (back)
\n
16. See the rather complicated discussion of this term at Clark's entry for the day. (back)
\n
17. The only one who mentions this act, Whitehouse gives no reason for the branding. It may have been done with Lewis's branding iron; see Lewis's entry for June 10, 1805. (back)
