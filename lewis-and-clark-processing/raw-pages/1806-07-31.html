\n July 31, 1806\n

\n
\n \n

[Lewis]

\n \n
\n Thursday July 31st 1806.\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nThe rain still continuing I set out early and proceeded on as fast as possible. \u00a0\u00a0 at 9 A. M. we fell in with a large herd of Elk of which we killed 15 and took their skins. \u00a0\u00a0 the bottoms in the latter part of the day became wider better timbered and abound in game. \u00a0\u00a0 the party killed 14 deer in the course of the day without attempting to hunt but little for them. \u00a0\u00a0 we also killed 2 bighorns and 1 beaver; saw but few buffaloe. \u00a0\u00a0 the river is still rising and excessively muddy more so I think than I ever saw it. \u00a0\u00a0 we experienced some very heavy showers of rain today. \u00a0\u00a0 we have been passing high pine hills all day. \u00a0\u00a0 late in the evening we came too on the N. E. side of the river and took sheter in some indian lodges built of sticks, about 8 ms. below the entrance of North mountain creek. [1] \u00a0\u00a0 these lodges appeared to have been built in the course of the last winter. \u00a0\u00a0 these lodges with the addition of some Elk skins afforded us a good shelter from the rain which continued to fall powerfully all night. I think it probable that the minnetares of Fort de Prarie visit this part of the river; we meet with their old lodges in every bottom.\u2014\n

[Clark]

\n \n
\n Saturday 31st of July 1806\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nI was much disturbed last night by the noise of the buffalow which were about me. \u00a0\u00a0 one gang Swam the river near our Camp which alarmed me a little for fear of their Crossing our Canoes and Splitting them to pieces. Set out as usial about Sun rise \u00a0\u00a0 passed a rapid which I call wolf rapid\n [3] from the Circumstance of one of those animals being at the rapid. here the river approaches the high mountanious Country on the \u00a0\u00a0 N W. Side. [4] \u00a0\u00a0 those hills appear to be composed of various Coloured earth and Coal without much rock [5] \u00a0\u00a0 I observe Several Conical pounds [NB: mounds] which appear to have been burnt. [6] \u00a0\u00a0 this high Country is washed into Curious formed mounds & hills and is cut much with reveens. \u00a0\u00a0 the Country again opens and at the distance of 23 miles below the Redston or \n War-har-sah\n \n [7] River I landed in the enterance of a Small river [8] on the Stard. Side 40 yards wid Shallow and muddy. \u00a0\u00a0 it has lately been very high. \u00a0\u00a0 haveing passed the Enterance of a River on the Lard Side 100 yards wide which has running water. [9] \u00a0\u00a0 this river I take to be the one the Menetarries Call little wolf or Sa-a-shah [NB: Shah] River [10] \u00a0\u00a0 The high Country is entirely bar of timber. \u00a0\u00a0 great quantities of Coal or carbonated wood is to be seen in every Bluff and in the high hills at a distance on each Side. [11] Saw more Buffalow and Elk and antilopes this evening than usial. \u00a0\u00a0 18 Miles below the last river on the Stard. Side, I passed one 60 yards wide which had running water. \u00a0\u00a0 this Stream I call \n oak-tar-pon-er or Coal \u2329R\u232a River\n [12] has very steep banks on each side of it. \u00a0\u00a0 passed Several large Brooks [13] Some of them had a little running water, also Several Islands \u00a0\u00a0 Some high black looking Bluffs and encamped on the Stard. Side on a low point. [14] \u00a0\u00a0 the country like that of yesterday is open extencive plains. \u00a0\u00a0 as I was about landing this evening Saw a white bear and the largest I ever Saw eating a dead buffalow on a Sand bar. \u00a0\u00a0 we fired two Shot into him, we Swam to the main Shore and walked down the bank. I landed and fired 2 more Shot into this tremendious animal without killing him. \u00a0\u00a0 night comeing on we Could not pursue him he bled profusely. Showers all this day

[Ordway]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nThursday 31st July 1806. \u00a0\u00a0 cloudy and rain. \u00a0\u00a0 we Set out as usal and procd. on verry well \u00a0\u00a0 at 9 Saw large gangs of Elk Swimming the River \u00a0\u00a0 we killed 15 of them mearly for the hides to cover our Canoes. Jo. Fields killed one Ibex \u00a0\u00a0 the hunters killed 14 deer and one beaver this day. \u00a0\u00a0 had Several Showers of rain. \u00a0\u00a0 the River verry muddy owing to the heavy rains washing those Clayey hills \u00a0\u00a0 came a long days roeing and Camped [26] at some old Indn. lodges on N. Side.\u2014\n\n\n

[Gass]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Thursday 31st. \u00a0\u00a0 We set out early, though it continued at intervals to rain hard. About 10 o'clock we saw a great gang of elk on a small island, where we halted and in a short time killed fifteen of them. We took the skins and the best parts of the meat, and proceeded. At noon we halted, and in a short time killed fifteen of them. We took the skins and the best parts of the meat, and proceeded. At noon we halted to dine, and had then a very heavy shower of rain. We also killed another of the large horned animals or mountain sheep.\u2014 We remained here about an hour, then proceeded on, and will soon be clear of this range of high rough country. In our way this afternoon, we killed two mule and twelve other deer, and two beaver. Though the afternoon was wet and disagreeable, we came 70 miles to day. [27]\n

1. \n Rock Creek, in Phillips County, Montana; see May 24, 1805. The course of the Missouri has changed considerably in this vicinity, so the mileage below the creek mouth may not now apply. The camp was in either Fergus or Phillips County. Atlas map 39; MRC map 70. (back)
\n
2. The words \"\n Coal R\n \" may have been added to a blank space; it is O'Fallon Creek (see later note in this entry). (back)
\n
3. \"Wolf rapid\" is in Prarie County, Montana, near the mouth of Conns Coulee, some four miles southwest of present Terry. Atlas map 121. (back)
\n
4. Including Little Sheep and Big Sheep mountains and other high country in Prairie County. Atlas map 121. (back)
\n
5. The Tullock, Lebo, and Tongue River members of the Fort Union Formation all are exposed in vertical succession in the hills northwest of the river. The sediments are principally sandstone, mudstone, claystone, siltstone, and shale with coal beds. Most of these rocks are soft and easily eroded except for occasional lenses of indurated sandstone. (back)
\n
6. The conical mounds form where resistant sandstone caps less resistant materials, protecting them from erosion. (back)
\n
7. \n Clark apparently added this word later to a blank space. The stream is Powder River; see notes at July 30. (back)
\n
8. \n O'Fallon Creek enters the Yellowstone River about a mile west of present Fallon in Prairie County. Both creek and town bear the name of Benjamin O'Fallon, trader, Indian agent, and nephew of William Clark. The creek was apparently named by one of O'Fallon's later friends or associates in the fur trade, since neither Clark's journals nor Atlas map 121 give it that name. On the Atlas map it is \"Oak Tar pon er River\" and \"Coal River\"; the latter is the name used in both sets of courses and distances, but not in the journal text. \"Poner\" may be the Mandan term, pas\u00e1\u014bh, \"creek,\" but the meaning of the first portion of the word is unknown (see \n Fort Mandan Miscellany). Wood & Moulton, 374\u201375. (back)
\n
9. \n Cherry Creek enters the Yellowstone from the north in Prairie County a little below Terry. On Atlas map 121 it is \"Shabonas River\" after Toussaint Charbonneau. (back)
\n
10. This cannot be the \"Little Wolf mountain Creek\" of the \n Fort Mandan Miscellany of this edition, although the name, as Clark indicates, would have been learned from the Hidatsas at Fort Mandan. Clark may have added the name to a blank space. The term is Hidatsa, cee\u0161a, \"wolf.\" (back)
\n
11. The coal is in the Ludlow Member of the Fort Union Formation near the river and in the Tongue River Member at a short distance from the river. (back)
\n
12. Not the stream that bears those names in the courses and distances or on Atlas map 121; this is Cabin Creek in Prarie County, \"Gibsons deep river\" on the Atlas map, after George Gibson of the party. The words \"Oak-tar-pon-er or Coal \u2329R\u232a\" may have been added later to a blank space and partly interlined. (back)
\n
13. Including Cherry, Cabin, and Cedar creeks. Atlas map 121. (back)
\n
14. The camp was in Dawson County, Montana, some seven miles southwest of present Glendive. Atlas map 121. The high, black-looking bluffs most likely are composed of the Pierre Shale near the center of the Cedar Creek anticline about ten miles southwest of Glendive. (back)
\n
15. This is hard to identify unless Clark means \"larboard,\" as he has it in the Voorhis version, in which case it could be Lost Boy Creek, which joins the Yellowstone from the northwest in Prairie County, some five miles west of Terry; on Atlas map 121 a nameless stream is shown among the \"High broken Hills\" below Wolf Rapid. (back)
\n
16. The Tullock, Lebo, and Tongue River members of the Fort Union Formation are exposed in vertical succession on this hill at the mouth of Lost Boy Creek. Each member has slightly different coloration and composition. (back)
\n
17. \"Shabono R\" may have been added to a blank space. (back)
\n
18. \n Ludlow Member of the Fort Union Formation at the mouth of O'Fallon Creek. (back)
\n
19. In the courses and distances and on Atlas map 121 \"Coal River\" and \"Oak Tar pon er\" are O'Fallon Creek; in the journal narrative (see earlier note in this entry) they are Cabin Creek. (back)
\n
20. Perhaps Hatchet Creek in Prairie County; \"Dry Brook\" on Atlas map 121. (back)
\n
21. The most prominent stream on this course is Bad Route Creek on the larboard side, in Prairie County. However, a \"Dry Brook\" appears on the starboard side on Atlas map 121. (back)
\n
22. Evidently Cracker Box Creek in Dawson County; the island appears on Atlas map 121, but not the stream. (back)
\n
23. \n Cabin Creek (see earlier note in this entry), the \"Oak-tar-pon-er or Coal River\" of the journal narrative. (back)
\n
24. \n Cedar Creek in Dawson County, \"Cat fish Creek\" on Atlas map 121. (back)
\n
25. \n Whoopup Creek in Dawson County, a nameless stream opposite the camp of July 31 on Atlas map 121. (back)
\n
26. Eight miles below the mouth of Rock Creek, Phillips County, Montana, according to\nLewis. (back)
\n
27. They camped at some old Indian lodges eight miles below the mouth of Rock Creek, \nPhillips County, Montana, by Lewis's account. (back)
