\n June 10, 1804\n

\n
\n \n

[Clark] [1]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n June 10th Sunday 1804 \u00a0\u00a0 Some rain last night \u00a0\u00a0 we set out early \u00a0\u00a0 Saw a number of Goslings this morning, Continued on the Course of last night, thence N. 8 E. 2\u00bd ms. to a pt. on the L. S. \u00a0\u00a0 passed a part of the River that the banks are falling in takeing with them large trees of Cotton woods [2] which is the Common groth in the Bottoms Subject to the flud \u00a0\u00a0 North 1 Me along the L. Side \u2329N. 23\u00b0 W to the Mo: of Chareton\u232a N. 40\u00b0 W. 1 ms. along the L, S. opposit the two Charletons [Charitons], [3] on the N. Side, those rivers mouth together, the 1st 40 yds. wide the next 90 yds. Wide and navagable Some distance in the Countrey, the land below is high & not verry good. Came to and took Mdnl. altd. of Sons U. L. back obsvn. with the octant \u00a0\u00a0 Made it 37\u00b0 12' 00\", delayed 1\u00bd Hour. N. 70\u00b0 W \u00bd of a me. along the L. Sd.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 S 60\u00b0 W \u00bd m. on L. S. the Same Course to the Pt. S. S. 1\u00bd Ms. We halted and Capt Lewis Killed a Buck \u00a0\u00a0 the Current is excessively Swift about this place N. 80\u00b0 W. 3 ms to [hole] a pt. on S. S. passed a Isd. Called Sheeco Islan\n [4] \u00a0\u00a0 wind from the N W \u00a0\u00a0 Camped in a Prarie on the L. S., [5]\n Capt Lewis & my Self Walked out 3 ms. \u00a0\u00a0 found the Country roleing open & rich, with plenty of water, great qts [quantities] of Deer \u00a0\u00a0 I discovered a Plumb\n [6] which grows on bushes the hight of Hasle [hazel], those plumbs are in great numbers, the bushes beare Verry full, about double the Sise of the wild plumb Called the Osage Plumb & am told they are finely flavoured.\n

\n 10th of June 1804
\u00a0
\n \n

\nA hard rain last night, we Set out this morning verry early \u00a0\u00a0 passed Some bad placies in the river \u00a0\u00a0 Saw a number of Goslings morning \u00a0\u00a0 pass near a Bank which was falling in at the time we passed, passed the two River of \n Charletons\n which mouth together, above Some high land which has a great quantity of Stone Calculated for whetstons [7] \u00a0\u00a0 the first of those rivers is about 30 yds. Wide & the other is 70 yds wd. and heads Close to the R: \u2329Dumoin\u232a [NB: du Moines] [8] The \u2329Aieways\u232a [NB: Ayauways] \u2329Nation\u232a have a Village on the head of these River \u00a0\u00a0 they run through \u2329an even Countrey\u232a [NB: a broken rich thickly timbered country] and is navagable for Perogues \u00a0\u00a0 Cap Lewis took Medn. altd. of \u2609 U. L with Octant, back obsvn. made it 37\u00b0 12' 00\"\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 delayd 1\u00bd hours.

\n

\n Capt. Lewis Killed a large Buck, passed a large Isd. called Shecco and Camped in a Prarie on the L. S. I walked out three miles, found the prarie composed of good Land and plenty of water roleing & interspursed with points of timbered land, Those Praries are not \u2329open\u232a like those, or a number of those E. of the Mississippi Void of every thing except grass, they abound with Hasel Grapes [9] & a wild plumb of a Superior [NB: size &] quallity, called the Osages Plumb \u00a0\u00a0 Grows on a bush the hight of a Hasel \u2329(and is three times the sise of other Plumbs,\u232a [10] and hang in great quantities on the bushes \u00a0\u00a0 I Saw great numbers of Deer in the Praries, the evening is Cloudy, our party in high Spirits.

[Ordway]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n\nSunday June 10th 1804. \u00a0\u00a0 we Set eairly after Some rain, a fair day, we passed hilley land on Right hand of the River. On the left the land is level \u00a0\u00a0 plenty of Mulbery Trees. [12] \u00a0\u00a0 the mulberys Ripe on them. The timber is walnut hickery ash &c. Grape vines Run over the trees &C.\u2014 [13] \u00a0\u00a0 at 10 oC we passed Deer Creek [14] on the Right \u00a0\u00a0 we passed the 2 Charlitons River on the North Side, those Rivers are navigable Some Distance up & the Mouths are near together \u00a0\u00a0 Capt Lewis killed a \u2329Deer\u232a Buck little above \u00a0\u00a0 We Camped on South Side of the River \u00a0\u00a0 Capts went hunting, Several men with them. Drewyer killed a Deer. \u00a0\u00a0 the land is excelent, large praries along the South Side of the River. \u00a0\u00a0 the Timber on the bank is white walnut hickery Some Cotton wood &C\u2014\n\n\n

[Gass]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Sunday 10th. \u00a0\u00a0 We proceeded five miles and passed a creek, called Deerlick creek on the north side; and three miles further the Two Charlottes on the same side. The mouths of these two rivers are very near each other; the first 70 and the other 100 yards wide. We encamped on the south side of the river at a prairie, and remained there the whole of the next day, the wind blowing too violent for us to proceed.\n\n\n

[Whitehouse]

\nSunday 10 \u00a0\u00a0 We got to the Charrotte River at 2 Oclock \u00a0\u00a0 Waited the Arrival \nof the hunters there \u2329in\u232a \u00a0\u00a0 Sufferd. by the Musquitoes On the N. E Side \u00a0\u00a0 \nThe Bigg Charrottoe is 100 Yds. at the mouth. \u00a0\u00a0 The little comees in to it at the Distance of 300 Yds. apart its Brenth at the mouth is 50 yds. Broad \u00a0\u00a0 Swem the horses and ferried the men across \u00a0\u00a0 had hard watter \u00a0\u00a0 Campd. On the Charrotte prarie \u00a0\u00a0 Roed 13 Miles\u2014

\n

\nSunday June 10th \u00a0\u00a0 We embark'd early this morning and proceeded on. \u00a0\u00a0 at 2 o'Clock we arrived at the River Charotto lying on the North side of the River Mesouri, where we came too; and waited for the arrival of our Hunters, We encamped on the North East side of the Island for a while, but found the Musqitoes so troublesome that we had to embark again. \u00a0\u00a0 The big Charotto River is 100 Yards wide at its mouth. \u00a0\u00a0 The little Charotto River empties itself into it, at about 300 Yards distance above it and is 50 Yards wide at its mouth. \u00a0\u00a0 We had great difficulty in swimming the horses, which the hunters had with them (which was brought by them from Saint Charles) to the Island & getting them on board, we sent the Pettiauger for the hunters, who came on board the boat, we proceeded on and went as far as the Charoto Priari, where we encamp'd. \u00a0\u00a0 The wind blowing hard, distance come this day 13 Miles.\u2014\n\t\n

1. \n Biddle's notation on the top of this sheet of the Field Notes (document 20), reads \"June 10 to 14.\" A column of figures is also at the top of this sheet: 2\u00bd, 1, 2, 1, 1, 1, 2 [\u00bd], and a total of 12. (back)
\n
2. \n Populus deltoides Marsh. var. deltoides, cottonwood. Steyermark, 507. (back)
\n
3. The Little Chariton River meets the Chariton a little above the mouth of the latter in Chariton County, Missouri, a short distance upstream from the present town of Glasgow. The name apparently derives from that of Jean Chariton, an early trader. See fig. 14. Stewart (APN), 87; MRC map 10. (back)
\n
4. \n Chicot Island, later Harrison Island. Coues (HLC), 1:20 n. 45; Thwaites (LC), 1:45 n. 2; MRC map 10. (back)
\n
5. In northeast Saline County, Missouri, some five miles above the Chariton River by Clark's estimate. MRC map 10. (back)
\n
6. Possibly Prunus hortulana Bailey, wild goose plum, hortulan plum, or P. munsoniana Wight & Hedrick, wild goose plum, which have the appropriate description and are known to occur in the area. Steyermark, 860\u201361. However, the captains may have been thinking of the Chickasaw (Osage) plum, which they had seen in St. Louis. See weather remarks for April 10, 1804. The hazel, mentioned for comparison, is Corylus americana Walt., hazelnut. Steyermark, 524. (back)
\n
7. Natural whetstones are produced from very fine-grained sandstones that are extremely uniform in texture, firmly cemented, and medium bedded (beds of one to six inches thick). Beds of this kind are known from lower Pennsylvanian rocks, which crop out along the bluffs in this area. (back)
\n
8. The source of the Chariton River, in south-central Iowa, is near the South and Middle branches of the Des Moines River. The Indians referred to are the Iowas (see above, April 4, 1804). Petersen, 305\u201311. Biddle is apparently crossing out words in this paragraph to substitute his own. (back)
\n
9. \n Vitis sp. (back)
\n
10. \n Biddle apparently crossed out this passage. (back)
\n
11. \n Lewis's observation from Codex O. (back)
\n
12. The mulberry is probably red mulberry, Morus rubra. (back)
\n
13. \n Ordway is the only one to mention the vegetation this day, other than Clark, who notes the discovery of a plum. (back)
\n
14. Perhaps Hurricane Creek, Howard County, Missouri; not mentioned by Clark but noted by other enlisted men. (back)
