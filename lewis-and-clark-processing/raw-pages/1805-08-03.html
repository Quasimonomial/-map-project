\n August 3, 1805\n

\n
\n \n

[Lewis]

\n \n
\n August the 3rd 1805.\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nSet out this morning at sunrise and continued our rout through the valley on the Lard. side of the river. \u00a0\u00a0 at eleven A. M. Drewyer killed a doe and we halted and took breakfast. \u00a0\u00a0 the mountains continue high on either side of the valley, and are but skantily supplyed with timber; small pine [1] appears to be the prevalent growth. \u00a0\u00a0 there is no timber in the valley except a small quantity of the narrow leafed cottonwood on the verge of the river. \u00a0\u00a0 the underwood consists of the narrowleafed or small willow, honeysuckle rosebushes, courant, goosbury [2] and service bury bushes allso a small quantity of a species of dwarf burch [3] the leaf of which, oval, deep green, finely indented and very small. \u00a0\u00a0 we encamped this evening after sunset having traveled by estimate 23 miles. [4] \u00a0\u00a0 from the width and appearance of the valley at this place I concieved that the river forked not far above me and therefore resolved the next morning to examine the adjacent country more minutely.

\n \n

[Lewis]

\n \n
\n Saturday August 3rd 1805.\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nSet out early this morning, or before sunrise; still continued our march through the level valley on the lard. side of the river. \u00a0\u00a0 the valley much as yesterday only reather wider; I think it 12 Miles wide, tho' the plains near the mountains rise higher and are more broken with some scattering pine near the mountain. \u00a0\u00a0 in the leaveler parts of the plain and river bottoms which are very extensive there is no timber except a scant proportion of cottonwood near the river. \u00a0\u00a0 the under wood consists of the narrow leafed or small willow, the small honeysuckle, rosebushes, currant, serviceberry, and goosbery bushes; also a small species of berch in but small quantities \u2329of a species\u232a the leaf which is oval finely, indented, small and of a deep green colour. [5] \u00a0\u00a0 the stem is simple ascending and branching, and seldom rises higher than 10 or 12 feet. \u00a0\u00a0 the Mountains continue high on either side of the valley, and are but scantily supplyed with timber; small pine apears to be the prevalent growth; it is of the pich kind, with a short leaf. \u00a0\u00a0 at 11 A. M. Drewyer killed a doe and we halted about 2 hours and breakfasted, and then continued our rout untill night without halting, when we arrived at the river in a level bottom which appeared to spread to greater extent than usual. \u00a0\u00a0 from the appearance of the timber I supposed that the river forked above us and resolved to examine this part of the river minutely tomorrow. \u00a0\u00a0 this evening we passed through a high plain for about 8 miles covered with prickley pears and bearded grass, tho' we found this even better walking than the wide bottoms of the river, which we passed in the evening; these altho' apparently level, from some cause which I know not, were formed into meriads of deep holes as if rooted up by hogs \u00a0\u00a0 these the grass covered so thick that it was impossible to walk without the risk of falling down at every step. some parts of these bottoms also possess excellent terf or peat, I beleive of many feet deep. \u00a0\u00a0 the mineral salts also frequently mentioned on the Missouri we saw this evening in these uneven bottoms. [6] \u00a0\u00a0 we saw many deer, Antelopes ducks, gees, some beaver and great appearance of their work. also a small bird and the Curlooe as usual. [7] \u00a0\u00a0 we encamped on the river bank on Lard. side having traveled by estimate 23 Miles. The fish of this part of the river are trout and a species of scale fish of a while [white] colour and a remarkable small long mouth which one of our men inform us are the same with the species called in the Eastern states bottlenose.\n [8] the snowey region of the mountains and for some distance below has no timber or herbage of any kind; the timber is confined to the lower and middle regions. Capt. Clark set out this morning as usual. \u00a0\u00a0 he walked on shore a small distance this morning and killed a deer. \u00a0\u00a0 in the course of his walk he saw a track which he supposed to be that of an Indian from the circumstance of the large toes turning inward. \u00a0\u00a0 he pursued the track and found that the person had ascended a point of a hill from which his camp of the last evening was visible; this circumstance also confirmed the beleif of it's being an Indian who had thus discovered them and ran off. they found the river as usual much crouded with islands, the currant more rapid & much more shallow than usual. \u00a0\u00a0 in many places they were obliged to double man the canoes and drag them over the stone and gravel. \u00a0\u00a0 this morning they passed a small creek on Stard. at the entrance of which Reubin Fields killed a large Panther. \u00a0\u00a0 we called the creek after that animal Panther Creek. [9] \u00a0\u00a0 they also passed a handsome litle stream on Lard. which is form of several large springs which rise in the bottoms and along the base of the mountains with some little rivulets from the melting snows. \u00a0\u00a0 the beaver have formed many large dams on this stream. they saw some deer Antelopes and the common birds of the country. \u00a0\u00a0 in the evening they passed a very bad rappid where the bed of the river is formed entrely of solid rock and encamped on an island just above. [10] the Panther which Fields killed measured seven and \u00bd feet from the nose to the extremity of the tail. \u00a0\u00a0 it is precisely the same animal common to the western part of our country. \u00a0\u00a0 the men wer compelled to be a great proportion of their time in the water today; they have had a severe days labour and are much fortiegued.\u2014

[Clark]

\n \n
\n August 3rd Saturday 1805\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\t\t\na fine morning wind from the N E \u00a0\u00a0 I walked on Shore & killed a Deer \u00a0\u00a0 in my walk I saw a fresh track which I took to be an Indian from the Shape of the foot as the toes turned in, I think it probable that this Indian Spied our fires and Came to a Situation to view us from the top of a Small knob on the Lard Side. \u00a0\u00a0 the river more rapid and Sholey than yesterday one R. F. man killed a large Panthor on the Shore \u00a0\u00a0 we are oblige to haul over the Canoes Sholey in maney places where the Islands are noumerous and bottom Sholey, in the evening the river more rapid and Sholey we encamped on an Island avove a part of the river which passed thro a rockey bed enclosed on both sides with thick willow current & red buries &c &c [11] \u00a0\u00a0 passed a bold Stream which heads in the mountains to our right and the drean of the minting Snow in the Montn. on that side ar in View\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 at 4 oClock passed a bold Stream which falls from a mountn in three Channels to our left, the Greater portion of the Snow on this mountain is melted, but little remaining near us \u00a0\u00a0 Some Deer Elk & antelopes & Bear in the bottoms. \u00a0\u00a0 but fiew trees and they Small \u00a0\u00a0 the Mountains on our left Contain pine those on our right but verry partially Supplied and what pine & cedar it has is on the Lower region, no wood being near the Snow. \u00a0\u00a0 great numbers of Beaver Otter &c. Some fish trout & and bottle nose. Birds as usial. Geese young Duks & Curlows\n

[Ordway]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nSaturday 3rd August 1805. \u00a0\u00a0 a clear morning. \u00a0\u00a0 we Set out as usal and proceeded on. Capt. Clark walked on Shore a Short time and killed a Deer. \u00a0\u00a0 the River verry crooked and filled with Islands. \u00a0\u00a0 we proceeded on. Saw 2 deer on the edge of the River \u00a0\u00a0 one of the hunters R. Fields went after them and killed a panther on an Island. \u00a0\u00a0 it was 7\u00bd feet in length. \u00a0\u00a0 it differs from those in the States. \u00a0\u00a0 it is of a redish brown, and the first we have killed. \u00a0\u00a0 passed verry rapid water \u00a0\u00a0 we have to double man the canoes and drag them over the Sholes and rapid places. \u00a0\u00a0 we have to be in the water half of our time. \u00a0\u00a0 passed level praries on each Side. \u00a0\u00a0 the bottoms has been burned over about 6 months past by the natives, as appears. \u00a0\u00a0 passed a large Spring on the Lard. Side at a low bottom of willows and high grass. The beaver has made a dam at the mouth on the bank of the River which causes a pond back Some distance and they have lodges all through the pond. \u00a0\u00a0 the water falls over the dam in the River abt. 4 feet. I drunk of the water found it verry cold. \u00a0\u00a0 it appears that there is 3 or 4 Springs running from under the mountains a Short distance to the South of us \u00a0\u00a0 Some Spots of Snow on it. \u00a0\u00a0 about one oC. we passed over a bad rapid and halted at a bottom covered with timber, to dine \u00a0\u00a0 the day pleasant and warm. \u00a0\u00a0 proceeded on \u00a0\u00a0 passed a large Spring run which is made by the Snow on the Mountains and runs from the foot of the Mo. through a Smooth plain. \u00a0\u00a0 the River gitting more rapid \u00a0\u00a0 the rapids longer \u00a0\u00a0 passed beaver ponds, bottom prarie & bottoms covred with timber &C. Came 17\u00bd miles this day, and Camped on the Lard. Side at a bottom of cotton timber. \u00a0\u00a0 the currents verry thick & rabit berrys &C.\n\n\n

[Gass]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Saturday 3rd. \u00a0\u00a0 A fine cool morning. We left a note for Capt. Clarke, continued our route along the valley; and passed several fine springs that issue from the mountains. Currants and service berries are in abundance along this valley, and we regaled ourselves with some of the best I had ever seen. We went about 22 miles and encamped. [12] The night was disagreeably cold.\n\n\n

[Whitehouse]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nSaturday 3rd August 1805. \u00a0\u00a0 a clear morning. \u00a0\u00a0 we Set out as usal and proceeded on. \u00a0\u00a0 Capt. Clark walked on Shore a Short time and killed a Deer. \u00a0\u00a0 the River verry crooked and filled with Islands. \u00a0\u00a0 proceeded on. \u00a0\u00a0 Saw 2 deer little a head, one of the hunters went after them and killed a panther on an Island. \u00a0\u00a0 it differs Some from those in the States it was 7\u00bd feet long, & of a redish coulour the turshes long the tallants large but not verry long. \u00a0\u00a0 passed verry rapid water So that we had to double man the canoes and drag them over the Sholes & rapids. \u00a0\u00a0 passed a large prarie on S. Side. \u00a0\u00a0 high grass & bushes along the River. \u00a0\u00a0 the bottoms has been burned over by the natives I \nexpect last fall. \u00a0\u00a0 passed a verry large Spring on L. S. which makes from under the mountains. \u00a0\u00a0 the beaver has damed up the mouth & built lodges all through the pond it forms. \u00a0\u00a0 it falls over the beaver dam in to the River verry Steep, about 4 feet.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 passed over a bad rapid and halted about one to dine at a bottom of timber on the S. Side. \u00a0\u00a0 the day pleasant & warm. \u00a0\u00a0 proceeded on \u00a0\u00a0 passed Several Springs one large one on L. S. plains and bottoms, Some of which is covred with cotton & birch [13] timber \u00a0\u00a0 the River Still getting more rapid and the rapids longer than below. \u00a0\u00a0 Came 11\u00bd miles this \nday and Camped on L. Side Cot. wood.

\n

\nSaturday August 3rd \u00a0\u00a0 A Clear morning, & we set out on our Voyage early, Captain Clark walked on Shore a short time, and killed a Deer.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 We halted our Canoes, and took the Deer on board, and then proceeded on, We find the River very crooked, and filled with Islands. \u00a0\u00a0 We continued on, and saw 2 Deer a small distance a head of us.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 One of our hunters went out after them, this hunter killed a Panther on a small Island, a small distance from us. \u00a0\u00a0 it differed but very little from those seen in the United States. \u00a0\u00a0 It measured 7\u00bd feet long, and was of a reddish colout, its tushes was very long, the Talons thick but not long, We passed many places in the River, that the water ran so rapid, that we were forced to double man the Tow ropes to drag the Canoes over the Shoals & rapids. \u00a0\u00a0 We passed a large Priari lying on the South side of the River, high Grass & bushes, growing along the River. \u00a0\u00a0 We also passed a very large spring, which lies on the South side of the River, and comes from under the mountains.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 The beaver had dammed up the Mouth of this large spring, and had built their houses all through the pond it had formed, it falls over the beaver dam into the River, about 4 feet, We passed over a bad rapid, and halted about One o'Clock P. M. to dine at a bottom of timber'd land, on the south side of the River\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 The day was warm but pleasant, we proceeded on and passed several springs, one of which was very large, lying on the south side of the river, & some plains, some of which was covered with Cotton wood Trees & birch timber.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 The River has this day run more rapid, & the Rapids much longer, than any we had yet \nseen, which fataigued our Men exceedingly.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 We came 11\u00bd Miles this day & encamped \u2329in\u232a on the South side of the River, in a place of Wood land.\u2014\n\n\n\n\n

\n
1. \n Lewis's small pine is limber pine. (back)
\n
2. The willow is sandbar, or coyote, willow, Salix exigua Nutt. ssp. interior (Rowlee) Conq.; honeysuckle is probably wolfberry, western snowberry, Symphoricarpos occidentalis Hook.; rosebushes are western wild rose, Rosa woodsii Lindl.; and the \"goosbury\" is probably swamp currant, Ribes lacustre (Pers.) Poir. Booth & Wright, 26, 234, 119, 107. (back)
\n
3. The scrub birch, Betula glandulosa Michx. Ibid., 30. (back)
\n
4. \n Lewis camped in Madison County, Montana, above the mouth of the Big Hole River as it was in 1805, at least. The rivers have meandered considerably in this region since Lewis and Clark's time. Atlas map 65. (back)
\n
5. \n Biddle probably drew the red vertical line through this passage about the birch. (back)
\n
6. Faulting and natural changes in the Jefferson River's course often cause swamps or waterlogged lands to form. The growth of vegetation in these areas is rapid, and the remains of plants are quickly covered by the remains of subsequent plants so that decay is incomplete. In such areas, peat bogs, several acres in extent, form. The mineral salts are those of sodium sulphate, sodium bicarbonate, and magnesium sulphate similar to those noticed earlier downstream of the Great Falls of the Missouri. These salts have formed here because they are derived from salt-rich Cretaceous formations through which the Ruby and Big Hole rivers pass. (back)
\n
7. Probably the long-billed curlew, Numenius americanus [AOU, 264]. (back)
\n
8. Evidently the northern sucker, Catostomus catostomus.\n Lewis gave a fuller description on August 19, 1805. Burroughs, 264. (back)
\n
9. Later Big Pipestone Creek, in Madison County. The animal was a mountain lion, Felis concolor. Atlas map 65. (back)
\n
10. \n Clark's camp was in Jefferson or Madison County, a few miles below present Waterloo. Atlas map 65. (back)
\n
11. The \"red buries\" are buffaloberry, Shepherdia argentea (Pursh) Nutt. Booth & Wright, 160; Little (MWH), 191-NW. (back)
\n
12. In Madison County, Montana, above the mouth of the Big Hole River, the party's Wisdom River, which Gass does not mention. (back)
\n
13. Perhaps scrub birch, Betula glandulosa Michx., which Lewis calls dwarf birch this day. (back)
