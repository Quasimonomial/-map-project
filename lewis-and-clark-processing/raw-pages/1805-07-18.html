\n July 18, 1805\n

\n
\n \n

[Lewis]

\n \n
\n Thursday July 18th 1805.\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nSet out early this morning. \u00a0\u00a0 previous to our departure saw a large herd of the Bighorned anamals on the immencely high and nearly perpendicular clift opposite to us; [1] on the face of this clift they walked about and bounded from rock to rock with apparent unconcern where it appared to me that no quadruped could have stood, and from which had they made one false step the[y] must have been precipitated at least a 500 feet. \u00a0\u00a0 this anamal appears to frequent such precepices and clifts where in fact they are perfectly secure from the pursuit of the wolf, bear, or even man himself.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 at the distance of 2\u00bd miles we passed the entrance of a considerable river on the Stard. side; about 80 yds. wide being nearly as wide as the Missouri at that place. \u00a0\u00a0 it's current is rapid and water extreamly transparent; the bed is formed of small smooth stones of flat rounded or other figures. \u00a0\u00a0 it's bottoms are narrow but possess as much timber as the Missouri. \u00a0\u00a0 the country is mountainous and broken through which it passes. \u00a0\u00a0 it appears as if it might be navigated but to what extent must be conjectural. \u00a0\u00a0 this handsome bold and clear stream we named in honour of the Secretary of war calling it \n Dearborn's river.\u2014 [2] \u00a0\u00a0 as we were anxious now to meet with the Sosonees\n [3] or snake Indians as soon as possible in order to obtain information relative to the geography of the country and also if necessary, some horses we thought it better for one of us either Capt. C. or myself to take a small party & proceed on up the river, some distance before the canoes, in order to discover them, should they be on the river before the daily discharge of our guns, which was necessary in procuring subsistence for the party, should allarm and cause them to retreat to the mountains and conceal themselves, supposing us to be their enemies who visit them usually by the way of this river. \u00a0\u00a0 accordingly Capt. Clark set out this morning after breakfast with Joseph Fields, Pots and his servant York. \u00a0\u00a0 we proceeded on tolerably well; the current stonger than yesterday we employ the cord and oars principally tho' sometimes the setting pole. \u00a0\u00a0 in the evening we passed a large creek about 30 yds. wide which disembogues on the Stard. side; it discharges a bold current of water it's banks low and bed frormed of stones altogether; this stream we called Ordway's creek after Sergt. John Ordway. [4] I have observed for several days a species of flax [5] growing in the river bottoms the leaf stem and pericarp of which resembles the common flax cultivated in the U' States. \u00a0\u00a0 the stem rises to the hight of about 2\u00bd or 3 feet high; as many as 8 or ten of which proceede from the same root. \u00a0\u00a0 the root appears to be perennial. \u00a0\u00a0 the bark of the stem is thick strong and appears as if it would make excellent flax. \u00a0\u00a0 the seed are not yet ripe but I hope to have an opportunity of collecting some of them after they are so \u00a0\u00a0 if it should on experiment prove to yeald good flax and at the same time admit of being cut without injuring the perennial root it will be a most valuable plant, and I think there is the greatest probability that it will do so, for notwithstanding the seed have not yet arrived at maturity it is puting up suckers or young shoots from the same root and would seem therefore that those which are fully grown and which are in the proper stage of vegitation to produce the best fax are not longer essencial to the preservation or support of the root. \u00a0\u00a0 the river somewhat wider than yesterday and the mountains more distant from the river and not so high; the bottoms are but narrow and little or no timber near the river. \u00a0\u00a0 some pine on the mountains which seems principally confined to their uper region. \u00a0\u00a0 we killed one Elk this morning and found part of the flesh and the skin of a deer this evening which had been kiled and left by Capt. Clark. \u00a0\u00a0 we saw several herds of the Bighorn but they were all out of our reach on inacessable clifts.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 we encamped on the lard. side in a small grove of narrow leafed cottonwood. [6] \u00a0\u00a0 there is not any of the broad leafed cottonwood on the river since it has entered the mountains. Capt Clark ascended the river on the Stard. side. [7] \u00a0\u00a0 in the early part of the day after he left me the hills were so steep that he gained but little off us; in the evening he passed over a mountain by which means he cut off many miles of the river's circuitous rout; the Indian road which he pursued over this mountain is wide and appears as if it had been cut down or dug in many places; he passed two streams of water, the branches of Ordway's creek, on which he saw a number of beaver dams succeeding each other in close order and extending as far up those streams as he could discover them in their couse towards the mountains. \u00a0\u00a0 he also saw many bighorn anamals on the clifts of the mountains. \u00a0\u00a0 not far beyond the mountain which he passed in the evening he encamped on a small stream of runing water. \u00a0\u00a0 having travelled about 20 m. \u00a0\u00a0 the water of those rivulets which make down from these mountains is extreemly cold pure and fine. \u00a0\u00a0 the soil near the river is of a good quality and produces a luxuriant growth of grass and weeds; among the last the sunflower holds a distinguished place. \u00a0\u00a0 the aspin is small but grows very commonly on the river and small streams which make down from the Mouts.

[Clark]

\n \n
\n July 18th Tursday 1805\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\na fine morning \u00a0\u00a0 passed a Considerable river which falls in on the Stard Side and nearly as wide as the Missouri \u00a0\u00a0 we call Dearbournes river after the Sety. of war. \u00a0\u00a0 we thought it prudent for a partey to go a head for fear our fireing Should allarm the Indians and cause them to leave the river and take to the mountains for Safty from their enemes who visit them thro this rout. I deturmined to go a head with a Small partey a few days and find the Snake Indians if possible \u00a0\u00a0 after brackfast I took J. Fields\n Potts & my Servent proceeded on. \u00a0\u00a0 the Country So Hilley that we gained but little of the Canoes untill in the evening I passed over a mountain on an Indian rode by which rout I cut off Several miles of the Meanderings of the River, the roade which passes this mountain is wide and appears to have been dug in maney places, we Camped on a Small run of Clear Cold water, musquitors verry troublesom the forepart of the evening \u00a0\u00a0 I Saw great maney Ibex. \u00a0\u00a0 we Crossed two Streams of running water [10] on those Streams I saw Several Beaver dams. \u00a0\u00a0 ordway \u2329river\u232a Creek \u00a0\u00a0 the Countrey is Mountanious & rockey except the valey &c. which is Covered with earth of a good quallity without timber, The timber which is principally pitch pine is Confined to the mountains, the Small runs & Creeks which have water running in them Contain Cotton-Willow, Willow, & aspin. \u00a0\u00a0 trees all Small \u00a0\u00a0 I Saw maney fine Springs & Streams of running water which Sink & rise alternately in the Valies \u00a0\u00a0 the water of those Streams are fine, those Streams which run off into the river are damed up by the beaver from near ther mouthes up as high as I could See up them\n\n

[Ordway]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n July 18th Thursday 1805. \u00a0\u00a0 a clear pleasant morning. \u00a0\u00a0 we Saw Mountain Sheep or Ibex on the top of a high Steep pricipice. \u00a0\u00a0 they ran along the rocks where it was all most perpentickelier and about 200 feet from the Surface of the water. \u00a0\u00a0 we set out at sun rise and proceeded on \u00a0\u00a0 about 3 miles passed the mouth of a river [11] on the N. S. about 100 yards wide at its mouth. \u00a0\u00a0 one mile further Capt. Clark killed an Elk. Saw Several others. \u00a0 \u00a0 we Saw a flock of mountain Rams on the Side of a hill which had large horns. Capt. Clark his Servant and 2 other men [12] Set out to go 1 or 2 days march a head to make discoverys &.C. \u00a0\u00a0 we proceeded on verry well with the canoes. \u00a0\u00a0 towards evening we passed a River [13] which came in on N. S. about 60 yards wide. \u00a0\u00a0 the mountains continues but not So high as yesterday. \u00a0\u00a0 we Came 19\u00bd miles and Camped in a narrow bottom on the Larbord Side \u00a0\u00a0 considerable of fine flax in this bottom now going to Seed. \u00a0\u00a0 we found a Deer Skin and Some meat which Capt. Clark had killed.\n\n\n

[Gass]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Thursday 18th. \u00a0\u00a0 The morning was fair and we proceeded on early: passed Clear-water river on the north side about 50 yards wide, rapid and shallow. There are a great quantity of currants [14] all along the river on both sides in the small bottoms. At breakfast time Captain Clarke with three men went on ahead. About 11 we got through the higher part of the mountains, and to where there is less timber and the rocks not so large. In the forenoon we passed two small creeks on the north side, [15] and in the afternoon a small river on the same side; above the mouth of which we got a deer skin, that Captain Clarke's man [16] had hung up. The country continues much the same. We made 20 miles this day.\n\n

[Whitehouse]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n July 18th Thursday 1805. \u00a0\u00a0 a clear pleasant morning. \u00a0\u00a0 we Saw Some Mountain Sheep on a verry high precipice which is nearly Steep from the river up 2 or 3 100 feet. \u00a0\u00a0 we Set out about Sun rise and proceded on about 3 miles \u00a0\u00a0 passed the mouth of a River about 100 yd. wide at the mouth \u00a0\u00a0 it came in on the N. Side and run Strong. \u00a0\u00a0 one mile further up \u00a0\u00a0 Capt. [17] Clark killed an Elk \u00a0\u00a0 Saw a nomber more. \u00a0\u00a0 we Saw a flock of Mountain Rams on the Side of the Mountain on S. Side with verry large horns. \u00a0\u00a0 Capt. Clark killd. one \u00a0\u00a0 we went near them before they run from us. \u00a0\u00a0 the mountains appear not So high ahead, but another range Seen a long distance above which appear much higher than any we See in this range and Some we allow to be 700 feet high \u00a0\u00a0 the pitch & yallow pine [18] continues Scatering along these mountains &c. \u00a0\u00a0 Capt. Clark & his Servant york & 2 [19] other men Set out to go up one or two days travel by land. \u00a0\u00a0 we proceded on with the canoes verry well. \u00a0\u00a0 towards evening we passed a Small River on N. S. about 60 yds wide named [blank] River. \u00a0\u00a0 the mountains continue but not So high as yesterday. \u00a0\u00a0 we Came 19\u00bd miles and Camped in a narrow bottom on the S. Side. \u00a0\u00a0 considerable of flax in this bottom half Seded. \u00a0\u00a0 we took on board Some deer meat & a Skin which Capt. Clark killed.

\n

\nThursday July 18th \u00a0\u00a0 This morning clear & pleasant; we saw some Mountain Sheep (Ibex) on a very high Precipice, which is nearly steep from the River, and \u2329nearly\u232a about 300 feet high, We set out about Sun rise, and proceeded on our way, and about 3 Miles from the place we left this morning, we passed the Mouth of a River, about 100 Yards wide at its mouth called Smiths River, it came in on the North side of the Mesouri, its stream run strong for a mile up it, Captain Clark who went ashore here, killed an Elk, and saw a number more of them.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 We saw another large flock of Mountain Sheep (Ibex[)] on the Side of a mountain; lying on the South Side of the Mesouri River\u2014

\n

\nThose animals had larger horns, than any that we had yet seen.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 Captain Clark kill'd one of them, We got near to them before they ran from us, The mountains appear not to be so high a head of us, as those we are in at present, We saw another range of Mountains a long distance further above us, Which appear to be much higher, than any that we have seen in this range, (some of which are 700 feet high) \u00a0\u00a0 The Pitch & Yellow pine appear to be growing, Scattering along these Mountains \u00a0\u00a0 Captain Clark, 3 of the party, & his black Servant, left us, and set out one days travel, up the River by land to make discoveries.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 We continued on our Voyage with our Canoes, till towards evening, and passed a small River lying on the North side 60 Yards wide at its mouth which Captain Lewis named Dearbornes River. \u00a0\u00a0 The Mountains continue, but not so high as they was Yesterday, We came 19\u00bd Miles this day, and encamped in a narrow bottom, lying on the South side of the River; here we found growing considerable quantity's of wild Flax, having seed about half ripe, This flax was in every appearance like the Flax, which is planted & grows in the United States. \u00a0\u00a0 We found hanging up at this place the Meat & Skin of a deer, that Captain Clark had killed and left for our party.\u2014\n\n\n\n

1. Later called Big Rock, now Eagle Rock. Atlas map 62; MRC map 79; USGS map South Great Falls. (back)
\n
2. \n Dearborn River forms the boundary between Cascade and Lewis and Clark counties, Montana, for a short distance above its mouth. Henry Dearborn, a Revolutionary War veteran, was secretary of war during Jefferson's entire presidency, and a notably unsuccessful general in the War of 1812. Atlas map 62; MRC map 79. (back)
\n
3. \n Lewis's first use of this word, a variant of \"Shoshone,\" and apparently the only one before meeting these people in August. It is not clear whether he wrote the entry after the August meeting or learned the word earlier. It was not the Shoshone name for themselves. (back)
\n
4. Later Little Prickly Pear Creek, in Lewis and Clark County. Atlas map 62; MRC map 79. (back)
\n
5. \n Linum perenne L. var. lewisii (Pursh) Eat. & Wright, blue flax. This variety of wild flax was named by Pursh in honor of Lewis from the expedition's botanical collections which he examined. The annual cultivated flax is L. usitatissimum L., common flax, linseed. Pursh, 210; Hitchcock & Cronquist, 282; Cutright (LCPN), 173. It may have been Biddle who drew a vertical line through this passage about the flax. (back)
\n
6. In Lewis and Clark County, above the present Holter Dam. Atlas map 62; MRC map 80. (back)
\n
7. \n Clark's route is marked by a dotted line on Atlas maps 62 and 63. He appears not to have kept separate course tables during this period of separation. MRC maps 79, 80. Robert N. Bergantino suggests the following for his route and camp of this day. Clark probably left the Missouri River near Holter Dam and continued south-southeast to Falls Gulch. He then followed that gulch to Towhead Gulch and down that to Hilger Valley. Clark's camp appears to be south of the summit of the pass on Towhead Gulch about two miles west of Beartooth Mountain. A jeep trail and power line now follow this route. Information of Bergantino, July 18, 1986. (back)
\n
8. Later Stickney Creek, in Lewis and Clark County. Atlas map 62; MRC map 79. (back)
\n
9. \n Campanula rotundifolia L., roundleaf harebell. The flower color and leaves are superficially similar to the flax. Booth & Wright, 237. It was probably Biddle who drew a red vertical line through this paragraph. (back)
\n
10. Perhaps later Rock Creek and Little Prickly Pear Creek. Atlas map 62; MRC maps 79, 80. (back)
\n
11. \n Dearborn River, named by Lewis and Clark for Jefferson's secretary of war, Henry Dearborn, forms the boundary between Cascade and Lewis and Clark counties, Montana, for a short distance above its mouth. (back)
\n
12. \n Clark was joined by York, Joseph Field, and Potts. (back)
\n
13. Named by the captains Ordway's Creek, for Sergeant John Ordway of the party, later Little Prickly Pear Creek, Lewis and Clark County. (back)
\n
14. See Lewis's entry of July 17, 1805, for a discussion of the currants of the region. (back)
\n
15. The two small creeks should be Stickney and Werner creeks, Lewis and Clark County, \nexcept it is hard to see how Gass places them on the north side of the Missouri, or on the same side of the river as the \"small river\" which follows. Perhaps this is an error by McKeehan. (back)
\n
16. Presumably York. (back)
\n
17. The word \"Capt.\" is written over \"we.\" (back)
\n
18. \"Pitch\" is the familiar ponderosa pine. The most likely species for the \"yallow pine\" is \nlimber pine, Pinus flexilis James. (back)
\n
19. The number \"2\" is written over \"3\"; 2 is correct. The men were Joseph Field and John Potts. (back)
