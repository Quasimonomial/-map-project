\n September 6, 1805\n

\n
\n \n

[Clark]

\n \n
\n September 6th Friday 1805 [1]\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nSome little rain, purchased two fine horses & took a Vocabiliary of the language litened our loads & packed up, rained contd. untill \u23292\u232a 12 oClock \u00a0\u00a0 we Set out at 2 oClock at the Same time all the Indians Set out on Ther way to meet the Snake Indians at the 3 forks of the Missouri. Crossed a Small river [2] from the right we call [blank] [NB: This was the main river or Clarks\n ] Soon after Setting out, also a Small Creek [3] from the North all three forks Comeing together below our Camp at which place the Mountains Close on each Side of the river, We proceeded on N 30 W. Crossed a Mountain and Struck the river Several miles down, at which place the Indians had Encamped two days before, we Proceeded on Down the River which is 30 yds. wide Shallow & Stoney. Crossing it Several times & Encamped in a Small bottom on the right side. [4] \u00a0\u00a0 rained this evening nothing to eate but berries, our flour out, and but little Corn, the hunters killed 2 pheasents only\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 all our horses purchased of the \u2329flat heads\u232a oote lash Shutes we Secured well for fear of their leaveing of us, and watched them all night for fear of their leaving us or the Indians prosuing & Steeling them.\n

\n \n
\n
\n \n

[Ordway]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nFriday 6th Sept. 1805. \u00a0\u00a0 a clear cold morning. \u00a0\u00a0 we packed up our baggage \u00a0\u00a0 the natives got up their horses also and Struck their Lodges in order to Set out for the Missourie. \u00a0\u00a0 we have now got 40 good pack horses and three Colts. \u00a0\u00a0 four hunters were furnished horses without loads in order to hunt constant. \u00a0\u00a0 about 1 oClock we Set out again on our journey. \u00a0\u00a0 the natives Set out at the Same time for the Missourie \u00a0\u00a0 we proceeded on \u00a0\u00a0 soon crossed a large creek [5] in this valley then Soon took the mountains. \u00a0\u00a0 one of the hunters left us. \u00a0\u00a0 we went over a Mountain about 7 miles and descended\ndown the Mountain on a creek and Camped. [6] \u00a0\u00a0 eat a little parched corn. \u00a0\u00a0 light Sprinkling of rain, through the course of this day\u2014\n\n\n

\n \n
\n
\n \n

[Gass]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Friday 6th. \u00a0\u00a0 A cloudy morning. We exchanged some of our horses, that were fatigued, with the natives; about 12 o'clock some rain fell; and we prepared to move on. At 1 we started, when the Indians also set out. We proceeded over a mountain to a creek, and went down the creek, [7] our course being northwest; found the country mountainous and poor; and the game scarce. Having travelled about 7 miles we encamped. Four hunters had \nbeen out to day, but killed nothing; we therefore supped upon a small quantity of corn we had yet left.\n\n\n

\n \n
\n
\n \n

[Whitehouse]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Friday 6th Sept. 1805. \u00a0\u00a0 a clear cold morning. \u00a0\u00a0 we began to pack up our baggage and look up our horses &c. \u00a0\u00a0 bought a nomber of lash chords and other Small articles from the natives \u00a0\u00a0 at 10 oClock A. m. the natives all got up their horses and Struck their lodges in order to move over on the head of the Missourie after the buffalow. \u00a0\u00a0 they make a large Show as they are numerous and have abundance of horses. \u00a0\u00a0 we take these Savages to be the Welch Indians\n [8] if their be any Such from the Language. \u00a0\u00a0 So Capt. Lewis took down the Names of everry thing in their Language, in order that it may be found out whether they are or whether they Sprang or origenated first from the welch or not. \u00a0\u00a0 about noon we got ready to Set out. \u00a0\u00a0 we have now 40 good pack horses, and three Colts. \u00a0\u00a0 we loadd. the horses \u00a0\u00a0 Several men had to take 2 horses &c. \u00a0\u00a0 4 hunters were furnished horses without loads to hunt constant. \u00a0\u00a0 about 1 oClock P. m. we Set out. \u00a0\u00a0 the natives Set out at the Same time to go over on the missourie. \u00a0\u00a0 we proceeded on our journey. \u00a0\u00a0 crossed a large creek went over a mountain about 7 miles came down on the Same creek and Camped \u00a0\u00a0 nothing to eat but a little pearched corn. \u00a0\u00a0 on[e] hunter Stayed out all night. \u00a0\u00a0 light Sprinklings of rain through the course of the day.

\n

\nFriday Septemr\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 6th \u00a0\u00a0 A clear cold morning, and we began to pack up our baggage & collect our horses in order to get ready to proceed on our Journey, we purchased a number of Cords & other small articles from the \nIndians, for some small articles of merchandise. \u00a0\u00a0 About 10 o'Clock A. M. the Indians collected their horses, & struck their lodges, in order to move over on the head waters of the Mesouri River after Buffalo. \u00a0\u00a0 they made a \nlarge show & were numerous and had abundance of horses. \u00a0\u00a0 We all suppose these Indians to be the Welch nation of Indians, if there be any such a Nation; & from their language we believe them to be the same. \u00a0\u00a0 Captain Lewis took down the names of almost every thing in their language in order to find whether they are the same,\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 or if possible to find out from their language & if there is any thing similiarity between it, & the Antient Welch language, \n& [illegible, crossed out] whether they originated from the Welch.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 About noon we got ready to set out on our Journey, & we have 40 good pack horses which our officers had purchased \u2329from\u232a & exchanged with these Indians.

\n

\nThey had also purchased 3 Colts; that in case we should be without provisions, that we might have something for to subsist on.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 We loaded our horses, & our hunters were also furnish'd with horses without loads, to hunt on.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 About 1 oClock P. M we set out. \u00a0\u00a0 The natives set out at the same time, to go over on the Mesouri River to hunt buffalo, after taking an affectionate leave of us.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 We proceeded on our Journey, and crossed a large Creek, and went over a Mountain about 7 Miles across.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 We asscended this Mountain, and came to the same Creek that we crossed this day, & encamped.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 We had nothing to eat except a little parched Corn Meal, but our party are all contented. \u00a0\u00a0 One of our hunters did not return to us this night\n\n\n

\n \n
\n \n \n
\n
1. \n Clark's courses for September 6\u20139 are found at his entry of September 9, 1805. Again heavily marked by Coues and with some incorrect place-names. (back)
\n
2. \n East Fork Bitterroot River, unnamed on Atlas map 68. (back)
\n
3. Probably Cameron Creek. (back)
\n
4. A few miles northwest of Sula, Ravalli County, Montana, on the East Fork Bitterroot River, apparently above Warm Springs Creek and on the opposite side; unnamed on Atlas map 68. The party had apparently passed over Sula Peak on their way. (back)
\n
5. \n East Fork Bitterroot River, Ravalli County, Montana. (back)
\n
6. Perhaps following Cameron Creek back down to the East Fork Bitterroot River and camping on that stream in Ravalli County above Warm Springs Creek which comes in on the opposite side. (back)
\n
7. The East Fork Bitterroot River, Ravalli County, Montana. (back)
\n
8. \n Whitehouse recalls the myth that some interior Indians may have descended from legendary Welsh travelers. Some persons also applied this myth to the Mandans. (back)
