\n July 30, 1806\n

\n
\n \n

[Lewis]

\n \n
\n Wednesday July 30th 1806.\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nThe rain still continued this morning it was therefore unnecessary to remain as we could not dry our baggage \u00a0\u00a0 I Consequently set out early as usual and pursued my rout downwards. \u00a0\u00a0 the currant being strong and the men anxious to get on they plyed their oars faithfully and we went at the rate of about seven miles an hour. \u00a0\u00a0 we halted several times in the course of the day to kill some bighorns being anxious to procure a few more skins and skeletons of this animal; I was fortunate enough to procure one other malle and female for this purpose which I had prepared accordingly. \u00a0\u00a0 seven others were killed by the party also 2 buffaloe one Elk 2 beaver with & a female brown bear with tallons 6\u00bc inches in length. I preserved the skin of this bear also with the tallons; it was not large and in but low order. \u00a0\u00a0 we arrived this evening at an island about 2 ms. above Goodriches Island and encamped on it's N. E. side. [1] \u00a0\u00a0 the rain continued with but little intermission all day; the air is cold and extreemly disagreeable. \u00a0\u00a0 nothing extraordinary happened today\n

\n \n
\n Friday 30th July 1806\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nSet out early this morning \u00a0\u00a0 at 12 miles arived at the Commencement of Shoals the Chanel on the Stard Side near a high bluff. \u00a0\u00a0 passed a Succession of those Shoals for 6 miles the lower of which was quit across the river and appeared to have a decent of about 3 feet. \u00a0\u00a0 here we were Compeled to let the Canoes down by hand for fear of their Strikeing a rock under water and Splitting. This is by far the wost place which I have Seen on this river from the Rocky mountains to this place \u00a0\u00a0 a distance of 694 miles by water. \u00a0\u00a0 a Perogu or large Canoe would with Safty pass through the worst of those Shoals, which I call the Buffalow Sholes\n [3] from the Circumstance of one of those animals being in them. \u00a0\u00a0 the rock which passes the river at those Sholes appear hard and gritty of a dark brown Colour. [4] \u00a0\u00a0 the Clifts on the Stard. Side is about 100 feet in hight, on the Lard Side the Country is low and the bottom rises gradually back. \u00a0\u00a0 here is the first appearance of Birnt hills\n [5] which I have Seen on this river they are at a distance from the river on the Lard Side. I landed at the enterance of a dry Creek [6] on the Lard side below the Shoals and took brackfast. Those Dry Rivers, Creeks &c are like those of the Missouri which take their rise in and are the Conveyance of the water from those plains. \u00a0\u00a0 they have the appearanc of dischargeing emence torrents of water. \u00a0\u00a0 the late rains which has fallen in the plains raised Sudenly those Brooks which receive the water of those plains on which those Suden & heavy Showers of rain must have fallen, Several of which I have Seen dischargeing those waters, whiles those below heading or takeing their rise in the Same neighbourhood, as I passed them appears to have latterly been high. \u00a0\u00a0 those Broods discharge emencely of mud also, which Contributes much to the muddiness of the river. \u00a0\u00a0 after Brackfast proceeded on \u00a0\u00a0 the river much narrower than above \u00a0\u00a0 from 3 to 400 yards wide only and only a fiew scattering trees to be Seen on the banks. \u00a0\u00a0 at 20 miles below the Buffalow Shoals passed a rapid which is by no means dangerous, it has a number of large rocks in different parts of the river which Causes high waves \u00a0\u00a0 a very good Chanel on the Lard. Side. \u00a0\u00a0 this rapid I call Bear rapid\n [7] from the Circumstance of a bears being on a rock in the Middle of this rapid when I arived at it. \u00a0\u00a0 a violent Storm from the N. W. obliged us to land imediately below this rapid, draw up the Canoes and take Shelter in an old Indian Lodge above the enterance of a river which is nearly dry \u00a0\u00a0 it has laterly been very high and Spread over nearly \u00bc a mile in width. its Chanel is 88 yards and in this there is not more water than could pass through an inch auger hole. I call it Yorks dry R.\n [8] \u00a0\u00a0 after the rain and wind passed over I proceeded on at 7 Miles \u00a0\u00a0 passed the enterance of a river [9] the water of which is 100 yds wide, the bead of this river nearly \u00bc of a mile \u00a0\u00a0 this river is Shallow and the water very muddy and of the Colour of the banks a darkish brown. [10] I observe great quantities of red Stone [11] thrown out of this river that from the appearance of the hills at a distance on its lower Side induced me to call this red Stone river. \u00a0\u00a0 [NB: By a coincidence I found the Indian name Wa ha Sah\n ] [12] \u00a0\u00a0 as the water was disagreeably muddy I could not Camp on that Side below its mouth. \u00a0\u00a0 however I landed at its enteranc and Sent out and killed two fat Cows, and took as much of the flesh as the Canoes would conveniently Carry and Crossed the river and encamped at the enterance of a Brook on the Lard. Side under a large Spredding Cotton tree. [13] The river on which we passed to day is not So wide as above \u00a0\u00a0 containing but fiew islands with a Small quantity of Cotton timber. \u00a0\u00a0 no timber of any kind to be Seen on the high lands on either Side.

\nIn the evening below the enterance of redstone river I observed great numbers of Buffalow feeing on the plains, elk on the points and antilopes. I also Saw Some of the Bighorn animals at a distance on the hills. Gibson is now able to walk, he walked out this evening and killed an antilope.\n\n\n\n

\u00a0
\n \n

\nWednesday 30th July 1806. \u00a0\u00a0 cloudy and wet. \u00a0\u00a0 we Set out eairly and proceeded on verry well \u00a0\u00a0 our hunters killed 2 buffaloe \u00a0\u00a0 I and Willard killed a white bear. Some of the hunters killed 4 Ibex and 2 beavers. \u00a0\u00a0 rained all day. Camped on an Island.\u2014\n\n

\n \n

[Gass]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Wednesday 30th. \u00a0\u00a0 We embarked early in a cloudy morning with some rain. In our way through this high range of mountains, we killed four more of the large horned animals, two buffaloe, two beaver and a bear.

\n

\nThe water of the river is thick and muddy, on account of the late falls of rain, which wash those clay hills very much. We went down the river upwards of 70 miles to day, and encamped on a prairie island. Heavy rain fell at intervals during the day.\n\n

\n
1. \n Atlas map 40 seems to place Lewis's campsite on Goodrich's Island, just below the campsite of May 25, 1805. However, Lewis's journal entry places the camp on the upriver side of the sharp bend above Goodrich's Island. His wording leaves it unclear whether he actually camped on the nameless island or on the bank northeast of it in Blaine County, Montana. Ordway and Gass make clear that the camp was on the island. This area is a few miles below Cow Creek and Cow Island Crossing. MRC map 71. (back)
\n
2. The following figures appear in a column in the right margin beside the last course of this day: 191, 189, 380, 80, 73, 44; the third figure is a sub-total. For the first two figures see the draft courses for July 27, 1806. The last three appear to be the mileages for July 27, 28, and 29, with discrepancies. (back)
\n
3. \n Buffalo Shoals are in Custer County, Montana, just below the mouth of Sand Creek, \"Little dry River\" on Atlas map 120. (back)
\n
4. The shoals are formed of more indurated sandstone or, possibly, concretions in the Tullock Member of the Fort Union Formation. (back)
\n
5. The Tongue River Member of the Fort Union Formation approaches the river near here, but caps the higher hills only. It contains thicker and more continuous coal beds than the Tullock Member, and many of these beds have been burned extensively forming red clinker. (back)
\n
6. \n Muster Creek in Custer County, meeting the Yellowstone River near Kinsey; on Atlas map 120 it appears as \"Dry creek.\" (back)
\n
7. \n Bear Rapid is in Prairie County, Montana, a little above the mouth of Custer Creek. Atlas map 121. (back)
\n
8. The name, of course, derived from Clark's servant York; the stream is present Custer Creek in Prairie County. Atlas map 121. The words appear to be a later addition, interlined by Clark in a different shaded ink. (back)
\n
9. \n Powder River meets the Yellowstone in Prairie County; on Atlas map 121 it is \"War har sah River \u00a0\u00a0 red stone R.\" See an additional note below. (back)
\n
10. The color of the material in the banks is derived largely from coal detritus. (back)
\n
11. The red stone is clinker. Much of the clinker comes from the area along the Powder River nearly due east of Miles City. (back)
\n
12. The Indian name, presumably learned from the Mandans or Hidatsas, is \"War-rah-sash\" in \n Fort Mandan Miscellany. Biddle seems to imply here in this interjected sentence that the translation is \"red stone river,\" but in the Fort Mandan document the implied meaning is Powder River, the source of the modern name. It is a Mandan term, w\u00e1ra\u0161u\u014bte, \"powder.\" (back)
\n
13. \n Clark's camp was in Prairie County, a little below and opposite the mouth of Powder River (\"War har sah\"), at the mouth of Crooked Creek, a nameless stream on Atlas map 121. The site is not marked on the Atlas map. (back)
\n
14. \n Jones Creek meets the Yellowstone in Custer County, just above Tusler; on Atlas map 120 it is a nameless stream nearly opposite the mouth of Sunday Creek (\"Big dry River\"). (back)
\n
15. In addition to his penciled interlineations, Coues has underscored some words in pencil, but the underlinings are not shown here. The stream is Sunday Creek (\"Big dry River\") and given as one hundred and twenty yards wide on Atlas map 120. (back)
\n
16. This is difficult to identify with any known stream, unless Clark meant \"larboard,\" in which case it could be Sand Creek in Custer County, just above Buffalo Shoals, evidently the \"Little dry River\" of Atlas map 120. (back)
\n
17. The shoals are formed of more indurated sandstone or, possibly, concretions in the Tullock Member of the Fort Union Formation. (back)
\n
18. \n Muster Creek on the larboard and Dixon Creek on the starboard, both in Custer County; they are \"Dry creek 40 yards wide\" and \"dry brook,\" respectively, on Atlas map 120. The interlined \"Sand Cr.\" is Coues's guess as to the larboard creek's identity. (back)
\n
19. \n Deep Creek in Custer County; a nameless stream meeting the Yellowstone from the south on Atlas map 120. (back)
\n
20. \n Harris Creek meets the Yellowstone in Custer County west of present Shirley; on Atlas map 120 it is a nameless stream just below an island in the river. The interlined \"Muster Cr.\" is Coues's guess as to its identity. (back)
\n
21. \n Hay Creek in Custer County, opposite and a little above Harris Creek; it is nameless on Atlas map 120. (back)
\n
22. \n Dead Horse Creek in Custer County now empties into Buffalo Rapids Ditch which runs parallel to the Yellowstone; another small, nameless stream on the south side of the river on Atlas map 120. (back)
\n
23. \n Cabin Creek in Custer County, on the north side of the Yellowstone, nameless on Atlas map 120. (back)
\n
24. \n Cottonwood Creek, as Coues interlined, in Custer County, meeting the Yellowstone south of Bonfield, a nameless stream on Atlas map 120. (back)
\n
25. \n Mack Creek on the starboard and an unnamed creek on the larboard, both in Custer County, both nameless on Atlas map 120. \"Wolf Cr (N.)\" would be Coues's name for the larboard stream. (back)
\n
26. \n Williams Creek in Custer and Prairie counties, nameless on Atlas map 121. \"Devils Backbone,\" interlined by Coues, is a name in use in his time for the larboard bluffs. Coues (HLC), 3:1162 n. 23. That name may have referred to an irregular hill about a mile west of the river across from the mouth of Williams Creek rather than to the bluffs. (back)
\n
27. \n Camp Creek in Prairie County, reaching the Yellowstone at \"Bear Rapid,\" nearly opposite the mouth of Custer Creek (\"Yorks dry River\" on Atlas map 121). (back)
