\n August 1, 1805\n

\n
\n \n

[Lewis]

\n \n
\n August 1st 1805\n [1]\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nAt half after 8 A. M. we halted for breakfast and as had been previously agreed on between Capt. Clark and myself I set out with 3 men in quest of the Snake Indians. \u00a0\u00a0 the men I took were the two Interpreters Drewyer and Sharbono and Sergt. Gass who by an accedental fall had so disabled himself that it was with much pain he could work in the canoes tho' he could march with convenience. \u00a0\u00a0 the rout we took lay over a rough high range of mountains on the North side of the river. [2] \u00a0\u00a0 the rive entered these mountains a few miles above where we left it. [3]\n Capt Clark recommended this rout to me from a belief that the river as soon as it past the mountains boar to the N. of W. he having a few days before ascended these mountains to a position from which he discovered a large valley passing between the mountains and which boar to the N. West. \u00a0\u00a0 this however poved to be the inlet of a large creek which discharged itself into the river just above this range of mountains, [4] the river bearing to the S. W. we were therefore thrown several miles out of our rout. \u00a0\u00a0 as soon as we discovered our mistake we directed our course to the river which we at length gained about 2 P. M. much exhausted by the heat of the day the roughnes of the road and the want of water. \u00a0\u00a0 the mountains are extreemly bare of timber and our rout lay through the steep valleys exposed to the heat of the sun without shade and scarcely a breath of air; and to add to my fatiegue in this walk of about 11 miles I had taken a doze of glauber salts in the morning in consequence of a slight desentary with which I had been afflicted for several days; being weakened by the disorder and the opperation of the medecine I found myself almost exhausted before we reached the river. I felt my sperits much revived on our near approach to the river at the sight of a herd of Elk of which Drewyer and myself killed two. \u00a0\u00a0 we then hurried to the river and allayed our thirst. I ordered two of the men to skin the Elk and bring the meat to the river while myself and the other prepared a fire and cooked some of the meat for our dinner. \u00a0\u00a0 we made a comfortable meal of the Elk and left the ballance of the meat on the bank of the river the party with Capt. Clark. \u00a0\u00a0 this supply was no doubt very acceptable to them as they had had no fresh meat for near two days except one beaver Game being very scarce and shy. \u00a0\u00a0 we had seen a few deer and some goats but had not been fortunate enough to kill any of them. \u00a0\u00a0 after dinner we resumed our march and encamped about 6 m. above on the Stard side of the river. [5]\n

[Lewis]

\n \n
\n Thursday August 1st 1805.\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nThis morning we set out early and proceeded on tolerably well untill 8 OC'k. by which time we had arrived within a few miles of a mountain through which the river passes. \u00a0\u00a0 we halted on the Stard. side and took breakfast. \u00a0\u00a0 after which or at \u00bd after 8 A. M. as had been previously concerted between Capt. Clark and myself I set out with three men in surch of the Snake Indians or Sosonees. \u00a0\u00a0 our rout lay over a high range of mountains on the North side of the river. Capt C. recommended this rout to me no doubt from a belief that the river as soon as it passed this chain of mountains boar to the N. of W. he having on the 26th ult. ascended these mountains to a position from whence he discoved a large valley passing between the mountains which boar to the N. W. and presumed that the river passed in that direction; this however proved to be the passage of a large creek which discharged itself into the river just above this range of mountains, the river bearing to the S. W. we were therefore thrown several miles out of our rout. \u00a0\u00a0 as soon as we discovered our error we directed our course to the river which we at length gained about 2 P. M. much exhausted by the heat of the day, the roughness of the road and the want of water. \u00a0\u00a0 the mountains are extreemly bare of timber, and our rout lay through the steep and narrow hollows of the mountains exposed to the intese heat of the midday sun without shade or scarcely a breath of air: to add to my fatiegue in this walk of about 11 miles, I had taken a doze of glauber salts in the morning in consequence of a slight disentary with which I had been afflicted for several days. \u00a0\u00a0 being weakened by the disorder and the operation of the medicine I found myself almost exhausted before we reached the river. I felt my sperits much revived on our near approach to the river at the sight of a herd of Elk, of which Drewyer and myself soon killed a couple. \u00a0\u00a0 we then hurryed to the river and allayed our thirst. I ordered two of the men to skin the Elk and bring the meat to the river, while myself and the other prepared a fire and cooked some of the meat for our dinner. \u00a0\u00a0 we made a comfortable meal on the Elk, and left the ballance of the meat and skins on the bank of the river for Capt. Clark and party. \u00a0\u00a0 this supply will no doubt be acceptable to them, as they had had no fresh meat when I left them for almost 2 days except one beaver; game being very scarce and shy above the forks. \u00a0\u00a0 we had seen a few deer and antelopes but had not been fortunate enough to kill any of them. \u00a0\u00a0 as I passed these mountains I saw a flock of the black or dark brown phesants; [6] the young phesant is almost grown we killed one of them. \u00a0\u00a0 this bird is fully a third larger than the common phesant of the Atlantic states. \u00a0\u00a0 it's form is much the same. \u00a0\u00a0 it is booted nearly to the toes and the male has not the tufts of long black feathers on the sides of the neck which are so conspicuous in those of the Atlantic. \u00a0\u00a0 their colour is a uniform dark brown with a small mixture of yellow or yelloish brown specks on some of the feathers particularly those of the tail, tho' the extremities of these are perfectly black for about one inch. \u00a0\u00a0 the eye is nearly black, the iris has a small dash of yellowish brown. \u00a0\u00a0 the feathers of the tail are reather longer than that of our phesant or pattridge as they are Called in the Eastern States; are the same in number or eighteen and all nearly of the same length, those in the intermediate part being somewhat longest. \u00a0\u00a0 the flesh of this bird is white and agreeably flavored. \u00a0\u00a0 I also saw near the top of the mountain among some scattering pine a blue bird [7] about the size of the common robbin. \u00a0\u00a0 it's action and form is somewhat that of the jay bird and never rests long in any one position but constantly flying or hoping from sprey to sprey. I shot at one of them but missed it. \u00a0\u00a0 their note is loud and frequently repeated both flying and when at rest and is char \u00e2h', char'\u00e2h, char \u00e2h', as nearly as letters can express it. \u00a0\u00a0 after dinner we resumed our march and my pack felt much lighter than it had done about 2 hours before. \u00a0\u00a0 we traveled about six miles further and encamped on the stard. bank of the river, making a distance of 17 miles for this day. \u00a0\u00a0 the Musquetoes were troublesome but I had taken the precaution of bringing my bier.

\n

\nShortly after I left Capt. Clark this morning he proceed on and passed through the mountains; they formed tremendious clifts of ragged and nearly perpendicular rocks; the lower part of this rock is of the black grannite before mentioned and the upper part a light coloured free-stone. [8] \u00a0\u00a0 these clifts continue for 9 miles and approach the river very closely on either side. \u00a0\u00a0 he found the current verry strong. Capt. C. killed a big horn on these clifts which himself and party dined on. \u00a0\u00a0 after passing this range of mountains he entered this beautifull valley in which we also were it is from 6 to 8 miles wide. \u00a0\u00a0 the river is crooked and crouded with islands, it's bottoms wide fertile and covered with fine grass from 9 inches to 2 feet high and possesses but a scant proportion of timber, which consists almost entirely of a few narrow leafed cottonwood trees [9] distributed along the verge of the river. \u00a0\u00a0 in the evening Capt. C. found the Elk I had left him and ascended a short distance above to the entrance of a large creek which falls in on Stard. and encamped opposite to it on the Lard. side. [10] \u00a0\u00a0 he sent out the two Fieldses to hunt this evening and they killed 5 deer, which with the Elk again gave them a plentifull store of fresh provisions. this large creek we called Field's Creek after Reubin Fields one our party. \u00a0\u00a0 on the river about the mountains wich Capt. C. passed today he saw some large cedar trees and some juniper also [11] \u00a0\u00a0 just at the upper side of the mountain there is a bad rappid here the toe line of our canoe broke in the shoot of the rapids and swung on the rocks and had very nearly overset. \u00a0\u00a0 a small distance above this rapid a large bold Creek falls in on Lard. side which we called Frazier's Creek after Robt. Frazier. [12] They saw a large brown bear feeding on currants but could not get a shoot at him.

[Clark]

\n August 1st Wednesday 1805\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nA fine day \u00a0\u00a0 Capt. Lewis left me at 8 oClock just below the place I entered a verrey high mountain which jutted its tremedious Clifts on either Side for 9 Miles, the rocks ragide Some verry dark & other part verry light rock the light rocks is Sand Stone. [14] The water Swift & very Sholey. I killed a Ibix on which the whole party Dined, after passing through the Mountain we entered a wide extesive vallie of from 4 to 8 Miles wide verry leavell a Creek falls in at the Commencement of this Vallie on the Lard Side, the river widens & spreds into Small Chanels. W[e] encamped on the Lard Side opposit a large Creek \u00a0\u00a0 I sent out Jo: & R fields to hunt this evening they killed 5 Deer, I saw a large Bear eateing Currents this evining \u00a0\u00a0 The river so rapid that the greatest exertion is required by all to get the boats on wind S W Murckery at sun rise 50\u00b0 Ab. 0\n

\n Thursday, August the first 1805. \u00a0\u00a0 a fine morning \u00a0\u00a0 we Set out as usal, and proceeded on. Some of the hunters killed a goose and a beaver. \u00a0\u00a0 about 8 oClock A. m. we halted \u00a0\u00a0 took breakfast under a handsom ceeder tree on S. Side. Capt. Lewis, Sergt. Gass, G. Drewyer and our Intrepter Sharbonoe Set out to go on by land 4 or 5 days expecting to find Some Indians. \u00a0\u00a0 we proceeded on \u00a0\u00a0 the current Swift. \u00a0\u00a0 we find currents of different kinds as usal, and wild or choak cherries which are now gitting ripe. \u00a0\u00a0 the hills & gin to git higher and more pine timber on them, and ceeder along the River. \u00a0\u00a0 we passed clifts of rocks about 500 feet from the Surface of the water. \u00a0\u00a0 considerable of pine timber along the Sides of the hills. Saw Some timber or trees along the Shores, resembling ceeder which Some call juniper wood. \u00a0\u00a0 about noon Capt. Clark killed a Mountain Sheep out of a flock on the Side of a redish hill or clifts on L. Side \u00a0\u00a0 he Shot it across the River and the rest of the flock ran up the clifts which was nearly Steep. \u00a0\u00a0 the one killed roled down Some distance when it fell. \u00a0\u00a0 we got it and dined hearty on it. \u00a0\u00a0 we proceeded on. \u00a0\u00a0 passed over a bad rapid at the upper end of an Island jest above high rough clifts of rocks. \u00a0\u00a0 the towing line of the Captains canoe broke in the pitch of the rapid and the canoe was near turning over nocking again the rocks. \u00a0\u00a0 little further up passed a creek or large Spring run, which came in on L. S. and ran rapid. \u00a0\u00a0 came to a large valley which Capt. Clark had Seen before when he came up a fiew days ago. \u00a0\u00a0 passed large bottoms covered with timber, on each Side of the River. Saw a white bear. \u00a0\u00a0 took on board 2 Elk which Capt. Lewis had killed and left for us. \u00a0\u00a0 the hunters killed\nin these bottoms 5 deer this evening. \u00a0\u00a0 passed the mouth of a large creek [15] on the Stard. Side and a Spring. \u00a0\u00a0 came 13\u00bd miles and Camped on the Lard. Side in a bottom of cotton timber. \u00a0\u00a0 high hills on each Side, and Saw the mountain a Short distance to the South of us.\u2014 [16]\n

[Gass]

\n Thursday 1st August, 1805. \u00a0\u00a0 We set out early in a fine morning and proceeded on till breakfast time; when Capt. Lewis, myself and the two interpreters [17] went on ahead to look for some of the Snake Indians. Our course lay across a large mountain on the north side, [18] over which we had a very fatiguing trip of about 11 miles. We then came to the river again, and found it ran through a handsome valley of from 6 to 8 miles wide. At the entrance of this valley, which is covered with small bushes, but has very little timber, we killed two elk and left the meat for the canoes to take up, as the men stood much in need of it, having no fresh provisions on hand. We crossed a small creek [19] on the north shore, and encamped [20] on the same side.\n\n

\n Thursday 1st day of August 1805. \u00a0\u00a0 a clear morning. \u00a0\u00a0 we Set out as usal and proceeded on. \u00a0\u00a0 Some of the men killed a goose & a beaver \u00a0\u00a0 about 8 oClock A. M. we took breakfast under Some handsome ceeder trees on S. Side. \u00a0\u00a0 Capt. Lewis\n Sergt. Gass\n Sharbonoe & Drewyer Set out by land to go on up the River to make discoverys &c expecting to find Indians &c. \u00a0\u00a0 we proceeded on. \u00a0\u00a0 find currents as usal and choak cherrys along the River. \u00a0\u00a0 the current Swift \u00a0\u00a0 the hills higher and more pine and ceeder timber on them. \u00a0\u00a0 we passed high clifts about 500 [21] feet high in many places. \u00a0\u00a0 considerable of pine on the Sides of the hills \u00a0\u00a0 all the hills rough and uneven. \u00a0\u00a0 at noon Capt. Clark killed a mountain Sheep, on the side of a Steep redish hills or clifts \u00a0\u00a0 the remainder of the flock run up the Steep clifts. \u00a0\u00a0 the one killed roled down Some distance So we got it and dined eairnestly on it. \u00a0\u00a0 it being Capt. Clarks birth day he ordered Some flour gave out to the party. \u00a0\u00a0 we Saw Some timber along the Shores resembling ceeder which Some call Juniper, which had a delightfull Shade. \u00a0\u00a0 I left my Tommahawk on the Small Island where we lay last night, which makes me verry Sorry that I forgot it as I had used it common to Smoak in. \u00a0\u00a0 proceeded on \u00a0\u00a0 passed verry high ragid clifts, and a bad rapid at the upper end of a Small Island \u00a0\u00a0 the toe rope broke of the Capts. perogue and it was in danger of upsetting. \u00a0\u00a0 passed a Spring run or creek on L. Side. \u00a0\u00a0 came in to a valley. \u00a0\u00a0 passed bottoms of timber and the mouth of a large creek on S. Side, and a Spring also. \u00a0\u00a0 we came 13\u00bd miles and Caped opposite the Spring in a fine bottom covered with cotton timber and thick bushes &c. \u00a0\u00a0 Saw a white bear. \u00a0\u00a0 the hunters killed 5 deer \u00a0\u00a0 we took on board 2 Elk which Capt. Lewis had killed and left on Shore for us. \u00a0\u00a0 Saw Snow on the Mountains a Short distance to the South of us.

\n

\nThursday August 1st \u00a0\u00a0 This morning, Clear & pleasant. \u00a0\u00a0 We set out as usual, and proceeded on our Voyage, some of our party killed a Goose and a Beaver. \u00a0\u00a0 We halted about 8 o'Clock A. M\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 where we stopped & took breakfast under some handsome Cedar trees, lying on the South side of the River.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 Captain Lewis, Serjeant Gass & George Drewyer, and Sharbono (\u2329who\u232a the latter of which men had joined us at the Mandan Village,) set out shortly after, to go by land, up Jefferson River, in Order to make discoveries, & to try & find out some Indians. \u00a0\u00a0 We proceeded on, and found Currants, & Choke cherries growing along the Shores in great abundance. \u00a0\u00a0 the current of the River still running very strong against us; and the hills appearing to us, to be much higher, and more Pine & Cedar growing on them, than those we have passed for several days past, We passed some very high Clifts, of Rocks which were in many places 1,200 feet high &\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 on the sides of the hills we saw considerable quantities of Pine & Cedar Trees growing. \u00a0\u00a0 About noon Captain Clark killed a mountain Sheep or Ibex, out of a flock, which were on the side of Steep reddish hills or Clifts

\n

\nThe remainder of the Flock of mountain sheep or Ibex, ran up the steep Clifts, out of Gunshot, and to such a heighth as is most incredible\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 The Mountain sheep that was killed, rolled down the Hill, and we got it.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 We stopped at this place to dine, which was amongst the high Clifts, and it being Captain Clarkes birth day; he ordered some flour to be served out to the party, which with the mountain Sheep made us an excellent meal,\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 We proceeded on at 3 o'Clock P. M. and passed by some Trees, growing along the Shore; which resembled in look the Cedar Tree, but it was what is called the Wild Juniper, This Tree afforded a most delightfull shade. \u00a0\u00a0 we also passed very high rugged Clifts, lying on both sides of the River, and a very bad Rapid, at the upper end of an Island; where the Tow Rope broke of the Canoe, that Captain Clarke was on board & it had nearly upset. \u00a0\u00a0 We came by a Run or Creek, which lies on the South side of the River, and came in at a Valley, & bottoms of timber'd land, the Mouth of a large Creek, and a large Spring lying also on the South side of the River, We came 13\u00bd Miles this day & encamped opposite this spring, in a bottom covered with Cotton wood Timber, & thick Brush.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 We saw a White or brown Bear on the hills some distance from our Camp. \u00a0\u00a0 The hunters who were on Shore since morning, returned to us, and had killed 5 Deer which we took on board the Canoes & also 2 Elk which Captain Lewis & party had killed and left at this place for us, We also saw Snow on the Mountains, a short distance to the South of our Camp.\u2014\n\n\n\n

14. During late Tertiary and early Quaternary time, the Jefferson River cut through this area leaving an imposing, rugged canyon. The exposed rocks are primarily light gray to yellow-gray Mississippian limestones, but sandstones and shales ranging in age from Pennsylvanian through Cretaceous are also present. Dark gray sedimentary rocks of Precambrian age are exposed along the upper portion of the canyon. (back)
