\n August 1, 1806\n

\n
\n \n

[Lewis]

\n \n
\n Friday August 1st 1806.\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nThe rain still continuing I set out early as usual and proceeded on at a good rate. \u00a0\u00a0 at 9 A. M. we saw a large brown bear swiming from an island to the main shore we pursued him and as he landed Drewyer and myself shot and killed him; we took him on board the perogue and continued our rout. \u00a0\u00a0 at 11 A. M. we passed the entrance of Mussel shell river. [1] \u00a0\u00a0 at 1 in the evening we arrived at a bottom on S. W. side where there were several spacious Indian lodges built of sticks and an excellent landing. as the rain still continued with but little intermission and appearances seemed unfavorable to it's becomeing fair shortly, I determined to halt at this place at least for this evening and indeavour to dry my skins of the bighorn which had every appearance of spoiling, an event which I would not should happen on any consideration as we have now passed the country in which they are found and I therefore could not supply the deficiency were I to loose these I have. I halted at this place being about 15 ms. below Missel shell river, [2] had fires built in the lodges and my skins exposed to dry. \u00a0\u00a0 shortly after we landed the rain ceased tho' it still continued cloudy all this evening. \u00a0\u00a0 a white bear came withint 50 paces of our camp before we perceived it; it stood erect on it's hinder feet and looked at us with much apparent unconsern, we seized our guns which are always by us and several of us fired at it and killed it. \u00a0\u00a0 it was a female in fine order, we fleesed it and extracted several gallons of oil. \u00a0\u00a0 this speceis of bar are rearly as poor at this season of the year as the common black bear nor are they ever as fat as the black bear is found in winter; as they feed principally on flesh, like the wolf, they are most fatt when they can procure a sufficiency of food without rispect to the season of the year. the oil of this bear is much harder than that of the black bear being nearly as much so as the lard of a hog. \u00a0\u00a0 the flesh is by no means as agreeable as that of the black bear, or Yahkah or partycoloured bear [3] of the West side of the rocky mountains. \u00a0\u00a0 on our way today we killed a buck Elk in fine order the skins and a part of the flesh of which we preserved. \u00a0\u00a0 after encamping this evening the hunters killed 4 deer and a beaver. The Elk are now in fine order particularly the males. \u00a0\u00a0 their horns have obtained their full growth but have not yet shed the velvet or skin which covers them. \u00a0\u00a0 the does are found in large herds with their young and a few young bucks with them. \u00a0\u00a0 the old bucks yet herd together in parties of two to 7 or 8.\u2014\n\n

[Clark]

\n \n
\n Sunday 1st of August 1806.\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nWe Set out early as usial the wind was high and ahead which caused the water to be a little rough and delayed us very much \u00a0\u00a0 aded to this we had Showers of rain repeetedly all day at the intermition of only a fiew minits between them. My Situation a very disagreeable one. \u00a0\u00a0 in an open Canoe wet and without a possibility of keeping my Self dry. \u00a0\u00a0 the Country through which we passed is in every respect like that through which I passed yesterday. The brooks have all Some water in them from the rains which has fallen. \u00a0\u00a0 this water is excessively muddy. Several of those brooks have Some trees on their borders as far as I can See up them. I observe Some low pine an cedar [4] on the Sides of the rugid hills on the Stard. Side, and Some ash timber [5] in the high bottoms. \u00a0\u00a0 the river has more Sand bars today than usial, and more Soft mud. \u00a0\u00a0 the current less rapid. \u00a0\u00a0 at 2 P. M. I was obliged to land to let the Buffalow Cross over. \u00a0\u00a0 not withstanding an island of half a mile in width over which this gangue of Buffalow had to pass and the Chanel of the river on each Side nearly \u00bc of a mile in width, this gangue of Buffalow was entirely across and as thick as they could Swim. \u00a0\u00a0 the Chanel on the Side of the island the went into the river was crouded with those animals for \u00bd an hour. \u00a0\u00a0 [NB: I was obliged to lay to for an hour] the other Side of the island for more than 3/4 of an hour. I took 4 of the men and killed 4 fat Cows for their fat and what portion of their flesh the Small Canoes Could Carry \u00a0\u00a0 that which we had killed a few days ago being nearly Spoiled from the wet weather. \u00a0\u00a0 encamped on an Island Close to the Lard Shore. [6] \u00a0\u00a0 two gangues of Buffalow Crossed a little below us, as noumerous as the first.

[Ordway]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nFriday August 1st 1806. \u00a0\u00a0 hard rain \u00a0\u00a0 we Set out early and procd. on \u00a0\u00a0 about 9 A M a Brown bear and Drewyer and Capt. Lewis killed it \u00a0\u00a0 we took it on board and procd. on \u00a0\u00a0 Colter killed a beaver Shortly after \u00a0\u00a0 about noon we killed a buck Elk \u00a0\u00a0 Saved the hide and the best of the meat. \u00a0\u00a0 about 2 P. M. we halted at Some old Indn. Lodges on S. Side. \u00a0\u00a0 here we delayd. [16] this afternoon to dry our deer Skins Mountn. Sheep Skins &. C. \u00a0\u00a0 which were near Spoiling as the weather has been Some time wet. \u00a0\u00a0 about 3 P. M. a large white bear approached our Camp \u00a0\u00a0 as Soon as he discovred us Stood up on his hind feed and looked at us. Some of the hunters Shot him down, for the Skin & oil. \u00a0\u00a0 the other hunters killd. four deer and a beaver, this afternoon.\u2014\n\n

[Gass]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Friday 1st of Aug. 1806. \u00a0\u00a0 We embarked early in a wet disagreeable morning, and in a short time saw a large brown or grizly bear swimming in the river, which we killed, and took on board; passed the mouth of Muscle shoal river; [17] and at noon halted to dine at some old Indian lodges. Captain Lewis being afraid, from the dampness of the weather, that the skins he had procured of these big-horned animals would spoil, thought it adviseable to stay here this afternoon, and dry them by a fire in these old lodges; and some of the men went out to hunt. About an hour after we landed here, a large bear came so close to our camp, that one of the men [18] shot and killed it from our fire. In the evening our hunters came in and had killed several deer. The afternoon was cloudy with some rain; and having made a fire and put the skins to dry with two men to attend them, made our arrangements \nfor the night.\n\n\n

2. This camp was in Petroleum or Phillips County, some two to three miles below the camp of May 19, 1805, just above what was later called Horseshoe Point. The area is now inundated by Fort Peck Reservoir. They remained here until August 3. Atlas map 38; MRC map 68. (back)
\n
3. The Yahkah is the cinnamon phase of the black bear; see May 31 and June 20, 1806. Here Lewis seems to use \"partycolored\" for the same bear, but perhaps he means the grizzly, which he calls the \"variagated\" bear on May 14, 1806. (back)
\n
4. Limber pine and probably Rocky Mountain juniper, or possibly creeping juniper. Both limber pine and Rocky Mountain juniper are commonly found together along the low-elevation foothills of the Montana Rockies and in isolated populations on the eastern Great Plains. (back)
\n
5. Green ash as noticed by Lewis on August 7. (back)
\n
6. The island on which Clark camped is in Dawson County, Montana, just below the mouth of Cottonwood Creek in Wibaux County, a nameless stream on Atlas maps 112 and 122. (back)
\n
7. It was probably Coues who underlined in pencil some words in this table; the underlinings have not been kept. (back)
\n
8. \"Pine Brook\" on Atlas map 121, present Sand Creek entering the Yellowstone from the east in Dawson County. (back)
\n
9. Upper Sevenmile Creek meets the Yellowstone in Dawson County a mile or so above present Glendive; it is nameless on Atlas map 121. (back)
\n
10. \n Cains Coulee in Dawson County at Glendive, a nameless stream just below the \"High black hills\" on Atlas map 121. Clark apparently missed the more prominent Glendive Creek a few miles ahead. (back)
\n
11. The Ludlow Member of the Fort Union Formation at the mouth of Morgan Creek. (back)
\n
12. The buffalo crossing appears to be misplaced on Atlas maps 112 and 122; it should be at the bend above \"Buffalow Creek,\" present Thirteenmile Creek, in Dawson County. (back)
\n
13. \n Thirteenmile Creek; see earlier note in this entry. Atlas maps 112, 122. (back)
\n
14. \n Cottonwood Creek; see earlier note in this entry. (back)
\n
15. A weather observation at the end of Clark's Codex M, placed here by date. (back)
\n
16. The party's camp was in Petroleum or Phillips County, some two or three miles below\nthe camp of May 19, 1805, just above what was later called Horseshoe Point. The area is now inundated by Fort Peck Reservoir. Lewis says they were about fifteen miles below the mouth of the Musselshell River. They remained here until August 3. (back)
\n
17. \n Musselshell River, on the Petroleum-Garfield county line, Montana. (back)
