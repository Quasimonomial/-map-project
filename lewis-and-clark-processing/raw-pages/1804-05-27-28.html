\n May 27th Sunday 1804

\nas we were pushing off this Morning two Canoos Loaded with fur &c. Came to from the Mahars [NB: Mahar\n ] nation, [NB: living 730 miles above on the Missouri\n ] which place they had left two months, at about 10 oClock 4 Cajaux or rafts loaded with furs and peltres came too one from the \n Paunees\n , [NB: Paunees on the river Platt\n ] the other from Grand Osage, [5] they informed nothing of Consequence, passed a Creek on the Lbd Side Called \n ash Creek\n \n [6] 20 yds wide, passed the upper point of a large Island [7] on the Stbd Side back of which Comes in three Creeks one Called Orter Creek, [8] her[e] the men [9] we left hunting Came in \u00a0\u00a0 we camped on a Willow Island in the mouth of Gasconnade River. George Shannon Killed a Deer this evening

[Gass]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Sunday 27th. \u00a0\u00a0 We passed Ash creek where there are high cliffs on the south side, and at five in the afternoon arrived at the mouth of Gaskenade river. On the south side one of our party killed a deer. We encamped for the night on an island opposite the mouth of Gaskenade river. This is a very handsome place,\u2014a rich soil and pleasant country:\n\n

River.\u2014

\n

\nSunday May 27th \u00a0\u00a0 This morning we set out early, having fine weather, passed a creek, called Ash Creek, laying on the North side of the River. \u00a0\u00a0 On the south side of the River lay high Clifts. \u00a0\u00a0 we proceeded on, and in the afternoon we arrived at the Mouth of the Gasconade River; laying on the south side of the Mesouri. \u00a0\u00a0 We encamped on an Island opposite the Mouth of said River. \u00a0\u00a0 The Gasconade River lies in Latitude 38\u00b0 44' 35 North, and course of the River Mesouri being still nearly West. \u00a0\u00a0 The Gasconade River is 104 Miles from the River due De Bois (or Wood River) & lies in Latitude 38\u00b0 44' 35S North. [13]\n

\n
1. To the side of this entry and covered by it is a column of barely legible numbers that appear to be the previous day's distances, with a total of 18. (back)
\n
2. \n Cajeu (plural, cajeux) was a French-Canadian term for a small raft, especially one made by lashing together two canoes. McDermott (GMVF), 41. (back)
\n
3. At this point there is a small sketch under the entry, of document 15, showing a river bend and an island. (back)
\n
4. The Gasconade River enters the Missouri at present Gasconade, Gasconade County, Missouri. MRC map 5; MRM map 13. (back)
\n
5. These men had been trading with the Great (or Grand) Osages, probably on the Osage River in Missouri. (back)
\n
6. Probably later Frame, or Frene, Creek, entering the Missouri at present Hermann, Gasconade County, Mo. . MRC map 5; MRM map 13. (back)
\n
7. Probably L'Outre (more properly \n la loutre\n in French) Island. MRC map 5. (back)
\n
8. The literal translation of its early name, Rivi\u00e8re \u00e0 la Loutre, in Montgomery County, Missouri. Ibid. (back)
\n
9. \n George Drouillard and John Shields; see above, May 26. (back)
\n
10. \n Gasconade River at Gasconade, Gasconade County. (back)
\n
11. White-tailed deer, Odocoileus virginianus. (back)
\n
12. There is no ready explanation for Whitehouse using this later date. It seems inconceivable that he would be rewriting the journal in 1806 and get his dates confused. (back)
\n
13. The latitude and mileage do not appear in the original version of Whitehouse's journal, but they agree with Clark's figures; see Clark's Field Notes entry for August 17, 1804. The sentence appears to have been crowded in between entries. (back)
