\n November 21, 1805\n

\n
\n \n

[Clark]

\n \n
\n November 21st Thursday 1805\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\na Cloudy morning \u00a0\u00a0 most of the Indians left us, The nation on the opposit Side is Small & Called \n Clap-sott,\n [1] Their great chief name \n Stil-la-sha\n \u2014The nation liveing to the North is Called \n Chieltz.\n [2] \u00a0\u00a0 The chief is name \n Malaugh\n \u00a0\u00a0 not large nation and wore \u2329their\u232a his beards as informed by the Inds. \u00a0\u00a0 In my absence the hunters Kild. 7 Deer, 4 brants & a Crane.

\n

\nGreat numbers of the dark brant passing Southerley, the white yet Stationary, no gees & Swan to be Seen. The wind blew hard from the S. E. which with the addition of the flood tide raised emence Swells & waves which almost entered our Encampment \u00a0\u00a0 morng. dark & Disagreeable, a Supriseing Climent. We have not had One cold day Since we passed below the last falls or great Shute & Some time before \u00a0\u00a0 the Climent is temperate, and the only change we have experienced is from fair weather to rainey windey weather\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 I made a chief & gave a medel \u00a0\u00a0 this man is name \n Tow-w\u00e2ll\n and appears to have Some influence with the nation and tells me he lives at the great Shute\u2014 [3] \u00a0\u00a0 we gave the Squar a Coate of Blue Cloth for the belt of Blue Beeds we gave for the Sea otter Skins purchased of an Indian. \u00a0\u00a0 at 12 oClock it began to rain, and continued moderately all day, Some wind from the S. E., waves too high for us to proceed on our homeward bound journey. Latitude of this place is 46\u00b0 19' 11 1/10\" North \u00a0\u00a0 Several Indians and Squars came this evening I beleave for the purpose of gratifying the passions of our men, Those people appear to View \u2329horedom\u232a Sensuality as a necessary evile, and do not appear to abhore this as Crime in the unmarried females. The young women Sport openly with our men, and appear to receive the approbation of their friends & relations for So doing \u00a0\u00a0 maney of the women are handsom. They are all low both men and women, I saw the name of J. Bowmon marked or picked on a young Squars left arm. The women of this nation Pick their legs in different figures as an orniment. \u00a0\u00a0 the[y] were their hair loose, Some trinkets in their ears, none in the nose as those above, their Dress is as follows, i,e the men, were a roabe of either the skins of [blank] a Small fured animal, & which is most common, or the Skins of the Sea orter, Loon, Swan, Beaver, [4] Deer, Elk, or blanketes either red, blu, or white, which roabes cover the sholders arms & body, all other parts are nakd.

\n

\nThe women were a Short peticoat of the iner bark of the white Ceder or Arber Vita, which hang down loose in Strings nearly as low as the knee, with a Short Robe which fall half way down the Thigh. \u00a0\u00a0 no other part is Covered. The orniments are beeds, Blue principally, large Brass wire around their rists Som rings, and maney men have Salors Clothes, many have good fusees & Ball & Powder\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 The women ware a String of Something curious tied tight above the anckle, all have large Swelled legs & thighs \u00a0\u00a0 The men Small legs & thighs and Generally badly made\u2014 [5] They live on Elk Deer fowls, but principally fish and roots of 3 Kinds, Lickorish, Wapto &c. The women have more privaleges than is Common amongst Indians\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 Pocks & Venerial [X: venereous] is Common amongst them [6] \u00a0\u00a0 I Saw one man & one woman who appeared to be all in Scabs, & Several men with the venereal, their other Disorders and the remides for them I could not lern \u00a0\u00a0 we divided Some ribin between the men of our party to bestow on their favourite Lasses, this plan to Save the knives & more valuable articles.

\n

\nThose people gave me Sturgion Salmon & wapto roots, & we bought roots, \u2329high bush Cranberies,\u232a Some mats &c. &c. for which we were obliged to give emence prices\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 we also purchased a kind of Cranberry [7] which the Indians Say the geather in the low lands, off of Small either vines or bushes just abov the ground\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 we also purchased hats made of Grass &c. [8] of those Indians, Some very handsom mats made of flags\u2014 [9] \u00a0\u00a0 Some fiew curious baskets made of a Strong weed & willow or [blank] Splits\u2014 [10], also a Sweet Soft black root, [11] about th Sise & Shape of a Carrot, this root they Value verry highly\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 The Wapto root is Scerce, and highly valued by those people, this root they roste in hot ashes like a potato and the outer Skin peals off, tho this is a trouble they Seldom perform.\n

[Clark]

\n \n
\n Thursday November 21st 1805\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\na cloudy morning \u00a0\u00a0 most of the Chinnooks leave our Camp and return home, great numbers of the dark brant passing to the South, the white Brant have not yet commenced their flight. The wind blew hard from the S. E. which with the addition of the flood tide raised verry high waves which broke with great violence against the Shore throwing water into our Camp\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 the fore part of this day Cloudy \u00a0\u00a0 at 12 oClock it began to rain and Continued all day moderately, Several Indians Visit us to day of different nations or Bands Some of the \n Chiltz\n Nation who reside on the Sea Coast near Point Lewis, Several of the \n Clotsops\n who reside on the opposit Side of the Columbia imediately opposit to us, and a Chief from the Grand rapid to whome we gave a Medal.

\n

\nAn old woman & wife to a Cheif of the \n Chinnooks\n \n [12] came and made a Camp near ours \u00a0\u00a0 She brought with her 6 young Squars [NB: her daughters & nieces] I believe for the purpose of gratifying the passions of the men of our party and receving for those indulgiences Such Small as She (the old woman) thought proper to accept of, Those people appear to view Sensuality as a Necessary evel, and do not appear to abhor it as a Crime in the unmarried State\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 The young females are fond of the attention of our men and appear to meet the sincere approbation of their friends and connections, for thus obtaining their favours; the womin of the Chinnook Nation have handsom faces low and badly made with large legs & thighs which are generally Swelled from a Stopage of the circulation in the feet (which are Small) by maney Strands of Beeds or curious Strings which are drawn tight around the leg above the anckle, their leges are also picked with different figures, I Saw on the left arm of a Squar the following letters \n J. Bowmon, all those are Considered by the natives of this quarter as handsom deckerations, and a woman without those deckorations is Considered as among the lower Class \u00a0\u00a0 they ware their hair lose hanging over their back and Sholders maney have blue beeds \u2329in\u232a threaded & hung from different parts of their ears and about their neck and around their wrists, their dress other wise is prosisely like that of the Nation of \n Wa ci a cum\n as already discribed. \u00a0\u00a0 a Short roab, and tissue or kind of peticoat of the bark of Cedar which fall down in Strings as low as the knee behind and not So low before \u00a0\u00a0 maney of the men have blankets of red blue or Spotted Cloth or the common three & 2\u00bd point blankets, [13] and Salors old clothes which they appear to prise highly, they also have robes of Sea Otter, Beaver, Elk, Deer, fox and Cat [14] common to this countrey, which I have never Seen in the U States. They also precure a roabe from the nativs above, which is made of the Skins of a Small animal [15] about the Size of a Cat, which is light and dureable and highly prized by those people\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 the greater numbers of the men of the Chinnoks have Guns and powder and Ball\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 The Men are low homely and badly made, Small Crooked legs \u2329and Small thighs\u232a large feet, and all of both Sects have flattened heads\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 The food of this nation is principally fish & roots \u00a0\u00a0 the fish they precure from the river by the means of nets and gigs, and the Salmon which run up the Small branches together with what they collect drifted up on the Shores of the Sea coast near to where they live\u2014

\n

\nThe roots which they use are Several different kinds, the Wappato which they precure from the nativs above, a black root which they call Shaw-na t\u00e2h que\n [16] & the wild licquorish is the most Common, they also kill a fiew Elk Deer & fowl\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 maney of the Chinnooks appear to have venerious and pustelus disorders. \u00a0\u00a0 one woman whome I saw at the beech appeared all over in Scabs and ulsers &c.

\n

\nwe gave to the men each a pece of ribin \u00a0\u00a0 We purchased Cramberies Mats verry netely made of flags and rushes, Some roots, Salmon and I purchased a hat made of Splits & Strong grass, which is made in the fashion which was common in the U States two years ago also Small baskets to hold Water made of Split and Straw, for those articles we gave high prices\u2014. [17]\n

[Ordway]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nThursday 21st Nov. 1805. \u00a0\u00a0 a cloudy and a little rain \u00a0\u00a0 The Latitude of Hailys bay or at our Incampment at the point above is 46\u00b0 19' 11 7/10\" Min North. \u00a0\u00a0 the Natives value their Sea otter Skins verry high. \u00a0\u00a0 our officers being anxious to purchase a robe made of two of those animels, they offered great prices in cloaths trinkets &C. but they would not take any thing except blue beeds. \u00a0\u00a0 at length they purchasd the Robe for a beeded belt which our Intrepters Squaw had \u00a0\u00a0 these animels are Scarse & hard to kill.\n\n

[Gass]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Thursday 21st. \u00a0\u00a0 A cloudy morning. About 8 o'clock, all the natives left us. The wind blew so violent to day, and the waves ran so high, that we could not set out on our return, which it is our intention to do as soon as the weather and water will permit. The season being so far advanced, we wish to establish our winter quarters as soon as possible. One of the natives here had a robe of sea-otter skins, of the finest fur I ever saw; which the Commanding Officers wanted very much, and offered two blankets for it, which the owner refused, and said he would not take five. He wanted beads of a blue colour, of which we had none, but some that were on a belt belonging to our interpreter's squaw; so they gave him the belt for the skins. In the evening more of the natives came to our camp, and the night was very wet and disagreeable.\n\n

[Whitehouse]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nThursday Novemr 23d [18] \u00a0\u00a0 A Cloudy morning, and a light sprinkling of rain fell. \u00a0\u00a0 The Indians all left our Camp. \u00a0\u00a0 Two of our hunters left the Camp to go out ahunting. \u00a0\u00a0 The Swell in the River ran so high that it detain'd us, at our Camp from going up the River again, to look out for Winter Quarters, \nwhich our officers intended as soon as the Weather would permit, and the Season of the Year advancing made it absolutely necessary that it should be the case.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 The Season of the Year, is generally cold at this place, but at \nthe present time it was very pleasant. \u00a0\u00a0 We are now convinced that the wide part of the River, or bay we entered into a few days past; is a bay only, and is called \n Haleys bay\n \u00a0\u00a0 the Latitude is as follows of the point above the said Bay, & Bay; both these lying in the same Latitude which is 46\u00b0 19' 7S North. \u00a0\u00a0 The \nIndians here, set a high value on the Sea Otter skins. \u00a0\u00a0 Our officers were very anxious to purchase \u2329an\u232a a Robe made out of the Skins of two of these animals. \u00a0\u00a0 They offered the Indians a great price in Cloths & trinkets for it; but they refused their offer, & would take nothing but beads for them. \u00a0\u00a0 They at last offered to let them have it for 5 New blankets, which our Officers would not give them. \u00a0\u00a0 They at last purchas'd it from them for a Belt which had a number of beads on it, which our officers procured from the Indian woman our Interpreter, which we got at the Mandan Nation, as Interpreter to the Snake nation; who is still with us. \u00a0\u00a0 I mention this in Order to show the high value that they set on these Skins, which were very beautiful.\u2014

\n

\nI also mention this circumstance, in order, to show the very high value, they also set on Beads. \u00a0\u00a0 The Sea Otter is plenty, between this and the great falls of the Columbia River; but are very difficult to be got. \u00a0\u00a0 They are rarely \nto be caught in traps, & when shot they sink immediately, which makes the procuring of them so difficult. \u00a0\u00a0 The evening was rainey & a number of Indians both Male & female came to our Camp this evening.\u2014\n\n\n\n

\n \n \n
\n
1. The Clatsops, for whom the party's winter quarters on the coast were named, occupied the south bank of the Columbia River from Point Adams at the river's mouth and upstream as far as Tongue Point, and south along the Oregon coast to Tillamook Head, all in Clatsop County, Oregon. Atlas map 82; Hodge, 1:305; Berreman, 15; Taylor (CkI); Hajda, 102\u20134. Their name derives from Chinookan\n \u0142ac'\u01ddp or \u0142ak'\u00edlak (two dialectical variants), \"those who have pounded salmon,\" whie one of their main villages on Point Adams was called \u0142iak'\u00edlaki(x), \"where there is pounded salmon.\" Silverstein. The Clatsops and the Chinooks across the river spoke dialects which were practically identical and which together comprise the Lower Chinook language. These languages were distinct from the related but mutually unintelligible Upper Chinook language spoken by Chinookan peoples upriver. Although they possessed many skills which the newcomers admired, many aspects of their culture aroused negative reactions in Anglo-Americans and British. The ambivalence of the captains was far from unique. The chief's name as given here does not appear again. The name is not recorded on the undated list placed with entries of January 1, 1806. Perhaps it was an error in communication. (back)
\n
2. The Chehalis, or more precisely the Lower Chehalis, were a Salish-speaking people living in Grays Harbor and Pacific counties on the southern Washington coast. Their term is apparently c\u0313x\u0323i'ls, from Chinookan\n gi\u0142ac\u0313x\u0323i'ls, \"Lower Chehalis\n Salish people.\" Their Salish name is \u02a6\u0169'\u01ddx\u0323il'\u01dds, \"sand,\" for a large and important village at Westport on Grays Harbor. Lower Chehalis territory centered around Grays Harbor and extended southward to Willapa Bay, where the north shore was claimed by both the Lower Chehalis and the Chinooks proper. Hodge, 1:241; Spier, 29\u201331; Ray (LCEN), 36; Swanton, 415\u201316; Taylor (CsI); Hajda, 96; Silverstein. \"Ma-laugh\" may be the same as \"Mar-lock-ke\" in an undated entry at January 1, 1806. His name is not identifiable linguistically. (back)
\n
3. A vertical line runs through these words in the Elkskin-bound Journal, beginning with \"I made a chief.\" Also in a passage below, from \"otter skins\" to \"homeward bound journey.\" Tow-w\u00e2ll (\n T\u00fawal\n ) is unknown. (back)
\n
4. American beaver, Castor canadensis.\n (back)
\n
5. One word is added interlinearly near here in the Elkskin-bound Journal but is difficult to read. It appears in the same hand and ink as \"venereous\" below and may be \"Perstetus\" for \"prostitutes.\" (back)
\n
6. The pox, or syphilis, and venereal disease. (back)
\n
7. Wild cranberry, Vaccinium oxycoccos L., much used by the natives of the lower Columbia. The cranberry was plentiful in the Lower Chinook territory and was an item of trade. Hitchcock et al., 4:34\u201335; Gunther (EWW), 45; Ray (LCEN), 122; Swan (NC), 89. (back)
\n
8. These hats were made of cedar bark and beargrass; see illustrations of the conical style in figs. 10, 11, 21, 22. Clark's brief notes here about the ethnobotany of the Lower Chinooks are treated more expansively by Lewis during the winter at Fort Clatsop. Cutright (LCPN), 264\u201369. (back)
\n
9. Flags are again the common cat-tail, and woven mats of the plant were widely used by coastal Indians. Gunther (EWW), 21. (back)
\n
10. Here Clark does not clearly distinguish between baskets for cooking, woven watertight of cedar bark and beargrass, and other baskets, less closely woven of the same materials or of rushes, grasses, and sedges. Split willow stems or willow bark, as well as split spruce roots, were often used with rushes for making baskets. Gunther (EWW), 17, 26\u201327; Ruby & Brown (CITC), 15; Cutright (LCPN), 267\u201368. See also Swan (ICF), as a source for the material culture of Northwest Coast Indians. (back)
\n
11. Edible thistle, Cirsium edule Nutt., a species new to science. See below, January 21, 1806, for Lewis's description. Hitchcock et al., 5:137\u201338; Ray (LCEN), 120; Cutright (LCPN), 264, 274, 406. (back)
\n
12. Evidently the wife of Delashelwilt. See below, March 15, 1806. Ruby & Brown (CITC), 106. (back)
\n
13. A 2\u00bd point blanket would be 5 feet 4 inches by 4 feet 3 inches; see August 20, 1805. (back)
\n
14. Probably the Oregon bobcat, Lynx rufus fasciatus, a subspecies then unknown to science. See below, February 18 and 21, 1806. Burroughs, 92\u201393; Cutright (LCPN), 273, 387, 442; Hall, 2:1053. (back)
\n
15. The mountain beaver, Aplodontia rufa,\n Lewis and Clark's \"sewelel.\" A rodent, not a beaver, it was then unknown to science. See below, February 26, 1806. Cutright (LCPN), 263, 271, 387, 439; Hall, 1:334\u201336. (back)
\n
16. The term may be Lower Chinookan\n [i|a]\u0161an\u00e1ta(n)qi, \"thistle\" for the edible thistle above. Gibbs (AVC), 19. (back)
\n
17. It is apparent that strong weed (of first entry), strong grass, and straw all refer to beargrass which was used to weave both watertight hats as well as baskets. Swan (NC), 162\u201363; Gunther (EWW), 23. There is a vertical line through the botany passage, perhaps done by Biddle but not in his red ink. The hat mentioned here is not the typical, conical style of hat worn by the natives (see below, December 29, 1805, and January 29, 30, 1806). It is probably one like that pictured in Gunther (ILNC), 185. The popular hat of the United States was simimilar to a top hat, but with a lower crown and a wider brim. See Lewis's entry, January 19, 1806. (back)
\n
18. Again a misdate, this time for November 21. (back)
