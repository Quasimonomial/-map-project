\n October 10, 1804\n

\n
\n \n

[Clark]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n 10th of October 1804 \u00a0\u00a0 at 11 oClock the wind Shifted from S. E to N W. Mr. Taboe visited us\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 we hear that Some jealousy exists as to the Chiefs to be made\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 at 1 oclock the Cheifs all assembled under an orning near the Boat, and under the American Flag. \u00a0\u00a0 we Delivered a Similar Speech to those delivered the Ottoes & Sioux, made three Chiefs, one for each Village and gave them Clothes & flags\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 1s Chief is name \n Ka-ha-wiss assa\n \n lighting ravin \u00a0\u00a0 2d Chief \n Po-casse\n (Hay) & the 3rd \n Piaheto\n or Eagles Feather\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 after the Council was over we Shot the Air gun, which astonished them, & they all \u2329Departed\u232a left us, \u2329we\u232a I observed 2 Sioux in the Council one of them I had Seen below, they Came to interceed with the Ricaras to Stop us as we were told\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 the Inds. much astonished at my black Servent, who made him Self more turrible in thier view than I wished him to Doe as I am told telling them that before I cought him he was wild & lived upon people, young children was verry good eating \u00a0\u00a0 Showed them his Strength &c. &c.\u2014 [1] \u00a0\u00a0 Those Indians are not fond of Licquer of any Kind\u2014 [2]\n

\n \n
\n
\n \n

[Clark]

\n \n
10th of October Wednesday 1804. [3]\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\na fine forming wind from the S. E \u00a0\u00a0 at about 11 oclock the wind Shifted, to the N. W. \u00a0\u00a0 we prepare all things ready to Speak to the Indians, Mr. Tabo & Mr. Gravolin Came to brackfast with us \u00a0\u00a0 the Chiefs &. came from the lower Town, but none from the 2 upper Towns, which is the largest, we Continue to delay & waite for them \u00a0\u00a0 at 12 oClock Dispatchd Gravelin to envite them to Come down, we have every reason to believe that a jellousy exists between the Villages for fear of our makeing the 1st Cheif from the lower Village, at one oClock the Cheifs all assembled & after Some little Cerrimony the Council Commenced, we informd them what we had told the others before i' e' Ottoes & Seaux. \u00a0\u00a0 made 3 Cheif 1 for each Village. \u00a0\u00a0 gave them presents.

\n

\nafter the Council was Over we Shot the air guns [4] which astonished them much, the[y] then Departed and we rested Secure all night,

\n

\nThose Indians wer much astonished at my Servent, They never Saw a black man before, all flocked around him & examind. him from top to toe, he Carried on the joke and made himself more turibal than we wished him to doe. \u00a0\u00a0 (Thos Indians were not fond of Spirits Licquer. of any kind[)]\n

[Ordway]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Wednesday 10th Oct. \u00a0\u00a0 a pleasant morning. I went down to the village which was built on the Island. \u00a0\u00a0 found their lodges in this village about 60 in nomber and verry close compact. \u00a0\u00a0 in a round form large & warm covered \u00a0\u00a0 first after the wood is willows and Grass. Then a thick coat of Earth &.C\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 except the chimney hole which Goes out at center & top\u2014 [5] \u00a0\u00a0 they Raise considerable of Indian corn, beans pumkins Squasshes water millons a kind of Tobacco &.C. &.C. [6] \u00a0\u00a0 they Supply Several nations around them in corn as we are told. \u00a0\u00a0 their is a 2 frenchman who trade here, Mr. Tabbo\n [7] livees here now. \u00a0\u00a0 has Some Goods & trades with them for their peltry &.C. \u00a0\u00a0 we left one of our frenchman with Mr. Tabbow & took his Soon in his place. [8] \u00a0\u00a0 all things made ready to hold a counsel with the nation. \u00a0\u00a0 they have used us in the most friendly manner. Gave us corn & beans dryed pumkins & Squasshes &.C. &.C\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 Some of their women are verry handsome & clean &.C. &.C.\n\n

[Ordway]

\n \n
Wednesday October 10th 1804 [9]\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nA Journal continued from 90th page [10] at R. Ree villge. \u00a0\u00a0 10th Oct. About 2 oClock P. M. the chiefs & Warrirs of the Rick a Rees Nation assembled at our Camp under the american flag to Counsel with our Officers. Capt. Lewis read a Speech to them Giving them Good counsel &.C. \u00a0\u00a0 after the talk was inded to them three Guns was fired from our Bow peace. \u00a0\u00a0 then our officers Gave the three Chiefs Some presents & \u2329a\u232a 3 flags & each Chief a meddel. \u00a0\u00a0 their is 3 villages of this nation & three chiefs one at each village. [11] \u00a0\u00a0 our officers Gave Each an equal proportion of the Goods. Each an american flag, a red coat (& cocked hat & feathers) & Meddels as abo. mn. [mentioned] \u00a0\u00a0 Some Paint \u00a0\u00a0 they divided the Goods & paint among themselves & tobacco &.C. &.C \u00a0\u00a0 after all was over our Capts. Shot the air Gun. \u00a0\u00a0 they appeared to be astonished at the Site of it & the execution it would doe. \u00a0\u00a0 they were verry\nthankful to us for what they had Recd. from us, & Sd. that we were So Good that we must go where we pleased after they would have a talk tomorrow & Give us Some Corn &.C. \u00a0\u00a0 the chiefs Shook hands with [our?] officers in the most friendly manner, & returned to their villages. I & one man went to the 2nd village with them in the evening which is about 4 miles from the lower village. \u00a0\u00a0 the chief took us into his lodge which was verry large & their village is built nearly in the Same form as the lower village. \u00a0\u00a0 the chiefs wife Brought us a bowl full of Beans & corn. \u00a0\u00a0 we Eat Some of it. She then brought 3 more one after another of different kinds of victuls. \u00a0\u00a0 we Eat Some of each & found it verry Good. \u00a0\u00a0 we Smoaked a while with them. \u00a0\u00a0 they were verry friendly to us & Seemed to be desirous to talk with us & Scarcely kept their Eyes off us (we returned to camp late)

\n

[Gass]

\u00a0
\n \n

\n Wednesday 10th. \u00a0\u00a0 This day I went with some of the men to the lodges, about 60 in number. The following is a description of the form of these lodges and the manner of building them.

\n

\nIn a circle of a size suited to the dimensions of the intended lodge, they set up 16 forked posts five or six feet high, and lay poles from one fork to another. Against these poles they lean other poles, slanting from the ground, and extending about four inches above the cross poles: these are to receive the ends of the upper poles, that support the roof. They next set up four large forks, fifteen feet high, and about ten feet apart, in the middle of the area; and poles or beams between these. The roof poles are then laid on extending from the lower poles across the beams which rest on the middle forks, of such a length as to leave a hole at the top for a chimney. The whole is then covered with willow branches, except the chimney and a hole below to pass through. On the willow branches they lay grass and lastly clay. At the hole below they build a pen about four feet wide and projecting ten feet \nfrom the hut; and hang a buffaloe skin, at the entrance of the hut for a door. This labour like every other kind is chiefly performed by the squaws. They raise corn, beans, and tobacco. Their tobacco [14] is different from any I had before seen: it answers for smoking, but not for chewing. On our return, I crossed from the island to the boat, with two squaws in a buffaloe skin stretched on a frame made of boughs, wove together like a crate or basket for that purpose. [15]\n Captain Lewis and Captain Clarke held a Council with the Indians, and gave them some presents.\n\n\n

\n Wednesday 10th Oct. 1804.\n [16] \u00a0\u00a0 our officers held a counsel with the natives and gave them Some presents.

\n

\nWednesday October 10th \u00a0\u00a0 This morning some of our Men, with the Two frenchmen that stayed with us during last night, went off to the Indian Village, they found \u2329there to be\u232a that Village to contain 60 lodges in number, forming 16 Square \u2329in\u232a the whole forming a Circle of about 30 feet \u00a0\u00a0 \u2329they are\u232a \u00a0\u00a0 These lodges were about Six feet high\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 The lodges are constructed by laying poles from One fork to the other, and the whole is laid on, in the like manner and they had cover'd the Tops of them over with Willows and Grass, and a thick Coat of mud over all, and had left \u2329in\u232a a hole in the Top which served for a chimney\u2014and a place for a door, at the entry place.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 The\u2329ir\u232a labour is chiefly performed by their Squaws, The Men returned, with three bands of the Rick a Rees, being the whole Nation; and our Officers held a treaty with them on the bank of the River, and made them some presents, And gave them a talk, which they received & seemed highly pleased & They went back to their Villages in the Evening\u2014\n\n\n

1. Legends grew up about York among the Arikaras and also certain stereotypes were added by later writers because of such episodes. Betts (SY), 16\u201318, 58, 65\u201366, 69\u201370. (back)
\n
2. According to Biddle's account the captains offered the Arikaras whiskey, as was customary in such negotiations, but the chiefs refused, \"with this sensible remark that, they were surprised that their father should present to them a liquor which would make them fools.\" See October 18, 1805. Coues (HLC), 1:160. (back)
\n
3. On this date Ordway writes, \"we left one of our frenchman with Mr Tabbow & took his Soon in his place.\" It is unclear whether this is the son of Tabeau (who is not known to have had a son) or the son of the unnamed engag\u00e9. Nor is it clear whether the Frenchman intended to remain at the Arikara villages, leaving the expedition, or was only left at the lowest village temporarily. If the former, he was probably paid off in cash, like others discharged that fall, leaving no written record of his departure. Nothing further appears about the son. On February 28, 1805, Ordway notes that a \"Mr Roie\" came up to Fort Mandan from the Arikara villages with Gravelines. This could be Peter Roi, an expedition engag\u00e9, who may be the man left at the Arikaras. Roi could have been discharged at Fort Mandan, however\u2014the lack of a record of his discharge indicating payment in cash in the fall of 1804\u2014and then have gone down to the Arikara villages. (back)
