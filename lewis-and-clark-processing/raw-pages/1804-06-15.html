Osages) is Situated in Butifull Plain at the foot of Some riseing land, in front of their Villges next the river is a butifull bottom Plain in which they raised their Corn &c. \u00a0\u00a0 back of the Village the high Prarie extends back to the Osarge River, about 3 Ms. above & in view the Missouries Nation resided under the protection of the \n Osarges, after their nation was riducd by the Saukees below, [4] thos built their Village in the Same low Prarie and lived there many years, the war was So hot & both nations become So reduced that the Little Osage & a fiew of the Missoures moved & built a village 5 ms near the Grand Osage, [5] the rest of the Missoures went and took protection under the \n Otteaus\n [Otos] on Platt river\n

[Clark]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n15th, June, Friday 1804 \u00a0\u00a0 Set out early and had not proceeded far e'er we wheeled on a Sawyer which was near injuring us Verry much, passed a plain on the L. S. \u00a0\u00a0 a Small Isd. in the midle \u00a0\u00a0 the river riseing, water verry Swift \u00a0\u00a0 Passed a Creek on the L. S. \u00a0\u00a0 passed between two Islands, a verry bad place, Moveing Sands, we were nearly being Swallowed up by the roleing Sands over which the Current was So Strong that we Could not Stem it with our Sales under a Stiff breese in addition to our ores, we were Compelled to pass under a bank which was falling in, and use the Toe rope occasionally, Continued up pass two other Small Islands and Camped on the S. S. Nearly opposit the Antient Village of the \n Little Osarges\n and below the Antt. Village of the \n Missoures\n \u00a0\u00a0 both Situations in view an within three Ms. of each other, the \n Osage\n were Settled at the foot a hill in a butifell Plain which extends back quite to the Osage River, in front of the Vilg: Next to the river is an ellegent bottom Plain which extends Several miles in length \u00a0\u00a0 on the river in this low Prarie the \n Missouries\n lived after They were reduced by the \n Saukees\n [NB: Saukees\n ] at Their Town Some Dists. below. The little osage finding themselves much oppressed by the Saukees & other nations, left this place & built a village 5 ms. from the Grand Osarge\n \n Town about [blank] years ago. \u00a0\u00a0 a few of the Missoures accompanied them, the remainder of that nation went on the Otteaus on the River Platt. The River at this place is about \u23293\u232a 1 [NB: one] ms. wide \u00a0\u00a0 our hunters did not Come in this evening \u00a0\u00a0 the river beginning to fall

[Ordway]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n\nFriday June 15th 1804. \u00a0\u00a0 we Set out eairly \u00a0\u00a0 a fair day. \u00a0\u00a0 we passed high Land on South Side of the River \u00a0\u00a0 we passed Indian Creek\n [7] little above \u00a0\u00a0 we stopped at 12 O.C. to eat dinner & for Capt. Lewis to take the Meridan altitude &C\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 We camped on the N. Side of the R. \u00a0\u00a0 their is Beautiful high Good praries on the South Side &C\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 pleasantest place I have ever Seen. \u00a0\u00a0 their is five Islands & a nomber of Sand bars in the River about this place.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 and the Current is exceedingly Reapid all this day.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 the aforesaid prarie is called village La pero, formerly Ind. Town. [8]\n

[Gass]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Friday 15th. \u00a0\u00a0 We renewed our voyage at five in the morning, and had very rapid water. There is a beautiful Prairie on the south side and the land high. Mulberries [10] are in great abundance almost all along the river. We encamped on the north side, opposite an old Indian village.\n\n\n

[Whitehouse]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nFriday 15 \u00a0\u00a0 Got on Our way at the willow prarie \u00a0\u00a0 the wind S. E fresh \u00a0\u00a0 We Crouded Sail and Saild 16 miles \u00a0\u00a0 Campd at the Indian Settlement namd. little Zoe prarie \u00a0\u00a0 the hunters met us with four bears And three deer \u00a0\u00a0 the party drank a Drachm of whisky and Roe on\u2014

\n

\nFriday June 15th \u00a0\u00a0 This morning we left Willow point (or Willow Priari,[)] with a fair Wind from the South east, and in the evening encamped at an Old deserted Indian settlement (formerly belonging to the Caw Nation[)], [11] called the little Zoe Priari, where we were met by our hunters, who had kill'd four Bears and Three Deer, The Captains order'd a dram of whiskey, to be served to each man. \u00a0\u00a0 We had during this day, crouded all Sail and the distance we run being 16 Miles.\u2014\n\n\n\t\n

\n
1. \n Biddle's notation at the head of this sheet of the Field Notes (document 21) reads \"15 to 16.\" (back)
\n
2. A submerged tree with one end stuck in the mud, the other bobbing up and down in the current with a sawing motion, one of the great menaces to river navigation. Criswell, 75. (back)
\n
3. The Missouri Indian village that is identified as the Gumbo Point site. See entries for June 8 and 13, 1804, and Chapman (LOM). Nicollet shows an \"Old Village of Little Osages\" in the vicinity, although he may have relied on Clark's information. Nicollet (MMR), 362; MRC map 11. (back)
\n
4. The area below where the Missouris resided may be a reference to the Utz site, where they lived from prehistoric to early historic times. Bray (ETG). (back)
\n
5. They would have moved near one of the villages on the Big, or Grand, Osage River in west-central Missouri. Chapman (OOIT), 146, 279 (fig. 2), 280 (fig. 3); MRC map 11. (back)
\n
6. \n Lewis's observation from Codex O. (back)
\n
7. Perhaps Van Meter Ditch, Saline County, Missouri; mentioned only by Ordway and Floyd. (back)
\n
8. Only Ordway applies this name to what is apparently the Gumbo Point site, Saline County, where the party camped. Its meaning is unknown. See entries of Floyd and Clark for this day and also Clark for June 13. (back)
\n
9. \n Grand, or Great, Osage Indians. (back)
\n
10. Red mulberry, Morus rubra L. (back)
\n
11. Referring to the Kaw, or Kansa, Indians, but the site is generally considered a Missouri village. (back)
