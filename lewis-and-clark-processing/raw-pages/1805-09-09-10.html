\n September 9, 1805\n

\n
\n \n

[Lewis]

\n \n
\n Monday September 9th 1805\n [1]\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nSet out at 7 A M. this morning and proceeded down the Flathead river leaving it on our left, the country in the valley of this river is generally a prarie and from five to 6 miles wide \u00a0\u00a0 the growth is almost altogether pine principally of the longleafed kind, with some spruce and a kind of furr resembleing the scotch furr. [2] \u00a0\u00a0 near the wartercourses we find a small proportion of the narrow leafed cottonwood [3] some redwood honeysuckle [4] and rosebushes form the scant proportion of underbrush to be seen. \u00a0\u00a0 at 12 we halted on a small branch which falls in to the river on the E. side, where we breakfasted on a scant proportion of meat which we had reserved from the hunt of yesterday added to three geese which one of our hunters killed this morning. \u00a0\u00a0 two of our hunters have arrived, one of them brought with him a redheaded woodpecker of the large kind common to the U States. [5] \u00a0\u00a0 this is the first of the kind I have seen since I left the Illinois. \u00a0\u00a0 just as we were seting out Drewyer arrived with two deer. \u00a0\u00a0 we continued our rout down the valley about 4 miles and crossed the river; it is hear a handsome stream about 100 yards wide and affords a considerable quantity of very clear water, the banks are low and it's bed entirely gravel. \u00a0\u00a0 the stream appears navigable, but from the circumstance of their being no sammon in it I believe that there must be a considerable fall in it below. \u00a0\u00a0 our guide could not inform us where this river discharge itself into the columbia river, he informed us that it continues it's course along the mountains to the N. as far as he knew it and that not very distant from where we then were it formed a junction with a stream nearly as large as itself [6] which took it's rise in the mountains near the Missouri to the East of us and passed through an extensive valley generally open prarie which forms an excellent pass to the Missouri. \u00a0\u00a0 the point of the Missouri where this Indian pass intersects it, is about 30 miles above the \n gates of the rocky mountain,\n [7] or the place where the valley of the Missouri first widens into an extensive plain after entering the rockey mountains. \u00a0\u00a0 the guide informed us that a man might pass to the missouri from hence by that rout in four days. \u00a0\u00a0 we continued our rout down the W. side of the river about 5 miles further and encamped on a large creek which falls in on the West [8] \u00a0\u00a0 as our guide informes that we should leave the river at this place and the weather appearing settled and fair I determined to halt the next day rest our horses and take some scelestial Observations. \u00a0\u00a0 we called this Creek \n Travellers rest. \u00a0\u00a0 it is about 20 yards wide a fine bould clear runing stream \u00a0\u00a0 the land through which we passed is but indifferent a could white gravley soil. [9] \u00a0\u00a0 we estimate our journey of this day at 19 M.

[Gass]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Monday 9th. \u00a0\u00a0 The morning was fair, but cool; and we continued our journey down the river. The soil of the valley is poor and gravelly; and the high snow-topped mountains are still in view on our left: [13] Our course generally north a few degrees west. We halted at noon: on our way the hunters had killed 3 wild geese; so we have plenty of provisions at present. At 2 o'clock we again went forward, and crossed over the Flathead river, [14] about 100 yards wide, and which we called Clarke's river; passed through a close timbered bottom of about two miles, and again came into beautiful plains. The timber on this bottom is pitch pine. We travelled 19 miles and encamped on a large creek, which comes in from the south. Our hunters this day killed 3 deer.\n\n\n

\n Monday 9th Sept. 1805. \u00a0\u00a0 a cloudy cold morning, wind from the N. W. \u00a0\u00a0 we Set out as usal, and proceeded on down the valley. \u00a0\u00a0 Smooth pleasant plains, large pitch pine timber along the River. \u00a0\u00a0 no timber on the plains but they are covred with grass and wild hysop. \u00a0\u00a0 the Soil poor. \u00a0\u00a0 crossed Several branches on which is pine timber, also, a little cotton timber &c. \u00a0\u00a0 the Snow continues on the Mount. each Side of the valley. \u00a0\u00a0 about 11 oClock we halted at a branch to dine \u00a0\u00a0 one of the hunters had killed three geese and a wood pecker. \u00a0\u00a0 Capt. Clark killed 4 fessents or prarie hens. \u00a0\u00a0 we find wild or choke cherries along the branches. \u00a0\u00a0 we delayed about 2 hours and a half. \u00a0\u00a0 then proceeded on down the valley. \u00a0\u00a0 passed through a large bottom covred with handsom pitch pine timber, from that a pleasant plain the remainder of this day. \u00a0\u00a0 the afternoon pleasant, but the Snow Still continues on the Mountains as usal. \u00a0\u00a0 Came about [blank] Miles this day and Camped on a plain near a creek which runs in to the River about 2 mls. below. \u00a0\u00a0 our hunters all but one joined us \u00a0\u00a0 had killed 3 [15] Deer and Several ducks this day, &c. \u00a0\u00a0 course N. W. and North all day.\u2014

\n

\nMonday Septemr 9th \u00a0\u00a0 This morning was cold & the wind blew from the No. West. \u00a0\u00a0 We set out as usual, & proceeded on our Journey down the Valley, We passed along smooth plains, covered with high Grass & wild hysop, but the soil poor.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 There was no timber to be seen here excepting some pitch pine trees, which grew along the large Creek or river side. \u00a0\u00a0 We proceeded on, and crossed several branches on the sides of which grew Pitch pine & Cotton wood trees.\u2014

\n

\nThe Snow still continued on the Mountains, on both sides of the Valley. \u00a0\u00a0 About 11 o'Clock A. M we halted at a branch to dine, where one of our hunters had killed three Geese & a wood peckar, which he brought to us. \u00a0\u00a0 Captain Clark went out here to hunt & killed 4 Pheasants, which he brought to us. \u00a0\u00a0 we found here wild or choke cherries, growing along this branch\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 We delayed at this place about 2\u00bd hours and then proceeded on down this Valley & passed through a bottom covered with handsome pitch pine timber, and the remainder of this day we passed through pleasant plains. \u00a0\u00a0 The Snow continuing as usual on the Mountains on both sides of us. \u00a0\u00a0 We came about 20 Miles this day, & encamped on a plain, near a Creek which run into the River about 2 Miles below where we were encamped.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 Our hunters all but one joined us here. \u00a0\u00a0 They had killed 3 Deer & a number of ducks this day which they brought to us.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 our Course this day has been from North to North West.\u2014\n\n \t\t\t \n\n\n\n

1. Here begins Lewis's fragmentary Codex Fc, consisting of four pages torn from Codex P and covering September 9 and 10, 1805. See Appendix C. (back)
\n
2. The \"longleafed\" pine is ponderosa pine. The spruce is Engelmann spruce, Picea engelmannii Parry. See September 12, 1805, where Clark refers to the latter as \"spruce pine.\" The \"furr\" is probably Douglas fir, Pseudotsuga menziesii (Mirb.) Franco, which is typical of lower elevations in the Rocky Mountains. The \"scotch furr\" used for comparison may refer to either the cultivated European fir, Abies alba Mill., or one of the balsam fir species of the eastern United States. Little (CIH), 37-W, 80-W; Bailey, 113. (back)
\n
3. Probably the black cottonwood, Populus trichocarpa T. & G., which is the most common cottonwood species at lower elevations in Montana and Idaho. It is unclear whether Lewis was distinguishing between the two species of cottonwood which occur in the area (P. trichocarpa and P. angustifolia, the typically designated narrowleaf cottonwood), or did not observe this as a separate species. The narrowleaf cottonwood is more common at higher elevations; the black cottonwood has slightly broader leaves but is otherwise similar. Booth & Wright, 22; Little (CIH), 153-W. (back)
\n
4. Utah honeysuckle, Lonicera utahensis Wats., or bearberry honeysuckle, L. involucrata (Rich.) Banks ex Spreng., new to science and collected on the return trip. Cutright (LCPN), 210, 212, 261 n. 19, 410; Hitchcock et al. 4:458. (back)
\n
5. Burroughs considers this the red-headed woodpecker, Melanerpes erythrocephalus [AOU, 406], but Coues and Holmgren identify it as the pileated woodpecker, Dryocopus pileatus [AOU, 405]. Burroughs, 242; Coues (HLC), 2:590; Holmgren, 34. (back)
\n
6. The Bitterroot meets the Clark Fork, or Hellgate, River just west of present Missoula, Missoula County, Montana. The stream continues northwest as the Clark Fork. Atlas map 69. (back)
\n
7. In the vicinity of present Helena, Montana. The Hidatsas told them of this route, but they had not recognized its eastern approaches on the voyage up the Missouri. Appleman (LC), 171; Allen (PG), 302. (back)
\n
8. \n Lewis and Clark's\n Travelers' Rest Creek is now Lolo Creek. The camp was where they remained until September 11, in the vicinity of modern Lolo, Missoula County, perhaps one or two miles upstream from the Bitterroot River, on the south side of the creek. Peebles (LT), 3; Space, 4; Atlas map 69. (back)
\n
9. Although the east side of the Bitterroot Valley contains soils that are quite fertile, soils that are thin and gravelly or cobbly occur on parts of the alluvial valley bottom and on the benches east of it. Similar soils are found on alluvial fans such as the Burnt Fork fan. The white soil to which Lewis refers may be the moderately saline soils of the Burnt Fork fan, clay layers in Tertiary sediments exposed in cutbanks, or the thin wash of light-colored Tertiary clay that covers the ground in some places, especially north of Eightmile Creek. (back)
\n
10. See the note on Scattered Creek in the previous entry. (back)
\n
11. Probably a reference to Lewis's Codex Fc. See n. 1, above. (back)
\n
12. This note in Clark's hand, giving certain latitudes from the Marias River to Travelers' Rest, is on the front flyleaf of Codex G. It is placed here at Travelers' Rest, the last position given. (back)
\n
13. The Bitterroot Mountains, on the Montana-Idaho border. (back)
\n
14. The Bitterroot River. At this time they called it the Flathead River and, as Gass notes, later renamed it Clark's River. (back)
\n
15. The number \"3\" is written over \"2.\" (back)
