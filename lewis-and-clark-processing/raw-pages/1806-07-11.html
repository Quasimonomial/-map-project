\n July 11, 1806\n

\n
\n \n

[Lewis]

\n \n
\n July 11th 1806.\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nthe morning was fair and the plains looked beatifull \u00a0\u00a0 the grass much improved by the late rain. \u00a0\u00a0 the air was pleasant and a vast assemblage of little birds which croud to the groves on the river sung most enchantingly. \u00a0\u00a0 we set out early. I sent the hunters down Medicine river to hunt Elk and proceeded with the party across the plain to the white bear Islands which I found to be 8 ms. distant \u00a0\u00a0 my course S. 75 E.\u2014 through a level beautifull and extensive high plain covered with immence hirds of buffaloe.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 it is now the season at which the buffaloe begin to coppelate and the bulls keep a tremendious roaring we could hear them for many miles and there are such numbers of them that there is one continual roar. \u00a0\u00a0 our horses had not been acquainted with the buffaloe they appeared much allarmed at their appearance and bellowing. \u00a0\u00a0 when I arrived in sight of the whitebear Islands\n [1] the missouri bottoms on both sides of the river were crouded with buffaloe \u00a0\u00a0 I sincerely belief that there were not less than 10 thousand buffaloe within a circle of 2 miles arround that place. I met with the hunters at a little grove of timber opposite to the island where they had killed a cow [2] and were waiting our arrival. \u00a0\u00a0 they had met with no elk. I directed the hunters to kill some buffaloe as well for the benefit of their skins to enable us to pass the river as for their meat for the men I meant to leave at this place. \u00a0\u00a0 we unloaded our horses and encamped opposite to the Islands. \u00a0\u00a0 had the cow skined and some willows sticks collected to make canoes of the hides \u00a0\u00a0 by 12 OCk. they killed eleven buffaloe most of them in fine order. \u00a0\u00a0 the bulls are now generally much fatter than the cows and are fine beef. I sent out all hands with the horses to assist in buthering and bringing in the meat by 3 in the evening we had brought in a large quantity of fine beef and as many hides as we wanted for canoes \u2329covering\u232a shelters and geer. I then set all hands to prepare \u2329the\u232a two canoes \u2329in order to pass the river\u232a \u00a0\u00a0 the one we made after the mandan fassion with a single skin in the form of a bason [3] and the other we constructed of two skins on a plan of our own. we were unable to compleat our canoes this evening. \u00a0\u00a0 the wind blew very hard. \u00a0\u00a0 we continued our operations untill dark and then retired to rest. I intend giving my horses a couple of [4] days rest at this place and deposit all my baggage which is not necessary to my voyage up medicine [EC?: maria's] river.\n

\n

\n

\n
\n Lewis's Route from Traveler's Rest to the Missouri River,
\nca. July 3\u201311, 1806, Codex N, pp. 149\u201350\n
\n (American Philosophical Society library, \n
used with permission.)

[Clark]

\n \n
\n Friday 11th July 1806\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nSent on 4 of the best hunters in 2 Canoes to proceed on a fiew miles a head and hunt untill I came up with them, after an early brackfast I proceeded on down a very crooked Chanel, at 8 a. m I overtook one Canoe with a Deer which Collins had killed, at meridian passed Sergt. Pryors camp near a high point of land on the left Side which the Shoshones call the beavers head. [5] \u00a0\u00a0 the wind rose and blew with great violence from the S W imediately off Some high mountains Covered with Snow. \u00a0\u00a0 the violence of this wind retarded our progress very much and the river being emencly Crooked we had it imediately in our face nearly every bend. \u00a0\u00a0 at 6 P M I passed Phalanthrophy river which I proceved was very low. \u00a0\u00a0 the wind Shifted about to the N. E. and bley very hard tho' much wormer than the forepart of the day. \u00a0\u00a0 at 7 P M I arrived at the Enterance of Wisdom River and Encampd. in the Spot we had encamped the [NB: 6th] of August last. [6] \u00a0\u00a0 here we found a Bayonet which had been left & the Canoe quite safe. [7] \u00a0\u00a0 I directed that all the nails be taken out of this Canoe and paddles to be made of her Sides & here I came up with Gibson & Colter whome I had Sent on a head for the purpose of hunting this morning, they had killed a fat Buck and 5 young gees nearly grown. Wisdom river is very high and falling. I have Seen great Nos. of Beaver on the banks and in the water as I passed down to day, also some Deer and greater numbers young gees, Sand hill cranes &c. &c. Sgt. Pryor left a deer on the shore\n\n\n

\n \n
\n
\n \n

[Ordway]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nFriday 11th July 1806. \u00a0\u00a0 a fair morning. \u00a0\u00a0 we took breakfast eairly and Set off \u00a0\u00a0 proceeded on \u00a0\u00a0 the wind hard a head which is unfavourable to us. \u00a0\u00a0 in the evening we arived at the mouth of Wisdom River. Camped [8] where we left the Small canoe last year. \u00a0\u00a0 wisdom river is verry high at this time \u00a0\u00a0 2 hunters went out. Collins killed 2 deer and Gibson killed a fat buck this evening \u00a0\u00a0 the beaver Sign and lodges without number all this day. \u00a0\u00a0 they are pleantier in this valley than I have Seen on the route &C\n\n\n

\n \n
\n
\n \n

[Gass]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Friday 11th. \u00a0\u00a0 This was a fine morning, and we set out early to cross the point, [9] and having gone eight miles, came to the Missouri at the Bear islands, nearly opposite our old encampment. [10] Here our hunters, in a short time, killed five buffaloe; and we saved the best of the meat; and of the skins made two canoes [11] to transport ourselves and baggage across the river. The buffaloe are in large droves about this place.\n\n\n

\n \n
\n \n \n
\n
1. \n Lewis's party camped here on the west bank of the Missouri, in Cascade County, Montana, opposite the White Bear Islands (see June 18, 1805) and a little below the mouth of Sand Coulee Creek on the opposite bank (see fig. 4). Atlas maps 42, 54, 61. (back)
\n
2. The first buffalo, Bison bison, they had killed since July 16, 1805. (back)
\n
3. The hemispherical \"bullboat\" of the Mandans and Hidatsas; see October 6, 1804. (back)
\n
4. At this point Lewis has the crossed-out date \"July 12th 1806.\" The last sentence in this entry was probably an afterthought, added when the next day's date had already been written in. (back)
\n
5. In Madison County, Montana; see August 8, 1805. (back)
\n
6. On the east side of Jefferson River, opposite the mouth of the Big Hole River, in Beaverhead County, Montana, some two miles northeast of present Twin Bridges. Atlas map 65. Again, Biddle has added the date to Clark's blank space. (back)
\n
7. For the leaving of this canoe, see August 7, 1805. Probably some of the men who had been serving in regular infantry units before the expedition brought along their bayonets. Almost certainly it was a spike bayonet with a socket to fit over the muzzle of a musket. Peterson, 84\u201386. (back)
\n
8. On the east side of Jefferson River, opposite the mouth of the Big Hole (Wisdom) River,\nBeaverhead County, Montana, some two miles northeast of Twin Bridges. (back)
\n
9. Apparently the point referred to is the area between the Sun River and the Missouri, \nsouthwest of Great Falls, Cascade County, Montana. (back)
\n
10. They camped here on the west bank of the Missouri, Cascade County, opposite the \nWhite Bear Islands and a little below the mouth of Sand Coulee Creek on the other side. (back)
\n
11. \n Mandan bullboats. (back)
