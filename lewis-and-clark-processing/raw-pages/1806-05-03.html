\n May 3, 1806\n

\n
\n \n

[Lewis]

\n \n
\n Saturday May 3rd 1806.\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nThis morning we set out at 7 A. M. steered N. 25 E 12 ms. to Kimooenem Creek\n [1] through a high level plain. \u00a0\u00a0 this creek is about 12 yds. wide pebbly bottom low banks and discharges a considerable body of water \u00a0\u00a0 it heads in the S. W. mountains and discharges itself into Lewis's river a few miles above the narrows. \u00a0\u00a0 the bottoms of this creek are narrow with some timber principally Cottonwood and willow. \u00a0\u00a0 the under brush such as mentioned on N. East Creek. [2] \u00a0\u00a0 the hills are high and abrupt. \u00a0\u00a0 the land of the plains is much more fertile than below, less sand and covered with taller grass; very little of the aromatic shrubs appear in this part of the plain. [3] \u00a0\u00a0 we halted and dined at this creek; after which we again proceeded N. 45 E. 3 M. through the high plain to a small creek [4] 5 yds. wide branch of the Kimooenem C. \u00a0\u00a0 this stream falls into the creek some miles below. \u00a0\u00a0 the hills of this creek like those of the Kimooenem are high it's bottoms narrow and possess but little timber, lands of a good quality, a dark rich loam. [5] \u00a0\u00a0 we continued our rout up this creek, on it's N. side. N. 75 E. 7 Ms. \u00a0\u00a0 the timber increases in quantity the hills continue high. East 4 Ms. up the creek. \u00a0\u00a0 here we met with We-ark-koomt\n [6] whom we have usually distinguished by the name of the bighorn Cheif from the circumstance of his always wearing a horn of that animal suspended by a cord to he left arm. \u00a0\u00a0 he is the 1st Cheif of a large band of the Chopunnish nation. \u00a0\u00a0 he had 10 of his young men with him. \u00a0\u00a0 this man went down Lewis's river by land as we decended it by water last fall quite to the Columbia and I beleive was very instrumental in procuring us a hospitable and friendly reception among the natives. \u00a0\u00a0 he had now come a considerable distance to meet us. \u00a0\u00a0 after meeting this cheif we continued still up the creek bottoms N. 75. E. 2 m to the place at which the road leaves the creek and ascends the hills to the plain \u00a0\u00a0 here we encamped [7] in small grove of cottonwood tree which in some measure broke the violence of the wind. \u00a0\u00a0 we came 28 ms. today. \u00a0\u00a0 it rained hailed snowed and blowed with great violence the greater portion of the day. \u00a0\u00a0 it was fortunate for us that this storm was from the S. W. and of course on our backs. \u00a0\u00a0 the air was very cold. \u00a0\u00a0 we divided the last of our dryed meat at dinner when it was consumed as well as the ballance of our dogs nearly \u00a0\u00a0 we made but a scant supper and had not anything for tomorrow; however We-ark-koomt consoled us with the information that there was an indian lodge on the river at no great distance where we might supply ourselves with provision tomorrow. \u00a0\u00a0 our guide and the three young Wallahwollahs left us this morning reather abruptly and we have seen nothing of them since. [8] \u00a0\u00a0 the S. W. mountains [9] appear to become lower as they proceede to the N. E. \u00a0\u00a0 this creek [10] reaches the mountains. \u00a0\u00a0 we are nearer to them than we were last evening\n

[Clark]

\n Saturday 3rd May 1806\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nThis morning we Set out at 7 A. M. Steared N. 25\u00b0 E. 12 m. to Kimoo e nimm Creek through a high leavel plain \u00a0\u00a0 this Creek is 12 yds. wide pebbly bottom low banks and discharges a Considerable quanty of water \u00a0\u00a0 it head in the S W. Mountains and discharges it Self into Lewis's river a fiew miles Above the narrows. \u00a0\u00a0 the bottoms of this Creek is narrow with Some timber principally Cotton wood & Willow. \u00a0\u00a0 the under brush Such as mentioned in the N. E. Creek. The hills are high and abrupt. \u00a0\u00a0 the lands of the plains is much more furtile than below, less Sand and Covered with taller grass; very little of the aramatic Shrubs appear in this part of the plain. \u00a0\u00a0 we halted and dined at this Creek. \u00a0\u00a0 after which we again proceeded N. 45\u00b0 E. 3 mes. through a high plain to a Small Creek 5 yds. wide, a branch of the Kimoenimm Creek. \u00a0\u00a0 the hills of this Stream like those of the Ki moo enimm are high its bottoms narrow and possess but little timber. \u00a0\u00a0 the land of a good quallity dark rich loam. \u00a0\u00a0 we Continued our rout up this Creek on it's N. Side N. 75\u00b0 E 7 mes. \u00a0\u00a0 the timber increas in quantity \u00a0\u00a0 the hills continue high. \u00a0\u00a0 we met with the \n We arh koont\n whome we have usially distinguished by the name of the big horn Chief from the circumstance of his always wareing a horn of that animal Suspended by a Cord to his left arm. \u00a0\u00a0 he is a 1st Chief of a large band of the Chopunnish Nation. \u00a0\u00a0 he had ten of his young men with him. \u00a0\u00a0 this man Went down Lewis's river by Land as we decended it by water last fall quite to the Columbia, and I believe was very instremental in precureing us a hospital and friendly reception among the nativs. \u00a0\u00a0 he had now come a Considerable distance to meet us. \u00a0\u00a0 after meeting this Cheif we Continued Still up the Creek bottoms N. 75\u00b0 E. 2 m. to the place at which the roade leaves the Creek and assends the hill to the high plains: \u00a0\u00a0 here we Encamped in a Small grove of Cotton trees which in some measure broke the violence of the wind. \u00a0\u00a0 we Came 28 miles today. \u00a0\u00a0 it rained, hailed, Snowed & blowed with Great Violence the greater portion of the day. \u00a0\u00a0 it was fortunate for us that this Storm was from the S. W. and of Course on our backs. \u00a0\u00a0 the air was very cold. \u00a0\u00a0 we devided the last of our dried meat at dinner when it was Consumed as well as the ballance of our Dogs nearly \u00a0\u00a0 we made but a Scant Supper, and had not anything for tomorrow; however We-ark-koomt Consoled us with the information that there was an Indian Lodge on the river at no great distnace where we might Supply our Selves with provisions tomorrow. \u00a0\u00a0 our Guide and the three young Wallah wallah's left us this morning reather abruptly and we have Seen nothing of them Sence. \u00a0\u00a0 the S W. Mountains appear to become lower as \u2329we\u232a they receed to the N E. This Creek reaches the mountains. \u00a0\u00a0 we are much nearer to them than we were last evening. \u00a0\u00a0 they are Covered with timber and at this time Snow.\n\n

[Ordway]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nSaturday 3rd May 1806. \u00a0\u00a0 a little rain the later part of last night, and continues Showery and cold \u00a0\u00a0 a little hail & Snow intermixed. \u00a0\u00a0 one of the hunters horses broke his hobbles and got away. \u00a0\u00a0 about 7 we Set out \u00a0\u00a0 proceeded on over high plains and hills. \u00a0\u00a0 road bearing to the left from the branch. \u00a0\u00a0 the wind blew verry high and cold \u00a0\u00a0 Showers of hail & rain \u00a0\u00a0 about noon we descended a hill. \u00a0\u00a0 came on an other large creek [11] where we halted to dine on the last of our meat. \u00a0\u00a0 our hunters Came up \u00a0\u00a0 had found the lost horse a long distance back the road. \u00a0\u00a0 our Indians went on this morning intending to git to the forks to day \u00a0\u00a0 considerable of Snow fell on the high hills Since yesterday. \u00a0\u00a0 we delayed about 1 hour & left the creek named ke-moo-e-nim Creek \u00a0\u00a0 ascended a high hill and procd. on over high plains. \u00a0\u00a0 crossed 2 creeks, and followed up the third creek [12] \u00a0\u00a0 the big horn chief who we Saw at the big forks last fall met us \u00a0\u00a0 Several other Indians with him \u00a0\u00a0 he appeared verry glad to See us and turned back with us \u00a0\u00a0 we had considerable of hail & verry high winds. \u00a0\u00a0 in the evening we Camped having made 28 miles this day, having nothing to eat bought the only dog the Indians had with them. \u00a0\u00a0 the air is very cold.\u2014\n\n

[Gass]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Saturday 3rd. \u00a0\u00a0 We had a wet uncomfortable morning, and when the horses were collected one was found missing, and one of our hunters went back after him, while the rest of us continued our journey. This morning our guide and the three other Indians went on ahead. We continued our route about 10 miles, when we struck a creek, having left the other entirely to our right; and halted. Our hunter came up with the horse. The wind was very high this forenoon, and rather cold for the season; with some rain. We continued about two hours and eat the last of our dried meat; and are altogether without other provisions, as our stock of dogs is exhausted, and we can kill no game in these plains. In the evening we met a chief and nine of his men, who appeared glad to see us. We encamped on a small branch or spring, as it was too far to go over the hills. The Indians say we can get over to-morrow by noon. The wind continued to blow hard and some snow showers fell in the afternoon.\n\n\n

1. \n Tucannon River, reached near the Columbia-Garfield county line, in Washington. See Atlas map 73, where the route appears as a dotted line. The Tucannon reaches the Snake in northwest Columbia County. Kimooenem may represent Nez Perce\n qem\u00faynem, etymology and meaning unclear. See October 13, 1805. (back)
\n
2. Either Touchet River or Patit Creek. (back)
\n
3. \n Lewis's ecological observation describes the party's passage from the drier, shrub-steppe region of south-central Washington, characterized by the presence of sagebrush and rabbitbrush to a new vegetation zone, the steppe region. It is characterized by perennial bunchgrasses, such as bluebunch wheatgrass, Agropyron spicatum (Pursh) Scribn. & Smith, Idaho fescue, Festuca idahoensis Elmer, and Sandberg bluegrass. Franklin & Dyrness, 219\u201320. (back)
\n
4. \n Pataha Creek, in Garfield County, nameless on Atlas map 73. (back)
\n
5. The soils on the uplands adjacent to the stream are residual soils developed on Columbia River Basalt and volcanic ash from Cascade volcanoes. Weathered volcanic rocks typically form rich soils. (back)
\n
6. His real name was Apash Wyakaikt, 'ap\u00e1swahayqt, \"flint necklace.\" Men of the same name, very likely his son and grandson, were later among the most prominent Nez Perce leaders, known to whites as Looking Glass, senior and junior. The last was a leading figure in the 1877 war, in which he was killed. Josephy (NP), 11; Ronda (LCAI), 221; Aoki (NPT), 123 ff. (back)
\n
7. In Garfield County, on Pataha Creek east of Pataha City, near U.S. Highway 12 at about the point where the creek turns from a northerly to a westerly course. Atlas map 73. (back)
\n
8. It was probably Biddle who placed a vertical line through this sentence. (back)
\n
9. The Blue Mountains. (back)
\n
10. \n Pataha Creek. (back)
\n
11. \n Tucannon River, reached near the Columbia-Garfield county line, Washington. It is the\nparty's Kimooenem, variously spelled. (back)
\n
12. The last is Pataha Creek, in Garfield County. (back)
