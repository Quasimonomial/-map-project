\n May 21, 1805\n

\n
\n \n

[Lewis]

\n \n
\n Tuesday May 21st 1805.\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nA delightfull morning \u00a0\u00a0 set out at an early hour and proceeded on very well, imployed the chord principally; the shores are abbrupt and bould and composed of a black and yellow clay: see no extensive collection of pure sand, the bars are composed black mud and a small poportion of fine sand; [1] the courant still pretty strong. \u00a0\u00a0 the Missouri in it's course downward makes a suddon and extensive bend to receive the Muscle shell river, the point of country thus formed tho' high is still much lower than that surrounding it, thus forming a valley of wavey country which extends itself for a great distance in a Northerly direction; the soil is fertile, produces a fine turf of low grass and some herbs, also immence quantities of the Prickley pear, without a stick of timber of any discription. \u00a0\u00a0 the country on the South side is high broken and crowned with some scrubby pines and dwarf cedar; the leaf of this pine is much longer than the common pitch or red pine of Virginia, the cone is also longer and slimer, and the imbrications wider and thicker, and the whole frequently covered with rosin. Mineral appearances as usual. \u00a0\u00a0 the growse or praire hen are now less abundant on the river than they were below; perhaps they betake themselves to the open plains at a distance from the river at this season. [2]\n

\n

\nThe wind which was moderate all the fore part of the day continued to encrease in the evening, and about dark veered about to N. W. and blew a storm all night, in short we found ourselves so invelloped with clouds of dust and sand that we could neither cook, eat, nor sleep; and were finally compelled to remove our lodge about eight oClock at night to the foot of an adjacent hill where we were covered in some measure from the wind by the hills. \u00a0\u00a0 several loose articles blown over board and lost. \u00a0\u00a0 our first station was on a bar on Stard. opposite the lower point of a small Island, which we now called windy Island. [3] \u00a0\u00a0 the bends of the river are short and suddon, the points covered with some cottonwood, larger willow, or broadleafed willow with an abundance of the wild rose and some small honeysuckle bushes constitute the undergrowth, the redwood is also found in small quantities. Capt. C walked on shore today and killed 2 Elk; the party killed several deer and a buffaloe Cow.\u2014

[Clark]

\n \n
\n May 21st Tuesday 1805.\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\t\na butifull morning, wind from the West, river falling a little, we Set out at an early hour and proceed on in the usial way by the assistance of the Coard principally, but little use of the Oares & less with the poles as the bottoms are muddey, we Se no great bodies of pure Sand the bars & points are rich mud mixed with fine Sand. I walked on Shore Stard. Side \u00a0\u00a0 the river makes a great bend to the South to receve Shell River, the boint for many miles out in a Northerley direction is a rich uneaven valley Contain Some Short grass, and Prickley pears without timber \u00a0\u00a0 The Countrey on the South Side of the Missouri is high, Soil and mineral appearance as usial, more Scattering pine & Cedar on the hills, the wind which blew moderatly all the forepart of the day increassd and about Dark Shifted to the N W. and Stormed all night, Several loose articles were blown over board, our lodge & Camp which was on a Sand bar on the Std. Side & \u2329Sit\u232a opposite to the lower point of an Island we were obliged to move under the hills, the dust & Sand blew in clouds. The bends of the river are Short and points Covered with Cotton wood \u00a0\u00a0 under groth wild rose bushes \u00a0\u00a0 I killed 2 Elk to day Several Deer Killd. & a Buffalow Cow.

[Ordway]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n May 21st Tuesday 1805. \u00a0\u00a0 a butiful morning. \u00a0\u00a0 wind from the west. \u00a0\u00a0 river falling a little. \u00a0\u00a0 we Set out at an eairly hour and proceeded on in the usal way by the assistance of the chord principally, but little use for the oars & less withe the poles, as the bottom are muddy. \u00a0\u00a0 we See no great bodies of pure Sand \u00a0\u00a0 the bars & points are rich mud mixed with fine Sand. Capt Clark walked on Shore Stard. Side \u00a0\u00a0 the river makes a great bend to the South \u00a0\u00a0 in a Northerly direction is a rich vallie contain Some Short grass, and prickly pears [6] without timber \u00a0\u00a0 the Country on the South Side of the Missourie is high Soil and mineral appearence as usal \u00a0\u00a0 Some Scatering pine &.C. ceeder on the hills. The wind which moderately all the fore part of the day increased and about dusk Shifted to the N. W. and blew high & Stormed all night \u00a0\u00a0 Several loose articles were blown overboard, our Camp [7] which was on a Sand\nbar on the Stard. Side at the lower point of an Island \u00a0\u00a0 we were obledged to move under the hills \u00a0\u00a0 the dust & Sand blew in clouds. \u00a0\u00a0 the bends of the river are Short and points covered with cotton wood under grooths wild rose bush. Capt. Clark killed 2 Elk to day. Several deer killed and a buffaloe cow. \u00a0\u00a0 we Came 20 miles to day.\u2014\n\n\n

[Gass]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Tuesday 21st. \u00a0\u00a0 We proceeded on early and had a fine morning; towards the middle of the day the wind blew hard; but we went on very well for 20 miles, and encamped on a sand-beach on the North side.\n\n\n

[Whitehouse]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nTuesday 21st 1805. \u00a0\u00a0 a clear pleasant morning. \u00a0\u00a0 the 2 men returned who Stayed out all night had caught one beaver & killed a deer. \u00a0\u00a0 they Swam across the Mussel Shell River before Sun rise. \u00a0\u00a0 Soon after we Set off. \u00a0\u00a0 another beaver was caught \u00a0\u00a0 we proceeded on. \u00a0\u00a0 passed timbred bottoms & hilly land on each Side, but the River hills are not So high, as they were for Some distance below. \u00a0\u00a0 Some of the party yesterday discovered a high range of mountains [8] to the west, a long distance off. \u00a0\u00a0 we Saw Some old Indian Camps in a timbred bottom on N. S. where Capt. Clark killed an Elk. \u00a0\u00a0 about 1 oC. P. M. we halted to dine at a handsom timbred bottom on the South Shore. \u00a0\u00a0 one of the hunters killed an Elk. \u00a0\u00a0 the wind rose So high from the N. W. that we delayed about 2 hours and proceeded on \u00a0\u00a0 passed bottoms & pine hills as usal. \u00a0\u00a0 Came about 15 Miles and Camped on a large Sand beach on N. S. \u00a0\u00a0 one of the hunters killed a buffaloe another killed a beaver. \u00a0\u00a0 the wind rose verry high Soon after we Camped, and made the Sand fly So that it was verry disagreeable. \u00a0\u00a0 the most of the party moved back towards the hills.

\n

\nTuesday May 21st \u00a0\u00a0 This morning we had clear and pleasant Weather; the Men that were out catching beaver returned this Morning at day light. \u00a0\u00a0 they had bad success, having caught only one Beaver, however they kill'd a deer, both of which they brought to our Camp. \u00a0\u00a0 We set out early, and passed bottoms covered with Timber, and hilly land lying on both sides of the River. \u00a0\u00a0 The Hills here are not so high, as they were below,\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 Some of our party that was out hunting Yesterday, reported that they had seen, a high ridge of Mountains, which lay to the West, but appeared to be a very great distance from them, We proceeded on, and passed by some Bottom land, lying on the North side of the River where we saw some Indian Camps, that were old; \nthese Camps were surrounded with Cotton Wood Trees, \u2329where\u232a Captain Clark went on shore at that place, and killed an Elk, we shot a Beaver in the River, on our way here this day.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 About 1 o'Clock P. M. we halted to dine in a bottom of Timber, lying on the South side of the River, where one of our hunters killed an Elk.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 The wind rose here, to a very great height, from the North west so that we were forced to halt for two hours at that place.\u2014

\n

\nWe proceeded on our Voyage about 3 o'Clock P. M and passed bottom land & Pine hills, lying on both sides of the River, and came about 5 Miles and encamped on a Sand beach, lying on the North side of the River, our hunters that were out, killed a Buffalo, and a Beaver which was brought to our Camp, Soon after we encamped, the Wind blew so very hard, and the sand flew so much; that it made the place disagreeable, that most of our party moved back to some hills not far distant from the River.\u2014\n\n\n
