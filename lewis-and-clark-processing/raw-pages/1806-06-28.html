\n June 28, 1806\n

\n
\n \n

[Lewis]

\n \n
\n Saturday June 28th 1806.\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nThis morning we collected our horses and set out as usual after an early breakfast. \u00a0\u00a0 several of our horses had straggled to a considersble distance in surch of food but we were fortunate enough to find them in good time they look extreemly gant this morning, however the indians informed us that at noon we would arrive at a place where there was good food for them. \u00a0\u00a0 we continued our rout along the dividing ridge passing one very deep hollow and at the distance of six miles passed our encampment of the \u232916\u232a [NB: 15th] [1] of September last, one and a half miles further we passed the road which leads by the fishery falling in on the wright immediately on the dividing ridge. [2] \u00a0\u00a0 about eleven O'clock we arrived at an untimbered side of a mountain with a Southern aspect just above the fishery \u00a0\u00a0 here we found an abundance of grass for our horses as the Indians had informed us. \u00a0\u00a0 as our horses were very hungary and much fatiegued and from information no other place where we could obtain grass for them within the reach of this evening's travel we determined to remain at this place all night having come 13 miles only. [3] \u00a0\u00a0 the water was distant from our encampment we therefore melted snow and used the water principally. \u00a0\u00a0 the whole of the rout of this day was over deep snows. \u00a0\u00a0 we find the traveling on the snow not worse than without it, as the easy passage it gives us over rocks and fallen timber fully compensate for the inconvenience of sliping, certain it is that we travel considerably faster on the snow than without it. \u00a0\u00a0 the snow sinks from 2 to 3 inches with a hors, is coarse and firm and seems to be formed of the larger and more dense particles of the snow; the surface of the snow is reather harder in the morning than after the sun shines on it a few hours, but it is not in that situation so dense as to prevent the horse from obtaining good foothold. \u00a0\u00a0 we killed a small black pheasant; this bird is generally found in the snowey region of the mountains and feeds on the leaves of the pine and fir. [4] \u00a0\u00a0 there is a speceis of small whortleburry [5] common to the hights of the mountains, and a speceis of grass with a broad succulent leaf which looks not unlike a flag; [6] of the latter the horses are very fond, but as yet it is generally under the snow or mearly making it's appearance as it confined to the upper parts of the highest mountains.\u2014\n

[Clark]

\n \n
\n Saturday June 28th 1806\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nThis morning we Colected our horses and Set out as usial after an early brackfast. \u00a0\u00a0 we continued our rout along the dividig ridge over knobs & through deep hollows \u00a0\u00a0 passed our encampmt of the 14 Sept. last near the forks of the road leaving the one on which we had Came one leading to the fishery to our right imediately on the dividing ridge. \u00a0\u00a0 at 12 oClock we arrived at an untimberd side of a mountain with a southern aspect just above the fishery \u00a0\u00a0 here we found an abundance of grass for our horses as the guids had informed us. \u00a0\u00a0 as our horses were hungary and much fatiegued and from information no other place where we could obtain grass for them within the reach of this evening's travel we deturmined to remain at this place all night haveing come 13 m. only. \u00a0\u00a0 the water was distant from our Encampment we therefore melted Snow and used the water. \u00a0\u00a0 the whole of the rout of this day was over deep Snow. \u00a0\u00a0 we find the travelling on the Snow not worse than without it, as easy passage it givs us over rocks and fallen timber fully compensates for the inconvenience of sliping, certain it is that we travel considerably faster on the snow than without it. \u00a0\u00a0 the Snow Sinks from 2 to 3 inches with a horse, is course and firm and seems to be formed of the larger particles \u00a0\u00a0 the surface of the snow sees to be rather harder in the morning than after the Sun Shines on it a fiew hours, but it is not in that situation so dense as to prevent the horses from obtaining good foothold. I killed a Small black pheasant; this bird is generally found in the Snowey region of the mountains and feeds on the leaves of the pine & fir. \u00a0\u00a0 there is a Species of Small huckleberry common to the hights of the mountains, and a Species of grass with a broad succulent leaf which looks not unlike a flag; of the latter the horses are very fond, but as yet it is generally under the Snow, or mearly makeing it's appearance as it confined to the upper part of the highest mountains.\n

[Ordway]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n\u2329Saturday 28th June 1806. \u00a0\u00a0 a fair cool morning. \u00a0\u00a0 we Set out as usal and proceeded on \u00a0\u00a0 [four words illegible] the vallies on to the ridge [thick?] \u00a0\u00a0 2 hunters went on a head to hunt. \u00a0\u00a0 we descended a mountn. down on a fork of Collinses Creek, found the meat of a deer which one of the hunters had killed. \u00a0\u00a0 we crossed the creek and ascended \u00a0\u00a0 a high Steep mountn. \u00a0\u00a0 came in [our old] road \u00a0\u00a0 had a bad Shower of hail and [Some?] Thunder\u232a

\n

\nSaturday 28th June 1806. \u00a0\u00a0 a clear cool morning \u00a0\u00a0 we Set out as usal and proceeded on verry well \u00a0\u00a0 the Snow continues as yesterday. \u00a0\u00a0 our guides took us on a ridge different from that we went last fall, but a better way. [7] \u00a0\u00a0 about one oClock we halted on the top of a bald mountain on the South Side of which our horses git tollarable feed. So we delay this afternoon to let our horses refresh themselves &C \u00a0\u00a0 in the evening we had Thunder & hail.\n\n\n

\n \n

[Gass]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Saturday 28th. \u00a0\u00a0 The morning was pleasant, we set out early, and passed the place where we had encamped on the 15th Sept. last when the snow fell on us. After passing this place about a mile, we took a left-hand path, [8] and travelled along high ridges till noon, when we came to a good place of grass; where we halted and remained all the afternoon to let our horses feed, as they had but little grass last night. Some hunters went out, as we saw some elk signs here, and our meat is exhausted. We still have a good stock of roots, which we pound and make thick soup of, that eats very well. In the evening our hunters came in but had not killed any thing. On the south side of this ridge there is summer with grass and other herbage in aboundance; and on the north side, winter with snow six or eight feet deep.\n\n\n

\n \n
\n \n \n
\n
1. Again, a substitution and a revision in an apparent blank space. Clark gives the date of the previous camp as September 14. Biddle has it right; they were passing the camp of September 15. (back)
\n
2. They deviated here from their westbound route, which went down into the valley of the Lochsa River in Idaho County, Idaho. Now they continued eastward on the Lolo Trail, as it is known today, along the ridge. This portion of the eastbound route does not appear on Atlas map 70. Space, 38; Peebles (LT), map. (back)
\n
3. The camp was near Powell Junction on the present Forest Road 500 in Idaho County, also near Papoose Saddle and a few miles north of Powell Ranger Station and the camp of September 14, 1805, on Atlas map 70. Space, 38; Peebles (LT), map. (back)
\n
4. The middle portion of this sentence is crossed through with a red vertical line, perhaps done by Biddle. (back)
\n
5. Grouseberry, whortleberry, Vaccinium scoparium Leiberg, is a common subalpine, low shrub that produces very small, but delicious, red berries. Hitchcock et al., 4:35. (back)
\n
6. Smooth woodrush, Luzula hitchcockii Hamet-Ahti, is not a grass but a member of the woodrush family. It occurs only at high elevations and is particularly abundant where snow accumulations regularly provide moister conditions. (back)
\n
7. Instead of going down to the valley of the Lochsa River as on the westbound route, the\nparty continued eastward on the Lolo Trail along the ridge. (back)
\n
8. They followed the Lolo Trail along the ridge in Idaho County, Idaho, instead of retracing \ntheir westward route, which would have taken them down into the valley of the Lochsa River. (back)
