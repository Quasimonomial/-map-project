\n September 15, 1806\n

\n
\n \n

[Clark]

\n \n
\n Monday 15th of September 1806\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nwe set out early with a Stiff Breeze a head \u00a0\u00a0 saw Several deer Swiming the river soon after we Set out. \u00a0\u00a0 at 11 A. M. passed the enterance of the Kanzas river\n [1] which was very low, about a mile below we landed and Capt Lewis and my Self assended a hill which appeared to have a Commanding Situation for a fort, the Shore is bold and rocky imediately at the foot of the hill, [2] from the top of the hill you have a perfect Command of the river, this hill fronts the Kanzas and has a view of the Missouri a Short distance above that river. \u00a0\u00a0 we landed one time only to let the men geather Pappaws or the Custard apple of which this Country abounds, and the men are very fond of. \u00a0\u00a0 we discovered a Buck Elk on a Small Island, and sent the 2 fields and Shannon in pursute of it \u00a0\u00a0 they Soon Came up with and killed the Elk, he was large and in fine order we had his flesh Secured and divided. \u00a0\u00a0 as the winds were unfabourable the greater part of the day we only decended 49 Miles and encamped a Short distance Above Hay Cabin Creek. [3] \u00a0\u00a0 we are not tormented by the Musquetors in this lower portion of the river, as we were above the river plat and as high up as the Rochejhone and for a fiew miles up that river, and above its' enterance into the Missouri. \u00a0\u00a0 we passd Some of the most Charming bottom lands to day and the uplands by no means bad, all well timberd. \u00a0\u00a0 the weather disagreeably worm and if it was not for the constant winds which blow from the S. and S E. we Should be almost Suficated Comeing out of a northern Country open and Cool between the Latd. of 46\u00b0 and 49\u00b0 North in which we had been for nearly two years, rapidly decending into a woody Country in a wormer Climate between the Latds. 38\u00b0 & 39\u00b0 North is probably the Cause of our experiencing the heat much more Senceable than those who have Continued within the parralel of Latitude.\n

\n \n
\n
\n \n

[Ordway]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nMonday 15th Sept 1806. \u00a0\u00a0 a fair morning. \u00a0\u00a0 we Set off at eight and procd. on \u00a0\u00a0 the wind a head as usal. \u00a0\u00a0 our hunters shot a buck Elk from their canoe on the lower point of an Isld. and was fat. \u00a0\u00a0 we took the meat on board and procd. on \u00a0\u00a0 towards evening the hunters Shot a buck from their canoe. \u00a0\u00a0 we have seen about 20 deer on the shores this day \u00a0\u00a0 we Camped at Sunset on N. Side an emence Site of pappaws & as the men were gathering them Saw a number of rattle Snakes and killed one of them and saved the skin.\n\n\n

\n \n
\n
\n \n

[Gass]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Monday 15th. \u00a0\u00a0 The morning was pleasant and we embarked early. In a short time we killed a fine large elk; at 11 o'clock passed the Kanzon river, and encamped at sun set.\n\n

\n \n
\n \n \n
\n
1. The Kansas River was first reached on June 26, 1804; it joins the Missouri in Wyandotte County, Kansas. MRC map 14. (back)
\n
2. The hill would be in downtown Kansas City, in Jackson County, Missouri. MRC map 14. (back)
\n
3. As on other occasions on this downriver journey, Clark does not indicate which side of the river the camp was on; it would be in either Jackson or Clay County, Missouri. However, Ordway says the camp was on the north side, so it would be in Clay County. \"Hay Cabin Creek,\" first passed on June 24, 1804, is the present Little Blue River in Jackson County. MRC map 13. (back)
