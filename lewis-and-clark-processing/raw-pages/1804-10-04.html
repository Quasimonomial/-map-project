\nthe wind blew all night from the NW. Some rain, we were obliged to Drop down 3 miles to get the Chanel Suft. deep to pass up, Several Indians on the Shore viewing of us Called to us to land \u00a0\u00a0 one of them gave 3 yels & Sciped [X: Skipped] a ball before us, we payed no attention to him, proceeded on and Came too on the L. S. to brackft \u00a0\u00a0 one of those Indians Swam across to us beged for Powder, we gave him a piece of Tobacco & Set him over on a Sand bar, and Set out, the wind hard a head \u00a0\u00a0 (1) passed a Island in the middle of the river about 3 miles in length, we call \n Goodhope Island, (2) at 4 miles passed a (2) Creek on the L. S. about 12 yards wide Capt. Lewis and 3 men walked on Shore & crossed over to an (3) Island Situated on the S. S. of the Current & near the Center of the river \u00a0\u00a0 this Isld. is about 1\u00bd miles long & nearly \u00bd as wide, in the \u2329S. S. nearly opposit\u232a Center of this Island was an old Village of the rickeries Called \n La ho catt\n \u00a0\u00a0 it was Circular and walled Containing 17 lodges and it appears to have been deserted about five years, the Island Contains but little timber. \u00a0\u00a0 we Camped on the Sand bar makeing from this Island, the day verry Cool.

[Gass]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Thursday 4th. \u00a0\u00a0 We set out early; but were obliged to return to the place where we halted yesterday at 12 and to take the other side of the river; the water was so shallow and sand bars so numerous. At 9 o'clock an Indian swam across the river to see us, when we stopped for breakfast. We informed him that we were not traders, that we had seen his chief and told him all we had to say. We proceeded on, passed a creek on the south side, called Teel creek, and encamped on the upper part of an island.\n\n

\n Thursday 4 Oct. 1804. \u00a0\u00a0 Set off eairly. \u00a0\u00a0 at 9 oClock we halted for breakfast \u00a0\u00a0 an Indian Swam the river & came to us. \u00a0\u00a0 proceeded on \u00a0\u00a0 passed a creek on S. S. called Teed creek \u00a0\u00a0 Camped on the upper point of an Island.

\n

\nThursday October 4th \u00a0\u00a0 We set out early this morning, at 9 o'Clock A. M. We stopp'd to take our break fast, soon after, an Indian swam the River, and came to us; We proceeded on, and passed a Creek, lying on the South side of the River, called Teel Creek, and in the Evening, we encamped on the upper point of an Island\n\n

\n \n
\n \n \n
\n
1. The Indian fired his musket and sent the ball skipping over the water. (back)
\n
2. Probably later Pascal Island. See above, October 3, 1804; Atlas maps 10, 24; MRC map 43. (back)
\n
3. Nameless on Atlas map 24; probably later Pascal Creek. MRC map 43. (back)
\n
4. On Atlas map 24 this stream is Teel Creek, presumably from a teal duck. It is the later Stove (may be Stone), or Cherry, Creek, in Dewey County, South Dakota. MRC map 43. (back)
\n
5. The island was the later Dolphees, or Lafferty, Island, lying between Dewey and Potter counties, South Dakota. The village appears clearly in Atlas map 24. The name of the village appears on Evans's map 4 (Atlas map 10). The area is now inundated by Oahe Reservoir. Mattison (OR), 63; Ronda (LCAI), 43; MRC map 43; MRY map 91. The word, NAhuuka\u00e1tA or \"by the water,\" comes from the name of an Arikara band. Parks (BVAP), 225. (back)
