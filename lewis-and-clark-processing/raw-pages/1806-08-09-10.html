\n August 9, 1806\n

\n
\n \n

[Lewis]

\n \n
\n Saturday August 9th 1806.\n [1]\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nThe day proved fair and favourable for our purposes. \u00a0\u00a0 the men were all engaged dressing skins and making themselves cloathes except R & J. Fields whom I sent this morning over the river with orders to proceed to the entrance of the White earth river in surch of Capt. C. and to hunt and kill Elk or buffaloe should they find any convenient to the river. \u00a0\u00a0 in the evening these men returned and informed me that they saw no appearance of Capt. Clark or party. \u00a0\u00a0 they found no game nor was there a buffaloe to be seen in the plains as far as the eye could reach. \u00a0\u00a0 nothing remarkable took place in the course of the day. Colter and Collins have not yet overtaken us \u00a0\u00a0 I fear some missfortune has happened them for their previous fidelity and orderly deportment induces me to beleive that they would not thus intentionally delay. [2] \u00a0\u00a0 the Perogue is not yet sufficiently dry for reparing. \u00a0\u00a0 we have no pitch and will therefore be compelled to use coal and tallow.\n\n

\n \n
\n
\n \n

[Clark]

\n \n
\n Monday 9th August 1806\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\na heavy dew this morning. \u00a0\u00a0 loaded the Canoes and proceeded on down about 6 miles and landed at the Camp of the 2 hunters Shields and Gibson whome I had Sent down to hunt last evening, they had killed five deer two of which were in good order which they brought in. \u00a0\u00a0 here I took brackfast and proceeded on a fiew miles and I walked on Shore across a point of near 10 miles in extent in this bottom which was mostly open I saw Some fiew deer and Elk. I killed 3 of the deer which were Meagure the Elk appeared fat. I did not kill any of them as the distance to the river was too great for the men to Carry the meat \u00a0\u00a0 at the lower part of this bottom a large Creek of runnig water 25 yds wide [3] falls in which meanders through an open roleing plain of great extent. \u00a0\u00a0 in the low bottoms of this Creek I observed Some timber Such as Cottonwood, ash & Elm. \u00a0\u00a0 on my arival at the lower part of the bottom found that the canoes had been in waiting for me nearly two hours. The Squar brought me a [NB: 1800 Miles up the Missouri I found a] large and well flavoured Goose berry [4] of a rich Crimsin Colour, and deep purple berry of the large Cherry of the Current Speces [5] which is common on this river as low as the Mandans, the engagees Call it the Indian Current. I landed opposit to a high plain on the S. E. Side late in the evening and walked in a Grove of timber where I met with an Elk which I killed. \u00a0\u00a0 this Elk was the largest Buck I ever Saw and the fattest animal which have been killed on the rout. I had the flesh and fat of this Elk brought to Camp [6] and cut thin ready to dry. \u00a0\u00a0 the hunters killed nothing this evening.\n\n\n\n

\n \n
\n
\n \n

[Ordway]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nSaturday 9th August 1806. \u00a0\u00a0 a cool windy morning \u00a0\u00a0 we continued on dressing our deer Skins \u00a0\u00a0 the 2 fieldses went across the river a hunting \u00a0\u00a0 returnd. towards evening \u00a0\u00a0 had killed 1 Elk and one deer. \u00a0\u00a0 all hands employed makeing themselves comfortable.\n\n

\n \n
\n
\n \n

[Gass]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Saturday 9th. \u00a0\u00a0 This was another fine day; and most of the men were employed as yesterday; and in making small oars for our canoes. Two of them [7] went over the river and killed an elk and a deer.\n\n\n

\n \n
\n \n \n
\n
1. Here begins Lewis's journal Codex Lb, consisting of pages torn from one of the red notebooks. It runs only to August 12, after which Lewis ceased writing because of the discomfort of the wound he received on August 11. See Appendix C, vol. 2. (back)
\n
2. Evidently Collins's \"deportment\" had improved greatly since the early days of the expedition; see vol. 2. (back)
\n
3. This stream could be Tobacco Creek, in Williams County, North Dakota (the captains' \"Halls Strand Creek\"), or Tobacco Garden Creek in McKenzie County (their \"Pumic Stone Creek\"). Atlas maps 47, 56; MRC maps 57, 58. (back)
\n
4. Either hawthorn gooseberry, Ribes oxyacanthoides L., or bristly gooseberry, R. setosum Lindl., both of which seem to reach their southern distributional limit along the Missouri River near this area of North Dakota. Barkley, 135\u201336. It was probably Biddle who drew a red vertical line through most of this botanical passage. (back)
\n
5. Probably the golden currant again. (back)
\n
6. \n Biddle locates this area as being 1800 miles up the Missouri. One of Clark's statements of creeks and rivers places \"Halls Strand Creek\" (present Tobacco Creek in Williams County, North Dakota, see Postexpeditionary Miscellany) 1790 miles up the river; thus the camp would be approximately ten miles above that stream, in McKenzie County. Biddle has also added the phrase \"1800 miles up the Missouri\" next to the August 10 entry's dateline. The site is probably now inundated by Garrison Reservoir. This was the camp Lewis passed on August 11, after his wounding by Cruzatte, which probably occurred nearby. Atlas maps 34, 47, 56; MRC map 58. (back)
\n
7. The Field brothers, as Lewis and Ordway note. (back)
