\n September 28, 1804\n

\n
\n \n

[Clark]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n 28th of Septr 1804 Friday \u00a0\u00a0 I made maney attempts in defferent ways to find our anchor without Sukcess, the Sand had Covered her up, we Deturmined to proceed on to Day\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 and after Brackfast we with great Dificuelty got the Chiefs out of the boat, and when we were about Setting out the Class Called the Soldiars took possession of the Cable\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 the 1st Cheif [Black Buffalo] was Still on board and intended to go a Short distance up with us, was informed that the men Set on the Cable, he went out and told Capt Lewis who was at the Bow, they wanted tobacco \u00a0\u00a0 The 2d Chief [Partisan] Demanded a flag & Tobacco which we refused to give, Stateing proper reasons to them for it, after much rangleing, we gave a \u2329twist\u232a Carrot of Tobacco to the 1st Cheif [1] and he to the men & jurked the Cable from them & proceeded on under a Breeze from the S E. \u00a0\u00a0 we took in the 3rd Cheif [Buffalo Medicine] who was Sitting on a Sand bar 2 miles above\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 he told us the Rope was held by order of the 2d Chief who was a Double Spoken man\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 Soon after we Saw a man rideing full Speed up the bank, we brought him on board, & he proved to be the Sun of the 3d Cheif, by him we Sent a talk to the nation, explanitory of our hoisting the red flag under the white, if they were for Peace Stay at home and doe as we had Derected them and if they were for war or deturmined to attempt to Stop us, we were ready to defend our Selves (as I had before Said)\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 we Substituted large Stones in place of an Anchor, we came to at a Small Sand bar in the middle of the river and Stayed all night\u2014 [2] I am verry unwell I think for the want of Sleep\u2014

[Clark]

\n \n
\n 28th of September 1804 Friday\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nMade many attemps in different ways to \u2329get up\u232a find our Anchor but could not, the Sand had Covered it, from the misfortune of last night our boat was laying at Shore in a verry unfavourable Situation, after finding that the anchor Could not be found we deturmined to proceed on, with great difficuelty got the Chiefs out of our boat, and when we was about Setting out the Class Called the Soldiers took possession of the Cable \u00a0\u00a0 the 1s Chief which was Still on board & intended to go a Short distance up with us, I told him the men of his nation Set on the Cable, he went out & told Capt Lewis who was at the bow the men who Set on the Roap was Soldiers \u2329they\u232a and wanted [two words illegible, crossed out] Tobacco \u2329& then we might proceed,\u232a \u00a0\u00a0 Capt. L. Said would not agree to be forced into any thing, the 2d Chief Demanded a flag & Tobacco which we refusd. to Give Stateing proper reasons to them for it \u00a0\u00a0 \u2329the necssity\u232a \u00a0\u00a0 after much difucelty\u2014which had nearly reduced us to hostility \u00a0\u00a0 I threw a Carot of Tobacco to 1s Chief Spoke So as to touch his pride \u00a0\u00a0 took the port fire [3] from the gunner \u00a0\u00a0 the Chief gives the Tobaco to his Soldiers & he jurked the rope from them and handed it to the bows man \u00a0\u00a0 we then Set out under a Breeze from the S. E. \u00a0\u00a0 about 2 miles up we observed the 3rd Chief on Shore beckining to \u2329him\u232a us \u00a0\u00a0 we took him on board \u00a0\u00a0 he informed us the roap was held by the order of the 2d Chief who was a Double Spoken man, Soon after we Saw a man Comeing full Speed, thro: the plains left his horse & proceeded across a Sand bar near the Shore we took him on board & observed that he was the Son of the Chief we had on board \u00a0\u00a0 we Sent by him a talk to the nation Stateent the Cause of our hoisting the red flag undr. the white, if they were for peace Stay at home & do as we had Derected them, if the were for war ore were Deturmined to Stop us we were ready to defend our Selves, we halted one houre & \u00bd on the S. S. & made a Substitute of Stones for a ancher, refreshed our men and proceeded on about 2 miles higher up & came too a verry Small Sand bar in the middle of the river & Stayed all night, I am Verry unwelle for want of Sleep \u00a0\u00a0 Deturmined to Sleep to night if possible, the men Cooked & we rested well.

[Ordway]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Friday 28th Sept. 1804. \u00a0\u00a0 a clear and pleasant morning. Capt. Clark went with the pearogues eairly. \u00a0\u00a0 this morning to hunt for the anker. Searched Some time with the Boat hook & poles, could not find it. \u00a0\u00a0 they took a chord and put Sinkers to the middle and took each end to the 2 pearogues and dragged the river diligently along time but could not find it. \u00a0\u00a0 took breakfast about 10 O.Clock. \u00a0\u00a0 the whole lodge of Indians were waiting on the bank to See us Start as we intended if the excident had not of happened last night. \u00a0\u00a0 we gave up the Idea of finding our anker.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 We then were about to Set off. \u00a0\u00a0 Some of the chiefs were on bord insisting on our Staying untill the others came. We told them we could not wait any longer.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 they then did not incline to let us go on \u00a0\u00a0 they Sayed we might return back with what we had or remain with them, but we could not go up the Missouri any further, about 200 Indians were then on the bank. Some had fire arms. Some had Spears. Some had a kind of cutlashes, and all the rest had Bows and Steel or Iron pointed arrows. Several of the warries Set by the chord where our boat the big Barge was tied \u00a0\u00a0 the 2 pearogues were tied on the outside of the Barge. Capt. Clark was Speaking to the chiefs in the cabbin. Capt. Lewis asked the chiefs if they were going out of the boat. \u00a0\u00a0 they did not incline to. \u00a0\u00a0 then Capt. Lewis came out. \u00a0\u00a0 ordered every man to his place \u00a0\u00a0 ordered the Sail hoisted, then one man went out untied the chord, which the warrier had in his hand, then 2 or 3 more of their warries caught hold of the chord and tyed it faster than before. Capt. Lewis then appeared to be angerry, and told to Go out of the Boat and the chief then went out and Sayd we are Sorry to have you go. But if you will Give us one carret of tobacco we will be willing for you to go on & will not try to Stop you. Capt. Lewis Gave it to them. \u00a0\u00a0 the head chief Sayd then that we must Give him one more carrit of tobacco more for his warries who held the chord and then we might go, boath of our Captains told him that we did not mean to be trifled with. \u00a0\u00a0 nor would not humer them any more, but would Give him 1 carrit more for the warriers, if he would be a man of his word and Stand to his word like a man. \u00a0\u00a0 the chief Sayd he was mad too, to See us S[t]and Som much for 1 carrit of tobacco. \u00a0\u00a0 if we would Give it we might go on. Capt. Lewis Gave it to him. \u00a0\u00a0 he then took the chord in his hand & Gave it to us. \u00a0\u00a0 we then Set off under a gentle Breeze which happened to be favourable. \u00a0\u00a0 we proceeded on \u00a0\u00a0 passd bottom prarie on S. S. \u00a0\u00a0 high land on N. S. \u00a0\u00a0 went 4 miles and halted. \u00a0\u00a0 we fixed 2 large Stone to our boats to answer as ankers, as we did not intend to Camp on Shore again untill we Got to an other Nation. We Saw one of the four mentioned chiefs comming up the river S. S. \u00a0\u00a0 we took him on board. \u00a0\u00a0 he informed us that their was 300 Indians around which was the other lodge.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 they wished to have us Stop. \u00a0\u00a0 we told his Son to tell them that we Should not Stop. \u00a0\u00a0 this old chief Sayd he was our friend and wished to go with us for a while. \u00a0\u00a0 the Capts. alowed him to Stay on board. \u00a0\u00a0 we then proceeded on. \u00a0\u00a0 at Sunset we cast anker near a small Sand bar in the middle of the river, where we Stayed all night. [4] \u00a0\u00a0 heared Some Indians on boath Sides of the river. \u00a0\u00a0 corn cooked on the little Sand bar for the next day \u00a0\u00a0 all but the cooks Slept on board.\u2014\n\n\n

[Gass]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Friday, 28th. This morning we dragged the river all around where the boat lay, but could not find the anchor. At 9 o'clock we made preparations to sail; some of the chiefs were on board, and concluded to go some distance with us. When we went to shove off, some of the Indians took hold of the rope and would not let it go. This conduct had like to be attended with bad consequences, as Captain Lewis was near giving orders to cut the rope and to fire on them. The chiefs, however, went out and talked with them: they said they wanted a carrot of tobacco, and that if we gave that we might go. The tobacco was given them, and we went off under a gentle breeze of wind. \nWe passed high land on the north side and bottom on the south. We proceeded 4 miles, and then saw an Indian [5] following us along the beach, when Captain Lewis went in a periogue and brought him on board. He informed us that 300 more Indians had come to their camp, and desired we should stop and talk with them. We did not then stop, but proceeded on, and he remained on board. We passed a fine bottom covered with timber on the north side, and bare hills on the south. We made two large stones serve the purpose of an anchor, and at sunset anchored for the night, near a small sand-bar in the middle of the river.

\n

\nWhile I was at the Indian camp yesterday they yoked a dog to a kind of car, which they have to haul their baggage from one camp to another; the nation having no settled place or village, but are always moving about. [6] The dogs are not large, much resemble a wolf, and will haul about 70 pounds \neach.\n\n\n

[Whitehouse]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\t\t\nFriday 28th Sept. 1804. \u00a0\u00a0 we draged the river in hopes to find our anker but it was in vain. \u00a0\u00a0 about 9 oClock we went to Set off \u00a0\u00a0 Some of the chiefs was then on board and concluded to go a little ways with us. \u00a0\u00a0 when we were about to Shove off a nomber of warries on Shore caught hold of our cable \nand another whiped of[f] the children \u00a0\u00a0 the women went off also \u00a0\u00a0 only about 60 warries on the edge of the bank and we jest under the bank. \u00a0\u00a0 Some of them had fire arms and the rest had Good bows and arrows ready for war. \u00a0\u00a0 the consequence had like to have been bad as Capt. Lewis was near cutting \nthe cable with his Sword and giving orders for the party to fire on them. \u00a0\u00a0 then the chiefs went out and Spoke to them. \u00a0\u00a0 they Said if we would Give them a carrit of tobacco they would loose the rope. \u00a0\u00a0 we gave them tobacco. \u00a0\u00a0 the chief after Some hesitation loosed the rope himself. \u00a0\u00a0 we then Set of[f] under a fine breese of wind. \u00a0\u00a0 passed high land on N. S. & bottom on S. S. \u00a0\u00a0 Saw an Indian comming up the Shore. \u00a0\u00a0 we hoisted a white flag, and a red flag for peace or war, and was determined to fight our way, if we could not Go without. \u00a0\u00a0 Capt. Lewis Got into a perogue and went on Shore to See what the Indian wished brought him on board. \u00a0\u00a0 he Informed us that 300 more of Savages had arived at the village they wished us to Stop and talk with them, but we did not Stop. \u00a0\u00a0 he remained on board. \u00a0\u00a0 about Sunset we ankered out near a Small Sand bar in the middle of the river for to Stay all night.

\n

\nFriday September 28th \u00a0\u00a0 This morning the hands were all employed, (excepting the Guard) in dragging for our Anchor and Cable; but without any success, about 9 oClock A. M. we went to sett off. \u00a0\u00a0 some of the Indian Chiefs was then on board of the boat, and concluded to go a little way with us, We then proceeded to shove off the boat, a number of their Warriors who were on the shore caught fast hold of the Cable, one of their Warriors drove off their Women and Children from the Boat, Whilst about Sixty of their Warriors stood on the Edge of the bank of the River; and our boat lay just under the bank of it. \u00a0\u00a0 The Warriors were all Arm'd some of them had fire Arms, and the remainder Bows & Arrows, they appeared all ready for Warr, which would have been attended with dangerous consequences to us, they having such a superiority to us in regard to Number.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 Captain Lewis was going to cut the Cable of the Boat with his sword; and to give Orders for our party, to fire on the Indians. \u00a0\u00a0 When their chiefs who were on board the Boat; went out and spoke to them, they told our Officers that if they would give the Warriors that held the Rope, a few Carrots of Tobacco; that they would loose the Rope. \u00a0\u00a0 The Officer then threw \u2329a\u232a some Carrotts of tobacco among them, and they left the Rope and ran to get it, One of the Chiefs which had been on board, the boat after some hesitation loosed the Cable.\u2014

\n

\nWe then set out with a fair breeze of Wind, and passed some high land, lying on the North side of the River; and fine bottoms of land, lying on the South side of the River, We proceeded on, when we espied an Indian coming \nrunning up the Shore, the Officers order'd a White flag, and one of Red to be hoisted, as a sign to the Indians, that we were either for peace, or Warr; they being determined if we could not proceed on without fighting, that it \nshould be the case; but if possible, to get on peacably; \u00a0\u00a0 Captain Lewis got on board one of the Pettyaugers, and proceeded to shore, to see what the Indian wanted, and to gain what information; he could from him, respecting the intention of the Indians toward us; Captain Lewis soon returned, and brought the Indian on board with him;\u2014

\n

\nThis Indian told the Officers, that Three hundred more Indians, had arrived at the Village, that we had just left; and that they wished us to stop, and have a talk with them.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 and that he had come to us, for that purpose; Our officers proceeded on with the Boat & Pettyaugers, not wishing to have any further connection with such a banditti of Villains.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 The Indian remained on board the boat with us, at Sun set we anchor'd the Boat & Pettyaugers out near a Sand Barr, lying in the middle of the River; Where we remained 'till the morning\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 The Tribe of the Teton Indians are part of the Soix Nation, they inhabit on both sides & near the Mesouri River. \u00a0\u00a0 They are a fierce looking Sett of Savages, & chiefly delight in Warr, plundering &ca.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 they are stout well made Indians and their Women are in general handsome, If I may be allowed to judge from those which I saw in the lodges that we left Yesterday.\u2014\t\n\n\n\n

1. \n Clark later explained his action as attempting to touch the chief's pride and get him to use his influence to allay hostilities. See second entry for this day and Nicholas Biddle Notes [ca. April 1810], Jackson (LLC), 2:518. (back)
\n
2. \n Atlas map 23 erroneously shows the camp of September 26 and 27 as being also that of the twenty-eighth. The actual camp for this day was apparently at the site the map shows as the camp for September 29. It lay between Stanley and Hughes counties, South Dakota, perhaps three miles above the present Oahe Dam; the area is now inundated by Oahe Reservoir. Mattison (OR), 30\u201333; MRC map 41. (back)
\n
3. A portfire was a slow-burning fuse, probably a cord impregnated with gunpowder or some other flammable substance; the burning end could be touched to the touchhole of a cannon to fire the weapon. Ordway notes on September 25 that the swivel cannon was loaded with sixteen musket balls and the two smaller swivels (blunderbusses) with buckshot. (back)
\n
4. About three miles above Oahe Dam, Hughes and Stanley counties, South Dakota; the\narea is now inundated by the reservoir. (back)
\n
5. \n Buffalo Medicine. (back)
\n
6. \n McKeehan's note: \"It appears that these people, (in some respects resembling the wandering Arabs) are an unsettled, ferocious, blood-thirsty race, and have been great destroyers of the Algonquin nation, who inhabit the country about lake Superior. Mr. McKenzie states the following circumstance, 'Within three miles of the last portage' (a place near lake Superior) 'is a remarkable rock, with a smooth face, but split and cracked in different parts, which hang over the water. Into one of its horizontal chasms a great number of arrows have been shot, which is said to have been done by a war party of the Nadowasis or Sieux, who had done much mischief in this country, and left these weapons as a warning to the Chebois or natives, that, notwithstanding its lakes, rivers and rocks, it was not inaccessible to their enemies.' General History of the Fur Trade.\" (back)
