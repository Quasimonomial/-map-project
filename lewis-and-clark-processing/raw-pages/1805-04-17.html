\n April 17, 1805\n

\n
\n \n

[Lewis]

\n \n
\n Wednesday April 17th 1805.\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nA delightful morning, set out at an erly hour. \u00a0\u00a0 the country though which we passed to day was much the same as that discribed of yesterday; there wase more appearance of birnt hills, furnishing large quanties of lava and pumice stone, [1] of the latter some pieces were seen floating down the river. Capt. Clark walked on shore this morning on the Stard. side, and did not join us untill \u2329near sunset\u232a half after six in evening. \u00a0\u00a0 he informed me that he had seen the remains of the Assinniboin encampments in every point of woodland through which he had passed. \u00a0\u00a0 we saw immence quantities of game in every direction around us as we passed up the river; consisting of herds of Buffaloe, Elk, and Antelopes with some deer and woolves. \u00a0\u00a0 tho' we continue to see many tracks of the bear we have seen but very few of them, and those are at a great distance generally runing from us; I thefore presume that they are extreemly ware and shy; the Indian account of them dose not corrispond with our experience so far. \u00a0\u00a0 one black bear passed near the perogues on the 16th and was seen by myself and the party but he so quickly disappeared that we did not shoot at him.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 at the place we halted to dine on the Lard. side we met with a herd of buffaloe of which I killed the fatest as I concieved among them, however on examining it I found it so poar that I thought it unfit for uce and only took the tongue; the party killed another which was still more lean. \u00a0\u00a0 just before we encamped this evening we saw some tracks of Indians who had passed about 24 hours; they left four rafts of tim[ber] on the Stard. side, on which they had passed. \u00a0\u00a0 we supposed them to have been a party of the Assinniboins who had been to war against the rocky mountain Indians, and then on their return. \u00a0\u00a0 Capt. Clark saw a Curlou today. [2] \u00a0\u00a0 there were three beaver taken this morning by the party. the men prefer the flesh of this anamal, to that of any other which we have, or are able to procure at this moment. I eat very heartily of the beaver myself, and think it excellent; particularly the tale, and liver. \u00a0\u00a0 we had a fair wind today which enabled us to sail the greater part of the distance we have travled, encamped on the Lard shore the extremity of the last course [3]\n

[Clark]

\n \n
17th of April Wednesday 1805\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\na fine morning \u00a0\u00a0 wind from the S E. Genly \u00a0\u00a0 to day handsom high extencive rich Plains on each Side, the mineral appearances continue with greater appearances of Coal, much greater appearance of the hills having been burnt, more Pumice Stone & Lava washed down to the bottoms and some Pumice Stone floating in the river, I walked on the S. S. Saw great numbs. of Buffalow feeding in the Plains at a distance \u00a0\u00a0 Capt. Lewis killed 2 Buffalow buls which was near the water at the time of dineing, they were So pore as to be unfit for use. I Saw Several Small parties of antelopes large herds of Elk, Some white wolves, and in a pond (formed on the S. S. by the Missouries Changeing its bead) I Saw Swan Gees & different kinds of Ducks in great \u2329quantity\u232a numbers also a Beaver house. Passed a Small Creek on the S. S. & Several runs of water on each Side, Saw the remains of Indian camps in every point of timbered land on the S. S. \u00a0\u00a0 in the evining a thunder gust passed from the S W, without rain, about Sunset Saw Some fresh Indians track and four rafts on the shore S. S. Those I prosume were Ossinniboins who had been on a war party against the Rockey Mountain Indians\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 Saw a Curlow, Some verry large beaver taken this morning. \u00a0\u00a0 those animals are made use of as food and preferred by the party to any other at this Season

[Ordway]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Wednesday 17th April 1805. \u00a0\u00a0 a clear beautiful morning. \u00a0\u00a0 we Set off eairly. Some of the men caught 2 beaver and Several Small fish. \u00a0\u00a0 a fair wind \u00a0\u00a0 we Sailed on \u00a0\u00a0 Saw large Gangs of buffaloe on the Side hills S. S. \u00a0\u00a0 proceeded on \u00a0\u00a0 passed a beautiful plain on the N. S. \u00a0\u00a0 passed Several bottoms on each Side of the River. \u00a0\u00a0 abt. 1 oClock we halted to dine \u00a0\u00a0 Saw a gang of buffaloe Swimming the river. \u00a0\u00a0 one of the hunters killed one \u00a0\u00a0 Capt. Lewis killed one large one in a fiew minutes \u00a0\u00a0 they being poor we took only the tongues of them \u00a0\u00a0 Saw Several gangs of Elk on each Side of the river. \u00a0\u00a0 late in the afternoon we Saw a gang of buffaloe on S. S. R. Potts\n [7] killed one of them. \u00a0\u00a0 passed Several runs in the course of the day & red hills on each Side and high raged hills which are rough barron broken & Steep. Came 26 miles this day by Sailing &.c. Camped on a large Sand beach S. S. \u00a0\u00a0 one of the men caught a nomber of Small cat fish in the river.\n\n\n

\n \n

[Gass]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Wednesday 17th. \u00a0\u00a0 We proceeded on early as usual with a fair wind. The day was fine and we made good way. Passed a beautiful plain and two large creeks on the North side, and another creek on the South. [8] We saw a great many buffaloe and elk on the banks. At 1 o'clock we halted for dinner, when two men went out and in a few minutes killed 2 buffaloe. We made 26 miles and encamped on the South side, and found that some rain had fallen during the day, where we encamped, though there was none where we had been.\n\n\n

[Whitehouse]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nWednesday April 17th \u00a0\u00a0 We got underway at the usual hour of day light, we proceeded on, and passed a high bluff to a sand barr, and a beautifull Priari, which put in near the River, likewise a Creek [9] that Runs a North \nCourse under the Bluffs, and a small distance further passed two Creeks [10] lying on the same Side of the River; and one Creek [11] lying on the South side.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 We encamped on the South side of the River, having come 26 Miles this day\n\n\n\n

\n
1. Here Lewis is passing through country underlain by the Bullion Creek Formation until late in the day, when he again enters country underlain by the Sentinel Butte Formation; both formations contain lignite coal. (back)
\n
2. Probably the long-billed curlew, Numenius americanus [AOU, 264], and if so, a bird new to science. Cutright (LCPN), 125. (back)
\n
3. In McKenzie County, North Dakota. Mattison (GR), 57; Atlas maps 34, 47, 56; MRC map 58. (back)
\n
4. Also given on Atlas map 34, in Lewis's hand. (back)
\n
5. \n Atlas map 34 says 65\u00b0, in opposition to both captains' journals. (back)
\n
6. \"Halls \u00a0\u00a0 Strand Lake\" is named for Hugh Hall, a member of the party. It is present-day Tobacco Creek, Williams County, North Dakota. (back)
\n
7. Actually John Potts. (back)
\n
8. The captains have little to say about any of these streams. The one on the south is \napparently later Clark Creek in McKenzie County, North Dakota. The first on the north is \nprobably their Hall's Strand Creek, later Tobacco Creek in Mountrail County. The last is probably the \"run\" which Clark notes falling in among burning hills, perhaps later Garden Creek. (back)
\n
9. No one else seems to mention this stream, but since it is apparently on the north side, \nit may be later Beaver Creek, Mountrail County, North Dakota. (back)
\n
10. Presumably the two creeks on the north side mentioned by Gass, the first Hall's Strand Creek, later Tobacco Creek, the second perhaps later Garden Creek, both in Mountrail\nCounty. (back)
\n
11. Apparently later Clark Creek, in McKenzie County, North Dakota. (back)
