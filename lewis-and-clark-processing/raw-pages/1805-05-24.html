\n \n \n Friday May 24th 1805. [2]\n \n \n

\n

The water standing in the vessels freized during the night \u215b of an inch thick, ice also appears along the verge of the river. \u00a0\u00a0 the folage of some of the cottonwood trees have been entirely distroyed by the frost and are again puting forth other buds. \u00a0\u00a0 the high country in which we are at present and have been passing for some days I take to be a continuation of what the Indians as well as the French Engages call the Black hills. This tract of country so called consists of a collection of high broken and irregular hills and short chain of mountains sometimes 120 miles in width and again becomeing much narrower, but always much higher than the\ncountry on either side; they commence about the head of the Kanzas river and to the West of that river near the Arkansas, from whence they take their course a little to the W. of N. W. approaching the rockey Mountains obliquely, passing the river platte above the forks and intercepting the Yellowstone river near the big bend and passing the Missouri at this place and probably continuing to swell the country as far North as the Saskashawan river tho' they are lower here than they are discribed to the Sth. and may therefore probably terminate before they reach the Suskashawan. \u00a0\u00a0 the black hills in their course nothwardly appear to approach more nearly to the Rocky Mountains. [3]\n

\n

We set out at an early hour this morning and proceed on principally by the chord untill about 9 A. M. when a fine breeze sprung up from the S. E. and enabled us though the ballance of the day to employ our sails to advantage; we proceed at a pretty good pace notwithstanding the\ncourant of the river was very strong. \u00a0\u00a0 we passed two large and four small Islands; also several streams on either side; the first of these is a large Creek or small river which disinboged on the Stard. side about 1 \u00bd miles above our encampment of last evening, it is 30 yards wide and contains some water. \u00a0\u00a0 the bed is gravley and intermixed with some stone, it takes its rise in the mountains which are situated in a Northwardly direction from its entrance, distant about 30 miles. [4] \u00a0\u00a0 the air is so pure in this open country that mountains and other elivated objects appear much nearer than they really are; these mountains do not appear to be further than 15 m. \u00a0\u00a0 we sent a man up this creek to explore the country he returned late in the evening and informed that he had proceeded ten miles directly towards these mountains and that he did not think himself by any mean half way \u00a0\u00a0 these mountains are rockey and covered with some scattering pine. This stream we call \n North Mountain creek. \u00a0\u00a0 the next stream in order is a creek which falls in on Lard. 2 \u00bd miles higher; this is 15 yds. wide no water; a large village of the burrowing or barking squirrels on the Stard. side opposite it's entrance, hence the name \n \n Little dog Ck.\n \n [5] that being the name by which the French Engages call this anamal. \u00a0\u00a0 at three miles and at 10 ms. from hence still ascending 2 Small creek fall in on the Stard. side, no water. \u00a0\u00a0 5 \u00bd miles higher a small river falls in on Lard. side \u00a0\u00a0 this we called South Mountain creek as from it's direction it appeared to take it's rise in a range of Mountains lying in a S. Westerly direction from it's entrance distant 50 or 60 m.; this creek is 40 yards wide and discharges a handsome stream of water. [6] \u00a0\u00a0 it's bed is rockey with gravel and sand, the\nbanks high and country broken it's bottom narrow and no timber. The country high and broken, a considerable portion of black rock and brown sandy rock appear in the faces of the hills; the tops of the hills covered with scattering pine spruce and dwarf cedar; the soil poor and sterile, sandy near the tops of the hills, the whole producing but little grass; the narrow bottoms of the Missouri producing little else but Hysop or southern wood and the pulpy leafed thorn. Capt. Clark walked on shore this evening and killed a buffaloe cow, we left 2 Canoes and six men to dress the Cow and bring on the meat, they did not overtake us this evening. \u00a0\u00a0 game is becoming more scarce, particularly beaver, of which we have seen but few for several days the beaver appears to keep pace with the timber as it declines in quantity they also become more scarce.

\n May 24th Friday 1805. \u00a0\u00a0 a cold night \u00a0\u00a0 the water in the Small vessels froze \u215b of an Inch thick & the Thurmot. Stood this morning at the freezeing point \u00a0\u00a0 we Set out at an eairly hour and proceeded on \u00a0\u00a0 at 9 oClock we had a breeze of wind from the S. E. which continued all day \u00a0\u00a0 this Breeze aforded us good Sailing the River riseing fast \u00a0\u00a0 current verry rapid \u00a0\u00a0 passed Several Small Islands two large & 2 Small creeks \u00a0\u00a0 the 1st of these creeks or small rivers [11] \u00bd a mile above our Camp is 30 yds. wide and contains water and appears to take its rise in the North Mountain which is Situated in a Northely direction abt. 20 miles distant. 2\u00bd miles higher up a creek [12] falls in on the Lard. Side opposite a large village of Barking Squerrells \u00a0\u00a0 3 miles Still higher a Small Creek falls in on the [13] Lard. Side which is 40 yards wide & has running water this Stream appears to take its rise in the South Mountains\n [14] which is Situated in a Southerly direction 30 or 40 miles distant. Capt. Clark walked on the high Country, on the Stard. Side \u00a0\u00a0 found it broken & dry \u00a0\u00a0 Some pine, Spruce & Dwarf ceeder on the hills Sides. \u00a0\u00a0 one man went 10 miles out \u00a0\u00a0 he reported a Simelarity of a country back. Capt. Clark killed a fat buffalow a Short distance below the place we dined \u00a0\u00a0 2 canoes & 6 men waited & got the best of the meat. \u00a0\u00a0 did not joine the party this evening. [Gass]

\n \n

\n Friday 24th. \u00a0\u00a0 There was again some white frost this morning. We embarked early; passed a large creek on the North side and a beautiful island close on the southern shore. At the head of the island, came in another creek on the South side. The bottom of the river, and sand-bars have become much more gravelly than we found them at any place lower down. The water is high, rapid and more clear. At dinner time a party was sent out to bring the meat of some animals that had been killed at a distance. Here we left two canoes to wait for them and proceeded on. We passed a creek on the North side, and having made 24\u00bc miles encamped on the South side. The hills are near, on both sides of the river, and very high.\n\n

\n Friday 24th May 1805. \u00a0\u00a0 clear & pleasant. \u00a0\u00a0 we Set off as usal, & and proceeded on \u00a0\u00a0 passed Several Creeks [15] & Several Small Islands in the river \u00a0\u00a0 passed pitch pine hills & timbred bottoms on each Side. \u00a0\u00a0 about 3 oClock P. M. we halted to dine at a Small & narrow bottom covered with timber on N. S. \u00a0\u00a0 Capt. Clark who walked on Shore had killed a fat buffaloe \u00a0\u00a0 Some of the party went for the meat, high black bluffs on the S. S. & a large Creek [16] which came in a Short distance below. \u00a0\u00a0 the wind from the S. E. So that we Sailed Some part of the time \u00a0\u00a0 about 4 we proceeded on. \u00a0\u00a0 2 canoes waited for the five men to come with the meat. \u00a0\u00a0 Came 24\u00be miles to day and Camped at a bottom covered with c. wood timber which the leaves were dead. \u00a0\u00a0 they had been killed by the frost. \u00a0\u00a0 the 2 Canoes & 6 men Stayed behind all night. \u00a0\u00a0 we Saw a nomber of old Indian Camps in the bottoms near the River.

\n

\nFriday May 24th \u00a0\u00a0 This morning we had Clear pleasant weather, We sett of early, and proceeded on our Voyage, we passed several Creeks, & small Islands lying in the middle of the River, and bottoms and hills with pitch pine growing on them on both sides.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 At 3 o'Clock P. M. we halted to dine at a small narrow bottom, covered with Timber lying on the North side of the River; Captain Clark, who walked along shore, killed a fat buffalo, some of our party was sent to bring the Meat to us. \u00a0\u00a0 we proceeded on, and passed high black bluffs, lying on the South side of the River, & a large Creek, which came in, just below them.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 The wind began to blow from the South east, and we set all Sail.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 leaving 2 Pettyaugers behind, to waite for the five men that had went for the Buffalo meat.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 At 4 o'Clock P. M. we encamped at a bottom, covered with Cotton wood timber, lying on the North side of the River.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 The leaves of these Trees were killed by the frost, The Men that went for the Meat, did not return to us this night, we saw a number of old Indian Camps lying in the River bottoms.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 on both sides of it\n\n\n
