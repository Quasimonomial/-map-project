\n April 29, 1805\n

\n
\n \n

[Lewis]

\n \n
\n Monday April 29th 1805.\n
\u00a0
\n \n
\nSet out this morning at the usual hour; the wind was moderate; I walked on shore with one man. [1] \u00a0\u00a0 about 8 A. M. we fell in with two brown or \u2329yellow\u232a [X: White] bear; both of which we wounded; one of them made his escape, the other after my firing on him pursued me seventy or eighty yards, but fortunately had been so badly wounded that he was unable to pursue so closely as to prevent my charging my gun; we again repeated our fir and killed him. [2] \u00a0\u00a0 it was a male not fully grown, we estimated his weight at 300 lbs, not having the means of ascertaining it precisely. The legs of this bear are somewhat longer than those of the black, as are it's tallons and tusks incomparably larger and longer. \u00a0\u00a0 the testicles, which in the black bear are placed pretty well back between the thyes and contained in one pouch like those of the dog and most quadrupeds, are in the yellow or brown bear placed much further forward, and are suspended in seperate pouches from two to four inches asunder; it's colour is yellowish brown, the eyes small, black, and piercing; the front of the fore legs near the feet is usually black; the fur is finer thicker and deeper than that of the black bear. \u00a0\u00a0 these are all the particulars in which this anamal appeared to me to differ from the black bear; it is a much more furious and formidable anamal, and will frequently pursue the hunter when wounded. \u00a0\u00a0 it is asstonishing to see the wounds they will bear before they can be put to death. \u00a0\u00a0 the Indians may well fear this anamal equiped as they generally are with their bows and arrows or indifferent fuzees, but in the hands of skillfull riflemen they are by no means as formidable or dangerous as they have been represented. \u00a0\u00a0 game is still very abundant we can scarcely cast our eyes in any direction without percieving deer Elk Buffaloe or Antelopes. \u00a0\u00a0 The quantity of wolves appear to increase in the same proportion; they generally hunt in parties of six eight or ten; they kill a great number of the Antelopes at this season; the Antelopes are yet meagre and the females are big with young; the wolves take them most generally in attempting to swim the river; in this manner my dog caught one drowned it and brought it on shore; they are but clumsey swimers, tho' on land when in good order, they are extreemly fleet and dureable. \u00a0\u00a0 we have frequently seen the wolves in pursuit of the Antelope in the plains; they appear to decoy a single one from a flock, and then pursue it, alturnately relieving each other untill they take it. \u00a0\u00a0 on joining Capt Clark he informed me that he had seen a female and faun of the big-horned anamal; that they ran for some distance with great aparent ease along the side of the river bluff where it was almost perpendicular; two of the party fired on them while in motion without effect. \u00a0\u00a0 we took the flesh of the bear on board and proceeded. Capt. Clark walked on shore this evening, killed a deer, and saw several of the bighorned anamals. \u00a0\u00a0 there is more appearance of coal today than we have yet seen, the stratas are 6 feet thick in some instances; the earth has been birnt in many places, and always appears in stratas on the same level with the stratas of coal. [3] \u00a0\u00a0 we came too this evening in the mouth of a little river, which falls in on the Stard. side. \u00a0\u00a0 This stream is about 50 yards wide from bank to bank; the water occupyes about 15 yards. \u00a0\u00a0 the banks are of earth only, abrupt, tho' not high\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 the bed, is of mud principally. Capt Clark, who was up this streeam about three miles, informed me that it continued about the same width, that it's current was gentle and it appeared navigable for perogus \u00a0\u00a0 it meanders through an extensive, fertile, and beautifull vally as far as could bee seen about N. 30\u00b0 W. \u00a0\u00a0 there was but one solitary tree to be seen on the banks of this river after it left the bottom of the Missouri. \u00a0\u00a0 the water of this river is clear, with a brownish yelow tint. \u00a0\u00a0 here the highlands receede from the Missouri, leaving the vally formed by the river from seven to eight miles wide, and reather lower then usual.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 This stream my friend Capt. C. named Marthas river\n [4] \u2329in honor of Miss M F\u2014\u232a\n

[Clark]

\n \n
\n 29th of April Monday 1805\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nSet out this morning at the usial hour. \u00a0\u00a0 the wind is moderate & from the N E \u00a0\u00a0 had not proceeded far eer we Saw a female & her faun of the Bighorn animal on the top of a Bluff lying, the noise we made allarmed them and they came down on the Side of the bluff which had but little Slope being nearly purpindicular, I directed two men to kill those anamals, one went on the top and the other man near the water \u00a0\u00a0 they had two Shots at the doe while in motion without effect, Those animals run & Skiped about with great ease on this declivity & appeared to prefur it to the leavel bottom or plain. Capt Lewis & one man walkd on Shore and he killed a yellow Bear & the man with him wounded one other, after getting the flesh of the bear on bord which was not far from the palce we brackfast, we proceeded on \u00a0\u00a0 Saw 4 gangus of buffalow and great numbers of Antelopes in every direction \u00a0\u00a0 also Saw Elk and Several wolves, I walked on Shore in the evening & killed a Deer which was So meager as to be unfit for use \u00a0\u00a0 The hills Contain more Coal, and has a greater appearance of being burnt that below, the burnt parts appear on a parrilel with the Stratiums of Coal, we Came too in the mouth of a Little river on the S. S. which is about 50 or 60 yards from bank to bank, I was up this Stream 3 miles it continues its width and glides with a gentle Current, its water is about 15 yards wide at this time, and appears to be navagable for Canoes &c. \u00a0\u00a0 it meanders through a butifull & extencive vallie as far as can be Seen about N 30\u00b0 W. I saw only a Single tree in this fertile vallie \u00a0\u00a0 The water of the River is clear of a yellowish Colour, we call this river Martheys river in honor to the Selebrated M. F

\n

\nHere the high land widen from five to Eight miles and much lower than below, Saw Several of the big horn animals this evening. The Wolves distroy great numbers of the antilopes by decoying those animals Singularly out in the plains and prosueing them alternetly, those antelopes are Curious and will approach any thing which appears in motion near them &c.

[Ordway]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Monday 29th April 1805. \u00a0\u00a0 a clear pleasant morning. \u00a0\u00a0 we Set off eairly. \u00a0\u00a0 proceeded on round a bend \u00a0\u00a0 Saw a bay horse in a beautiful Smooth plain on the N. S. where we Saw a great quantity of wild Hop [7] Growing \u00a0\u00a0 we Suppose that this horse had Strayed from Some Savages \u00a0\u00a0 he appeared to be a tollarable Good horse but wild. \u00a0\u00a0 proceeded on a Short distance. Saw a Mountain Sheep [8] on a high Steep bluff on N. S. which had a lamb with it \u00a0\u00a0 one man went up the bluff to Shoot them. \u00a0\u00a0 they took down the bluffs and ran along whare it was nearly Steep where there was a black Stripe in the\nbluffs \u00a0\u00a0 he Shot at them but at too Great a distance. \u00a0\u00a0 they run untill they got round the bluffs and ran in to the prarie. \u00a0\u00a0 the coulour of the Sheep was white \u00a0\u00a0 had large crooked horns, & resembled our tame Sheep only much larger Size & horns. Capt. Lewis and one hunter who walked on Shore this\nmorning. \u00a0\u00a0 came to us about \u00bd past 9 oClock \u00a0\u00a0 had killed a Whiteish bair what is called the white bair, but is not white but light coullour \u00a0\u00a0 we delayed untill \u00bd past 10 to git the meat on board. \u00a0\u00a0 then proceeded on \u00a0\u00a0 passed high bluffs & bottoms on each Side. Saw large flocks of the Cabberree or antilopes and handsom bottom on S. S. also buffaloe & elk. [9] \u00a0\u00a0 Saw a nomber of Mountain Sheep & lambs on a verry high bluffs as nearly like rough mountains \u00a0\u00a0 Some red ceeder in the hollows & gullies in the Mountains. \u00a0\u00a0 these Sheep are verry wild, and keep mostly in these bare hills or mountains \u00a0\u00a0 Some of these hills are red Earth resembling Spanish brown, but the most of them are whitish & naked. Some large Stone at the foot of the bluffs, the country\nback from the river is I belive is barron & no timber & Good for nothing but Game. \u00a0\u00a0 proceeded on \u00a0\u00a0 passed a large timber bottom on the S. S. Camped [10] after dark at the Mouth of a Small river which came in on the N. S. at a beautiful Smoth plain. \u00a0\u00a0 we named it little yallow River. [11] \u00a0\u00a0 Came 25 miles this day.\u2014\n\n

[Gass]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Monday 29th. \u00a0\u00a0 We again set out early, had a clear morning and went on at a good rate. This forenoon we passed some of the highest bluffs I had ever seen; and on the top of the highest we saw some Mountain sheep, \nwhich the natives say are common about the Rocky mountains. These were the first we had seen, and we attempted to kill some of them but did not succeed. Captain Lewis, and one of the men, travelled some distance \nby land and killed a white bear. [12] The natives call them white, but they are more of a brown grey. They are longer than the common black bear, and have much larger feet and talons. We went 25 miles and encamped [13] on the bank of a small river, which comes in on the North side about 70 \nyards wide.\n\n

[Whitehouse]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nMonday April 29th \u00a0\u00a0 We set out Early as usual this morning, and proceeded on; and passed in the forenoon, some very high bluffs, being much higher, than any that we had seen, since we entered the Mesouri River. \u00a0\u00a0 On the Top of one of the highest of those Bluffs, we saw the Animal called the Ibex, or mountain Sheep, they were in a large Flock.\u2014

\n

\nThis animal is about the size of a large Buck deer,\u2014 the Colour Grey, and has hair coarse & like that of a Goat, it ears small and its body lengthy, the horns like that of a Ram, (sheep) but four times as large. \u00a0\u00a0 They are very nimble, and generally are to be found on high Mountains and Bluffs, and are very Shy, and difficult to be come at.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 The Indian women that was with us, inform'd us that those animals were very common to be found On the Rocky mountains.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 Captain Lewis, and one of the hunters, went out a hunting for a short time, and killed a Bear which they brought to the Pettyaugers \u00a0\u00a0 This Bear was of a Yellow brownish colour, and had prodigious large Claws, and \u2329are\u232a is what is called the White Bear by the Natives; We continued on our Voyage, & in the Evening, we encamp'd on the bank of a River, which emtied itself into the Mesouri on the North side, which is 70 Yards wide & by our Officers called Martha's River, having come 25 Miles \nthis day.\n\n
