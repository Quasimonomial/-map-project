\n March 26, 1806\n

\n
\n \n

[Lewis]

\n \n
\n Wednesday March 26th 1806.\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nThe wind blew so hard this morning that we delayed untill 8 A. M. we gave a medal of small size to a man by the name of Wal-lal'-le, [1] a principal man among the Cathlahmahs, he appeared very thankfull for the honour conferred on him and presented us a large sturgeon. \u00a0\u00a0 we continued our rout up the river to an old village on the Stard. side where we halted for dinner. [2] \u00a0\u00a0 we met on the way the principal Cheif of the Cathlahmahs, S\u00e2h-h\u00e2h-w\u00f4h-cap, [3] who had been up the river on a trading voyage. \u00a0\u00a0 he gave us some Wappetoe and fish; we also purchased some of the latter. \u00a0\u00a0 soon after we halted for dinner the two Wackiacums who have been pursuing us since yesterday morning with two dogs for sale, arrived. \u00a0\u00a0 they wish tobacco in exchange for their dogs which we are not disposed to give as our stock is now reduced to a very few carrots. [4] \u00a0\u00a0 our men who have been accustomed to the use of this article Tobaco and to whom we are now obliged to deny the uce of this article appear to suffer much for the want of it. \u00a0\u00a0 they substitute the bark of the wild crab which they chew; it is very bitter, and they assure me they find it a good substitute for tobacco. the smokers substitute the inner bark of the red willow and the sacacommis. [5] \u00a0\u00a0 here our hunters joined us having killed three Eagles and a large goose. I had now an oportunity of comparing the bald with the grey Eagle; [6] I found that the greay Eagle was about \u00bc larger, it's legs and feet were dark while those of the bald Eagle wer of a fine orrange yellow; the iris of the eye is also of a dark yellowish brown while that of the other is of a bright silvery colour with a slight admixture of yellow. \u00a0\u00a0 after dinner we proceeded on and passed an Elegant and extensive bottom on the South side and an island near it's upper point which we call Fanny's Island and bottom. [7] \u00a0\u00a0 the greater part of the bottom is a high dry prarie. \u00a0\u00a0 near the river towards the upper point we saw a fine grove of whiteoak trees; [8] we saw some deer and Elk at a distance in the prarie, but did not delay for the purpose of hunting them. \u00a0\u00a0 we continued our rout after dinner untill late in the evening and encamped on the next island above fanny's Island. [9] \u00a0\u00a0 we found it difficult to obtain as much wood as answered our purposes. \u00a0\u00a0 the hunters [10] who had proceeded on before us after dinner did not join us this evening. \u00a0\u00a0 some indians visited us after dark, but did not remain long. \u00a0\u00a0 agreeably to our estimate as we decended the river, we came 16 m. 23rd, 16 m. the 24th, 15 the 25th, and 18 m. the 26th, tho' I now think that our estimate in decending the river was too short.\n

[Clark]

\n \n
\n Wednesday March 26th 1806\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nThe wind blew So hard untill 8 A M. that we detained, we gave a Medal to a Man by the name of Wal-lal-le a principal man among the Cath lah mahs, he appeared very thankfull for the honor Confured on him and presented us with a large Sturgion. \u00a0\u00a0 we Continued our rout up the river to an old Village on the South Side where we halted for dinner. \u00a0\u00a0 we met on the way the principal Chief of the Cathlahmahs, \n S\u00e2h-h\u00e2h-w\u00e2h-cop, who had been up the river on a trading voyage, he gave us some Wappato and fish, we also purchased Some Wappato Soon after halted for dinner at an Old Village \u2329at\u232a on the South point opposit the lower pt. of Fannys Island. The two Warkiacums who had been pursueing us Since yester day morning with two dogs for Sale, arrived. \u00a0\u00a0 they wish Tobacco in exchange for their dogs which we are not disposed to give, as our Stock is now reduced to 3 carrots. \u00a0\u00a0 our men who have been acustomed to the use of this article, and to Whome we are now obliged to deny the use of this article appear to Suffer Much for the want of it. \u00a0\u00a0 they Substitute the bark of the wild Crab which they Chew; it is very bitter and they assure me they find it a good Substitute for tobacco. \u00a0\u00a0 the Smokers Substitute the iner bark of the redwillow and the saccommis.

\n

\nhere our hunters joined us haveing killed 3 Eagles and a large Wild goose. I had now an oppertunity of Comparing the bald \u2329and\u232a with the grey Eagle; I found the grey Eagle about \u00bc largest, its legs and feet were dark which those of the bald eagle were of a fine orrange yellow; the iris of the eye is also of a dark yellowish brown, while that of the Grey is of a light Silvery colour with a Slight admixture of yellow. \u00a0\u00a0 after dinner I walked on Shore through an eligant bottom on the South Side opposit to Fannys Island.

\n

\nThis bottom we also Call fannys bottom \u00a0\u00a0 it is extensive and an open leavel plain except near the river bank which is high dry rich oak land. I saw Some deer & Elk at a distance in the Prarie. \u00a0\u00a0 we continued untill late in the evening and encamped on a Small Island near the Middle of the river haveing made 18 Miles. \u00a0\u00a0 2 Indians Visited us this evining\n

[Ordway]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nWednesday 26th March 1806. \u00a0\u00a0 the wind ran high last night and the tide rose higher than common and came in under my blankets before I awoke and obledged me to move twise \u00a0\u00a0 Several more of the party camps were\nrouted also.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 our officers gave one of the Cath lih mahs a meddel. \u00a0\u00a0 he gave them in return a large Sturgeon. \u00a0\u00a0 we proceeded on our way and halted on fanneys Island to dine then proceed. on as usal \u00a0\u00a0 Camped on an Island in thick brush &C.\u2014\n\n

\n \n
\n
\n \n

[Gass]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Wednesday 26th. \u00a0\u00a0 After a disagreeable night's rain, and wind, we continued our voyage. As we passed along, I saw a great many flowers full blown of different colours; and grass and other herbage growing fast: I saw nettles [11] two feet high of this spring's growth. [12]\n

\n \n
\n
\n \n

[Whitehouse]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nWednesday March 26th \u00a0\u00a0 The wind & tide rose very high during last night. \u00a0\u00a0 the water raised so much that it obliged several of our party to move their Camps. \u00a0\u00a0 Our Officers gave one of the Indians, who belonged to the fishing Camp near us, A. Medal, & the Indian in return, gave them a large Sturgeon. \u00a0\u00a0 At 8 o'Clock A. M. we proceeded on ascending the Columbia River, & halted at an Island called by our Officers Fannys Island to dine. \u00a0\u00a0 We continued on our way at 2 o'Clock P. M. and towards evening we halted at an Island & encamped for the night in a Thicket of Woods on the same.\n\n\n\n

\n \n \n
\n
1. A Jefferson medal, probably of 55mm. Wal-lal'-le, a Cathlamet personal name, is \n Wal\u00e1li.\n (back)
\n
2. Noted as \"old V\" on Atlas map 81, near the west end of \"Fannys Isd.,\" present Crims Island, Columbia County, Oregon. It is opposite Oak Point, with the dividing line of Cowlitz and Wahkiakum counties, Washington, just to the west. The village was mentioned by many later travelers, including Alexander Ross, Robert Stuart, and Gabriel Franch\u00e8re. Ross, 113; Spaulding, 44; Franch\u00e8re (JV), 78. (back)
\n
3. \n S\u00e2h-h\u00e2h-w\u00f4h-cap, another Cathlamet name, is Sax\u0323awaq\u0313ap.\n (back)
\n
4. Tobacco was stored and transported in long twists called \"carrots\" for their shape; see August 19, 1804. Beginning here, a vertical line runs nearly to the end of the entry, perhaps Biddle's work. (back)
\n
5. The wild crab is Oregon crabapple, Malus diversifolia (Bong.) Roem., also given as Pyrus fusca Raf. and more recently as M. fusca (Raf.) Schneid. See Lewis's description at January 28, 1806. Saccacommis was the traditional smoking mixture of the plains Indians and the French engag\u00e9s, consisting of the dried leaves of bearberry or kinnikinnick, Arctostaphylos uva-ursi (L.) Spreng, and of the dried inner bark or phloem tissue of red osier dogwood (Lewis's red willow). (back)
\n
6. The bald eagle is Haliaeetus leucocephalus [AOU, 352]; the \"grey\" eagle is the golden eagle, Aquila chrysaetos [AOU, 349]. The bald is actually larger than the golden. Lewis may have compared a female bald eagle to a male golden. Burroughs, 204\u20138, 325 n. 7. (back)
\n
7. Supposedly named for Clark's youngest sister, Frances; later Crims Island. \"Fanny's bottom\" became \"Fanny's Valley\" on Atlas map 81, perhaps because Clark had second thoughts. Coues (HLC), 3:909 n. 11. (back)
\n
8. Oregon white, or Garry, oak, Quercus garryana Dougl. ex Hook. Lewis also collected a specimen of the plant this day. Hitchcock et al., 2:85; Little (CIH), 166-W; Cutright (LCPN), 290. (back)
\n
9. On one of the small islands below present Longview, Cowlitz Country, Washington, in Columbia County, Oregon. The possibilities include Walker and Dibblee (formerly Lord) islands; see November 6, 1805.\n Atlas map 81; cf. Thwaites (LC), 4:204 n. 1. (back)
\n
10. \n Drouillard and the Field brothers. (back)
\n
11. Stinging nettle, Urtica dioica L. ssp. gracilis (Ait.) Seland. (back)
\n
12. They camped on one of the small islands, including Walker and Dibblee islands, below \nLongview, Cowlitz County, Washington, in Columbia County, Oregon. (back)
