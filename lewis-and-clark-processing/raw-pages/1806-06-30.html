\n June 30, 1806\n

\n
\n \n

[Lewis]

\n \n
\n Monday June 30th 1806.\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nWe dispatched Drewyer and J. Fields early this morning to hunt on the road and indeavour to obtain some meat for us. \u00a0\u00a0 just as we had prepared to set out at an early hour a deer came in to lick at these springs and one of our hunters killed it; this secured us our dinners, and we proceeded down the creek sometimes in the bottoms and at other times on the top or along the steep sides of the ridge to the N. of the Creek. \u00a0\u00a0 at one mile from the springs we passed a stout branch of the creek on the north side and at noon having travelled 13 ms. we arrived at the entrance of a second Northen branch of the creek [1] where we had nooned it on the 12th of Septr. last. \u00a0\u00a0 here we halted, dined and graized our horses. \u00a0\u00a0 while here Sheilds took a small tern [2] and killed a deer. \u00a0\u00a0 at this place a road turns off to the wright which the indians informed us leads to Clarks river\n [3] some distance below where there is a fine extensive vally in which the Shalees or Ootslashshoots sometimes reside. \u00a0\u00a0 in descending the creek this morning on the steep side of a high hill my horse sliped and both his hinder feet out of the road and fell, I also fell off backwards and slid near 40 feet down the hill before I could stop myself such was the steepness of the declivity; the horse was near falling on me in the first instance but fortunately recovers and we both escaped unhirt. I saw a small grey squirrel [4] today much like those of the Pacific coast only that the belly of this was white. I also met with the plant in blume which is sometimes called the lady's slipper or mockerson flower. [5] \u00a0\u00a0 it is in shape and appearance like ours only that the corolla is white, marked with small veigns of pale red longitudinally on the inner side. \u00a0\u00a0 after dinner we resumed our march. soon after seting out Sheilds killed another deer and in the course of the evening we picked up three others which Drewyer had killed along the road making a total of 6 today. Deer are very abundant in the neighborhood of travellers rest of both speceis, [6] also some bighorns [7] and Elk. \u00a0\u00a0 a little before sunset we arrived at our old encampment on the south side of the creek a little above it's entrance into Clark's river. [8] \u00a0\u00a0 here we encamped with a view to remain two days in order to rest ourselves and horses & make our final arrangements for seperation. \u00a0\u00a0 we came 19 ms. after dinner the road being much better than it has been since we entered the mountains \u00a0\u00a0 we found no appearance of the Ootslashshoots having been here lately. \u00a0\u00a0 the indians express much concern for them and apprehend that the Minnetares of fort de Prarie have distroyed them in the course of the last winter and spring, and mention the tracks of the bearfoot Indians which we saw yesterday as an evidence of their being much distressed.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 our horses have stood the journey supprisingly well, most of them are yet in fine order, and only want a few days rest to restore them perfectly.\u2014

[Clark]

\n \n
Monday June 30th 1806
\u00a0
\n \n

\nWe dispatched Drewyer & Jo Field early this morning ahead to hunt. just as we had prepard. to set out at an early hour, a deer Came in to lick at the Springs and one of our hunters killed it; this Secired to us our dinner. \u00a0\u00a0 and we proceeded down the Creek, Sometimes in the bottoms and at other times on the tops or along the Steep Sides of the ridge to the N or the Creek. \u00a0\u00a0 at 1\u00bd m. we passd our encampment of the 12th of Septr. last \u00a0\u00a0 we noon'd it at the place we had on the 12 of Septr. last \u00a0\u00a0 whiles here Shields killed a deer on the N. fork near the road. \u00a0\u00a0 here a rode leads up the N. fork and passed over to an extensive vally on Clarks river at Some distance down that river as our guids inform us. \u00a0\u00a0 after dinner we resumed our march. Soon after Setting out Shields killed another deer, and we picked up 3 others which G Drewyer had killed along the road. Deer are very abundant in the neighbourhood of travellers rest of boath Specis, also Some big horn and Elk. \u00a0\u00a0 a little before Sunset we arrived at our old encampment on the S. Side of the Creek a little above its enterance into Clarks river. \u00a0\u00a0 here we Encamped with a view to remain 2 days in order to rest ourselves and horses and make our final arrangements for Seperation. \u00a0\u00a0 we found no signs of the Oatlashshots haveing been here lately. \u00a0\u00a0 the Indians express much Concern for them and apprehend that the Menetarries of Fort d Prar have destroyed them in the course of the last Winter and Spring, and mention the tracts of the bear-footed indians which we Saw yesterday as an evidence of their being much distressed\u2014. \u00a0\u00a0 our horses have stood the journey Supirisinly well and only want a fiew days rest to restore them.\n

\nDescended the mountain to travellers rest leaveing those tremendious mountanes behind us\u2014in passing of which we have experiensed Cold and hunger of which I shall ever remember. \u00a0\u00a0 \u2329as we\u232a in passing over this part of the Rocky mountains from Clarks river, to the quawmash flats from the 14th to the 19th of Septr. 1805 we marched through Snow, which fell on us on the night of the 14th and nearly all the day of the 15 in addition to the [c]old rendered the air cool and the way difficuelt. \u00a0\u00a0 our food was horses of which we eate three.\u2014 On our return we Set out from the quawmash flats on the 15th of June and commenes the assent of the rocky mountains; the air became cool and vigitation backward\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 on the 16th we met with banks of Snow and in the hollars and maney of the hill Sides the Snow was from 3 to 4 feet deep and Scercely any grass vegitation just commencing where the Snow had melted\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 on the 17th at meridian, the Snow became So deep in every derection from 6 to 8 feet deep we could not prosue the road \u2329or direction,\u232a \u00a0\u00a0 there being no grass for our horses we were obliged to return to the quawmash flatts to precure meat to live on as well as grass for our horses\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 leaveing our baggage on the mountains We precured 5 Indians as pilots and on the 24th of June 1806 we again under took those Snowey regn. \u00a0\u00a0 on the 26th we with our baggage arived at an open plain serounded with Snow where there was grass for horses \u00a0\u00a0 on the 27th & 28th also passing over Snow 6 or 8 feet deep all the way \u00a0\u00a0 on 29th passed over but little Snow\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 but saw great masses of it lying in different directions\n

[Ordway]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nMonday 30th June 1806. \u00a0\u00a0 a clear morning. \u00a0\u00a0 we got up our horses as usal \u00a0\u00a0 R. Fields killed a deer near the hot Springs in Scite of the Camp. \u00a0\u00a0 two hunters went on a head. \u00a0\u00a0 we Set out \u00a0\u00a0 proceed. on a muddy bad road down the creek & over bad hills &C \u00a0\u00a0 about noon we halted to dine 12th Sept last [11] \u00a0\u00a0 Shields killed a deer. \u00a0\u00a0 we dined and proceed. on \u00a0\u00a0 took the meat of a deer which the hunters had killed. Shields killed another deer. \u00a0\u00a0 proceed. on to the bottoms or plains of travvellers rest creek \u00a0\u00a0 Drewyer had killed three deer, we wrode fast untill about Sunset at which time we arived at travvellers rest where we Camped the 9th & 10th of Sept. last. \u00a0\u00a0 we Camped [12] here in order to Stay 2 or 3 days to refresh our horses and kill Some meat &C. \u00a0\u00a0 the Musquetoes verry troublesome here.\u2014\n\n\n

[Gass]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Monday 30th. \u00a0\u00a0 We continued our march early and had a fine morning. When we were ready to set out, we saw a deer coming to a lick at the hot spring, and one of our hunters shot it. Two hunters went on ahead. At noon another went out a short time, and killed a fine deer. We halted for dinner at the same place, where we dined on the 12 of Sept. 1805, [13] as we passed over to the Western ocean. After dinner we proceeded on, and on \nour way found three deer that one of the hunters [14] had killed and left for us. \nIn the evening we arrived at Travellers'-rest creek, [15] where the party rested \ntwo days last fall, and where it empties into Flathead (called Clarke's) river, a beautiful river about one hundred yards wide at this place; but there is no fish of any consequence in it; and according to the Indian account, there are falls on it, between this place and its mouth, where it empties into the Columbia, six or seven hundred feet high; [16] and which probably prevent the fish from coming up. Here we encamped and meet with the hunters.\n\n\n\n

16. The captains gave the name Clark's River to a combination of the Bitterroot, Clark Fork, and Pend Oreille rivers, the last of which finally flows into the Columbia. The falls referred to may be Thompson Falls, on the Clark Fork, Sanders County, Montana. (back)
