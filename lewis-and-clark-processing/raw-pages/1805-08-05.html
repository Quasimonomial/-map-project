\n Monday August 5th 1805\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nAs Charbono complained of being unable to march far today I ordered him and Sergt. Gass to pass the rappid river near our camp and proceed at their leasure through the level bottom to a point of high timber about seven miles distant on the middle fork which was in view; I gave them my pack that of Drewyer and the meat which we had, directing them to remain at that place untill we joined them. I took Drewyer with me and continued my rout up the stard. side of the river about 4 miles and then waded it; found it so rapid and shallow that it was impossible to navigate it. \u00a0\u00a0 continued up it on the Lard. side about 1\u00bd miles further when the mountains put in close on both sides and arrose to great hight, partially covered with snow. \u00a0\u00a0 from hence the course of the river was to the East of North. I took the advantage of a high projecting spur of the mountain which with some difficulty we ascended to it's summit in about half an hour. \u00a0\u00a0 from this eminance I had a pleasing view of the valley through which I had passed many miles below and the continuation of the middle fork through the valley equally wide above me to the distance of about 20 miles when that also appeared to enter the mountains and disappeared to my view; however the mountains which termineate the valley in this direction appears much lower than those up either of the other forks. \u00a0\u00a0 on the rapid fork they appeared still to rise the one range towering above another as far as I could perceive them. \u00a0\u00a0 the middle fork as I suspected dose bear considerably to the West of South and the gap formed by it in the mountains after the valley terminates is in the same direction. \u00a0\u00a0 under these circumstances I did not hesitate in beleiving the middle fork the most proper for us to ascend. [2] \u00a0\u00a0 about South from me the middle fork approached within about 5 miles. I resolved to pass across the plains to it and return to Gass and Charbono, accordingly we set out and decended the mountain among some steep and difficult precipices of rocks. \u00a0\u00a0 here Drewyer missed his step and had a very dangerous fall, he sprained one of his fingers and hirt his leg very much. \u00a0\u00a0 in fifteen or 20 minutes he was able to proceed and we continued our rout to the river where we had desighned to interscept it. I quenched my thirst and rested a few minutes examined the river and found it still very navigable. \u00a0\u00a0 an old indian road very large and plain leads up this fork, but I could see no tracks except those of horses which appeared to have passed early in the spring. \u00a0\u00a0 as the river mad a great bend to the South East we again ascended the high plain and steered our course as streight as we could to the point where I had directed Gass and Sharbono to remain. we passed the plain regained the bottom and struck the river about 3 miles above them; by this time it was perfectly dark & we hooped but could hear no tidings of them. \u00a0\u00a0 we had struck the river at the point of timber to which I had directed them, but [they] having mistaken a point of woods lower down, had halted short of the place. \u00a0\u00a0 we continued our rout after dark down the bottom through thick brush of the pulppy leafed thorn [3] and prickly pears for about 2 hours when we arrived at their camp. [4] \u00a0\u00a0 they had a small quantity of meat left which Drewyer and myself eat it being the first we had taisted today. \u00a0\u00a0 we had traveled about 25 miles. I soon laid down and slept very soundly untill morning. I saw no deer today nor any game except a few Antelopes which were very shy. the soil of the plains is a light yellow clay very meager and intermixed with a large proportion of gravel, [5] producing nothing except the twisted or bearded grass, sedge and prickly pears. \u00a0\u00a0 the dryer parts of the bottoms are also much more indifferent in point of soil to those below and are covered with the southernwood pulpy leafed thorn and prickley pears with but little grass. \u00a0\u00a0 the moist parts are fertile and covered with fine grass and sand rushes.

\n

\nThis morning Capt. Clark set out at sunrise and dispatched Joseph & Reubin Fields to hunt. \u00a0\u00a0 they killed two deer on one of which the party breakfasted. \u00a0\u00a0 the river today they found streighter and more rapid even than yesterday, and the labour and difficulty of the navigation was proportionably increased, they therefore proceeded but slowly and with great pain as the men had become very languid from working in the water and many of their feet swolen and so painfull that they could scarcely walk. \u00a0\u00a0 at 4 P. M. they arrived at the confluence of the two rivers where I had left the note. \u00a0\u00a0 this note had unfortunately been placed on a green pole which the beaver had cut and carried off together with the note; the possibility of such an occurrence never onc occurred to me when I placed it on the green pole. \u00a0\u00a0 this accedent deprived Capt. Clark of any information with ripect to the country and supposing that the rapid fork was most in the direction which it was proper we should pursue, or West, he took that stream and asscended it with much difficulty about a mile and encamped on an island that had been lately overflown and was yet damp; [6] they were therefore compelled to make beds of brush to keep themselves out of the mud. \u00a0\u00a0 in ascending this stream for about a quarter of a mile it scattered in such a maner that they were obliged to cut a passage through the willow brush which leant over the little channels and united their tops. \u00a0\u00a0 Capt. Clarks ankle is extreemly painfull to him this evening; the tumor has not yet mature, he has a slight fever.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 The men were so much fortiegued today that they wished much that navigation was at an end that they might go by land.\u2014

[Clark]

\n August 5th Monday 1805\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\na Cold Clear morning the wind from the S. E. \u00a0\u00a0 the river Streight & much more rapid than yesterday, I Sent out Jo. & R. Fields to kill Some meat \u00a0\u00a0 they killed 2 Deer & we brackfast on one of them and proceeded on with great dificuelety from the rapidity of the Current, and numerable rapids we had to encounter, at 4 oClock P M Murcury 49 ab. 0, passed the mouth [7] of principal fork which falls in on the Lard. Side, this fork is about the Size of the Stard. one less water reather not so rapid, its Course as far as can be Seen is S. E & appear to pass through between two mountains, the N W. fork being the one most in our course i.e. S 25 W. as far as I can See, deturmind me to take this fork as the principal and the one most proper the S E fork is of a Greenish Colour & contains but little timber. The S W fok contains more timber than is below for Some distance, [8] we assended this fork about one mile and Encamped on an island which had been laterly overflown & was wet \u00a0\u00a0 we raised our bead on bushes, we passed a part of the river above the forks which was divided and Scattered thro the willows in Such a manner as to render it dificuelt to pass through for a \u00bc of a mile, we were oblige to Cut our way thro' the willows\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 Men much fatigued from their excessive labours in hauling the Canoes over the rapids &c. verry weak being in the water all day. \u00a0\u00a0 my foot verry painfull

\n Monday 5th August 1805. \u00a0\u00a0 a clear cool morning \u00a0\u00a0 we Set out as usal \u00a0\u00a0 2 hunters Sent on a head to kill Some meat. \u00a0\u00a0 passd. rapids as usal. \u00a0\u00a0 one of the hunters killed a deer before brakfast. \u00a0\u00a0 the wind cold from the South. \u00a0\u00a0 the Shores and hills rocky \u00a0\u00a0 the bottom of the River covred with Slippery Small Stone and gravvel. \u00a0\u00a0 we proceeded on \u00a0\u00a0 passed over rapids worse than\never \u00a0\u00a0 it is with difficulty & hard fatigue we git up them Some of which are allmost perpinticular 3 or 4 feet fall in a Short distance. \u00a0\u00a0 our other hunter joined us at noon \u00a0\u00a0 had killed nothing but had Seen where the River forks again. \u00a0\u00a0 about 7 oC. P. m. clouded up \u00a0\u00a0 wind high \u00a0\u00a0 we proceeded on \u00a0\u00a0 about a mile further up came to a another forks [9] one nearly as the other \u00a0\u00a0 if any difference the right hand fork the largest. \u00a0\u00a0 we was not certian whether Capt. Lewis was up the left fork or right So \u2329we\u232a Capt Clark\n [10] left a note for him on the point which is level prarie, & proceeded on up the right hand fork, which is amazeing rapid Some of which falls nearly 3 feet in the length of a canoe, but with hard labour we draged them over. \u00a0\u00a0 we passed thro a channel which was filled with willows and young cotton wood & brush,\nSome of which was fell across by the beaver. \u00a0\u00a0 the currents So rapid we were oblidged to hall by the bushes, and Some places be out in the water where we could Scarsely kick our feet for the rapidity of the current. Saw Several high beaver dams. \u00a0\u00a0 passed Several Islands. \u00a0\u00a0 we could Scarsely croud the canoes through the bushes in Several places, night came on and we Camped on the Stard. Side at a bottom which is level and low, has been over flowed lately. \u00a0\u00a0 it appears this little Stream is verry high, but has been high by the Snow melting off the Mountains. \u00a0\u00a0 it is now falling a little. \u00a0\u00a0 was it low we could not proceeded by water any further. \u00a0\u00a0 our hunter killed a deer. Came 8 to forks miles this day. \u00a0\u00a0 the party much fatigued and wish to go by land.\n\n\n

\n Monday 5th August 1805. \u00a0\u00a0 a clear cool morning. \u00a0\u00a0 we Set out at Sunrise \u00a0\u00a0 2 hunters Sent on a head to kill Some meat. \u00a0\u00a0 one of them joined us with a deer he had killed before breakfast time. \u00a0\u00a0 the wind cold from the South. \u00a0\u00a0 the Shores and hills rockey & bottom of the River covd. with Small Stones. \u00a0\u00a0 our other hunter joined us at noon, had killed nothing. \u00a0\u00a0 the rapids gits worse that ever. \u00a0\u00a0 it is with difficulty we git over them, & verry fatigueing. \u00a0\u00a0 at 1 oC. P. M. clouded up. \u00a0\u00a0 wind high. \u00a0\u00a0 proceeded on \u00a0\u00a0 about a mile further up came to a fork [14] \u00a0\u00a0 we took the right hand fork which was amazeing rapid. \u00a0\u00a0 Some of the rapids falls 3 or 4 feet or their abouts in the length of our canoes. \u00a0\u00a0 we passed through a channel where the water was rapid and ran \nthrough the willows & young cotton wood \u00a0\u00a0 the beaver had fell Some of them across the channel and it crooked \u00a0\u00a0 it was with much difficulty we got thro. \u00a0\u00a0 obledged to forse our way through the bushes and hall by them. \u00a0\u00a0 Some places out in the water could Scarsely keep our feet for the rapidity of the current. \u00a0\u00a0 Saw Several beaver dams verry high. \u00a0\u00a0 night came on. \u00a0\u00a0 Camped on S. Side at a low bottom, which has lately been overflowed. \u00a0\u00a0 we expect this little Stream is high from the Snow melting on the mountains. \u00a0\u00a0 \nit appears it has lately been higher, but is now falling a little. \u00a0\u00a0 was it low their would not be water enofe in it for us to proceed any further by water. \u00a0\u00a0 our hunter killed a deer. \u00a0\u00a0 Came 8 miles this day. \u00a0\u00a0 the party much fatigued and wish to leave the canoes & go by land.

\n

\nMonday August 5th \u00a0\u00a0 A Clear, cool morning, we set out as usual, and sent 2 of our hunters ahead in order to kill some Game for us, One of which joined us before breakfast with a deer he had killed. \u00a0\u00a0 The wind blew cold from the South, the Shores & hills rockey & the bottoms of the river, covered with small Stones, The other hunter joined us at Noon, but had not kill'd anything. \u00a0\u00a0 The rapids of the River, we find here, worse than any that we have yet met with; and it is with great difficulty that we can pass them, and very fataigueing to our party\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 About 1 o'Clock P. M. the weather clouded up, and the wind got high. \u00a0\u00a0 We proceeded on about a Mile further up, & \ncame too.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 We halted here for a short time, & then proceeded on, and came to where the River forked, We here took the right hand fork, which we found run very rapid, some of the Rapids of the River; here fell 3 feet, and \nwas between 3 & 4 feet in length. \u00a0\u00a0 We passed through a channel where we found the water run rapid through Willows & Cotton wood trees, The Beaver had cut down some of those trees, which had fell across the Channel, & the \nRiver running Crooked, it was with much difficulty we got through them, being obliged to force our Canoes through the bushes, & hawl the Canoes by them. \u00a0\u00a0 Several of our party were forced to go out into the Water in several places, to hawl along the Canoes, and the rapidity of the Current made it very difficult for them to keep \u2329on\u232a their feet.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 We saw several beaver dams, which was very high; Night came on, & we encamped on the South side of the River in a low bottom, which had been lately overflowed. \u00a0\u00a0 The fork that we are at (or rather small stream of water) is high, which we suppose is occasioned from the Water melting on the Mountains. \u00a0\u00a0 this River has the appearance of having been considerable higher, than it is at present, and at any common time. \u00a0\u00a0 we are of oppinion that there would not be water sufficient for our Canoes to proceed any futher.\u2014

\n

\nWe came 8 Miles this day, & encamped; our party are much fataigued, & it is the wish of all of them, that we would proceed on our Voyage by land to the Columbia River\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 Our hunter joined us at our encampment, and brought with him a Deer, he had killed.\u2014\n\n\n\n

2. \n Lewis continued up the Big Hole (Wisdom) River about three miles beyond the Madison-Beaverhead county line where he climbed a ridge for a commanding view.
