\n April 1, 1806\n

\n
\n \n

[Lewis]

\n \n
\n Tuesday April 1st 1806.\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nThis morning early we dispatched Sergt. Pryar with two men in a small canoe up quicksand river with orders to proceed as far as he could and return this evening. \u00a0\u00a0 we also sent a party of three hunters [1] over the river to hunt a large bottom of woodland and prarie above the entrance of the Quicksand river; the ballance of the hunters we sent out in different directions on this side of the Columbia and employed those about camp in making a rope of Elkskin. \u00a0\u00a0 the Indians who encamped near us last evening continued with us untill about midday. \u00a0\u00a0 they informed us that the quicksand river which we have heretofore deemed so considerable, only extendes through the Western mountains as far as the S. Western side of mount hood where it takes it's source. [2] \u00a0\u00a0 this mountain bears E from this place and is distant about 40 miles. \u00a0\u00a0 this information was corroborated by that of sundry other indians who visited us in the course of the day. \u00a0\u00a0 we were now convinced that there must be some other considerable river which flowed into the columbia on it's south side below us which we have not yet seen, as the extensive valley on that side of the river lying between the mountainous country of the Coast and the Western mountains [3] must be watered by some stream which we had heretofore supposed was the quicksand river. \u00a0\u00a0 but if it be a fact that the quicksand river heads in Mount Hood it must leave the valley within a few miles of it's entrance and runs nearly parallel with the Columbia river upwards. \u00a0\u00a0 we indeavoured to ascertain by what steam the southern portion of the Columbia valley was watered but could obtain no satisfactory information of the natives on this head. \u00a0\u00a0 they informed us that the quicksand river is navigable a short distance only in consequence of falls and rapids; and that no nation inhabits it.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 Sergt. Pryar returned in the evening and reported that he had ascended the river six miles; that above the point at which it divides itself into two channels it is about 300 yds wide tho' the channel is not more than 50 yds and only 6 ft deep. \u00a0\u00a0 this is a large vollume of water to collect in so short a distance; I therefore think it probable that there are some large creeks falling into it from the S. W. the bed of this stream is formed entirely of quicksand; it's banks are low and at preasent overflows. \u00a0\u00a0 the water is turbid and current rapid.\u2014 the following are the courses taken by Sergt. Pryor. S. 10\u00b0 W. 1 M. to a point on the Lard. side passing a large Island on Stard. S. 24\u00b0 E. 2 m. to the head of an Island near the Lard. shore S 33\u00b0 E. 4 m. to a stard. point passing several islands on the Lard. side and a creek 50 yds. wide on Stard at 1 \u00bd miles. \u00a0\u00a0 the river from hence appeared to bend to the East. \u00a0\u00a0 he heard falls of water. \u00a0\u00a0 several different tribes informed us that it heads at Mount Hood. We were visited by several canoes of natives in the course of the day; most of whom were decending the river with their women and children. \u00a0\u00a0 they informed us that they resided at the great rapids and that their relations at that place were much streightened at that place for the want of food; that they had consumed their winter store of dryed fish and that those of the present season had not yet arrived. I could not learn wheather they took the Sturgeon but presume if they do it is in but small quantities as they complained much of the scarcity of food among them. \u00a0\u00a0 they informed us that the nations above them were in the same situation & that they did not expect the Salmon to arrive untill the full of the next moon which happens on the 2d of May. [4] \u00a0\u00a0 we did not doubt the varacity of these people who seemed to be on their way with their families and effects in surch of subsistence which they find it easy to procure in this fertile valley.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 This information gave us much uneasiness with rispect to our future means of subsistence. \u00a0\u00a0 above falls or through plains from thence to the Chopunnish there are no deer Antelope [5] nor Elk on which we can depend for subsistence; their horses are very poor most probably at this season, and if they have no fish their dogs must be in the same situation. \u00a0\u00a0 under these circumstances there seems to be but a gloomy prospect for subsistence on any terms; we therefore took it into serious consideration what measures we were to pursue on this occasion; it was at once deemed inexpedient to wait the arrival of the salmon as that would detain us so large a portion of the season that it is probable we should not reach the United States before the ice would close the Missouri; or at all events would hazard our horses which we lelft in charge of the Chopunnish\n [6] who informed us that they intended passing the rocky mountains to the Missouri as early as the season would permit them wich is as we believe about the begining of May. \u00a0\u00a0 should these people leave their situation near kooskooske\n [7] before our arrival we may probably find much difficulty in recovering our horses; without which there will be but little possibility of repassing the mountains; we are therefore determined to loose as little time as possible in geting to the Chopunnish Village. \u00a0\u00a0 at 3 P. M. the hunters who were sent over the river returned having killed 4 Elk and two deer; the Elk were in good order but the deer extreemly poor. \u00a0\u00a0 they informed us that game is very plenty in that quarter. \u00a0\u00a0 the hunters on this side of the river also returned but had killed nothing; they saw a few Elk and deer. \u00a0\u00a0 there was also much sign of the black bear [8] seen on the other side of the river. \u00a0\u00a0 we sent a party to bring in the flesh of the Elk and deer that were killed. \u00a0\u00a0 they did not return this evening. I purchased a canoe from an Indian today for which I gave him six fathoms of wampum beads; [9] he seemed satisfyed with his bargain and departed in another canoe but shortly after returned and canceled the bargain; took his canoe and returned the beads. \u00a0\u00a0 this is frequently the case in their method of traiding and is deemed fair by them. The last evening and this morning were so cloudy that I could neither obtain any Lunar observations nor equal altitudes.\u2014

[Clark]

\n \n
\n Tuesday April 1st 1806\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nThis morning early we dispatched Sergt. Pryor, with two men in a Small canoe up quick sand river with orders to proceed as far as he Could and return this evening. \u00a0\u00a0 we also Sent a party of three hunters over the river to hunt a large bottom of woodland and prarie above the enterance of Q. Sand River; the ballance of the hunters we Sent out in different directions on this Side of the Columbia, and employed those about Camp in makeing a rope of Elk Skin.

\n

\nThe information given by the indians to us last night respecting quick Sand river was corrobarated by Sundery other indians who visited us in the Course of this day. \u00a0\u00a0 we were now convinced that if there information \u2329must\u232a be just; that Some Considerable river which flowed into the Columbia on it's South Side below us which we have not yet Seen, as the extensive vally on that Side of the river lying between the mountanious Country of the Coast, and the western mountains must be watered by Some Stream, which we had heretofore Supposed was the quick Sand river. \u00a0\u00a0 but if it be a fact that Quick Sand river heads in Mount Hood it must leave the vally within a fiew miles of it's enterance, and runs nearly parrilal with the Columbia River upwards. \u00a0\u00a0 we indeavered to assertained by what Stream the South portion of the Columbia Vally was watered, but could obtain no Satisfactory information of the waters on this head. \u00a0\u00a0 they inform us that the quick Sand river is not naviagable any distance in consequence of falls and rapids; and that no nation inhabit it. Sergt. Pryor returned in the evening and reported that he had assended the river Six Miles; that above the point which it divides itself into two Chanels, it is about 300 yards wide tho' the Chanel is not more than 50 yards, and only 6 feet deep. \u00a0\u00a0 the other part of the river from 2 to 4 inches water, the bead of this river is formed entirely of quick Sand; its banks are low and at present overflown. \u00a0\u00a0 the water is turbed and current rapid.\u2014 The following are the Courses taken by Sergt. Pryor.\u2014 \"S. 10\u00b0 W. 1 mile to a point on the Lard. Side passing a large island on Stard Side. S 24\u00b0 E. 2 m. to the head of the island near the Lard Shore. S 33\u00b0 E 4 m. to a Stard. point passing several islands on the Lard Side and a Creek 50 yards wide on the Stard. Side at 1 \u00bd miles. \u00a0\u00a0 the river from hence upwards bend to the East. \u00a0\u00a0 a fall of water heard at no great distance up this river.\" Several diffirent tribes of indians inform us that it heads at Mount Hood which is in view.

\n

\nWe were visited by Several Canoes of the nativs in the Course of this day; most of whome were decending the river with their womin and children. \u00a0\u00a0 they inform us that they reside at the great rapids and that their relations at that place were much Streightened for the want of food; that they had consumed their winter Store of dryed fish and those of the present Season had not yet arived. I could not lern whether they took Sturgion but prosume if they do it is in but Small quantities as they complain much of the Scercity of food among them, they informed us that the nativs above them were in the Same Situation, and that they did not expect the Salmon to arrive untill the full of the next moon which happens on the 2nd of May. \u00a0\u00a0 we did not doubt the veracity of those people who Seamed to be on their way with their families and effects in serch of Subsistence which they find it easy to precure in this fertile Vally\u2014. This information givs us much uneasiness with respect to our future means of Subsistence, above the falls, on through the Plains from thence to the Chopunnish there are no Deer Antilopes or Elk on which we could depend for Subsistence; their horses are very poor most probably at this Season, and if they have no fish their dogs must be in the Same Situation. \u00a0\u00a0 under these circumstances there Seams to be a gloomey prospect for Subsistence on any terms; we therefore took it into Serious Consideration what measure we were to pursue on this Occasion; it was at once deemed inexpedient to waite the arival of the Salmon as that would detain us So long a portion of the Season that it is probable we Should not reach the U States before the ice would close the Missouri; or at all events would hazard our horses which we left in charge of the Chopunnish who informed us that they intended passing the Rocky Mountains to the Missouri as early as the Season would permit them which is about the first of May. Should these people leave their Situation near Kooskooske before our arival we may probably find much dificulty in recovering our horses; without which there will be but little possibility of repassing the Mountains; we are therefore determined to lose as little as possible to getting to the Cho punnish Village.

\n

\nat 3 P. M. the hunters who were Sent over the river returned, having Killed 4 Elk and 2 Deer; the Elk were in good order but the deer extreemly poor. \u00a0\u00a0 they informed us that game is very plenty in that quarter. \u00a0\u00a0 the hunters on this Side of the river also returned but had killed nothing; they Saw a fiew Elk and Deer. \u00a0\u00a0 there were also much Sign of the black bear Seen on the other Side of the river. \u00a0\u00a0 we Sent a party to bring in the flesh of the Elk and Deer that were killed. \u00a0\u00a0 they did not return this evening. We purchased a Canoe from an Indian today for Six fathoms of white wampom; he Seemed Satisfied with his bargin and departed in another Canoe but Shortly after returned and canseled the bargain, took his canoe and returned the beeds. \u00a0\u00a0 this is frequently the case in their method of tradeing and is deemed fair by them. The last evening and this morning were So cloudy that we could neither obtain any Lunar observation nor equal altitudes.\u2014.\n

[Ordway]

\nTuesday 1st of April 1806. Sergt. Pryor & three men was Sent 5 or 6 miles up Quick Sand River to make discovries & Several hunters went up the Seal [10]\u2014River a hunting & others went out in different directions a hunting. \u00a0\u00a0 a number of the natives visited us as they were passing down the River \u00a0\u00a0 late in the afternoon Sergt. Pryor returned \u00a0\u00a0 had been about 4 miles up quick Sand River found the current rapid & only about 4 feet deep. \u00a0\u00a0 he killed one\ndeer. \u00a0\u00a0 the other hunter returned \u00a0\u00a0 had killed 4 Elk and 2 deer and an otter \u00a0\u00a0 the hunters tells us that the country back from the River is rich land \u00a0\u00a0 Some praries and rich plains &C. \u00a0\u00a0 a number of Savages passing down the River in their canoes. \u00a0\u00a0 we discovred yesterday the top of a high white Mountain Some distance to the Southward \u00a0\u00a0 our officers name it Mount Jefferson. [11] \u00a0\u00a0 2 canoe loads of Savages Camped near us.\n\n\n

[Gass]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Tuesday 1st April, 1806. \u00a0\u00a0 We had a cloudy morning; and we agreed to stay \nhere all day, for the purpose of hunting. So 9 hunters [12] set out early; 3 of whom [13] went up Quicksand river, and killed a deer; the other six killed 4 elk and a deer. In the evening nine of us went to bring in the meat of the elk; but it being late we were obliged to encamp out all night.\n\n\n

[Whitehouse]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nTuesday April 1st \u00a0\u00a0 This morning our Officers sent Serjeant Pryor & 2 of our party in a Canoe in order to go 5 or Six Miles up \u2329Quick Sand or\u232a Quicksand or Sandy River, (the River we passed last Evening) & Several of our Men were sent out a hunting. \u00a0\u00a0 A number of the Natives visited us in their Canoes, as they were passing down the River. \u00a0\u00a0 these Canoes were loaded with fish Roots &ca\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 I went up Quick Sand River about 4 Miles. \u00a0\u00a0 The backwater from the Columbia River went only a quarter of a mile up that River, & then it is a continual rapid as far as I went, & full of Islands, and Sands barrs. \u00a0\u00a0 Serjeant Pryor killed on his route 1 Deer which he & his party brought to our Camp. \u00a0\u00a0 Our hunters also returned, & had killed 4 Elk 2 Deer & an Otter. \u00a0\u00a0 On my route up Quicksand River I saw a Creek [14] which lay on the East side of that River which was about 50 yards wide. \u00a0\u00a0 The Quick Sand River is 350 Yards wide & only 50 Yards of Water the remainder being entirely a Quick Sand.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 I found this river part of the way up it, 6 feet deep, & the remainder as far up it as I went, only 6 inches deep of water & 4 inches quick sand. \u00a0\u00a0 we saw a high mountain laying a great distance off to the Southward of us, which appeared to be covered with snow. \u00a0\u00a0 Our Officers named this Mountain Jefferson Mountain. \u00a0\u00a0 We had a number of Indians encamped near us for the Night. \u00a0\u00a0 they came in 2 Canoes\n\n\n
