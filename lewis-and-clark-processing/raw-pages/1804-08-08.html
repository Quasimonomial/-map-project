August 8th 1804 [8]\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nwe had seen but a few aquatic fouls of any kind on the river since we commenced our journey up the Missouri, a few geese accompanied by their young, the wood duck which is common to every part of this country & crains of several kinds which will be discribed in their respective places\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 this day after we had passed the river\n Souix as called by Mr. MacKay (or as is more properly called the stone river,[)] I saw a great number of feathers floating down the river \u00a0\u00a0 those feathers had a very extraordinary appearance as they appeared in such quantities as to cover pretty generally sixty or seventy yards of the breadth of the river. \u00a0\u00a0 for three miles after I saw those feathers continuing to run in that manner, we did not percieve from whence they came, at length we were surprised by the appearance of a flock of Pillican [EC: Pelecanus erythrorhynchus] at rest on a large sand bar attatched to a small Island the number of which would if estimated appear almost in credible; they apeared to cover several acres of ground, and were no doubt engaged in procuring their ordinary food; which is fish, on our approach they flew and left behind them several small fish of about eight inches in length, none of which I had seen before\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 the Pellican rested again on a sand bar above the Island which we called after them from the number we saw on it. \u00a0\u00a0 we now approached them within about three hundred yards before they \u2329attemted to fly\u232a flew; I then fired at random among the flock with my rifle and brought one down; the discription of this bird is as follows.\u2014

\n
\n Habits\n
\n

\nThey are a bird of clime \u00a0\u00a0 remain on the coast of Floriday and the borders of the Gulph of mexico & even the lower portion of the Mississippi during the winter and in the Spring (see for date my thermometrical observations at the river Dubois.\u2014) \u00a0\u00a0 [\n April 6, 1804\n ] \u00a0\u00a0 visit this country and that fa[r]ther north for the purpose of raising their young\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 this duty seems now to have been accomplished from the appearance of a young Pilacon which was killed by one of our men [Dame] this morning, and they are now in large flocks on their return to their winter quarters. \u00a0\u00a0 they lay usually two eggs only and chuise for a nest a couple of logs of drift wood near the water's edge and with out any other preperation but the thraught [throat] formed by the proximity of those two logs which form a trough \u00a0\u00a0 they set and hatch their young which after nurture with fish their common food

\n Discription of Colour &c\n
\n

\nThe beak is a whiteish yellow the under part connected to a bladder like pouch, this pounch is connected to both sides of the lower beak and extends down on the under side of the neck and terminates in the stomach\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 this pouch is uncovered with feathers, and is formed [of] two skins the one on the inner and the other on the center side \u00a0\u00a0 a small quantity of flesh and strings of which the anamal has at pleasure the power of moving or drawing in such manner as to contract it at pleasure. \u00a0\u00a0 in the present subject I measured this pouch and found it's contents 5 gallons of water\u2014

\n

\nThe feet are webbed large and of a yellow colour, it has four toes \u00a0\u00a0 the hinder toe is longer than in most aquatic fouls, the nails are black, not sharp and \u00bd an inch in length

\n

\nThe plumage generally is white, the feathers are thin compared with the swan goose or most aquatick fouls and has but little or no down on the body. \u00a0\u00a0 the upper part of the head is covered with black f[e]athe[r]s short, as far as the back part of the head\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 the yellow skin unfeathered extends back from the upper beak and opening of the mouth and comes to a point just behind the eye

\n

\nThe large f[e]athers of the wings are of a deep black colour\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 the 1st & 2nd joint of [the wings] from the body above the same is covered with a second layer of white feathers which extend quite half the length of those large feathers of the wing\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 the thye is covered with feathers within a quarter of an inch of the knee.

3. The Little Sioux River reaches the Missouri in northwest Harrison County, Iowa, near the present town of Little Sioux. Nicollet notes that the Sioux name implies a rock somewhere along its length. Nicollet (RIIM), 27; Atlas map 15; Warren map 6; MRC map 25; MRR map 70. (back)

4. Douglas R. Parks gives the Sioux word as I\u014bya\u014b ya\u014bke watpa, or \"Rock River.\" (back)

5. \n Spirit Lake in Dickinson County, Iowa, a source of one of the main branches of the Little Sioux River. The Little Sioux River heads in Jackson County, Minnesota. Petersen, 252\u201355, passim. (back)

6. Evidently Clark was carrying his small rifle, which seems to have been a long-barreled \"Kentucky\" of relatively small caliber, of the sort often called a squirrel gun. Killing a large mammal like an elk with such a weapon would call for great accuracy. Russell (GEF), 38. (back)

7. Some words here in red ink, apparently written and crossed out by Biddle, part of which are: \"North side\" and \"Ordway.\" (back)

8. \n Lewis's natural history notes from Codex Q. The bird, as Coues notes in his interlineation, is the American white pelican, first noticed in weather remarks for April 6, 1804. The \"green insect\" is the common katydid, which seemed familiar to Lewis. It could be any of a number of varieties. The \"prarie hen\" is probably the greater prairie chicken (see November 16, 1803). The notes on the katydid and greater prairie chicken may have been added later. (back)
\n
9. \n Lewis's observation from Codex O. (back)
\n
10. \n John Dame; apparently his only mention in the journals comes on this day, in Clark's and in Ordway's journals. (back)
\n
11. American white pelican, Pelecanus erythrorhynchos. (back)
\n
12. \n Little Sioux River, Harrison County, Iowa. (back)
\n
13. \n John Collins. (back)
\n
14. \"North\" is written over \"South.\" (back)
