\n July 21, 1805\n

\n
\n \n

[Lewis]

\n \n
\n Sunday July 21st 1805.\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nSet out early this morning and passed a bad rappid [1] where the river enters the mountain about 1 m. from our camp of last evening the Clifts high and covered with fragments of broken rocks. \u00a0\u00a0 the current strong; we employed the toe rope principally, and also the pole as the river is not now so deep but reather wider and much more rapid \u00a0\u00a0 our progress was therefore slow and laborious. \u00a0\u00a0 we saw three swans this morning, which like the geese have not yet recovered the feathers of the wing and could not fly [2] \u00a0\u00a0 we killed two of them \u00a0\u00a0 the third escaped by diving and passed down with the current; they had no young ones with them therefore presume they do not breed in this country \u00a0\u00a0 these are the first we have seen on the river for a great distance. \u00a0\u00a0 we daily see great numbers of gees with their young which are perfectly feathered \u2329but\u232a except the wings which are deficient in both young and old. \u00a0\u00a0 my dog caught several today, as he frequently dose. \u00a0\u00a0 the young ones are very fine, but the old gees are poor and unfit for uce. \u00a0\u00a0 saw several of the large brown or sandhill Crain today with their young. [3] \u00a0\u00a0 the young Crain is as large as a turkey and cannot fly they are of a bright red bey colour or that of the common deer at this season. \u00a0\u00a0 this bird feeds on grass prinsipally and is found in the river bottoms. \u00a0\u00a0 the grass near the river is lofty and green that of the hill sides and high open grounds is perfectly dry and appears to be scorched by the heat of the sun. \u00a0\u00a0 the country was rough mountainous & much as that of yesterday untill towards evening when the river entered a beautifull and extensive plain country of about 10 or 12 miles wide which extended upwards further that the eye could reach \u00a0\u00a0 this valley is bounded by two nearly parallel ranges of high mountains which have their summits partially covered with snow. \u00a0\u00a0 below the snowey region pine succeeds and reaches down their sides in some parts to the plain but much the greater portion of their surfaces is uncovered with timber and expose either a barren sterile soil covered with dry parched grass or black and rugged rocks. \u00a0\u00a0 the river immediately on entering this valley assumes a different aspect and character, it spreads to a mile and upwards in width crouded with Islands, some of them large, is shallow enough for the use of the seting pole in almost every part and still more rappid than before; it's bottom is smooth stones and some large rocks as it has been since we have entered the mountains. \u00a0\u00a0 the grass in these extensive bottoms is green and fine, about 18 inches or 2 feet high. \u00a0\u00a0 the land is a black rich loam and appears very fertile. \u00a0\u00a0 we encamped in this beatiful valley on the Lard. side [4] \u00a0\u00a0 the party complain of being much fatiegued with this days travel. we killed one deer today.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 This morning we passed a bold creek 28 yds. wide which falls in on Stard. side. \u00a0\u00a0 it has a handsome and an extensive valley. \u00a0\u00a0 this we called Pryor's Creek after Sergt. \u2329John\u232a Pryor one of our party. [5] I also saw two fesants today of a dark brown colour much larger than the phesant of the U' States. [6]\n

\n

\nthis morning Capt. Clark having determined to hunt and wait my arrival somewhere about his preset station was fearfull that some indians might still be on the river above him sufficiently near to hear the report of his guns and therefore proceeded up the river about three miles and [not] finding any indians nor discovering any fresh appearance of them returned about four miles below and fixed his camp near the river; [7] after refreshing themselves with a few hours rest they set out in different directions to hunt. Capt C. killed a buck and Fields a buck and doe. \u00a0\u00a0 he caught a young curlooe which was nearly feathered. \u00a0\u00a0 the musquetoes were equally as troublesome to them as to ourselves this evening; tho' some hours after dark the air becomes so cold that these insects disappear. \u00a0\u00a0 the men are all fortunately supplyed with musquetoe biers [NB: made of duck of gauze, like a trunk\u2014to get under] otherwise it would be impossible for them to exist under the fatiegues which they daily encounter without their natural rest which they could not obtain for those tormenting insects if divested of their biers. \u00a0\u00a0 timber still extreemly scant on the river but there is more in this valley than we have seen since we entered the mountains; the creeks which fall into the river are better supplyed with this article than the river itself.\u2014

[Clark]

\n \n
\n July 21st Sunday 1805\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\na fine morning \u00a0\u00a0 our feet So brused and Cut that I deturmined to delay for the Canoes, & if possible kill Some meat by the time they arrived, all the Creeks which fall into the Missouri on the Std. Side Since entering the Mountains have extencive Valies of open Plain. \u00a0\u00a0 the river bottoms Contain nothing larger than a Srub untill above the last Creek \u00a0\u00a0 the Creeks & runs have timber on them generally, the hills or mountains are in Some places thickly covered with pine & Cedar &c. &c. I proceeded on about 3 miles this morning \u00a0\u00a0 finding no fresh Indian Sign returned down the river four miles and Camped, turned out to hunt for Some meat, which if we are Suckessfull will be a Seasonable Supply for the partey assending. emence quantities of Sarvice buries, yellow, red, Purple & black Currents ripe and Superior to any I ever tasted particularly the yellow & purple kind. Choke Cheries are Plenty; Some Goose buries\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 The wild rose Continue \u00a0\u00a0 the Willow more abundant no Cotton wood of the Common kind \u00a0\u00a0 Small birds are plenty, Some Deer, Elk, Goats, and Ibex; no buffalow in the Mountains.

\n

\nThose mountains are high and a great perportion of them rocky; Vallies fertile \u00a0\u00a0 I observed on the highest pinicals of Some of the mountains to the West Snow lying in Spots Some Still further North are covered with Snow and cant be Seen from this point

\n

\nThe Winds in those mountains are not Settled generally with the river, to day the wind blow hard from the West at the Camp. The Missouri Continus its width the Current Strong and Crouded with little Islands and Cose graveley bars; but little fine Sand \u00a0\u00a0 the Chanel generally a Corse gravel or Soft mud. Musquetors & Knats verry troublesom. I killed a Buck, and J. Fields killed a Buck and Doe this evening. Cought a young Curlough [curlew].\n\n

[Ordway]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n July 21st Sunday 1805. \u00a0\u00a0 a clear morning. \u00a0\u00a0 we Set out as usal and proceeded on. Saw a nomber of large Swans \u00a0\u00a0 Some of the men killed two of them. \u00a0\u00a0 passed a Small creek on the Lard. Side and one on the Starbord Side. [9] \u00a0\u00a0 the grass in the valley and on the hills look dry and pearched up. \u00a0\u00a0 passed a hill & clifts of rocks in the afternoon \u00a0\u00a0 the River divides in many channels and full of Islands and Spreads about a mile wide. \u00a0\u00a0 the current Swift \u00a0\u00a0 our hunter on Shore killed a deer. \u00a0\u00a0 we Came 15\u00bd miles this day and Camped on a Smooth plain on the Larbord Side. \u00a0\u00a0 the party in general are much fatigued.\u2014\n\n\n

\n \n

[Gass]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Sunday 21st. \u00a0\u00a0 We set out at sunrise and had a pleasant morning; passed some middling high hills on the river, and rocks of a red purple colour; also two small creeks one on each side. [10] There are a few pines on the hills. At noon our course began to change more to the southwest again; the wind blew very hard and some drops of rain fell. In the afternoon we passed through a ridge, [11] where the river is very narrow; and close above a large cluster of small islands, where we had some difficulty to get along, the water being so much separated. We went 15 miles and an half and encamped on the south side, on a beautiful prairie bottom. One of our hunters killed a fine deer.\n\n\n

[Whitehouse]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Sunday 21st July 1805. \u00a0\u00a0 a clear morning. \u00a0\u00a0 we Set out as usal and proceeded on. \u00a0\u00a0 Saw a nomber of large Swans on the River. \u00a0\u00a0 Some of the hunters killed 2 of them. \u00a0\u00a0 considerable of pine Spruce and bollsom fer trees along the Shore. \u00a0\u00a0 we passed a Small Creek on the S. S. and one on the N. S. \u00a0\u00a0 the grass in the valley & on the hills look dry & pearchd. up. \u00a0\u00a0 the wind high from the N. W. \u00a0\u00a0 in the afternoon we passed through a hill & clifts of rocks on each Side. \u00a0\u00a0 the River divides in different channels & Spreads about a mile wide. \u00a0\u00a0 the Islands verry pleanty. \u00a0\u00a0 Saw beaver Sign on them. \u00a0\u00a0 this valley is Smoth in places. \u00a0\u00a0 Some timber along the Shores \u00a0\u00a0 our hunters on Shore killed a deer. \u00a0\u00a0 we Came 15\u00bd miles through verry rapid water. \u00a0\u00a0 the men party much fatigued. \u00a0\u00a0 Camped on a Smooth plain on the South \nSide.\u2014

\n

\nSunday July 21st \u00a0\u00a0 We had a clear morning, and set out early on our Voyage, and proceeded on; we saw a number of large Swans in the River, some of our hunters killed two of them, we saw pine, Spruce, and Balsam fir Trees growing along the Shores on both sides of the River, We passed two Creeks lying on each side of the river, the grass in the Valleys, and on the hills appearing to be dry & parched,\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 The wind was high from the Northwest.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 In the afternoon we passed \u2329through\u232a a hill, and clifts of rocks lying on each side of the River, We found the River divided here into different channels, & spreading to the width of \u2329a\u232a about a Mile. \u00a0\u00a0 We saw a number of small Islands as we passed along, & some signs of beaver on them, We passed a Valley, which was plain & Smooth, and timber growing along the Shores, on both sides of the River. \u00a0\u00a0 One of our hunters killed a deer, which he brought to us. \u00a0\u00a0 We came 15\u00bd Miles this day, the stream running rapid against us, which fataigued our party much and we encamped on a smooth plain, lying on the South side of the Mesouri River\u2014\n\n\n

1. Probably later Flume Ripple. Atlas map 62; MRC map 80. (back)
\n
2. These swans and geese were familiar to Lewis and Clark and are respectively, the trumpeter swan and the Canada goose. Holmgren, 33; Burroughs, 193\u201395, 199. It was probably Biddle who drew a red vertical line through the passages about the swans and geese and the sandhill crane. (back)
\n
3. The sandhill crane, Grus canadensis [AOU, 206], already known to science. Burroughts, 185\u201386. Holmgren considers the \"brown\" as an immature or smaller subspecies of the sandhill crane. Holmgren, 29. (back)
\n
4. In Lewis and Clark County, Montana, a few miles east of Helena, about five miles above present Canyon Ferry Dam, near the Lewis and Clark\u2013Broadwater County line. Canyon Ferry Lake probably covers the site today. Atlas map 63; MRC map 81. (back)
\n
5. \n Pryor's first name was Nathaniel; to Lewis he was probably just \"Sergeant Pryor,\" although he used Nathaniel in a deleted portion of the next day's entry. \"John\" was crossed out in red ink in this entry, probably by Biddle, but \"Nathaniel\" was not added, and it is \"John\" in Biddle's\n History. The stream is Spokane Creek, labeled \"Potts Vally Creek\" on Atlas map 62. See notes for July 20, 1805. MRC map 81. (back)
\n
6. Perhaps the blue grouse, Dendragapus obscurus [AOU, 297], then unknown to science. Lewis gives more detail about what may be the same bird on August 1, 1805. Burroughs, 215\u201316; Holmgren, 30, 32. Biddle corrected Lewis's spelling by overwriting \"fesants\" to make it \"Phesant.\" (back)
\n
7. Apparently Clark returned to a point about a mile below his previous night's camp, in the vicinity of the mouth of later Beaver Creek, Broadwater County, incorrectly given as \"Pryors Vally R or C\" on Atlas map 63. MRC map 81. (back)
\n
8. The wounded fish may be any of Salmonidae sp., whitefish or cisco. Lee et al., 76\u2013120. It was probably Biddle who drew a red vertical line through this paragraph. (back)
\n
9. \n Trout Creek (not mentioned by Lewis and Clark) and Spokane Creek, respectively, Lewis and Clark County, Montana. This is a very confused day in the captains' journals and on the maps. See their entries of this day. (back)
\n
10. \n Lewis notices one of these, Spokane Creek, Lewis and Clark County, Montana, which he called Pryor's Creek, after Sergeant Nathaniel Pryor of the party. There are various creeks on the other side of the Missouri, of which the first in the day's course, and the closest to Spokane Creek, is Trout Creek. (back)
\n
11. They passed the Spokane Hills into the valley, in Lewis and Clark and Broadwater counties, now largely covered by Canyon Ferry Reservoir. (back)
