\n August 5, 1804\n

\n
\n \n

[Clark]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nAugust 5th \u00a0\u00a0 Set out early \u00a0\u00a0 wind from N E. \u00a0\u00a0 Great appearance of Wind & rain, (I have remarked that I have not heard much thunder in this Countrey) \u00a0\u00a0 \u2329Lightning is Common as in other Countries\u232a \u00a0\u00a0 a verry large Snake was Killed to day called the Bull Snake, his Colour Some thing like a rattle Snake Something lighter\u2014 [1] \u00a0\u00a0 the bends of the river to day is washing away the banks, haveing nothing to oppose the turbelance of the [river] when Confined by large hard Sand Points, forceing this Current against the bends\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 the Soil of the entire bottom between the high land, being the mud or Ooze of the river of Some former period mixed with Sand & Clay easely melts and Slips, or washies into the river \u00a0\u00a0 the mud mixes with the water & the Sand collects on the points [2] \u00a0\u00a0 Camped on the S. S. [3] \u00a0\u00a0 I went on [4] Shore S. S. this evening \u00a0\u00a0 Saw Some turkeys and in persueing them Struk the river 12 miles below the place by water I went out, I think the Peninsuly is about 370 yards across Subjuct to overflow; & washes into numerous Channels, Great quantities of Graps ripe & of three Defferent Kind \u00a0\u00a0 Some large & fine. I Killed a Turkey, and made Camp in the Night, Musqutors verry troubleson\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 Reed the man who went back for his Knife has not yet joined us\n

5th of August Sunday 1804
\u00a0
\n \n

\nSet out early \u00a0\u00a0 great appearance of wind and rain \u00a0\u00a0 (I have observed that Thundor & lightning is not as common in this Countrey as it is in the atlantic States) \u00a0\u00a0 Snakes are not plenty, one was killed to day large and resembling the rattle Snake only Something lighter\u2014. \u00a0\u00a0 I walked on Shore this evening S. S. \u00a0\u00a0 in Pursueing Some Turkeys I [s]truck the river twelve miles below within 370 yards, the high water passes thro this Peninsulia; and agreeable to the Customary Changes of the river I Concld. [NB: Should Calculate] that in two years the main Current of the river will pass through. In every bend the banks are falling in from the Current being thrown against those bends by the Sand points which inlarges and the Soil I believe from unquestionable appearns. of the entire bottom from one hill to the other being the mud or ooze of the River at Some former Period mixed with Sand and Clay easily melts and Slips into the River, and the mud mixes with the water & the Sand is washed down and lodges on the points\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 Great quantities of Grapes on the banks, I observe three different Kinds at this time ripe, one Of the no. is large & has the flaver of the Purple grape. [6] \u00a0\u00a0 camped on the S. S. \u00a0\u00a0 the Musquitors verry troubleson. The man who went back after his Knife has not yet come up, we have Some reasons to believe he has Deserted

\nI have frequently observed an acquatic bird [EC: sterna antillarum] in the cours of asscending this river but have never been able to procure one before today, this day I was so fortunate as to kill two of them, they are here more plenty than on the river below. \u00a0\u00a0 they lay their eggs on the sand bars without shelter or nest, and produce their young from the 15th to the last of June, the young ones of which we caught several are covered with down of a yellowish white colour and on the back some small specks of a dark brown. \u00a0\u00a0 they bear a great resemblance to the young quale of ten days oald, and apear like them to be able to runabout and peck their food as soon as they are hatched\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 this bird, lives on small fish, worms and bugs which it takes on the virge of the water \u00a0\u00a0 it is seldom seen to light on trees an qu[i]te as seldom do they lite in the water and swim tho' the foot would indicate that they did it's being webbed \u00a0\u00a0 I believe them to be a native of this country and probly a constant resident.\u2014

[Ordway]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Sunday 5th 1804. \u00a0\u00a0 we Set out eairly \u00a0\u00a0 proceeded on till about 7 oClock. \u00a0\u00a0 a Shower came up from N. W. Some wind attending it. \u00a0\u00a0 which Detained us about 2 hours. \u00a0\u00a0 we then proceded on Round Sand bars. Delayed a Short time at 12 o.C. for Capt Lewis to take m. observation, proceded on along the Bank of a large prarie \u00a0\u00a0 one of the men kild a large spoted bull Snake under the bank \u00a0\u00a0 a nomber of birds [9] which live in the bank flying about this Snake \u00a0\u00a0 it is supposed the Snake charm them. [10] \u00a0\u00a0 a head wind.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 we proceded on round a long bend. \u00a0\u00a0 Capt. Clark went on Shore towards evening in the point of Cottonwood & Grape Vines \u00a0\u00a0 the Grape plenty \u00a0\u00a0 C. Clark was at the River below this point which is only 370 yards across, & 12 miles round. C. Clark killed a Turkey & came to the Boat at dark, where we Led \u00a0\u00a0 Roed abot 18 miles. Camped [11] on the N. S. of the Missouri below an Island.\u2014

[Gass]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Sunday 5th. \u00a0\u00a0 We set out early, but a storm of rain and wind obliged us to stop two hours. It then cleared and we continued our voyage; passed prairies on both sides, and encamped on the north side. The river here is very crooked and winding. To arrive at a point only 370 yards from this place, the passage by water is twelve miles.\n\n

1. The bullsnake is Pituophis melanoleucus sayi. See Lewis's description from Codex Q, below, on this date. Burroughs, 275\u201376; Benson, 89. (back)
\n
2. Records of wells drilled through the floodplain sediments of the Missouri River in this region generally show that thirty to more than fifty feet of \"blue\" or gray clay, post-Pleistocene alluvium, overlie the sand and gravel that fill the lower part of the valley. (back)
\n
3. In Harrison County, Iowa, near the Burt-Washington county line, Nebraska on the opposite shore. Shifts of the Missouri River and of the lower course of the Soldier River over the years have evidently been substantial. Atlas map 14; Warren map 6; MRC maps 24, 25; MRR maps 68, 69. (back)
\n
4. \n Clark continued the narrative from here following the courses and distances. It is rearranged for ease of reading. (back)
\n
5. This last course appears in Lewis's hand, as are also the words \"the same continued \u00bd miles\" in the previous line. (back)
\n
6. The three grape species are probably Vitis aestivalis Michx., summer grape, pigeon grape; V. riparia Michx., river-bank grape; and V. vulpina L., winter grape. The larger grape (V. riparia) tasted like the \"Purple grape,\" which may be the cultivated concord grape, V. labrusca L. Barkley, 219\u201320; Fernald, 997\u201398. (back)
\n
7. \n Lewis's natural history notes from Codex Q. See n. 1 this date for the snake. The \"acquatic bird\" is the least tern, Sterna antillarum [AOU, 74]. The bird used for comparison is the killdeer, Charadrius vociferus [AOU, 273]. Burroughs, 231\u201332. (back)
\n
8. \n Lewis's observation from Codex O. (back)
\n
9. Probably the bank swallow, Riparia riparia. (back)
\n
10. \n Ordway is the only journal keeper to relate the folk belief that snakes could somehow charm birds. Clark recorded other snake stories on June 14 and 24, 1804. (back)
\n
11. In Harrison County, Iowa, across the river and not far from the Burt-Washington county\nline in Nebraska. (back)
\n
12. \n Moses B. Reed had left on August 4, but this was no doubt the day they realized he was not coming back. (back)
