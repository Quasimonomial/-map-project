\n December 17, 1805\n

\n
\n \n

[Clark]

\n \n
\n December 17th Tuesday 1805 [1]\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nrained Some last night and this morning, all hands at work about the huts Chinking them, The 7 men left to bring in the Elk left in the woods Come with 2 \u00a0\u00a0 the 3rd they Could not find, as it was that left by the party that got lost night before last

\n

\nThe after part of the Day fair & Cool, fore part of the Day rain hailed & blew hard, The mountain [2] which lies S. E of this is covered with Snow to day \u00a0\u00a0 we fleece [3] all the meat and hang it up over a Small Smoke \u00a0\u00a0 The trees are hard to Split for Punchens to Cover our houses &c. [4]\n

\n

\n

\nElkskin-bound Journal\n

\nElkskin-bound Journal\n

\n \n
\n
\n \n

[Clark]

\n \n
\n Tuesday 17th of December 1805\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nSome rain last night and a continuation of it this morning. \u00a0\u00a0 all the men at work about the houses, Some Chiking, Dobbing Cutting out dores &c. &c. The 7 men left to bring in the Elk arrived and informed that they Could not find the meat that the party who Stayed out all night had left\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 the forepart of this day rained hailed and blew hard, the after part is fair and Cool\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 a Mountain which is S. [blank]\u00b0 E. about 10 mile distant has got Snow on its top which is ruged and uneavin

\n

\nCause a Small fire & Smoke to be made under the meat which is hung up in Small peaces: \u00a0\u00a0 The trees which our men have fallen latterly Split verry badly into boards. \u00a0\u00a0 The most of our Stores are wet. \u00a0\u00a0 our Leather Lodge has become So rotten that the Smallest thing tares it into holes and it is now Scrcely Sufficent to keep off the rain off a Spot Sufficiently large for our bead.\n

\n \n
\n
\n \n

[Ordway]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nTuesday 17th Decr. 1805. \u00a0\u00a0 a little Snow and hail fell last night and continues this morning. \u00a0\u00a0 we went at chinking up our huts and Splitting plank &C. \u00a0\u00a0 cut our meat & hung it in the meat house &C\n\n

\n \n
\n
\n \n

[Gass]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Tuesday 17th. \u00a0\u00a0 This was another cloudy day, with some light showers of rain and hail. About 11 o'clock the 7 men came with the canoe and the remainder of the meat. We still continued working at our huts.\n\n

\n \n
\n
\n \n

[Whitehouse]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nTuesday Decemr. 17th \u00a0\u00a0 We had during last night some Snow & hail, & it continued the same this morning. \u00a0\u00a0 We were all employed in building Chimneys in our huts, & splitting out planks to cover our huts with. \u00a0\u00a0 We cut up the Meat, & hung it up in the Meat house, in Order to save it. \u00a0\u00a0 A little snow remained on the pine trees the whole of this day. \u00a0\u00a0 The Man that was sick (George Gibson) got much better.\u2014\n\n\n

\n \n
\n \n \n
\n
1. Under this entry in the Elkskin-bound Journal is a sketch map which appears to show the area between Fort Clatsop and the Pacific Coast, in Clatsop County, Oregon. Opposite this entry is a sketch map of the area about the site of the expedition's salt works. See December 28, 1805, and Atlas map 84. (back)
\n
2. Perhaps Saddle Mountain, Clatsop County. Coues (HLC), 2:735. (back)
\n
3. Criswell, 39, derives this verb from the definition of \"fleece\" as the meat taken from the sides of the hump of the bison. As the entry in Codex I indicates below, Clark is clearly referring to cutting the meat into small pieces for smoking. (back)
\n
4. Perhaps in disagreement with his own comment of December 13, and that of Gass on December 14, who said that the timber \"splits freely and makes the finest puncheons I have ever seen.\" (back)
