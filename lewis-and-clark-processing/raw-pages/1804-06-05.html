\n June 5, 1804\n

\n
\n \n

[Clark]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nJune 5th Tuesday, Jurked the Vennison Killed yesterday, [1] after Seting over the Scouting Party or hunder of 3 men Set out at 6 oClock \u00a0\u00a0 Course N 57\u00b0 W to a pt. on S. Sd. 5 ms. \u00a0\u00a0 passed a Creek on L. Sd. I call Lead C\n [2] of 15 yds \u00a0\u00a0 passed one on the S. Called Lit: good-womans Creek\n [3] about 20 yds. wide \u00a0\u00a0 Passed a Willow Isd. \u00a0\u00a0 a Butifull Prarie approaching near the river above Lead C & extends to the Mine river\n [4] in a westerly Derection, passed the Mouth of the Creek of the Big Rock\n [5] 15 yds Wide at 4 ms. on the Lbd Sd. \u00a0\u00a0 at 11 oClock brought a Caissie [cajeu] in which was 2 men, from 80 League up the Kansias River, where they wintered and caught a great qty of Beever but unfortunattey lost it by the burning of the plains, the Kansas Nation [6] hunted on the Missourie last Winter and are now persueing the Buffalow in the Plains, passed a Projecting Rock called the \n Manitou\n a Painting [7] \u00a0\u00a0 from this Deavel to the Pt. on the Lbd Side N 23\u00b0 W 7\u00bd Ms. The Same course 2 \u00bd ms. Creek Cld. Manitou\n [8] \u00a0\u00a0 passed a \u2329large Isd.\u232a on the Lbd. Side about 40 yd. wide, a Sand bar in the middle of the River \u00a0\u00a0 passed up between the Sand & L. Shore one Mile to a Small Creek 10 yd. wide, (I call Sand C). [9] We run on the Sand and was obliged to return to the Starbd Side, I am verry unwell with a Slight feever from a bad cold caught three days ago at the \n Grand' so' [Osage?] R\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 passed a Small Willow Isd. on S. Side, a large one in the Middle of the river, [10]\n York Swam to the Isd. to pick greens, and Swam back with his greens, the Boat Drew too much water to cross the quick Sands which intervened, She draws 4 foot water, a fair wind \u00a0\u00a0 our mast being broke by accidence provented our takeing the advantage of it \u00a0\u00a0 passed the lower point of a large Island, opposit the Current devides between \u2329three\u232a 4 Small Isds on the St Side. \u00a0\u00a0 we found the water excessively hard for 12 Miles as we were oblged to pass up the center of the Current between two of the Isds. & round the heads of the other 2 \u00a0\u00a0 the Current Setting imediately against the points which was choked up with Drift for a mile\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 Above those Isd. on the St. Side we camped altogeth[er] [11] \u00a0\u00a0 our Hunter or Spis [spies] [12] discovered the sign of a war party of abt. 10 Men [13]\n

\n

\n

\n
\n Manitou on a Rock, June 5, 1804, Field Notes, document 18
\n Beinecke Library, Yale University\n

[Clark]

\n \n
\n June 5th Tuesday 1804
\u00a0
\n \n

\nafter Jurking the meet Killed yesterday and Crossing the hunting party we Set out at 6 oClock, from the last Course & distance, N 51\u00b0 W. 5 ms. to a pt. on the St: Sd. \u00a0\u00a0 passed a Small Creek on the Ld. S: I call \n Lead C. \u00a0\u00a0 passed a Creek on the S. S. of 20 yds. wide Cald. Lit: [NB: Little]\n Good Womans C. \u00a0\u00a0 on the L. S. a Prarie extends from Lead C. parrelel with the river to Mine river, at 4 ms. Passed the Creek of the big rock about 15 yds. wide on the L. Sd. \u00a0\u00a0 at 11 oClock brought too a Small Caissee [NB: raft made of two canoes tied together] in which was two french men, from 80 Leagues up the Kansias [NB: Kanzas] R. where they wintered, and Cought a great quantity of Beaver, the greater part of which they lost by fire from the Praries, those men inform that the Kansas Nation are now out in the plains hunting Buffalow, they hunted last winter on this river \u00a0\u00a0 Passed a projecting rock on which was painted a figue [14] and a Creek at 2 ms. above Called Little Manitou Creek from the Painted rock \u00a0\u00a0 this Creek 20 yds. wide on the L. Sd. \u00a0\u00a0 passed a Small Creek on L. S. opposit a Verry bad Sand bar of Several ms. in extent, which we named \n Sand C \u00a0\u00a0 here my Servent York Swam to the Sand bar to geather greens for our Dinner and returnd with a Sufficent quantity wild Creases [NB: Cresses] or Teng [NB: Tongue] grass, [15] we passed up for 2 ms on the L. S. of this Sand and was obliged to return, the Watr. uncertain the quick Sand Moveing \u00a0\u00a0 we had a fine wind, but could not make use of it, our Mast being broke, we passed between 2 Small Islands in the Middle of the Current, & round the head of three \u00a0\u00a0 a rapid Current for one mile and Camped on the S. S. opsd. a large Island in the middle of the river; one Perogue did not get up for two hours, our Scout discovd. the fresh sign of about 10 Inds. I expect that those Indians are on their way to war against the Osages nation probably they are the Saukees\n

\n

[Ordway]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nTuesday June 5th 1804. \u00a0\u00a0 a fair \u2329day\u232a \u00a0\u00a0 passed Lead Creek on South Side of the River & Little Good woman Creek on the N. Side, came 9 miles. \u00a0\u00a0 passed the Creek of the Big Rock 15 yds. wide, at a 11 oClock we met 2 Frenchmen in 2 canoes locked together Loaded with Peltry &C. \u00a0\u00a0 they came from 80 Leagues up the Kansias River, [16] where they wintered, then we passed a high Clifts of Rocks on which was painted the pickture of the Devil on South Side of the River. \u00a0\u00a0 we Encamped on the N. Side of the River at the upper point of an Island. \u00a0\u00a0 the land is Rich well watered &.C. \u00a0\u00a0 the hunters killed 2 Deer this day\u2014

\n \n

[Floyd]

\n \n
June 5th Tuesday 1804
\u00a0
\n \n

\nTusday June 5th \u00a0\u00a0 fair day \u00a0\u00a0 pased Lead Creek on South Side of the River\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 Littel Good woman Creek on the N. Side \u00a0\u00a0 Came 9 miles \u00a0\u00a0 past the Creek of the Big Rock 15 yads wide \u00a0\u00a0 at a 11 \u2329mil\u232a oClock we met 2 French in 2 conoes lashed together Loaded with peltry &c. \u00a0\u00a0 they Came from 80 Leages up the Kensier River whare they wintered \u00a0\u00a0 water Strong \u00a0\u00a0 past Severall Isd. Came 15 miles \u00a0\u00a0 encamped on the N. Side at the uper pint of Isd. \u00a0\u00a0 the land is Good well timberd well waterd \u00a0\u00a0 ouer hunters Kild one Deer\n

\n \n

\n \n

[Gass]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Tuesday 5th. \u00a0\u00a0 We passed Mine creek\n [17] on the south side, and Little Goodwoman creek on the north: also the creek of the Big Rock. We met two Frenchmen in two canoes laden with peltry; passed a high cliff of rocks on the south side, and encamped on the north side. The land about this place is good and well timbered.\n\n\n

[Whitehouse]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nTuesday 5th June 1804. [18] \u00a0\u00a0 a fair morning. \u00a0\u00a0 we Set out eairly and proceeded on \u00a0\u00a0 passed a creek on the South Side and Camped on the Same Side.\u2014

\n

\nTuesday June 5th \u00a0\u00a0 We embarked early this Morning & proceeded on our Voyage, and passed a Creek lying on the South side of the River, the name unknown, in the evening we encamped on the same side of the River, our \ncourse being still West by South\n\n\n

1. Jerking was done by cutting meat into very thin strips and drying it in the sun. The finished product was commonly used as a travel ration by Indians, trappers, and frontiersmen in general. \"Jerky\" comes from the Spanish charqui, itself derived from a Quechua Indian word. Wentworth; Fletcher & La Flesche, 2:344\u201345. (back)
\n
2. Perhaps either Rock Creek or Mud Creek, in Cole County. MRC map 7. (back)
\n
3. \n Bonne Femme Creek, in Boone County, Missouri. Ibid. (back)
\n
4. \n Lamine River, in Cooper County, Missouri. See below, June 8, 1804. (back)
\n
5. Possibly either Rock Creek or Mud Creek, in Cole County, Missouri. MRC map 7. (back)
\n
6. The Kansa, or Kaw, people were a small tribe residing on the Kansas River when first known to Europeans in the late seventeenth century. They spoke a Siouan language of the Dhegiha group, closely related to Osage. Their economy was based on hunting and horticulture. In 1804 their village was on the Blue River near present Manhattan, Kansas. The tribe gave its name to both the state and the river. Hodge, 1:653\u201356; Swanton, 293\u201394; Wedel (KA), 50; Unrau. (back)
\n
7. See fig. 11. A more elaborate version (fig. 12) is in the Codex A entry for this day. (back)
\n
8. \n Moniteau Creek reaches the Missouri just east of the Moniteau-Cole county line, Missouri. \"Manitou\" is a French version of an Algonquian word designating a spirit, and here probably refers to the figure on the rock, as Clark indicates. Hodge, 1:800\u2013801; MRC map 7. (back)
\n
9. Perhaps Factory Creek, near the Moniteau-Cole county line. MRC map 7. (back)
\n
10. Perhaps the later Ville Monteau Island. Ibid. (back)
\n
11. In Boone County, in the neighborhood of the later town of Sandy Hook on the opposite shore. \"Altogether\" may mean that they kept the camp compact because of the report of the war party. MRC maps 7, 8. (back)
\n
12. \"Spy\" on the frontier was virtually synonymous with \"scout.\" (back)
\n
13. Under the latter part of this entry on this sheet of the Field Notes (reverse of document 18) is a sketch map (fig. 14) of the Missouri River from about the mouth of the Osage River to some distance above the mouth of the Grand River. See Wood. (back)
\n
14. See fig. 12. (back)
\n
15. Probably one of the native species of Rorippa, yellow, or marsh, cress. The habitat description fits that of either Rorippa sinuata (Nutt.) Hitchc., spreading yellow cress; R. sessiliflora (Nutt.) Hitchc., sessile-flowered cress; or R. palustris (L.) Bess., bog yellow cress. Probably the reference is to the last, the most common species in Missouri. Steyermark, 758\u201360; Barkley, 123. (back)
\n
16. \n Kansas River. (back)
\n
17. \"Lead Creek\" to Charles Floyd, Whitehouse, and Ordway; present Rock Creek or Mud\nCreek, Cole County, Missouri. (back)
