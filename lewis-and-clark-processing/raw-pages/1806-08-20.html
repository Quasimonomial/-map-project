\n August 20, 1806\n

\n
\n \n

[Clark]

\n \n
\n Wednesday 20th of August 1806\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\na violent hard rain about day light this morning. \u00a0\u00a0 all wet except myself and the indians. \u00a0\u00a0 we embarked a little after Sun rise \u00a0\u00a0 wind moderate and ahead. \u00a0\u00a0 we proceeded on at meridn. \u00a0\u00a0 passed the enterance of Cannonball river\n [1] \u00a0\u00a0 imediately above is the remains of a large Sieoux encampment which appears to have been made this Spring. \u00a0\u00a0 at 3 P M passed the enterance of Wardepon River\n [2] [NB: Scioux boundary to which they claim the country] \u00a0\u00a0 Saw great number of wolves on the bank Some Buffalow & Elk, tho' not so abundant as near the River Rochejhone. passed the place where we left the last encampment of Ricaras in the fall 1804 and encamped on a Sandbar from the N. E. Side, [3] having made 81 miles only, the wind blew hard all day which caused the waves to rise high and flack over into the Small Canoes in Such a manner as to employ one hand in throwing the water out. The plains begin to Change their appearance \u00a0\u00a0 the grass is turning of a yellow colour. I observe a great alteration in the Corrent course and appearance of this pt. of the Missouri. \u00a0\u00a0 in places where there was Sand bars in the fall 1804 at this time the main Current passes, and where the current then passed is now a Sand bar\u2014 Sand bars which were then naked are now covered with willow Several feet high. \u00a0\u00a0 the enteranc of Some of the Rivers & Creeks Changed owing to the mud thrown into them, and a layor of mud over Some of the bottoms of 8 inches thick.\n

\n \n
\n
\n \n

[Ordway]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nWednesday 20th August 1806. \u00a0\u00a0 we Set out eairly and proceeded on verry well \u00a0\u00a0 the after part of the day pleasant. \u00a0\u00a0 in the evening we Camped on a Sand beach \u00a0\u00a0 the Musquetoes verry troublesome.\n\n\n

\n \n
\n
\n \n

[Gass]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Wednesday 20th. \u00a0\u00a0 We embarked early after a heavy gust of wind and rain, and proceeded on very well. The forenoon was cloudy, without rain; and in the afternoon the weather became clear and pleasant.\u2014 We went about seventy miles, and encamped; where we found the musquitoes very troublesome.\n\n\n

\n \n
\n \n \n
\n
1. The Cannonball River on the Morton-Sioux county line in North Dakota, first passed on October 18, 1804. Atlas map 27; MRC map 48. (back)
\n
2. \n Clark called this \"War-re-Con nee\" River when they passed it on October 16, 1804; it is now Beaver Creek in Emmons County, North Dakota. Possibly Clark has confused the name with the \"E\u0103-Ne\u0103h W\u00e1u-de-p\u00f3n,\" or Little Sioux River in Harrison County, Iowa; see August 8, 1804. Atlas map 27; MRC map 47. (back)
\n
3. The camp was in Campbell County, South Dakota, probably below the mouth of Spring Creek, on Atlas map 26 a nameless stream flowing from a lake; see October 13, 1804. The site would now be inundated by Oahe Reservoir. MRC map 46. (back)
