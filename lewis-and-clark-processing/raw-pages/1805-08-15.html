\n August 15, 1805\n

\n
\n \n

[Lewis]

\n \n
\n Thursday August 15th 1805.\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nThis morning I arrose very early and as hungary as a wolf. I had eat nothing yesterday except one scant meal of the flour and berries except the dryed cakes of berries which did not appear to satisfy my appetite as they appeared to do those of my Indian friends. I found on enquiry of McNeal that we had only about two pounds of flour remaining. \u00a0\u00a0 this I directed him to divide into two equal parts and to cook the one half this morning in a kind of pudding with the burries as he had done yesterday and reserve the ballance for the evening. \u00a0\u00a0 on this new fashoned pudding four of us breakfasted, giving a pretty good allowance also to the Chief who declared it the best thing he had taisted for a long time. \u00a0\u00a0 he took a little of the flour in his hand, taisted and examined very scrutinously and asked me if we made it of roots. I explained to him the manner in which it grew. \u00a0\u00a0 I hurried the departure of the Indians. \u00a0\u00a0 the Chief addressed them several times before they would move they seemed very reluctant to accompany me. I at length asked the reason and he told me that some foolish persons among them had suggested the idea that we were in league with the Pahkees and had come on in order to decoy them into an ambuscade where their enimies were waiting to receive them. \u00a0\u00a0 but that for his part he did not believe it. I readily perceived that our situation was not entirely free from danger as the transision from suspicion to the confermation of the fact would not be very difficult in the minds of these ignorant people who have been accustomed from their infancy to view every stranger as an enimy. I told Cameahwait that I was sorry to find that they had put so little confidence in us, that I knew they were not acquainted with whitemen and therefore could forgive them. \u00a0\u00a0 that among whitemen it was considered disgracefull to lye or entrap an enimy by falsehood. I told him if they continued to think thus meanly of us that they might rely on it that no whitemen would ever come to trade with them or bring them arms and amunition and that if the bulk of his nation still entertained this opinion I still hoped that there were some among them that were not affraid to die, that were men and would go with me and convince themselves of the truth of what I had asscerted. \u00a0\u00a0 that there was a party of whitemen waiting my return either at the forks of Jefferson's river or a little below coming on to that place in canoes loaded with provisions and merchandize. \u00a0\u00a0 he told me for his own part he was determined to go, that he was not affraid to die. I soon found that I had touched him on the right string; to doubt the bravery of a savage is at once to put him on his metal. \u00a0\u00a0 he now mounted his horse and haranged his village a third time; the perport of which as he afterwards told me was to inform them that he would go with us and convince himself of the truth or falsity of what we had told him if he was sertain he should be killed, that he hoped there were some of them who heard him were not affraid to die with him and if there was to let him see them mount their horses and prepare to set out. \u00a0\u00a0 shortly after this harange he was joined by six or eight only and with these I smoked a pipe and directed the men to put on their packs being determined to set out with them while I had them in the humour \u00a0\u00a0 at half after 12 we set out, several of the old women were crying and imploring the great sperit to protect their warriors as if they were going to inevitable distruction. \u00a0\u00a0 we had not proceeded far before our party was augmented by ten or twelve more, and before we reached the Creek [1] which we had passed in the morning of the 13th it appeared to me that we had all the men of the village and a number of women with us. \u00a0\u00a0 this may serve in some measure to ilustrate the capricious disposition of those people who never act but from the impulse of the moment. \u00a0\u00a0 they were now very cheerfull and gay, and two hours ago they looked as sirly as so many imps of satturn. \u00a0\u00a0 when we arrived at the spring on the side of the mountain where we had encamped on the 12th the Chief insited on halting to let the horses graize with which I complyed and gave the Indians smoke. \u00a0\u00a0 they are excessively fond of the pipe; but have it not much in their power to indulge themselves with even their native tobacco as they do not cultivate it themselves.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 after remaining about an hour we again set out, and by engaging to make compensation to four of them for their trouble obtained the previlege of riding with an indian myself and a similar situation for each of my party. I soon found it more tiresome riding without tirrups than walking and of course chose the latter making the Indian carry my pack. \u00a0\u00a0 about sunset we reached the upper part of the level valley of the Cove which now called Shoshone Cove. \u00a0\u00a0 the grass being birned on the North side of the river we passed over to the south and encamped near some willow brush about 4 miles above the narrow pass between the hills noticed as I came up this cove. [2] \u00a0\u00a0 the river was here about six yards wide, and frequently damed up by the beaver. I had sent Drewyer forward this evening before we halted to kill some meat but he was unsuccessful and did not rejoin us untill after dark \u00a0\u00a0 I now cooked and among six of us eat the remaining pound of flour stired in a little boiling water.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 Capt. Clark delayed again this morning untill after breakfast, when he set out and passed between low and rugged mountains which had a few pine trees distributed over them \u00a0\u00a0 the clifts are formed of limestone and a hard black rock intermixed. [3] \u00a0\u00a0 no trees on the river, the bottoms narrow river crooked shallow shoally and rapid. \u00a0\u00a0 the water is as coald as that of the best springs in our country. \u00a0\u00a0 the men as usual suffered excessively with fatiegue and the coldness of the water to which they were exposed for hours together. at the distance of 6 miles by water they passed the entrance of a bold creek on Stard. side 10 yds. wide and 3 f. 3 I. deep which we called Willard's Creek after Alexander Willard one of our party. [4] \u00a0\u00a0 at 4 miles by water from their encampment of las evening passed a bold branch which tumbled down a steep precipice of rocks from the mountains on the Lard. [5]\n Capt Clark was very near being bitten twice today by rattlesnakes, the Indian woman also narrowly escaped. \u00a0\u00a0 they caught a number of fine trout. Capt. Clark killed a buck which was the only game killed today. the venison has an uncommon bitter taist which is unpleasent. I presume it proceeds from some article of their food, perhaps the willow on the leaves of which they feed very much. \u00a0\u00a0 they encamped this evening on the Lard. side near a few cottonwood trees about which there were the remains of several old Indian brush lodges. [6]\n

Lewis had left at the entrence of the Mountains. \u00a0\u00a0 we passed Several bad rapids. \u00a0\u00a0 caught a nomber of Trout in the Eddys below the rapids. \u00a0\u00a0 proceeded on \u00a0\u00a0 passed Several fine Springs on L. Side. \u00a0\u00a0 the river more Shallow. \u00a0\u00a0 passed high clifts of rocks & rough knobs &C. \u00a0\u00a0 about 2 oClock we passed the mouth of a creek [8] on the Stard. Side 10 yds. wide & 3 feet 3 Inch deep, at the mouth. \u00a0\u00a0 2 hunters on a head a hunting. \u00a0\u00a0 we Saw where Capt. Lewis Camped the 10 ult. [9] \u00a0\u00a0 Some of the high knobs are covred with grass. \u00a0\u00a0 a fiew Scattering pine trees on them. \u00a0\u00a0 the River crooked Shallow and rapid. Some deep holes where we caught a nomber of Trout. Capt. \n Thursday 15th. \u00a0\u00a0 We had a fine morning and proceeded on about 8 o'clock. Having gone 2 miles, we came to the entrance of a mountain, where Captain Lewis and his party on the second day after their departure had taken dinner; and had left 4 deer skins. At the entrance of the mountain there are two high pillars [10] of rocks, resembling towers on each side of the river. The mountains are not very high and do not approach so near the river as some we have passed; they are about a quarter of a mile distant, and the river meanders along between them through the bushes and is not more than 20 yards wide, and about a foot and a half deep. The water is very cold, and severe and disagreeable to the men, who are frequently obliged to wade and drag the canoes. We went 15 miles and encamped on the South side.\n\n\n

\n Thursday 15th August 1805. \u00a0\u00a0 a cold clear morning. \u00a0\u00a0 we Set out as usal and proceeded on \u00a0\u00a0 entered the Mountains \u00a0\u00a0 verry high clifts of rocks near the River & Steep on each Side. \u00a0\u00a0 passed Several Springs on L. \nThursday August 15th \u00a0\u00a0 A Cold clear morning, We set out as usual, & proceeded on our Voyage, and entered the Mountains, where we found very high Clifts of Rocks lying near the River, and the shores steep on both sides of the River,\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 We passed several springs which lay on the South side of the River, and came from under the Mountains, and several rapid places, in the River. \u00a0\u00a0 below these rapids some of our party catch'd a quantity of fine Trout. \u00a0\u00a0 We passed some narrow bottoms, but found Timber very scarce.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 The River this day has been very shallow, We continued on, and passed Clifts of high Rocks, & rough mountains lying on each side of the river; and the \nmouth of a Creek, lying on the North side of the River; the water of which was of a reddish colour, \u2329and\u232a runs rapid, & is deep; and about 7 paces wide. \u00a0\u00a0 We passed where Captain Lewis had left 4 Deer Skins.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 Two of our hunters were sent out ahead of us.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 We found a note, with the deer Skins which Captain Lewis had left, which informed us that he had been at that place the 10th instant.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 We proceeded on, & found the River still growing \nshallower, which obliged our party to hawl the Canoes the greater part of the way, We also passed several Slifts of rocks which went steep up from the River, from 200 to 300 feet perpendicular in many places; some of the knobs, are covered with Grass, & Pitch pine trees. \u00a0\u00a0 The River got very crooked & difficult to pass, having a number of Shoals & deep holes in it. \u00a0\u00a0 In those deep holes our party caught a number of fine Trout.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 Captain Clarke was near being bit by a Rattle snake this day; it got between his legs, whilst he was standing fishing, he killed it, & a number of the same kind this day, We came 15 Miles this day, & encamped on the So Side of the River in a narrow plain on which was some old Indian Camps.\u2014\n\n\n\n
