\n July 9, 1804\n

\n
\n \n

[Clark]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n July the 9th Monday 1804 \u00a0\u00a0 Sent one man [1] back to the mouth of the River to mark a tree, to let the party on Shore See that the Boat had passed the river, Set out \u2329at Su\u232a early \u00a0\u00a0 passed (1) the head of the Island [2] Situated in the middle of the river a Sand bar at the head, (2) passed the mouth of a Creek or Bayou on the S. S. leading from a large Pond of about three miles in length, [3] \u00a0\u00a0 at 8 oClock it commenced raining, the wind changed from N E. to S. W. \u00a0\u00a0 (3) at 6 miles passed the mouth of a Small Creek on the L. S. called Monters Creek, [4] \u00a0\u00a0 the river at this place is wide with a Sand bar in the Middle, passed a place on the L. S. about 2 miles above the Creek, where Several french men camped two years to hunt\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 (4) passed a Island on the S S. of the river in a bend, opsd. a high Land on the L. S. [5] \u00a0\u00a0 wind Shifted to the N. W. in the evining, opsd. this Island, and on the L. S. \n Loup\n or \n Wolf River\n [6] Coms in, this river is about 60 yards Wide, but little water running at the mouth, this river heads with the waters of the Kanzas, and has a perogue navigation Some distance, it abounds with Beaver, Camped opposit the head of the Island on the L. S. [7] \u00a0\u00a0 Saw a fire on the S. S. \u00a0\u00a0 Supposedly the four flankers, to be theire, Sent a perogue for them, the Patroon & Bowman of the Perogue French, [8] they returned & informed, that when they approached the fire, it was put out, which caused them to return, this report causd. us to look out \u00a0\u00a0 Supposeing a pty. of Soux going to war, firierd the bow piec to allarm & put on their guard the men on Shore \u00a0\u00a0 everey thing in readiness for Defence.

[Clark]

\n \n
\n July 9th Monday 1804
\u00a0
\n \n

\none man Sent back to the river we passed last night to Blase [NB: notch] a tree with a view to notify the party on Shore of our passing \u00a0\u00a0 Set out and passed the head of the (1) Island which was Situated opposit to our Camp last night \u00a0\u00a0 a Sand bar at the head (2) opsd. this Island a \u2329Gut\u232a Creek or Bayaue Coms in from a large Pond on the Starboard Side, as our flanking party Saw great numbers of Pike in this Pond, I have laid it down with that name anex'd, [9] at 8 oClock the wind Shifted from the N, E to S W and it commenced raining. \u00a0\u00a0 (3) at Six miles passed the mouth of Creek on the L. S. Called \u2329Monter's\u232a [NB: Montain's] Creek, about two\n [10] mile above is some Cabins where our Bowman & Several frenchmen Campd. two years [NB: ago] [11] \u00a0\u00a0 (4) passed an Island on the S. S. in a Bend of the river opposit Some Clifts on the L. S. \u00a0\u00a0 the wind Shifted to the N W \u00a0\u00a0 opposit this Island and on the L. Side (Loup) or \n Wolf River\n Coms in, this river is about 60 yards wide and heads with the waters of the Kansis, and is navagable for Perogues \"Some destance up\" \u00a0\u00a0 Camped at a point on the L. S. opposit the head of the Island, our party was incamped on the Opposit Side, their not answering our Signals Caused us to Suspect the persons Camped opposit to us was a war party of Soux, we fired the Bow piece to alarm the party on Shore, alled prepared to oppose it attacted

[Ordway]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n\nMonday July the 9th 1804, we Set out eairly \u00a0\u00a0 Sent Bratton Back to the Creek to blaze some trees, So the Hunters might See we had passed. \u00a0\u00a0 proceeded on \u00a0\u00a0 passed a Creek or leading from a big pond called the Creek of the big pond. \u00a0\u00a0 this pond is near the River, and about 3 miles long & handsom \u00a0\u00a0 a great many beaver, & fish, fine land and well timbered about this place, Rainy. \u00a0\u00a0 the wind changed from the N. E. to the S. W. \u00a0\u00a0 at 6 miles passed the mouth of a Small Creek on the South Side called Monters or wolf Creek, passed a place on the South Side about 2 miles above the Creek where Several Frenchman Camped 2 years for to hunt & raise corn &c\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 high land on the South Side \u00a0\u00a0 we passed a Creek on the South Side called River DeLoup, the wind Shifted to the N. W. in the evening. Camped on the South Side of the Missouris, a Gun fired on the opposite Side \u00a0\u00a0 Supposed to be our hunters \u00a0\u00a0 the pearogue went over for them but did not find them nor any body else. \u00a0\u00a0 we fired our bow peace.\n\n\n

[Floyd]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n\nMonday July 9th 1804 \u00a0\u00a0 Set out erley this morning \u00a0\u00a0 prosed on \u00a0\u00a0 passed a Small Creek on the South Side Called monter Creek \u00a0\u00a0 High Land \u00a0\u00a0 Rain to day \u00a0\u00a0 Sailed the Gratist part of the day \u00a0\u00a0 passed a prarie on the South Side whare Seveal French famileys had Setled and made Corn Some Years ago \u2329and Black Smith or Gun Smith\u232a Stayed two years [12] \u00a0\u00a0 the Indians came Freckentley to See them and was verry frendley \u00a0\u00a0 passed a Creek on the South Side Called wolf Creek \u00a0\u00a0 it is about 60 yards wide \u00a0\u00a0 the Land is Good \u00a0\u00a0 water Strong \u00a0\u00a0 made 10 miles \u00a0\u00a0 encamt on the South Side \u00a0\u00a0 Saw a fire on the N. Side thougt it was \u2329Indians\u232a ouer flanken partey \u00a0\u00a0 Sent ouer perogue over for them and when they got over Saw no fire \u00a0\u00a0 Seposed it to be Indians \u00a0\u00a0 fired ouer Cannon for ouer men\n\n

[Gass]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Monday 9th. \u00a0\u00a0 Early this morning we continued our voyage. It rained hard till 12 o'clock. We passed a creek on the south side, called Wolf creek. The man that was snake bitten is become well. We encamped on the south side.\n\n

[Whitehouse]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nMonday 9 \u00a0\u00a0 Sat out the Usal hour of Day light \u00a0\u00a0 Rain Came on \u00a0\u00a0 Raind the Most part of the day the hunters did not Come in \u00a0\u00a0 We Rod 12 Miles at piettet River de louce or woolf River\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 Incampd. \u00a0\u00a0 it lies on the W. S. \u00a0\u00a0 the Mouth is about 20 yds. B. \u00a0\u00a0 the hunters Came \u2329in\u232a did not Come in

\n

\nMonday July 9th \u00a0\u00a0 We set off early this morning, shortly after we had started a Rain came on, which continued most part of the day.\u2014

\n

\nWe proceed on our Voyage and arrived at a River called Petit River De louce, or Little Wolf River, the mouth of which is 20 Yards wide. \u00a0\u00a0 This River lies on the So West side of the River Mesouri, our hunters that were out, did not come to us this day, The distance we rowed this day being 12 Miles.\n\t\n

\n
1. According to Ordway the man was Bratton. (back)
\n
2. Evidently the head of Nodaway Island. Nicollet (MMR), 376; MRC map 18. (back)
\n
3. Probably Little Tarkio Creek, in Holt County, Missouri, which seems to have had various beds in this vicinity over the years. One of them passed through what is now a sizable oxbow lake in the Missouri bottom. Nicollet shows \"Pike pond (L. & C.)\" near the head of Nodaway Island and downstream from the mouth of the creek; either the creek had already shifted its course or Nicollet misread Biddle. Nicollet (MMR), 376; MRC maps 18, 19. (back)
\n
4. Either Charleston Creek or Mosquito Creek, in Doniphan County, Kansas, depending on the course of the Missouri River in 1804. MRC map 18. (back)
\n
5. This high bluff in Doniphan County, was later grandiosely called Lookout Mountain. Ibid. (back)
\n
6. \n Wolf Creek, in Doniphan County. Loup is French for \"wolf.\" It should not be confused with the Loup River in Nebraska, which Lewis and Clark never saw although they heard of it. Ibid.; MRM map 51. (back)
\n
7. Precise location not possible because of shifts of the Missouri over the years; using the course of the river today, the camp would be near the present town of Iowa Point, Doniphan County. MRC map 18. (back)
\n
8. If the arrangements were still the same as those of May 26, 1804, this would be the red pirogue, with Patroon Baptiste Deschamps in charge. (back)
\n
9. The pike, strictly speaking, is northern pike, but various fishes resembling it are also loosely called pike. Lee et al., 133. The lake does not appear on Clark's maps; he may have \"laid it down\" on a lost map or in a list of streams. (back)
\n
10. The word could be read as either \"ones\" or \"two,\" but the Field Notes entry confirms the latter. (back)
\n
11. The bowman would be either Cruzatte or Labiche. This site is in Doniphan County, a few miles down the Missouri from Iowa Point. MRC map 18. (back)
\n
12. Either Cruzatte or Labiche, but not Shields, the man usually called the blacksmith. (back)
