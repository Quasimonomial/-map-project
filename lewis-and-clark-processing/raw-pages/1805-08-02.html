\n August 2, 1805\n

\n
\n \n

[Lewis]

\n \n
\n August 2nd 1805.\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nWe resumed our march this morning at sunrise \u00a0\u00a0 the weather was fair and wind from N. W. \u00a0\u00a0 finding that the river still boar to the south I determined to pass it if possible to shorten our rout \u00a0\u00a0 this we effected about five miles above our camp of last evening by wading it. [1] \u00a0\u00a0 found the current very rappid about 90 yards wide and waist deep \u00a0\u00a0 this is the first time that I ever dared to make the attempt to wade the river, tho' there are many places between this and the three forks where I presume it migh be attempted with equal success. \u00a0\u00a0 the valley though which our rout of this [day] lay and through which the river winds it's meandering course is a beatifull level plain with but little timber and that on the verge of the river. \u00a0\u00a0 the land is tolerably fertile, consisting of a black or dark yellow loam, [2] and covered with grass from 9 Inches to 2 feet high. \u00a0\u00a0 the plain ascends gradually on either side of the river to the bases of two ranges of mountains which ly parrallel to the river and which terminate \u2329it's\u232a the width of the vally. \u00a0\u00a0 the tops of these mountains were yet partially covered with snow while we in the valley. \u00a0\u00a0 were suffocated nearly with the intense heat of the midday sun. \u00a0\u00a0 the nights are so could that two blankets are not more than sufficient covering. \u00a0\u00a0 we found a great courants, two kinds of which were red, others yellow deep purple and black, also black goosburies and service buries now ripe and in full perfection, we feasted suptuously on our wild fruit particularly the yellow courant and the deep purple servicebury which I found to be excellent \u00a0\u00a0 the courrant grows very much like the red currant common to the gardens in the atlantic states tho' the leaf is somewhat different and the growth taller. \u00a0\u00a0 the service burry grows on a smaller bush and differs from ours only in colour and the superior excellence of it's flavor and size, it is of a deep purple. [3] this day we saw an abundance of deer and goats or antelopes and a great number of the tracks of Elk; of the former we killed two. \u00a0\u00a0 we continued our rout along this valley which is from six to eight Miles wide untill sun set when we encamped for the night on the river bank having traveled about 24 miles. [4] I feel myself perfectly recovered of my indisposition and \u2329have\u232a do not \u2329but little\u232a doubt \u2329but I\u232a being able to pursue my march with equal comfort in the morning.

[Lewis]

\n \n
\n Friday August 2cd 1805.\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nWe resumed our march this morning at sunrise; the [day] was fair and wind from N. W. \u00a0\u00a0 finding that the river still boar to the South I determined to pass it if possible in order to shorten our rout; this we effected by wading the river about 5 miles above our encampment of the last evening. \u00a0\u00a0 we found the current very rapid waist deep and about 90 yd. wide bottom smooth pebble with a small mixture of coarse gravel. \u00a0\u00a0 this is the first time that I ever dared to wade the river, tho' there are many places between this and the forks where I presume it might be attempted with equal success. The vally allong which we passed today, and through which the river winds it's meandering course is from 6 to 8 miles wide and consists of a beatifull level plain with but little timber and that confined to the verge of the river; the land is tolerably fertile, and is either black or a dark yellow loam, covered with grass from 9 inches to 2 feet high. \u00a0\u00a0 the plain ascends gradually on either side of the river to the bases of two ranges of high mountains, which lye parallel to the river and prescribe the limits of the plains. \u00a0\u00a0 the tops of these mountains are yet covered partially with snow, while we in the valley are nearly suffocated with the intense heat of the midday sun; the nights are so cold that two blankets are not more than sufficient covering. \u00a0\u00a0 soon after passing the river this morning Sergt. Gass lost my tommahawk in the thick brush and we were unable to find it, I regret the loss of this usefull implement, however accedents will happen in the best families, and I consoled myself with the recollection that it was not the only one we had with us. \u00a0\u00a0 the bones of the buffaloe and their excrement of an old date are to be met with in every part of this valley but we have long since lost all hope of meeting with that animal in these mountains. \u00a0\u00a0 we met with great quantities of currants today, two species of which were red, others yellow, deep perple and black; also black goosberries and serviceberries now ripe and in great perfection. \u00a0\u00a0 we feasted sumptuously on our wild fruits, particularly the yellow currant and the deep perple serviceberries, which I found to be excellent. \u00a0\u00a0 the serviceberry grows on a small bush and differs from ours only in colour size and superior excellence of it's flavour. \u00a0\u00a0 it is somewhat larger than ours. [5] \u00a0\u00a0 on our way we saw an abundance of deer Antelopes, of the former we killed 2. \u00a0\u00a0 we also saw many tracks of the Elk and bear. no recent appearance of Indians. \u00a0\u00a0 the Indians in this part of the country appear to construct their lodges with the willow boughs and brush; they are small of a conic figure and have a small aperture on one side through which they enter. \u00a0\u00a0 we continue our rout up this valley on the Lard. side of the river untill sunset, at which time we encamped on the Lard. bank of the river having traveled 24 miles. \u00a0\u00a0 we had brought wih us a good stock of venison of which we eat a hearty supper. \u00a0\u00a0 I feel myself perfectly recovered of my indisposition, and do not doubt being able to pursue my rout tomorrow with the same comfort I have done today.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 we saw some very large beaver dams today in the bottoms of the river several of which wer five feet high and overflowed several acres of land; these dams are formed of willow brush mud and gravel and are so closely interwoven that they resist the water perfectly. \u00a0\u00a0 the base of this work is thick and rises nearly perpendicularly on the lower side while the upper side or that within the dam is gently sloped. \u00a0\u00a0 the brush appear to be laid in no regular order yet acquires a strength by the irregularity with which they are placed by the beaver that it would puzzle the engenuity of man to give them.

\n

\n Capt. Clark continued his rout early this morning. \u00a0\u00a0 the rapidity of the current was such that his progress was slow, in short it required the utmost exertion of the men to get on, nor could they resist this current by any other means than that of the cord and pole. \u00a0\u00a0 in the course of the day they passed some villages of burrowing squirrels, [6] saw a number of beaver dams and the inhabitants of them, many young ducks both of the Duckanmallard and the redheaded fishing duck, gees, several rattle snakes, black woodpeckers, and a large gang of Elk; [7] they found the river much crouded with island both large and small and passed a small creek on Stard. side which we called \n birth Creek. [8]\n Capt. Clark discovers a tumor rising on the inner side of his ankle this evening which was painfull to him [NB?: Rec: boils]. \u00a0\u00a0 they incamped in a level bottom on the Lard. side.\u2014 [9]\n

[Clark]

\n August 2dnd Friday 1805\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\t\na fine day \u00a0\u00a0 Set out early \u00a0\u00a0 the river has much the Same kind of banks Chanel Current &c. as it had in the last vallie, I walked out this morning on Shore & Saw Several rattle Snakes in the plain, the wind from the S W \u00a0\u00a0 we proceeded on with great dificuelty from the rapidity of the current & rapids, abt. 15 miles and Encamped on the Lard Side, saw a large Gangue of Elk at Sunset to the S W. \u00a0\u00a0 passed a Small Creek on the Stard Side [ML?: called birth Creek\n ] and maney large and Small Islands. Saw a number of young Ducks as we have also Seen everry Day, Some geese\u2014 I saw Black woodpeckers\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 I have either got my foot bitten by Some poisonous insect or a tumer is riseing on the inner bone of my ankle which is painfull\n

\n Friday 2nd August 1805. \u00a0\u00a0 a fine pleasant morning. \u00a0\u00a0 we Set out eairly and proceeded on. The River is now Small crooked Shallow and rapid. \u00a0\u00a0 passed bottoms covered with cotton Timber. Saw abundance of beaver lodges & sign \u00a0\u00a0 Some trees newly cut down by them. Saw ponds where they damed up the water one dam above another which is curious to behold. \u00a0\u00a0 passed a high bank in which was villages of bank Swallows \u00a0\u00a0 passed large beautiful bottom praries on each Side and bottoms of timber &C. Saw Several old Indian Camps on S. Side. Some of the high hills look black with pine timber and Solid rocks &C. \u00a0\u00a0 the day warm. \u00a0\u00a0 we proceeded on. \u00a0\u00a0 passed a nomber of Islands, and bottoms covd. with cotton & birch [10] timber. \u00a0\u00a0 passed Smooth prarie. Saw Several grey Eagles &C [11] \u00a0\u00a0 we Came 14\u00be miles to day and Camped on the edge of a Smooth plain on L. Side. Saw a gang of Elk back under the hills. \u00a0\u00a0 the country in general back from the river is broken and mountaineous.\u2014\n\n\n

[Gass]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Friday 2nd. \u00a0\u00a0 The morning was fine and we went on at sunrise, proceeded 4 or 5 miles and crossed the river. In the middle of the day it was very warm in the valley, and at night very cold; so much so that two blankets were scarce a sufficient covering. On each side of the valley there is a high range of mountains, [12] which run nearly parallel, with some spots of snow on their tops. We killed a deer; went about 24 miles and encamped [13] on the south side.\n\n\n

\n Friday 2nd August 1805. \u00a0\u00a0 a fine pleasant morning. \u00a0\u00a0 we Set out eairly and proceeded on. \u00a0\u00a0 the River is now Small crooked Shallow and rapid. \u00a0\u00a0 passed bottoms of cotton timber &c. \u00a0\u00a0 Saw abundance of beaver Sign, trees a foot over which had newly been cut down. \u00a0\u00a0 Saw a pond which was made by the beaver damming up the water as in may places. \u00a0\u00a0 passed a high bank in which was a village of what is called bank Swallows. \u00a0\u00a0 high hills a little back from the River on each Side of the River. \u00a0\u00a0 considerable of pine on them, & covered with Short grass. \u00a0\u00a0 I have a pain in my Shoulder. \u00a0\u00a0 we proceeded on \u00a0\u00a0 passed large beautiful bottom prarie on each Side, & bottoms of timber. \u00a0\u00a0 Saw a nomber of old Indian Camps. \u00a0\u00a0 the beaver houses are verry pleanty & ponds where they resort. \u00a0\u00a0 the day warm. \u00a0\u00a0 we proceeded on \u00a0\u00a0 passed a nomber of Islands and bottoms. \u00a0\u00a0 the River Shallow and rapid. \u00a0\u00a0 passed Smoth praries &c. \u00a0\u00a0 Saw 2 grey Eagles which had nests on the top of dry trees. \u00a0\u00a0 Came 14\u00be miles this day & Camped on a Smoth plain on L. Side. \u00a0\u00a0 Saw a gang of Elk back under the hills. \u00a0\u00a0 the country back from the River is broken & Mountainous.

\n

\nFriday August 2nd \u00a0\u00a0 This morning we had fine & pleasant weather, we set out early & proceeded on our Voyage. \u00a0\u00a0 We found the River getting very narrow, crooked, shallow & rapid, We passed some rich bottoms of Cotton \nTimber, where we saw abundance of signs of Beaver, & trees that had been cut down by these animals lately, many of which measur'd a foot over, & a pond which was made by the beaver daming up the Water, We also passed a bank which was very high, and had a vast number of Swallows nests in them. \u00a0\u00a0 This bank lay a small distance back from the River, on each side of it, & had a considerable quantity of Pine trees growing on them & short grass.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 We proceeded on and passed a large beautiful bottom, and Priaries lying on both sides of the River, and some large bottoms of timbered land. \u00a0\u00a0 We saw in those bottoms, a number of Indian Camps which appear'd to have been built some time, & plenty of Houses built by the beaver, & a large pond where those animals resort to, We continued on, & saw a number of small \nIslands & bottoms, The River getting more shallow; Some level Priaries lying on both sides of the River.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 In the afternoon we saw 2 large Grey Eagles, whose nests we saw on the tops of high Trees which were dead.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 We came 14\u00be miles this day, & encamped on a smooth plain lying on the South side of the River, where we saw a Gang of Elk, back of our Camp under some hills.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 The Country this day lying back from the River, is broken, and Mountaneous.\u2014\n\n\n\n

1. \n Lewis crossed the Jefferson River from Jefferson to Madison County, Montana, somewhere between the present towns of Cardwell and Whitehall. The dotted line on Atlas map 65 shows his route. (back)
\n
2. The bottomlands, being well watered, produce a soil with a fairly well-developed humus horizon here. The uplands, being drier, develop a thin soil having a yellow or buff color. (back)
\n
3. The two kinds of red currants are problematic since there is only one species of red-fruited, unarmed, currant which grows in the area, squaw, or western red, currant, Ribes cereum Dougl. var. inebrians (Lindl.) C. L. Hitchc. See above, July 25, 1805. The yellow, deep purple currant refers to golden currant, R. aureum Pursh, with its yellow and purple fruits in the same population as desecribed earlier. The black currant is possibly R. americanum Mill., which is also known from the same area. The red currant of Eastern gardens is R. rubrum. The serviceberry is Amelanchier alnifolia Nutt. Booth & Wright, 107, 110; Hitchcock et al., 3:67\u201370; Little (MWH), 16-NW. (back)
\n
4. \n Lewis camped somewhere in the vicinity of present Waterloo, in Madison County. Atlas map 65. (back)
\n
5. Perhaps it was Biddle who drew a red vertical line from \"we met with\" to this point. (back)
\n
6. Prairie dogs, Cynomys ludovicianus.\n (back)
\n
7. The fishing duck is either the female red-breasted merganser, Mergus serrator [AOU, 130], or the female common merganser, Mergus merganser [AOU, 129]. Burroughs, 189; Holmgren, 29. The black woodpecker is Lewis's woodpecker, Melanerpes lewis [AOU, 408]. See descriptions at July 20, 1805, and May 27, 1806. (back)
\n
8. After Clark's thirty-fifth birthday, on August 1; it is later Whitetail Creek, in Jefferson County, which passes the town of Whitehall. Atlas map 65. (back)
\n
9. In Madison County, a little below Big Pipestone Creek, the party's Panther Creek. Atlas map 65. (back)
\n
10. Perhaps scrub birch, Betula glandulosa Michx., which Lewis notes the next day as dwarf birch, or perhaps the more common water birch, B. occidentalis Hook. (back)
\n
11. Golden eagle, Aquila chrysaetos. (back)
\n
12. The Bull and then the Highland Mountains to their right going up the Jefferson, and \nthe Tobacco Root Mountains to their left. (back)
\n
13. In the vicinity of Waterloo, Madison County, Montana. (back)
