\n June 13, 1804\n

\n
\n \n

[Clark] [1]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n13th June Wednesday \u00a0\u00a0 \u2329Papers for the Secretary at War\u232a [2] \u00a0\u00a0 we Set out early \u00a0\u00a0 passed a verry round bend to L. S. [3] \u00a0\u00a0 passed two Creeks 1 me. apt. Called Creeks of the round Bend, [4] between those Creeks Stbd S. is a butifull Prarie, in which the antient Missourie Indians had a Village, [5] \u00a0\u00a0 at this place 300 of them were killed by the Saukees, a fair Day. \u00a0\u00a0 Passed the antient Missouries villages on right \u00a0\u00a0 Course N 40\u00b0 W 2\u00bd pt. L S., S 29\u00b0 W 3 ms. pt. S. S., this nation once the Most Noumerous is now almost extinct, about 30 of them, liveing with Otteaus on the R. Platt, the remainder all distroyed, took altd. of S. U L with qdt. which gave N 28 W. 1\u00bd ms to a pt. S. S. \u00a0\u00a0 Passed some Charming land, I have not Seen any high hils above Charliton [Chariton River] and the hils below for Several days Cannot to turmed hills but high Land, not exceeding 100 abov the high water mark \u00a0\u00a0 N 30\u00b0 W, to a pt. L. S. 2 ms. \u00a0\u00a0 passed a verry bad Sand bar, where the boat was nearly turning & fastening in the quick Sand and came too in the mouth of Grand R. S. S. [6] this River is about 120 yards wide and navagable for Purogues a great distance, it heads with the River Dumoine [Des Moines] [7] \u2329St. Peters\u232a, passing the river \u2329Dumn.\u232a Carlton. [Chariton] \u00a0\u00a0 a Butifull open Prarie Coms to the river below its mouth, we landed and walked to the hills which is abt. \u00bd a mile. \u00a0\u00a0 the Lower prarie over flows. \u00a0\u00a0 the hunters Killd. a Bare & Dere, this is a butifull place \u00a0\u00a0 the Prarie rich & extinsive, Took Some Looner Observations which Kept Cap L. & my Self up untill half past 11 oClock.\n\n

[Clark]

\n \n
13th June Wednesday, 1804
\u00a0
\n \n

\nWe Set out early \u00a0\u00a0 passed a round bend to the S. S. and two Creeks Called the round bend Creeks \u00a0\u00a0 between those two Creeks and behind a Small willow Island in the bend is a Prarie in which the Missouries Indians once lived and the Spot where 300 [NB: 200] of them fell a Sacrifise to the fury of the \n Saukees\n \u00a0\u00a0 This nation (Missouries) once the most noumerous nation in this part of the Continent now reduced to about 80 fes. [8] and that fiew under the protection of the \n Otteaus\n [NB: Ottoes\n ] on R Platt who themselves are declineing \u00a0\u00a0 passed Som willow Isds. and bad Sand bars, Twook Medn. altitude with Octent back observation \u00a0\u00a0 it gave for altd. on its Low L 36\u00b0 58' 0\" the E [error] Enstrement 2\u00b0 00' 00\" +. \u00a0\u00a0 the Hills or high land for Several days past or above the 2 Charletons does not exceed 100 foot \u00a0\u00a0 passed a Batteau [9] or Sand roleing where the Boat was nearly turning over by her Strikeing & turning on the Sand. We came too in the Mouth of Grand River on S. S. and Camped for the night, this River is \u2329about\u232a from 80 to 100 yards wide at its Mouth and navagable for Perogues a great distance \u00a0\u00a0 This river heads with the R. Dumoine \u00a0\u00a0 below its mouth is a butifull Plain of bottom land \u00a0\u00a0 the hills rise at \u00bd a mile back \u00a0\u00a0 The lands about this place is either Plain or over flown bottom \u00a0\u00a0 Capt Lewis and my Self walked to the hill from the top of which we had a butifull prospect of Serounding Countrey \u00a0\u00a0 in the open Prarie we Caught a racoon, our hunters brought in a Bear & Deer \u00a0\u00a0 we took Some Luner observation this evening.

[Gass]

\n \n
\u00a0

\n Wednesday 13th. \u00a0\u00a0 We proceeded early on our voyage; passed a small creek on the north side in a long bend of the river; and encamped at the mouth of Grand river on the North side. This is as handsome a place as I ever saw in an uncultivated state.\n\n\n

[Whitehouse]

\u00a0

\nWendy 13th \u00a0\u00a0 Got On Our way at the three point Island Or the falling \u2329Do\u232a Banks whare all hands Breakfasted belonging to the three Crafts\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 On the Oppisite Shore S. W. Side neer two or about 2 Oclock the Barge Struck a Sandbar \u00a0\u00a0 She Keeld On her labord \u00a0\u00a0 the Sand being Quick Vanquishd Suddently from \u2329her\u232a Under her \u00a0\u00a0 the Currant Being Rappid Neerly Swept the men of their legs while Bearing her up from Sinquing. \u00a0\u00a0 Got to the Grand River at three Oclock \u00a0\u00a0 our hunters met us there with a bear and Some Venison \u00a0\u00a0 Incampd. there \u00a0\u00a0 Roed 14 Miles \u00a0\u00a0 this venison is [nice?] [15]\n

\n

\nWednesday June 13th \u00a0\u00a0 This Morning we set out early from the three point Island, we proceeded on till about 2 oClock P. M. on the South west side of the River, our Boat struck on a sand barr; she grounded on her larbourd side, the place being a quicksand and the current running strong. \u00a0\u00a0 The sand vanquish'd quick from under her, & so suddenly; that the Men on the deck were nearly swept off their legs, whilst they were bearing the boat up from sinking, At 3 o'Clock P. M. we arrived at the Mouth of the grand River, where we was met by the hunters we had sent out; who brought with them a Bear and some Venison which they had killed. \u00a0\u00a0 The Mesouri River running from the North [16] West. \u00a0\u00a0 We encamp'd at this place, having rowed 14 Miles this day.\u2014

\n

\n Grand River lays in Latitude 38\u00b0 47' 54 North\u2014\n\n

1. Some figures are written to the side of this entry: 2\u00bd, 3, 1\u00bd, 2, and a total of 9. (back)
\n
2. The words crossed out are immediately under the date. Possibly Clark intended to write here a list of papers to be sent back to Secretary Henry Dearborn with the intended return party. (back)
\n
3. It would appear that this bend in Chariton County, Missouri, was cut off by a change in the course of the Missouri River later in the nineteenth century. The curve and a remaining lake are quite evident on MRC map 10. It was later known as Bowling Green Bend. Coues (HLC), 1:22 n. 48. (back)
\n
4. One of those was probably later Palmer Creek. Ibid.; MRC map 10. (back)
\n
5. The Missouris, or Missourias, called after the river, when first noticed in 1673 were situated at the mouth of the Grand River (see below, n. 6) in Missouri, about where Clark places the village here. They are said to have been a large and important tribe before they were almost annihilated during the latter half of the eighteenth century by Mississippi River tribes, particularly the Sauks and the Fox. Their earliest known village was near Miami Landing, now called the Utz site. Eventually they moved to the southwestern tip of Saline County, to a spot known as the Gumbo Point site. See also notes for June 15. By about 1798 they were forced to move up the Missouri River into Nebraska, to join the culturally related Otos; both tribes spoke the Siouan language of the Chiwere group and had economies based on hunting and horticulture. Henceforward the two tribes acted together and were treated by the United States as one. The last full-blooded Missouri is said to have died in Oklahoma in 1907. Chapman (OM); Hodge 1:911\u201312; Irving (IS); Bray (MIT). (back)
\n
6. \n Grand River, one of the principal streams of northern Missouri, forms the boundary between Carroll and Chariton counties where it meets the Missouri River. It appears likely that the mouth of the Grand in 1804 was farther north above present Brunswick. It is not clear on which side of the Grand they camped. Nicollet (MMR), 361 ; MRC map 10. (back)
\n
7. The headwaters of the Grand and its principal tributary, the Thompson River, are near the heads of the South and Middle Des Moines in southern Iowa. Petersen, 301\u20135. (back)
\n
8. This may be an abbreviation for \"fires,\" that is, families. Thwaites (LC), 1:47. (back)
\n
9. \n Clark probably refers again to a bature, a sandy beach on the inside curve in a river. See June 4, 1804. (back)
\n
10. This entry and the next are Lewis's observations from Codex O. Proceeding this date is the symbol for the planet Mercury. (back)
\n
11. This may be the first of the \"round bend Creeks.\" See Clark's and Floyd's entries of this day. (back)
\n
12. Perhaps Palmer Creek, Chariton County, Missouri. Floyd's name, somewhat strange, is not used by others; Clark called it (with other streams at this point), \"round bend Creeks.\" (back)

13. \n Sauk Indians. (back)

14. \"Missourie Indianes\" and the passage of \"village \u00a0\u00a0 at this place . . . in former times\" was underlined in red by an unknown hand. (back)
\n
15. This last sentence appears to be in the hand of No. 3. (back)
\n
16. \"North\" is written over \"South.\" (back)
