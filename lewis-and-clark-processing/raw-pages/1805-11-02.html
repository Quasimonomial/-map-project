\n November 2nd Saturday 1805\n [9]\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nExamined the rapid below us more pertcelarly \u00a0\u00a0 the danger appearing too great to Hazzard our Canoes loaded, dispatched all the men who could not Swim with loads to the end of the portage below, I also walked to the end of the portage with the carriers where I delayed untill everry articles was brought over and canoes arrived Safe. \u00a0\u00a0 here we brackfast and took a Meridn. \u00a0\u00a0 altitude 59\u00b0 45' 45\" \u00a0\u00a0 about the time we were Setting out 7 Squars came over loaded with Dried fish, and bear grass neetly bundled up, Soon after 4 Indian men came down over the rapid in a large canoe. \u00a0\u00a0 passed a rapid at 2 miles & 1 at 4 miles opposite the lower point of a high Island on the Lard Side, and a little below 4 Houses on the Stard. Bank, [10] a Small Creek on the Lard Side opposit Straw berry Island, which heads below the last rapid, opposit the lower point of this Island passed three Islands covered with tall timber opposit the Beatin rock Those Islands are nearest the Starboard Side, imediately below on the Stard. Side passed a village of nine houses, [11] which is Situated between 2 Small Creeks, and are of the Same construction of those above; here the river widens to near a mile, and the bottoms are more extensive and thickly timbered, as also the high mountains on each Side, with Pine, Spruce pine, Cotton wood, a Species of ash, and alder. \u00a0\u00a0 at 17 miles passed a rock near the middle of the river, about 100 feet high and 80 feet Diamuter, [12] proceed on down a Smoth gentle Stream of about 2 miles wide, in which the tide has its effect as high as the Beaten rock or the Last rapids at Strawberry Island,\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 Saw great numbers of waterfowl of Different kinds, Such as Swan, Geese, white & grey brants, [13] ducks of various kinds, Guls, & Pleaver. Labeach killed 14 brant Joseph Fields 3 & Collins one. \u00a0\u00a0 we encamped under a high projecting rock on the Lard. Side, [14] here the mountains leave the river on each Side, which from the great Shute to this place is high and rugid; thickly Covered with timber principalley of the Pine Species. The bottoms below appear extensive and thickly Covered with wood. \u00a0\u00a0 river here about 2\u00bd miles wide. Seven Indians in a Canoe on their way down to trade with the nativs below, encamp with us, those we left at the portage passed us this evening and proceeded on down \u00a0\u00a0 The ebb tide rose here about 9 Inches, the flood tide must rise here much higher\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 we made 29 miles to day from the Great Shute\u2014\n

\nSaturday 2nd Nov. 1805. \u00a0\u00a0 a fair morning. \u00a0\u00a0 we carried Some of our baggage past the last rapid about one mile further and took one canoe down at a time partly loaded. \u00a0\u00a0 about 10 oClock A. M. we got all Safe below the last bad rapid we can git any account of from the natives. \u00a0\u00a0 then loaded up the canoes. \u00a0\u00a0 2 Indians came to us from a village below who had red and blue cloath blankets which appeared new \u00a0\u00a0 one had a brass musket and powder flask a little powder &C. \u00a0\u00a0 about 12 we Set out \u00a0\u00a0 passed a large Island mostly prarie which our officers name Strabery Island. \u00a0\u00a0 we halted a fiew minutes and killed Several geese. \u00a0\u00a0 passd. Several old villages about or a little below the of the big Shoote on the Stard. Side. \u00a0\u00a0 at the foot of Said Island we passd. over a rapid which high waves in it the hills high on each Side but mostly covred with pine timber. \u00a0\u00a0 proceeded on about 4 miles further \u00a0\u00a0 the River got more Smooth the current gentle wide and Strait. \u00a0\u00a0 passed the mo. of a creek [15] on the Lard. side \u00a0\u00a0 Saw a number of Spring runs flowing from the high clifts and Mountains. Some of which falls off about 100 feet perpinticular [16] \u00a0\u00a0 we came 21 miles this day & Camped [17] under a verry Shelving clift on the Lard. Side\u2014\n\n\n

[Gass]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Saturday 2nd. \u00a0\u00a0 There is here a small rapid below the falls, where the men had to carry part of the baggage across a portage of two miles and an half, while the rest took down the canoes. At 12 o'clock we proceeded on again; passed a narrow rapid part of the river of about 8 miles, the hills on both sides are very high, and a number of fine springs flowing out of them, some of which fall 200 feet perpendicular. The hills are mostly solid rock. On our way we passed two Indian lodges. At the end of eight miles, the river opens to the breadth of a mile, with a gentle current. We came 23 miles, and encamped at a high peak resembling a tower of the south side. The country here becomes level, and the river broader. One of the Indian canoes remained with us and the other three went on. On our way and at camp we killed 17 geese and brants. [18]\n

[Whitehouse]

\n Saturday 2nd Nov. 1805. \u00a0\u00a0 a clear morning. \u00a0\u00a0 we carried a part of the baggage below the other last rapids one mile further and ran over one canoe down the rapid at a time. \u00a0\u00a0 about 10 oClock A. m. we got all below the last rapids we have any acct. of from the Savages, then loaded the canoes agn. and about 12 oC Set out and proceeded on over Several more rapids at the foot of large Islands. \u00a0\u00a0 Saw 2 old ancient villages below the big rapids which was evacuated, the bark taken off of one of them and put in a pond to Soak. \u00a0\u00a0 the frames verry large. \u00a0\u00a0 the mountains verry high on the Lard. Side and on the S. Side a little back from the River. \u00a0\u00a0 proceed. on abt. 4 miles then the Riv. got Smooth the current verry gentle &c \u00a0\u00a0 the River wide and Strait the remdr. of the day \u00a0\u00a0 we passed a creek on L. Side &c. \u00a0\u00a0 great nomber of Spring runs, and Springs flowing from the high clifts and mountains and fell off down 100 feet or more. \u00a0\u00a0 high clifts on each Side. \u00a0\u00a0 the Indians over took us with their canoes, which they carryed past the big rapids \u00a0\u00a0 Saw 2 Indians dressed in red Scarlet and one in blue cloth which appeared new. \u00a0\u00a0 they had a Musket which was made all of brass and copper, a powder flask &c. \u00a0\u00a0 towards evening the River got more wide could Scarsely perceive any current at all. \u00a0\u00a0 the mont. and clifts cont. all day. \u00a0\u00a0 went 21 miles and Camped under a Shelving clift & a green on the Lard. Side. \u00a0\u00a0 Saw this day abundance of geese and killed 16 of them. \u00a0\u00a0 Saw Some Swan and brants ducks &c. \u00a0\u00a0 passd. one village this after noon on the S. Side. \u00a0\u00a0 passd. Some narrow bottoms high towers of rocks &c. \u00a0\u00a0 the country is timbred but mostly Spruce and Pine. \u00a0\u00a0 Some oak &c;\u2014

\n

\nSaturday Novemr. 2nd \u00a0\u00a0 A Clear morning but cool. \u00a0\u00a0 Our party were all engaged in carrying a part of our baggage &ca below these rapids, to a place below the last part of them. \u00a0\u00a0 On our party returning; they carried the whole of our Canoes down the last Rapid one at a time. \u00a0\u00a0 About 10 o'Clock A. M. we got them all safe below it. \u00a0\u00a0 This Rapid is the last, that we have any account of, that lays on the Columbia river, from the Indians. \u00a0\u00a0 We loaded our Canoes & about 12 o'Clock A. M. \u00a0\u00a0 We proceeded on down the Columbia River, & passed over several small rapids which lay at the foot of an Island. \u00a0\u00a0 We saw lying below the big Rapid this day, 2 old ancient looking Indian Villages, which were without any Inhabitants. \u00a0\u00a0 The bark had been taken off one of those huts in this Village & put into a pond to Soak.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 The frames of these huts were very large.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 We saw large high Mountains which lay on both sides of the River, a small distance back from it.\u2014

\n

\nWe proceeded on about 4 Miles when the River that had been running rapid this day & rough, became quite smooth & gentle. \u00a0\u00a0 The River at this place is wide & strait, & remained so during the whole of this day. \u00a0\u00a0 We passed a creek which lay on the So. side of the River, & a great number of springs & Spring runs flowing from the Clifts & mountains which lay high & fell from off these Clifts & Mountains upwards of 100 feet into the River. \u00a0\u00a0 The high Clifts & rocks lies on both sides of the River. \u00a0\u00a0 The Indians that we had left at the \u2329east\u232a first fall (or rapid) overtook us with their Canoes, which they had carried below the big rapid. \u00a0\u00a0 We saw on board those Indians Canoes 2 of the Indian Men, who were dressed in Scarlet & one of them dressed in blue Cloth all of which appeared to be new. \u00a0\u00a0 Those Indians had a Musket which the Stock was made of Brass & Copper & a Powder flask.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 Towards evening the River got rather wide, & we could scarcely perceive any current running in the River. \u00a0\u00a0 The Clifts and mountains continued back a small distance from the River all this day. \u00a0\u00a0 We passed in the afternoon an Indian Village which lay on the South side of the River. \u00a0\u00a0 We also passed some high towers of Rocks & Narrow bottoms of land which lay along the River on both sides of it. \u00a0\u00a0 The Country this day is Timbered land, the growth Spruce, Pine & some Oaks; & the soil very poor. \u00a0\u00a0 We saw this day in the River, Geese, Swans, Brants & ducks.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 Some of our party shot 16 Geese, which we got. \u00a0\u00a0 We came 21 Miles this day, & encamped under a shelving rock lying on the South side of the River, Our course Continuing West.\u2014\n\n\n

\n
2. This is the area of today's Cascades of the Columbia River, Skamania County, Washington, and Hood River County, Oregon. The rapids are now inundated by Bonneville Dam. The area is discussed in notes for October 30 and 31, 1805. Atlas map 79. (back)
\n
3. Beargrass, Xerophyllum tenax (Pursh) Nutt. Hitchcock et al., 1:812; Gunther (EWW), 23. (back)
\n
4. \n Hamilton Island, Skamania County. The larboard island is Bradford Island, in Hood River County, crossed by Bonneville Dam. The creek mentioned next is probably Hamilton Creek, unnamed on Atlas map 79; it is \"dry run\" on Atlas map 88, and \"Broad run\" or \"Broad Brook\" on figs. 28 and 29 in volume 5. Strawberry is woodland strawberry, Fragaria vesca L. var. crinita (Rydb.) C. L. Hitchc. (back)
\n
5. At the top of this page in the Elkskin-bound Journal. Clark has \"S 58 W.\" Perhaps this is a reminder for which course he is writing up, but it is different than the one given at the start and in the codex journal. \"Beaten Rock\" is Beacon rock, in Beacon Rock State Park, Skamania County. Clark's side estimate is remarkably close, its height is generally given as 840 feet above the river. See note at October 31, 1805. Atlas map 79. (back)
\n
6. The black rocks are basalts of the Miocene-age Columbia River Basalt Group; the point is today's Cape Horn, Skamania County. (back)
\n
7. The brant is Branta bernicla [AOU, 173], while the common goose is the Canada goose, B. canadensis [AOU, 172]. The smaller goose is evidently one of the subspecies of the Canada goose, perhaps the lesser Canada goose, B. canadensis leucopareia [AOU, 172.1], but other subspecies are possible. See above, May 5, 1805; below, March 8, 1806. Burroughs, 192\u201396. (back)
\n
8. In Multnomah County, Oregon, in the vicinity of present Latourell, within Rooster Rock State Park. Atlas maps 79, 88. At the top of the next page after this entry Clark has written \"17,\" a subtotal of the day's mileage. (back)
\n
9. The courses for this entry to November 16 are grouped with the codex entry of November 16, as Clark had them. (back)
\n
10. This sentence has a vertical line drawn through it. (back)
\n
11. Identified on Atlas map 79 as the \"Wah-clallah Tribe of Shahala Nation,\" probably the same people also referred to as the Watlalas, an Upper Chinookan-language people. The term Shahala is from Chinookan \n \u0161\u00e1x\u0323l(a), \"upriver, above,\" and \n \u0161\u00e1x\u0323latk\u0161, \"those upriver.\" These people, who resided along the \"Cascades of the Columbia,\" are often labeled under the general name of Cascades Indians. Like many Chinookan-speaking peoples, this group was greatly reduced by disease later in the nineteenth century, with most of the survivors eventually joining the Wascoes on the Warm Springs Reservation or the Wishrams on the Yakima Reservation. Hodge 2:922; Spier, 21, 24; Berreman, 18\u201319; Hajda, 67, 119\u201320. The village of nine houses was in Skamania County, in the vicinity of present Skamania. Spier & Sapir, 167\u201368, vaguely place several villages of the Cascades Indians in this area. The village observed by Lewis and Clark may correspond with a known archaeological site, where test excavations encountered evidence of late prehistoric occupation. Warren (RSWA), 10\u201311. Prehistoric occupation in the Cascades area has a limited time depth, as all sites investigated to date are situated on landforms created as a result of the Bonneville landslide around 800 years ago. Minor, Toepel, & Beckham; Minor (PH). (back)
\n
12. \n \"Phoca\" or \"Seal rock\" on Atlas map 79. \"Phoca\" is Greek for \"seal.\" Lewis and Clark's name has been restored to this formation, rising about thirty feet in the Columbia River. The name is for the harbor seal, Phoca vitulina richardii. See October 23, 1805. The rocks in this area are basalts of the middle Miocene Yakima Basalt Subgroup of the Columbia River Basalt Group. (back)
\n
13. The white and blue color phases of the snow goose, Chen caerulescens [AOU, 169], described on March 8, 1806. (back)
\n
14. Probably Crown Point, Multnomah County, with its scenic vantage of the Columbia gorge, and today's Vista House atop. (back)
\n
15. Perhaps Tanner Creek, Multnomah County, Oregon. (back)
\n
16. Perhaps scenic Multnomah and other falls in Multnomah County. (back)
\n
17. In the vicinity of Latourell, within Rooster Rock State Park, Multnomah County. (back)
\n
18. \n Branta bernicla.\n (back)
