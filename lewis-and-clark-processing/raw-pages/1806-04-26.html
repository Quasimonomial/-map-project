\n April 26, 1806\n

\n
\n \n

[Lewis]

\n \n
\n Saturday April 26th 1806.\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nThis morning early we set forward and at the distance of three miles entered a low level plain country of great extent. \u00a0\u00a0 here the river hills are low and receede a great distance from the river this low country commence on the S. side of the river about 10 miles below our encampment of last evening. \u00a0\u00a0 these plains are covered with a variety of herbatious plants, grass, and three speceis of shrubs specimines of which I have preserved. [1] \u00a0\u00a0 at the distance of twelve miles we halted near a few willows which afforded us a sufficient quantity of fuel to cook our dinner which consisted of the ballance of the dogs we had purchased yesterday evening and some jirked Elk. \u00a0\u00a0 we were overtaken today by several families of the natives who were traveling up the river with a number of horses; they continued with us much to our annoyance as the day was worm the roads dusty and we could not prevent their horses from crouding in and breaking our order of mach without using some acts of severity which we did not wish to commit. \u00a0\u00a0 after dinner we continued our march through the level plain near the river 16 Ms. and encamped about a mile below three lodges of the Wollah wollah nation, [2] and about 7 Ms. above our encampment of the 19 of October last. \u00a0\u00a0 after we encamped a little Indian boy caught several chubbs with a bone in this form [3] which he substituted for a hook. \u00a0\u00a0 these fish were of about 9 inches long small head large abdomen, small where the tail joined the body, the tail wide long is proportion and forked. \u00a0\u00a0 the back and ventral fins were equadistant from the head and had each 10 bony rays, the fns next the gills nine each and that near the tail 12. \u00a0\u00a0 the upper exceeded the under jaw, the latter is truncate at the extremity and the tonge and pallet are smooth. \u00a0\u00a0 the colour is white on the sides and belley and a blewish brown on the back. \u00a0\u00a0 the iris of the eye is of a silvery colour and puple black.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 we covered ourselves partially this evening from the rain by means of an old tent.\n

\n

\n

\n
Bone Fishhook, April 26, 1806, Codex K, p. 83\n
\n (American Philosophical Society library, \n
used with permission.)

[Clark]

\n \n
\n Saturday April 26th 1806\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nThis morning early we proceeded on and at the distance of three miles entered a low leavel plain Country of great extent. \u00a0\u00a0 here the river hills are low and receed a great distance from the river \u00a0\u00a0 this low Country Comenced on the South Side about 10 miles below our Encampment of the last night, those plains are Covered with a variety of herbatious plants, Grass and 3 Species of Shrubs. \u00a0\u00a0 at the distance of 12 miles halted near Some willows which afforded us a Sufficient quantity of fuel to cook our dinner which Consisted of the ballance of the dogs we had purchased yesterday evening and Some jerked Elk. \u00a0\u00a0 we were over taken to day by Several families of the nativs who were traveling up the river with a Numr. of horses; they Continued with us much to our ennoyance as the day was worm the roads dusty and we Could not prevent their horses Crouding in and breaking our order of March without useing Some acts of Severty which we did not wish to Commit. \u00a0\u00a0 after dinner we Continued our march through a leavel plain near the river 16 miles and encamped about a mile below 3 Lodges of the fritened band of the Wallah wallah nation, and about 7 miles above our encampment of the 19th of Octr. last. \u00a0\u00a0 after we encampd a little Indian boy Cought Several Chubbs with a bone in this form [4] which he Substituted for a hook. \u00a0\u00a0 those fish were of about 9 inches long. \u00a0\u00a0 we Covered our Selves perfectly this evening from the rain by means of an old tent. Saw a Goat and a Small wolf at a distance to day. \u00a0\u00a0 made 28 miles

\n

\n

\n
Bone Fishhook, April 26, 1806, Voorhis No. 3\n

[Ordway]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nSaturday 26th of April 1806. \u00a0\u00a0 we got up our horses. \u00a0\u00a0 took a light breakfast of a little dry Elk meat and Set out \u00a0\u00a0 proced on over a low level Smooth Sandy plain about 12 miles & halted & dined on a little dry Elk meat as we have nothing else. \u00a0\u00a0 the day warm. \u00a0\u00a0 we delayed about 1 hour and proceed. on \u00a0\u00a0 a number of the natives followed us who are mooveing up the river & Some of them are going over the rockey mountn. to kill buffaloe. Saw considerable of Snow on the mountains to the South & S East. \u00a0\u00a0 came 20 odd miles this day & Camped [5] on the bank of the river. \u00a0\u00a0 only small willows to burn &C\u2014\n\n\n

[Gass]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Saturday 26th. \u00a0\u00a0 Last night Capt. Lewis and Capt. Clarke got each a horse, and we set out early, had a fine morning, and proceeded on very well, most of the men having their knapsacks carried on the horses. At noon we halted and took a little of our dried meat, which is the only food we have. At 2 o'clock we continued our journey, and the officers were obliged to go on foot again, to let some of the men ride whose feet were very sore. The country is level and has a most beautiful appearance. On the plains there is a species of clover, [6] as large as any I have seen, and has a large red handsome blossom. The leaves are not quite so large as those of the red clover cultivated in the Atlantic States, but has seven and eight leaves on a branch. We were overtaken and passed by a great number of the natives, with large droves of horses, that look well and are in good order. We travelled about 25 miles and encamped at a small grove of willows.\n\n\n\n

\n
1. No pressed specimens exist from this date. Lewis must have been referring to species he had collected earlier. The three most common shrubs that characterize the shrub-steppe vegetation of the Columbia Basin, the most xeric of all the vegetation types encountered so far, include big sagebrush; rubber, or whitestem gray, rabbitbrush, Chrysothamnus nauseosus (Pall.) Britt. var. albicaulis (Nutt.) Rydb.; and twisted leaf, mountain, or tall green rabbit-brush, C. viscidiflorus (Hook.) Nutt. See also April 30, 1806. Franklin & Dyrness, 211\u201316; Hitchcock et al., 5:128\u201330. (back)
\n
2. This camp does not appear on Atlas maps 75 or 76; it would be in Benton County, Washington, perhaps below or in the vicinity of present Plymouth and opposite the mouth of the Umatilla River. The site conforms with the location of the camp of October 19, 1805. Works by Alexander; Gough (RIP); Lothson & Lindeman (PB); Lothson & Lindeman (PK); Rice & Lindeman; and Wilke & Dalan report surveys or testing in the Plymouth, Washington, area but none of these studies show evidence of the expedition. Alexander's map shows one Indian site very close to the location of this night's camp and he suggests that the village could have been occupied by Umatillas, Cayuses, or Walulas. Alexander, 63\u201367. The village would probably have been at or near a site described by Drucker (ARMR), 11. A brief description of the area as part of an overview of McNary Reservoir is found in Shiner (MR), 197\u2013200, 210\u201317. The most recent and competent archaeological report concerned with the confluence of the Columbia and Umatilla rivers is Schalk (IMD). (back)
\n
3. Here apepars a sketch of the implement in Codex K, p. 83 (fig. 12). A similar drawing is in Codex Q, p. 111, apparently by an unknown copyist. See Moulton (SJ), 196\u201398. The fish was probably a Columbia River chub, Mylocheilus lateralis, a new species. Cutright (LCPN), 426; Burroughs, 265. Biddle was probably the one who drew a line through this passage to nearly the end of the entry. (back)
\n
4. A sketch of the fishing implement in Voorhis No. 3 (fig. 13). (back)
\n
5. In Benton County, Washington, below or near Plymouth, roughly opposite the mouth of the Umatilla River. The captains note that it was seven miles above the camp of October 19, 1805. See their entries for this date. (back)
\n
6. Large-head clover, Trifolium macrocephalum (Pursh) Poiret. This species was collected by Lewis on April 17. The red clover used for comparison is T. pratense L., an introduced European species. (back)
