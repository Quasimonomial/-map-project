\n April 9, 1806\n

\n
\n \n

[Lewis]

\n \n
\n Wednesday April 9th 1806.\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nThis morning early we commenced the operation of reloading our canoes; at 7 A. M. we departed and proceeded on to the Camp of Reubin and Joseph Fields \u00a0\u00a0 they had not killed any game; we made no halt at this place but continued our rout to the Wah-clel-lah Village [1] which is situated on the North side of the river about a mile below the beacon rock; here we halted and took breakfast. John Colter one of our party observed the tomehawk in one of the lodges which had been stolen from us on the 4th of November last as we decended this river; the natives attempted to wrest the tomahawk from him but he retained it. \u00a0\u00a0 they indeavoured afterwards to exculpate themselves from the odium of having stolen it, they alledged that they had bought it from the natives below; but their neighbours had several days previously, informed us that these people had stolen the Tommehawk and then had it at their village. \u00a0\u00a0 this village appears to be the winter station of the Wah-clel-lahs and Clahclellars, [2] the greater part of the former have lately removed to the falls of the Multnomah, and the latter have established themselves a few miles above on the North side of the river opposite the lower point of brant island, [3] being the commencement of the rapids, here they also take their salmon; they are now in the act of removing, and not only take with them their furniture and effects but also the bark and most of the boards which formed their houses. \u00a0\u00a0 14 houses remain entire but are at this time but thinly inhabited, nine others appear to have been lately removed, and the traces of ten or twelve others of ancient date were to be seen in the rear of their present village. \u00a0\u00a0 they sometimes sink their houses in the earth, and at other times have their floors level with the surface of the earth; they are generally built with boards and covered with Cedar bark. \u00a0\u00a0 most of them have a devision in their houses near the entrance wich is at the end or in the event of it's bing a double house is from the center of a narrow passage. \u00a0\u00a0 several families inhabit one appartment. \u00a0\u00a0 the women of these people piece the cartelage of the nose in which they wear various ornaments in other rispects they do not differ from those in the neighbourhood of the Diamond island; tho' most of the women brad their hair which hanges in two tresses one hanging over each ear. \u00a0\u00a0 these people were very unfriendly, and seemed illy disposed had our numbers not detered them any acts of violence. \u00a0\u00a0 with some difficuly we obtained five dogs from them and a few wappetoe. \u00a0\u00a0 on our way to this village we passed several beautifull cascades which fell from a great hight over the stupendious rocks which cloles the river on both sides nearly, except a small bottom on the South side in which our hunters were encamped. the most remarkable of these casscades falls about 300 feet perpendicularly over a solid rock into a narrow bottom of the river on the south side. [4] \u00a0\u00a0 it is a large creek, situated about 5 miles above our encampment of the last evening. \u00a0\u00a0 several small streams fall from a much greater hight, and in their decent become a perfect mist which collecting on the rocks below again become visible and decend a second time in the same manner before they reach the base of the rocks. \u00a0\u00a0 the hills have now become mountains high on each side are rocky steep and covered generally with fir and white cedar. \u00a0\u00a0 we saw some turkey buzzards [5] this morning of the speceis common to the United states which are the first we have seen on this side the rocky mountains. \u00a0\u00a0 during our halt at this village the grand Cheif and two inferior Cheifs of the Chil-luck-kit-te-quaw\n [6] nation arrived with several men and women of their nation in two large canoes. \u00a0\u00a0 these people were on their return up the river, having been on a trading voyage to the Columbean vally, and were loaded with wappetoe dryed anchovies, with some beads &c which they had received in exchange for dryed and pounded salmon shappelell beargrass &c. [7] These people had been very kind to us as we decended the river we therefore smoked with them and treated them with every attention. \u00a0\u00a0 at 2 P. M. we renewed our voyage; passed under the beacon rock on the north side, to the left of two small islands situated near the shore. [8] \u00a0\u00a0 at four P.M. we arrived at the Clah-clel-lah village; here we found the natives busily engaged in erecting their new habitations, which appear to be reather of a temperary kind; it is most probable that they only reside here during the salmon season. \u00a0\u00a0 we purchased two dogs of these people who like those of the village blow were but sulky and illy disposed; they are great rogues and we are obliged to keep them at a proper distance from our baggage. \u00a0\u00a0 as we could not ascend the rapid by the North side of the river with our large canoes, we passed to the oposite side and entered the narrow channel which seperates brant Island from the South shore; the evening being far spent and the wind high raining and very cold we thought best not to attempt the rapids this evening, we therefore sought a safe harbour in this narrow channel and encamped on the main shore. [9] \u00a0\u00a0 our small canoe with Drewyer and the two feildses was unable to pass the river with us in consequence of the waves they therefore toed her up along the N. side of the river and encamped opposite the upper point of brant Island. [10] \u00a0\u00a0 after halting this evening I took a turn with my gun in order to kill a deer, but was unsuccessful. I saw much fresh sign. \u00a0\u00a0 the fir has been lately injured by a fire near this place and many of them have discharged considerable quantities of rozin. \u00a0\u00a0 we directed that Collins should hunt a few hours tomorrow morning and that Gibson and his crew should remain at his place untill we returned and employ themselves in collectng rozin which our canoes are now in want of.\n

[Clark]

\n \n
\n Wednesday April 9th 1806\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nlast night at a late hour the old amsiated [emaciated?] Indian who was detected in Stealing a Spoon yesterday, Crept upon his belley with his hands and feet, with a view as I Suppose to take Some of our baggage which was in Several defferent parcels on the bank. \u00a0\u00a0 the Sentinal observed the motions of this old amcinated retch untill he got with a fiew feet of the baggage at he hailed him and approached with his gun in a possion as if going to Shoote which allarmed the old retch in Such a manner that he ran with all his power tumbleing over brush and every thing in his way. \u00a0\u00a0 at 7 A. M. we Set out and proceeded on to the Camp of Joseph & Reubin Fields. \u00a0\u00a0 they had killed nothing. \u00a0\u00a0 here we did not delay but proceeded on to \n Wah-clel-lah\n Village on the North Side and brackfast \u00a0\u00a0 here one the men Colter observed the Tomahawk which was Stolen from on the 4th of Novr. last as we decended the Columbia, he took the tomahawk \u00a0\u00a0 the natives attempted to wrest it from him, he held fast the Tomahawk. Those people attempted to excuse themselves from odium of Stealing it, by makeing Signs that they had purchased the Tomahawk, but their nighbours informed me otherwise and made Signs that they had taken it. This Village appears to be the wintering Station of two bands of the \n Shah-ha-la\n Nation. One band has already moved the Falls of the Multnomah which is the place they take their Salmon. The other band is now moveing a fiew miles above to the foot of the first rapid on this river, at which place they take their Salmon. \u00a0\u00a0 14 houses only appear occupied and the inhabitants of those moveing off hourly, they take with them in their Canoes independent of all their household effects the bark of their houses, and boards. \u00a0\u00a0 9 houses has been latterly abandened and 14 others is yet is thinly inhabited at present, and the remains of 10 or 12 others are to be Seen and appears to have been enhabited last fall. \u00a0\u00a0 those people were not hospital and with Some dificuelty we precured 5 dogs and a fiew Wappato of them. Soon after we arived at this Village the Grand Cheif and two others of the \n Chee-luck-it-te-quar\n Nation arived from below. \u00a0\u00a0 they had with them 11 men and 7 womin and had been trading in the Columbia Vally for Wappato, beeds, and dried Anchovies &c in exchange for which they had given pounded fish Shappalell, bear grass, acorns boiled berries &c. &c. and are now on their return to their village. \u00a0\u00a0 as those people had been very Kind to us as we decended the river we gave them Smoke. \u00a0\u00a0 at 2 oClock P. M we Set out and passed under the Beacon rock on the North Side of two Small Islds. Situated nearest the N. side. \u00a0\u00a0 at 4 P. M. we arived at the first rapid at the head of Straw berry island\n [11] at which place on the N W. Side of the Columbia here we found the nativs from the last village rebuilding their habitations of the bark of \u2329from\u232a their old Village \u00a0\u00a0 16 Huts are already Compleated and appear only temporrary \u00a0\u00a0 it is most probable that they only reside here \u2329in\u232a dureing the Season of the Salmon. \u00a0\u00a0 as we Could not pass with the large Canoes up the N. W. Side for the rocks, the wind high and a rainey disagreeable evining. \u00a0\u00a0 our Smallest Canoe being too low to cross through the high waves, we Sent her up on the N W. side with Drewyer and the two Fields and after purchaseing 2 dogs Crossed and into the Sluce of a large high Island seperated from the S. E Side by a narrow chanel, in this chanel we found a good harbor and encamped on the lower Side. We Saw Some deer Sign and Collins to hunt in the mornig untill the Canoes were toed above the rapids. \u00a0\u00a0 made 16 Miles to day. evening wet & disagreeable.\n\n

[Ordway]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nWednesday 9th of April 1806. \u00a0\u00a0 a fair morning and calm \u00a0\u00a0 we bailed our canoes \u00a0\u00a0 found Some of them to leak \u00a0\u00a0 loaded up & about 9 oClock we departed and proceed on along the South Shore \u00a0\u00a0 overtook the 2 Fields who had killed nothing \u00a0\u00a0 about noon Some of the men killed an eagle. \u00a0\u00a0 we crossed over to the North Side & halted at a village of the wa-cla-lah nation [12] where we bought 5 or 6 fat dogs. \u00a0\u00a0 found Capt. Clarks pipe tommahawk\nwhich was Stole from him last fall, below Quick Sand River. \u00a0\u00a0 we took it from them. \u00a0\u00a0 they Signd. that they bought it below and appeared to be highly afronted at our taking it but were afraid to Show it \u00a0\u00a0 a number of these natives are moveing up to the big Shoote\n [13] to fish &C \u00a0\u00a0 a number of an other nation overtook us who belong up near the big falls [14] &C \u00a0\u00a0 a large creek [15] puts in close above the village which we did not discover last fall. \u00a0\u00a0 when we passd. down \u00a0\u00a0 we dined and proceed. on \u00a0\u00a0 passd Strabury Island\n [16] where the Swift water begins. \u00a0\u00a0 we halted at a village at the foot of the 1st rapid, on N. Side which was lately erected. \u00a0\u00a0 we purchased 2 fat dogs and crossed over to the South Shore and Camped [17] behind [blank] Island \u00a0\u00a0 commenced raining hard & high winds from N. W. \u00a0\u00a0 the River much higher at this time than it was last fall when we passd. down. Some Spots of Snow is now on the tops of these Mountains Near the River.\n\n\n

\n \n

[Gass]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Wednesday 9th. \u00a0\u00a0 The morning was plesant; we therefore loaded our canoes and proceeded on till 11 o'clock when we stopped at a large Indian village on the north side; but a number of the huts were unoccupied. They are of the Al-e-is nation. At the time we halted 3 canoe-loads of them were setting out for the falls to fish. We took breakfast here and bought 5 dogs from them. The women all wear the small leather bandage, but are quite naked otherwise, except what is covered by the small robe they wear round their shoulders. In the afternoon the weather became cloudy and some rain fell. In the evening we came to a large rapid at the lower end of Strawberry island; where there are a number of the natives about settling on the north side. [18] Here we crossed over, after buying two dogs from them, and encamped behind the island. Some rain continued falling.\n\n\n

1. Probably the Upper Chinookan-language people known as the Watlalas. See November 2, 1805, and Estimate of Western Indians. Atlas map 79. The term is a variation of Watlala or wal\\\u00e1la, designating the village. Silverstein, 535. (back)
\n
2. A branch of the Watlala Chinookans. See Estimate of Western Indians. The party visited their village of \"4 large houses\" on October 31, 1805, but did not apply a name to the people. Atlas map 79. The term is \n l\\\u00e1-l\\ala, \"those of Wal\\\u00e1la village.\" (back)
\n
3. Present Bradford Island, Multnomah County, Oregon, the site of Bonneville Dam. It is nameless on Atlas map 79. See figures. Archaeological work on the island was limited to brief excavations in 1934. Phebus, 116\u201324; Minor, Toepel, & Beckham (Rev), 15\u201318. (back)
\n
4. Either Multnomah Falls, formed by Multnomah Creek, or Horsetail Falls, formed by Horsetail Creek, both in Multnomah County. Three little unnamed streams are shown falling into the Columbia on Atlas map 79, with the word \"Cascades\" written to the side. (back)
\n
5. Turkey vulture, Cathartes aura [AOU, 325]. Burroughs, 203\u20134; Holmgren, 28. (back)
\n
6. The term was evidently mistaken as a tribal designation when it may actually be Upper Chinookan \n \u010d-i-l-kt\u00ed-gwa-x, \"he is pointing at him.\" They would be the people known as Wishram-Wasco Indians (see October 27, 1805). (back)
\n
7. Beargrass, Xerophyllum tenax (Pursh) Nutt. Hitchcock et al., 1:812. (back)
\n
8. The islands are Pierce and Ives islands, opposite Beacon Rock, Skamania County, Washington. Atlas map 79. (back)
\n
9. In Multnomah County, at or near present Bonneville. Atlas map 79. (back)
\n
10. In Skamania County, in the eastern part of North Bonneville. Atlas map 79. (back)
\n
11. Present Hamilton Island, in Skamania County. Atlas map 79. (back)
\n
12. \n Watlala Chinookans, whose village was in the vicinity of Skamania, Skamania County,\nWashington, which the group visited on November 2, 1805. (back)
\n
13. \n Cascades of the Columbia River. (back)
\n
14. \n Celilo Falls. (back)
\n
15. Perhaps Woodward Creek, Skamania County. (back)
\n
16. \n Hamilton Island, Skamania County. (back)
\n
17. The party camped on shore behind Bradford Island, site of Bonneville Dam, Multnomah County, Oregon. (back)
\n
18. Designated by the captains as Clahclellahs, a branch of the Watlalas. (back)
