\n \n September 18th Tuesday 1804\n

\n

\nWind from the N W. \u00a0\u00a0 we Set out early the boat much lightened, the wind a head proceed on verry Slowly \u00a0\u00a0 (1) Passed an I a Island about the middle of the river at 1 Mile \u00a0\u00a0 this Island is about a mile long, and has a great perpotion of red Cedir on it, a Small Creek comes in on the S. S. opposit the head of the Island, [2] proceeded on \u00a0\u00a0 passed many Sand bars and Camped on the L. S. [3] \u00a0\u00a0 before night the wind being verry hard & a head all Day. \u00a0\u00a0 the hunters Killed 10 Deer to day and a Prarie wolf, had it all jurked & Skins Stretchd after Camping

\n

\nI walked on Shore Saw Goats, Elk, Buffalow, Black tail Deer, & the Common Deer, I Killed a Prarie Wollf, about the Size of a gray fox bushey tail head & ear like a wolf, Some fur \u00a0\u00a0 Burrows in the ground and barks like a Small Dog.

\n

\nwhat has been taken heretofore for the Fox was those wolves, and no Foxes has been Seen; The large wolves are verry numourous, they are of a light Colr. large & has long hair with Corrs [X: Coarse] fur. [4]\n

\n

\nSome Goats of a Different Kind Wer Seen yesterday \u00a0\u00a0 Great many Porcupin rabits & Barking Squirils in this quarter. \u00a0\u00a0 Plumbs & grapes.\n

\n Tuesday 18th Sept. \u00a0\u00a0 a fair morning. \u00a0\u00a0 we Set off at \u00bd past 5 o.C. \u00a0\u00a0 proceeded on, passed an Island at our wright \u00a0\u00a0 Some Timber along the S. S. Elm, ash, Scrubby oak &.C\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 George Drewyer killed a prarie woolf Some larger than a fox. \u00a0\u00a0 long teeth & of a different discription from any in the States &.C. \u00a0\u00a0 we proceeded on to a Bottom prarie covered with thin cottonwood Timber where Jo Fields with the horse had killed a Buck Deer\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 took on board the meat killed yesterday\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 Capt. Clark & G. Drewyer walked on Short on S. S. \u00a0\u00a0 no Timber seen by the hunters back from the river\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 Capt. Clark & G. Drewyer returned towards evening. \u00a0\u00a0 had killed 10 Deer & one prarie woolf \u00a0\u00a0 We Camped on the South Side in a Small Grove of Timbers, 2 hours eairlier than usal the wind being a head, in order to jurk our meat &.C \u00a0\u00a0 the Bones of the woolf was taken apart and Saved as well as the Skins of them boath in order to Send back to the States next Spring, with the other curiousities we have or may have &.C.\u2014\n\n

[Gass]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Tuesday 18th. \u00a0\u00a0 We continued our voyage; the day was clear and pleasant: passed some timber land on the south side, and hills and prairies on the north; also an island and a great number of sand bars. Yesterday captain Lewis while hunting killed a bird [6] not common in the states: it is like a magpie and is a bird of prey. This day we killed eleven deer and a wolf, and halted and encamped on the south side of the river in order to jirk our meat.\n\n

\n Tuesday 18th Sept. \u00a0\u00a0 we Set off from camp pleasant \u00a0\u00a0 a clear day. \u00a0\u00a0 passed timbered land on the S. S. \u00a0\u00a0 hills and prarie on the N. S. \u00a0\u00a0 passd. an Isd. and a Great nomber of Sand bars. \u00a0\u00a0 Capt. Lewis in his yesterdays hunt killed a bird not common in the States a bird of pray resembling the Europian magpy. \u00a0\u00a0 \u2329as\u232a Capt. Clark\n Drewyer & jo Fields killed 11 deer and one wolf \u00a0\u00a0 we Camped before night in order to jerk our meat on the S. Side \u00a0\u00a0 Fields did not join us this night.\u2014

\n

\nSeptember 18th Tuesday \u00a0\u00a0 We set off from Pleasant Camp\n [7] early this morning, having Clear & pleasant weather; we proceeded on, and passed by fine timber'd land, lying on the South side of the River, the land on the North side being hilly Priaries. \u00a0\u00a0 we met with a great number of Sand Barrs, and an Island lying on the North side of the River, Captain Lewis kill'd in his hunt Yesterday, a bird, which is uncommon in the United States \u00a0\u00a0 It was a Bird of Prey, and had some resemblance of the Magpie.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 The hunters who had went out early this Morning return'd to us; having killed 11 deer, and one Wolf, which was brought to us. \u00a0\u00a0 We encamped in the afternoon on the South side of the River, in order to Jerk the meat which had been kill'd these two days,\n\n\n

1. Here and in his Codex B entry Clark gives a brief scientific description of the coyote, one of the most widespread and characteristic mammals of the Great Plains. Lewis gives a more detailed description on May 5, 1805. Cutright (LCPN), 85. (back)
\n
2. The island was the later American, or Cedar, Island, and the creek is American Creek, at the site of present Chamberlain, Brule County, South Dakota. Atlas maps 20, 21, 22; MRC map 37. The fort shown on these maps as \"Mr. Manuel's Fort\" and \"Cedar Fort,\" the site of which they passed a little below Cedar Island, was not there in 1804. It was one of the posts built by Manuel Lisa, probably the \"Cedar Fort\" his Missouri Fur Company established in 1809 to trade with the Sioux, which burned in 1810. The Lisa post has been thought to be either here or at the same site as R\u00e9gis Loisel's\n Cedar Fort, or Fort aux Cedres (see below, September 22, 1804); the Maximilian maps suggest that the site here, in Lyman County, South Dakota, just below present Chamberlain, is correct. The notations on Maximilian's map were probably on the basis of later information inserted on Clark's originals. Mattes, 522\u201328; Oglesby, 83, 97; Chittenden, 1:145, 2:952\u201353; MRC map 37. (back)
\n
3. In Lyman County, a few miles northeast of present Oacoma. Atlas maps 20, 21; MRC map 37. (back)
\n
4. The \"large wolves\" are gray wolves. References to \"prairie wolves\" in the journals are probably to coyotes. Cutright (LCPN), 85; Burroughs, 88. (back)
\n
5. \n Lewis's natural history notes from Codex Q. The brant is probably the Canada goose, Branta canadensis [AOU, 172]. See the Weather Diary, March 1804. (back)
\n
6. Black-billed magpie, Pica pica.\n (back)
\n
7. \n \"Pleasant Camp\" appears to be written over an erasure. (back)
