\n 7th of October Sunday 1804 \u00a0\u00a0 frost last night, passed a River 90 yds. wide the \n Ricaras\n Call \n Sur-war-kat-ne\n \n [1] \u00a0\u00a0 all the water of this river runs in a chanel of 20 yards, the Current appears jentle, I walked up this River a mile, Saw the tracks of white bear, verry large, also a old Ricara village partly burnt, fortified \u00a0\u00a0 about 60 Lodges built in the Same form of those passed yesterday, many Canoes & Baskets about the huts\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 about 10 oclock we Saw 2 Indians on the S. S. \u00a0\u00a0 they asked \u2329cours\u232a for Something to eat & told us they were Tetons of the band we left below on ther way to the \n Ricaras\n we gave them meat & wind hard from the South, passed a large open Island covered with grass and wild rye, I walked on the Isd & 4 men they \u2329our\u232a Killed a Braroe & a Black tale Doe with a black breast, the largest Deer I ever saw, the great numbers of Grous on it, we call it Grous Island, [2] Camped opposit the Island near the S. Side. [3]\n

7th of October Sunday 1804\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\na Cloudy morning, Some little rain frost last night, we Set out early proceeded on 2 \u2329\u00bd\u232a miles to the mouth of a (1) river on the L. S. and brackfast \u00a0\u00a0 this river whin full is 90 yards wide \u00a0\u00a0 the water is at this time Confined within 20 yards, the Current appears jentle, this river throws out but little Sand \u00a0\u00a0 at the mouth of this river we Saw the Tracks of White bear which was verry large, I walked up this river a mile\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 below the (2) mouth of this river, is the remains of a Rickorrie Village or Wintering Camp fortified in a circular form of a bout 60 Lodges, built in the Same form of those passed yesterday \u00a0\u00a0 This Camp appears to have been inhabited last winter, many of their willow & Straw mats, Baskets & Buffalow Skin Canoes remain intire within the Camp, \u2329we passed\u232a the Ricares Call this river \n Sur-war-kar-na\n or Park [NB: Rr.] [4] \u00a0\u00a0 from this river [NB: which heads in the 1st black mountains\n ] we proceeded on under a gentle Breeze from the S. W. \u00a0\u00a0 at 10 oClock we Saw 2 Indians, on the S. S. \u00a0\u00a0 they asked for Something to eate, & informed us they were part of the Beiffs De Medisons [NB: Beuffles de Medecines] Lodge on their way to the Rickerreis, passed (3) a willow Island in a bind to the S. S. \u00a0\u00a0 (4) at 5 miles passd. a willow Island on the S. S.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 wind hard from the South \u00a0\u00a0 in the evening I walked on an (5) Island nearly the middle of the river Called \u2329Shaved\u232a Grous Island, [NB: (the wall of a village on this island)] one of the men killed a Shee Brarrow, another man killed a Black tail Deer, the largest Doe I ever Saw (Black under her breast[)] \u00a0\u00a0 this Island is nearly 1\u00bc ms. Squar no timbr high and Covered with grass wild rye and Contains Great numbers of Grouse, we proceeded on a Short distance above the Island and Camped on the S. S. \u00a0\u00a0 a fine evening.

[Ordway]

\n Sunday 7th Oct. 1804. \u00a0\u00a0 a clear & pleasant morning. We Set off at day light. \u00a0\u00a0 proceeded on \u00a0\u00a0 passed a creek [5] on N. S. called [blank] \u00a0\u00a0 halted took breakfast at a River [6] named [blank] where their was an old Rickree village built in the Same Manner as that we passd yesterday on S. S. \u00a0\u00a0 passd a timbered Bottom on S. S. abo. mo. of this River \u00a0\u00a0 a Small Shower of rain \u00a0\u00a0 the wind more from the S. Sailed on \u00a0\u00a0 Saw 2 of the Souix Indians on N. S. Spoke to\nthem \u00a0\u00a0 they Sd. they wanted Something to eat & that their band was a going up to the Rickrees, we Gave them Some Venison & proceeded on to an Island about 4 oC. I went out with Capt. Clark & 2 men hunters on Sd. Island to hunt. \u00a0\u00a0 we killed a Black tailed Deer which was verry large especially the Ears & a handsome Brarow which Capts. had the Bones & skin Saved in order to Send back to the States. \u00a0\u00a0 we Camped on N. S. abo. the head of Sd. Isl. where I came on board\n\n\n

[Gass]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Sunday 7th. \u00a0\u00a0 We set forward early and had a clear day: passed a willow bottom on the south side, and a creek on the north. At the beginning of some timber land we passed a small river on the south side, called Cer-wer-cer-na, about 90 yards wide. It is not so sandy as the Missouri, and the water is clear, with a deep channel. At the mouth of this river is a wintering camp of the Rickarees of 60 lodges. We saw two Sioux Indians on the north side, gave them some meat and proceeded on. We passed an island, on which Captain Clarke and one of the men went to hunt and killed a deer and a prarow. We encamped on the north side opposite the head of the island.\n\n\n

\nSunday October 7th \u00a0\u00a0 This morning we had clear weather. \u00a0\u00a0 We set out Early and passed a Creek lying on the North side of the River, and a small River lying on the South side of the same, this River is called Sirawa, and is about 70 Yards wide at its Mouth.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 At the Mouth of this River \u2329is\u232a We saw, a Wintering Camp of the Rick a Rees Indians, containing about 60 lodges; We saw two of the Souix Indians on the North side of the River. \u00a0\u00a0 Captain Clark went on Shore on the South side of the River, & killed one deer and a brarerow which was brought on board. \u00a0\u00a0 We encamped on the North side of the River, opposite the head of an Island.\u2014\n\n\n

1. The Moreau River reaches the Missouri in Dewey County, South Dakota. The Arikara village appears on Atlas map 25. Mattison (OR), 78; MRC map 44. (back)
\n
2. \n Grouse Island is the later Blue Blanket Island, between Dewey and Walworth counties, South Dakota; it is now inundated by Oahe Reservoir. Clark noted the walls of an abandoned village on the island on Atlas map 25, but did not comment on it in the text, although there is an interlineation reference in the codex journal. The village appears to have been a short-lived Arikara site probably occupied during the 1780s to 1790s. Stephenson. The grouse are probably the sharp-tailed grouse. Burroughs, 211\u201312; Cutright (LCPN), 81. (back)
\n
3. Just above Blue Blanket (Grouse) Island, in Walworth County, near the present town of Mobridge. Mattison (OR), 85; Atlas map 25; MRC map 45. (back)
\n
4. The text of the entry is interrupted here by the courses and distances; the two parts are brought together for ease of reading. (back)
