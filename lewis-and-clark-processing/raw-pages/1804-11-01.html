\n November 1, 1804\n

\n
\n \n

[Clark]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n 1 November 1804 \u00a0\u00a0 Visited by Several Chiefs of the lower Village who requested we would call on them &c. \u00a0\u00a0 Spoke to the Same purpote [purport] with the Grand Chief. \u00a0\u00a0 we Set out in the evening & I with the Party droped down to the place we intended to winter [1] & Cap Lewis called at the Village 3 miles above &. &.

\n \n
\n
\n \n

[Clark]

\n \n
1st of November Thursday 1804
\u00a0
\n \n

\nthe wind hard from the N W. \u00a0\u00a0 Mr. McCrackin a Trader Set out at 7 oClock to the fort on the Ossiniboin \u00a0\u00a0 by him Send a letter, (incloseing a Copy of the British Ministers protection) [2] to the principal agent of the Company\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 at about 10 OClock the Cheifs of the Lower Village Cam and after a Short time informed us they wished they would us to call at their village & take Some Corn, that they would make peace with the \n Ricares\n \u00a0\u00a0 they never made war against them but after the \n rees\n Killed their Chiefs they killed them like the birds, and were tired [NB: of killing them] and would Send a Chief and Some brave men to the \n Ricares\n to Smoke with that people \u00a0\u00a0 in the evening we Set out and fell down to the lower Village where Capt. Lewis got out and continud at the Village untill after night \u00a0\u00a0 I proceeded on & landed on the S. S. at the upper point of the 1st Timber on the Starboard Side after landing & Continuinge\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 \u2329Some\u232a all night droped down to a proper place to build [3] \u00a0\u00a0 Capt Lewis Came down after night, and informed me he intended to return the next morning by the perticular Request of the Chiefs.

\n

\nWe passed the Villages on our Decent in veiw of Great numbers of the inhabitents

\n \n
\n
\n \n

[Clark]

\n \n
The 1st of Novr. \n Mandins 1s Village\n [4]\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nthe Main Chief Big White & 2 others i e the Big Man or \n Sha-ha-ca\n and [blank] Came early to talk, and Spoke as follows, after Smoking, Viz.

\n

\nIs it Certain that the ricares intend to make good with us \u00a0\u00a0 our wish is to be at peace with all, we will Send a Chief with the pania Chief and Some young men to Smoke and make good peace\u2014? \u00a0\u00a0 are you going to Stay abov or below this Cold [season?].\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 answer by C. L \u00a0\u00a0 We are going down a few miles to look a place \u00a0\u00a0 we can find no place abov proper.

\n

\nThe panias know's we do not begin the war, they allway begin, we Sent a Chief and a pipe to the Pania to Smoke and they killed them\u2014, we have killed enough of them \u00a0\u00a0 we kill them like the birds, we do not wish to kill more, we will, make a good peace

\n

\nWe were Sorry when we heard of your going up but now you are going down, we are glad, if we eat you Shall eat, if we Starve you must Starve also, \u00a0\u00a0 our village is too far to bring the Corn to you, but we hope you will Call on us as you pass to the place you intend to Stop

\n

\n \n C[aptain] L[ewis] answered the above\u2014!

\n \n
\n
\n \n

[Lewis]

\n \n
\n Thursday November 1st 1804\n [5]\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nThe wind blew so violently during the greater part of this day that we were unable to quit our encampment; in the evening it abated;\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 we droped down about seven miles and land on N. E. side of the river at a large point of Woodland. \n\n\n

\n \n
\n
\n \n

[Ordway]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Thursday 1st November 1804. \u00a0\u00a0 a clear & [illegible] morning \u00a0\u00a0 the wind high from the N. W., cool \u00a0\u00a0 at 3 oClock P. M. we Set off to return a Short distance down the River in order to find a Good place for winters quarters. \u00a0\u00a0 the wind abated. But the River So Shallow the we Struck the Sand bars. Capt. Lewis myself and Several more of the party halted at the 1st village of the Mandens in order to git Some corn. \u00a0\u00a0 the head chief told us that they had not Got the\ncorn ready. But if we would come tomorrow they [w]ould have it ready. \u00a0\u00a0 they Gave us 3 kinds of victuls to eat which was verry Good. \u00a0\u00a0 they were verry friendly Gave the pipe round everry fiew minutes &.C. \u00a0\u00a0 they live verry well. \u00a0\u00a0 have pleanty of corn Beans Squashes meat &.C. Capt. Lewis told the chief that he would come again tomorrow. \u00a0\u00a0 then we went on abt. 2 miles down to a Bottom covered with Timber, where we Camped on the N. Side of the Missouris River.\n\n

\n \n
\n
\n \n

[Gass]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Thursday 1st Nov. 1804. \u00a0\u00a0 At 3 o'clock P. M. we returned down the river, \nto look for a place where we could fix our winter quarters. At dark we had descended 9 miles, and came to a bottom covered with cotton wood, where we encamped. [6]\n

\n \n
\n
\n \n

[Whitehouse]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nNovember thur. 1st 1804. [7] \u00a0\u00a0 the wind blew So fresh from the South that \nwe Could not Get Under Way \u2329In\u232a at the time appointed as the Officers Intended falling down the [crossed out, illegible] the river before two Oclock P. M., to the place that they wishd. to build a fort. \u00a0\u00a0 at dark we arivd. with the boat and Peirouges 9\u00bd Miles \u2329bel\u232a Below the 2nd vilage of Mandans at a piece of woods On the N. E. Side whare we Commend. building the fort.\u2014 [8]\n

\n

\nNovember 1st Thursday \u00a0\u00a0 This morning, we had the Wind blowing hard from the South, that we could not set off at the time appointed; our Officers having agreed on, to return down the River, before 2 o'Clock P. M., to a \nplace that they had concluded on, to build a Fort; at 3 oClock we set of, and arrived at the place at dark; with the boat and Pettyaugers. \u00a0\u00a0 This place lies 9\u00bd Miles below the 2nd village of the Mandan Nation, and \u2329is\u232a lay in a piece of Woodland, lying on the North side of the River Mesouri, and lies in Latitude 47\u00b0 21' North.\u2014\n\n\n

\n \n
\n \n \n
\n
1. This passage is misleading; as Clark indicates in the Codex C entry, the party did not go as far as the future site of Fort Mandan this day. The camp is not shown on Atlas map 29, but was between, and somewhat north of Matootonha, the lower Mandan village, and the fort site, on the McLean County, North Dakota, side (starboard). Note how the river bends between the two places on the Atlas map. MRC map 52. (back)
\n
2. \n Edward Thornton, later Sir Edward, entered the British diplomatic service in 1791. He served in various posts in the United States from then until 1804, being charg\u00e9 d'affaires and acting minister in Washington from 1800. In February, 1803, he issued Lewis a passport, requesting all subjects of His Majesty to permit Lewis to pass and to render all aid and protection possible, on a mission he declared to be purely scientific. Lewis's British Passport [February 28, 1803], Jackson (LLC), 1:19\u201320. (back)
\n
3. \n Clark here refers to events of November 2, indicating that the Codex C entry of November 1 was composed later. (back)
\n
4. This transcript is on a loose sheet in the Voorhis Collection, Missouri Historical Society. See Indian Speeches, Miscellaneous Documents of Lewis and Clark, Appendix C. The talk is covered in Ronda (LCAI), 87\u201388. (back)
\n
5. \n Lewis's note from Codex O, though not concerned with astronomy. (back)
\n
6. In McLean County, North Dakota, a little north of Mitutanka village. (back)
\n
7. Here resumes in the original version the daily entries, now in the hand of No. 2. (back)
\n
8. They did not actually reach the Fort Mandan site until the next day. The camp for this day was between that site and Mitutanka village, McLean County, North Dakota. (back)
