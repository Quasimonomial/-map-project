\n October 21, 1804\n

\n
\n \n

[Clark]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n 21t of October Sunday 1804 \u00a0\u00a0 a verry Cold night \u00a0\u00a0 wind hard from the N. E. \u00a0\u00a0 Some rain in the night which feesed [froze] as it fell, at Day began to Snow and Continued all the fore part of the day, at \u00bc of a mile passed the Mouth of Chess-che tar (or Heart) River L. S. 38 yards wide, this river heads near Turtle mountain with Knife River\n [1] \u00a0\u00a0 on this River is a Smothe Stone which the Indians have great fath in & Consult the Stone on all great occasions which they Say Marks or Simblems [symbol + emblems] are left on the Stone of what is to \u2329pass\u232a take place &c. \u00a0\u00a0 an old mandan Village above the mouth of this Little River, [2] I saw a Single tree in the open Plains which the Mandans formerly paid great Devotion to \u00a0\u00a0 run Cords thro their flesh & tie themselves to the tree to make them brave, [3] \u00a0\u00a0 passed an old Village on a Small run on the S S. \u00a0\u00a0 one on the bank L. and Camped, [4] I Killed a fat Buffalow this evening\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 Little gun all my hunting [5]\n

S. Called by the Indians \n Chiss-Cho-tar\n \u00a0\u00a0 this river is about 38 yards wide Containing a good Deel of water \u00a0\u00a0 Some Distance up this River is Situated a Stone which the Indians have great fath in & Say they See painted on the Stone, [\"]all the Calemites & good fortune to hapin the nation & partes who visit it\"\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 a tree (an oak[)] which Stands alone near this place about 2 miles off in the open prarie which has with Stood the fire they pay Great respect to, make Holes and tie Strings thro the Skins of their necks \u2329Skin\u232a\nand around this tree to make them brave [NB: Capt. Clarke saw this tree] (all this is the information of Too ne is a whipper will) the Chief of the Ricares who accompanied us to the Mandins, at 2 miles (2) passed the 2nd Villages of the Manden, which was in existance at the Same time with the 1st \u00a0\u00a0 this village is at the foot of a hill on the S. S. on a butifull & extensive plain [NB: nearly opposite is another village in a bottom the other side of Missouri\n ]\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 at this time Covered with Buffalow\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 a Cloudy afternoon, I killed a fine Buffalow, we Camped on the L. S. \u00a0\u00a0 [NB: below an old Mandan village having passed another up a Creek 3 miles below on S. S] \u00a0\u00a0 verry Cold ground Covered with Snow. \u00a0\u00a0 one orter Killd. [6]\n

[Ordway]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Sunday 21st Oct. Some frozen rain last night \u00a0\u00a0 Snow this morning. \u00a0\u00a0 the wind from N. E. \u00a0\u00a0 we Set off as usal. \u00a0\u00a0 proceeded on \u00a0\u00a0 the current Swift. \u00a0\u00a0 we passed the mo. of a River called Chifschetar River [7] on S. S. Snowed Slowly untill 12 oClock. \u00a0\u00a0 passd Bottom on S. S. \u00a0\u00a0 barron hills on the N. S. on the River & back from the [river] is Smoth handsom plains. \u00a0\u00a0 about 3 o.C. Capt Clark & one hunter went out hunting on N. S. & in a Short time they Shot a Bull buffaloe which was Tollorable fat. \u00a0\u00a0 one of the hunters Shot an otter. \u00a0\u00a0 a cool & chilley day \u00a0\u00a0 We Camped on the S. S. at a high prarie.\u2014\n\n\n

[Gass]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Sunday 21st. \u00a0\u00a0 We had a disagreeable night of sleet and hail. It snowed during the forenoon, but we proceeded early on our voyage, passed bottom on the south side and hills on the north. We also passed a small river on the south side, called Chischeet river\n [8] and encamped on the south side. Two of the hunters, who had gone out in the morning came in, and had killed a buffaloe and an otter.\n\n

[Whitehouse]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nSunday October 21st \u00a0\u00a0 Last night we had rainy disagreeable Weather, We set out early this morning, Shortly after we had some Snow, we passed a small River, lying on the South side of the Mesouri, called Chief Charet. [9] \u00a0\u00a0 We proceeded on till Evening, & encamped on the South side of the River.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 Our hunters came to us, having killed a Buffalo, and One Otter which they brought to our Camp\n\n

\n
1. \n Clark's \"Chess-che tar\" or \"\n Chiss-Cho-tar\n \" is the Arikara word \n \u010dis\u010d\u00edtA, \"fork (of a river).\" Heart River reaches the Missouri at present Mandan, Morton County, North Dakota, opposite Bismarck. Clark's \"Turtle Mountain\" is the present Killdeer Mountain in western North Dakota. While the range can be considered the source of Knife River, the sources of Heart River in Stark County would more properly be associated with the Badlands. Coues (HLC), 1:174 n. 51; Atlas map 28; MRC map 49. (back)
\n
2. There are a number of prehistoric villages in the area just above the camp for this date. The various villages mentioned in the journals are shown on Atlas map 28, in the vicinity of present Bismarck, North Dakota. None have been investigated in the detail necessary to identify them with Clark's words or map. See Will & Hecker, 81\u201382. (back)
\n
3. \n Clark is apparently describing a version of the Sun Dance, practiced by many Great Plains tribes. Endurance of such physical pain was part of the ritual practiced by several, but not all, tribes. George Catlin has left a vivid record of such ceremonies among the Mandans. Catlin (NAI), 1:192\u201399 and pl. 66; Catlin (OKP); Liberty. (back)
\n
4. In or near present Mandan, North Dakota. Mattison (GR), 11; Atlas map 28; MRC map 50. (back)
\n
5. \n Clark evidently means that he did all his hunting with a relatively small-caliber \"Kentucky\" long rifle, or \"squirrel gun,\" probably his own property. Russell (FTT), 38. (back)
\n
6. Their first encounter with the river otter, an animal already known to science. Burroughs, 75. (back)
\n
7. \n Heart River, Morton County, North Dakota. (back)
\n
8. \n Heart River, Morton County, North Dakota. (back)
\n
9. One of the more interesting spellings for the Arikara name for the Heart River. See Clark's entry for this day. (back)
