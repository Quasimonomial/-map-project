\n August 6, 1806\n

\n
\n \n

[Lewis]

\n \n
\n Wednesday August 6th 1806.\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nA little after dark last evening a violent storm arrose to the N. E. and shortly after came on attended with violent Thunder lightning and some hail; the rail fell in a mere torrant and the wind blew so violently that it was with difficulty I could have the small canoes unloaded before they filled with water; they sustained no injury. \u00a0\u00a0 our situation was open and exposed to the storm. \u00a0\u00a0 in attending to the canoes I got wet to the skin and having no shelter on land I betook myself to the orning of the perogue which I had, formed of Elkskin, here I obtained a few hours of broken rest; the wind and rain continued almost all night and the air became very cold. \u00a0\u00a0 we set out early this morning and decended the river about 10 miles below Porcupine river\n [1] when the wind became so violent that I laid by untill 4 P. M. \u00a0\u00a0 the wind then abaiting in some measure we again resumed our voyage, and decended the river about 5 miles below our encampment of the 1st of May 1805 where we halted for the night on the S. W. side of the river. [2] \u00a0\u00a0 after halting we killed three fat cows and a buck. \u00a0\u00a0 we had previously killed today 4 deer a buck Elk and a fat cow. in short game is so abundant and gentle that we kill it when we please. the Feildses went on ahead this evening and we did not overtake them. we saw several bear in the course of the day.\u2014\n

\n \n
\n
\n \n

[Clark]

\n \n
\n Friday 6th August 1806\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nI rose very wet. \u00a0\u00a0 about 11 P M last night the wind become very hard for a fiew minits Suckceeded by Sharp lightning and hard Claps of Thunder and rained for about 2 hours very hard after which it continued Cloudy the balance of the night. \u00a0\u00a0 as we were about Setting out a female Big horn animal came on the bluff imediately above us and looked down. I derected Labeech to Shoot it which he did, after Skinning this animal we Set out and proceeded on to a Sand bar on the S W. Side below the enterance of White earth river where I landed and had the meat Skins and bedding all put out to dry. [3] \u00a0\u00a0 wind hard from the N W. I halted on the N W. Side of this river in the bend above the white earth river, where I saw where the Indians had been digging a root which they eate and use in Seup [NB: Soup], [4] not more than 7 or 8 days past. This morning a very large Bear of white Specis, discovered us floating in the water and takeing us, as I prosume to be Buffalow imediately plunged into the river and prosued us. I directed the men to be Still. \u00a0\u00a0 this animal Came within about 40 yards of us, and tacked about. \u00a0\u00a0 we all fired into him without killing him, and the wind So high that we could not pursue hi[m], by which means he made his escape to the Shore badly wounded. I have observed buffalow floating down which I suppose must have been drounded in Crossing above. \u00a0\u00a0 more or less of those animals drown or mire in passing this river. I observed Several floating buffalow on the R. Rochejhone imediately below where large gangues had Crossed. The wind blew hard all the after part of the day. I derected the men to dress their Skins except one which I took with me and walkd. through the bottom to the foot of the hills \u00a0\u00a0 I killed five deer and the man with me killed 2. \u00a0\u00a0 four others were killed in the Course of the day by the party only 2 of those deer were fat owing as I suppose to the Musquetors which are So noumerous and troublesom to them that they Cannot feed except under the torments of millions of those Musquetors.\n\n\n\n

\n \n
\n
\n \n

[Ordway]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nWednesday 6th August 1806. \u00a0\u00a0 a fair morning. \u00a0\u00a0 we loaded up our canoes and Set out as usal and procd. on \u00a0\u00a0 about 10 A. M. passd. the mo. of 2000 mile Creek. [5] \u00a0\u00a0 the wind rose high \u00a0\u00a0 So halted. \u00a0\u00a0 the hunters went out and killed Several deer. \u00a0\u00a0 we delayed about 3 hours then proceeded on \u00a0\u00a0 in the evening we Camped [6] at a large bottom S. Side \u00a0\u00a0 a fat Elk killd. and 2 fat buffaloe \u00a0\u00a0 we took Some of the fattest of the meat. \u00a0\u00a0 the buffaloe Elk and all kinds of game are pleanty and verry tame &C &C.\u2014\n\n\n

\n \n
\n
\n \n

[Gass]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Wednesday 6th. \u00a0\u00a0 We embarked early, and had a fine morning, but high wind. At 12 o'clock the wind blew so violent that it became dangerous to go on, and we halted; and some of the men went out and shot a large buck, but not dead and he got into the river; when two of them pursued in a canoe and caught him. Having remained here three hours, we again went on until night and encamped. We have yet seen nothing of the two hunters who had been left behind in the small canoe.\n\n\n

\n \n
\n \n \n
\n
1. \n Poplar River, in Roosevelt County, Montana; see May 3, 1805. Atlas map 36; MRC map 62. (back)
\n
2. \n Lewis camped in Richland County, Montana, some ten miles east of the present town of Poplar. Atlas map 36; MRC map 62. (back)
\n
3. \n Clark does not say how far below the mouth of Little Muddy River (White Earth River) this camp was; it was probably in McKenzie County, North Dakota, on a site now submerged by Garrison Reservoir, somewhere above Tobacco Creek (see August 9, 1806). (back)
\n
4. Breadroot, scurf pea, pomme blanche, prairie turnip, Psoralea esculenta Pursh. See Lewis's description at May 8, 1805. This ethnobotanical observation documents an early August harvest of breadroot. At this time the Indians would have been able to locate the roots before the above-ground stem had abscised and broken away from the storage root, and it would have been at a most nutritious stage, after the spring flowering and carbohydrate storage period. Booth & Wright, 137; Barkley, 181; Wedel (NPT). (back)
\n
5. \n Redwater River, McCone County, Montana. (back)
\n
6. In Richland County, about ten miles east of the town of Poplar. (back)
