\n May 16, 1805\n

\n
\n \n

[Lewis]

\n \n
\n Thursday May 16th\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nThe morning was fair and the day proved favorable to our operations; by 4 oClock in the evening our Instruments, Medicine, merchandize provision &c, were perfectly dryed, repacked and put on board the perogue. the loss we sustained was not so great as we had at first apprehended; our medicine sustained the greatest injury, several articles of which were intirely spoiled, and many others considerably injured; the ballance of our losses consisted of some gardin seeds, a small quantity of gunpowder, and a few culinary articles which fell overboard and sunk, the Indian woman to whom I ascribe equal fortitude and resolution, with any person onboard at the time of the accedent, caught and preserved most of the light articles which were washed overboard \u00a0\u00a0 all matters being now arranged for our departure we lost no time in seting out; proceeced on tolerably well about seven miles and encamped on the Stard. side. [1] \u00a0\u00a0 in the early part of the day two of our men fired on a panther, [2] a little below our encampment, and wounded it; they informed us that it was very large, had just killed a deer partly devoured it, and in the act of concealing the ballance as they discovered him. \u00a0\u00a0 we caught two Antelopes at our encampment in attempting to swim the river; these anamals are but lean as yet, and of course not very pleasant food. I walked on shore this evening and killed a buffaloe cow and calf, we found the calf most excellent veal. \u00a0\u00a0 the country on either side of the river is broken and hills much higher than usual, the bottoms now become narrow and the timber more scant; some scattering pine and cedar on the steep declivities of the hills.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 this morning a white bear toar Labuiche's coat which he had left in the plains.\u2014

[Clark]

\n \n
\n May 16th Thursday 1805\n
\u00a0
\n \n

\na fair morning \u00a0\u00a0 our articles all out to Dry \u00a0\u00a0 at 4 oClock we had every thing that was Saved dry and on bord, our loss is Some medison, Powder, Seeds, & Several articles which Sunk, and maney Spoiled had a medn. altitude which gave for Latd. \u00a0\u00a0 \u00b0 \u00a0\u00a0 ' \u00a0\u00a0 \" N.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 two of our men fired at a panthr a little below our Camp, this animale they say was large, had Caught a Deer & eate it half & buried the ballance. \u00a0\u00a0 a fiew antilope Swam the river near our Camp \u00a0\u00a0 two of them were Cought by the party in the river. \u00a0\u00a0 at half past 4 oClock we Set out and proceeded on verry well [blank] miles and incamped on the Std. Side \u00a0\u00a0 the Countrey as before hilley & broken verry Small proprotion of timber in the points, Some little pine & Ceader in the hills

[Ordway]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Thursday 16th May 1805. \u00a0\u00a0 a heavy diew last night. \u00a0\u00a0 a clear & pleasant morning. \u00a0\u00a0 we opened the goods &.c. to get them dry before we packed them up. \u00a0\u00a0 one of the party wounded a large panther \u00a0\u00a0 he had killed a deer & was covring it up \u00a0\u00a0 Capt. Clark made Search to find him but in vain \u00a0\u00a0 about 12 I killed a goat or antilope \u00a0\u00a0 about one we packed up the goods and loaded the Captains perogues \u00a0\u00a0 one of the party caught another goat which was mired in the mud. \u00a0\u00a0 about 3 oClock P M we Set off \u00a0\u00a0 proceeded on \u00a0\u00a0 passed high rough broken hills and round knobs on each Side of the River and narrow bottoms. \u00a0\u00a0 passed a Small willow Island on or near the N. Shore \u00a0\u00a0 towards night we killed 3 buffaloe 2 Deer and one buffaloe calf \u00a0\u00a0 we Came [blank] miles and Camped on the S. Side at a bottom covered with timber & ran back like the most of the bottoms for a long distance back.\n\n\n

\n
\n \n

[Gass]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Thursday 16th. \u00a0\u00a0 This was a fine day, and by 4 o'clock in the afternoon we had all our articles dry and on board again. At that time we proceeded on our voyage; passed high barren hills on both sides of the river, with only a \nfew pine trees on them. We advanced seven miles and encamped [5] in a handsome bottom on the South side where there are a number of old Indian huts.\n\n\n

\n \n
\n
\n \n

[Whitehouse]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Thursday 16th May 1805. \u00a0\u00a0 a heavy diew last night \u00a0\u00a0 a clear pleasant morning. \u00a0\u00a0 we opened the goods &c. in order to get them dry before we packed them up. \u00a0\u00a0 Some of the men wounded a large panther in this bottom, as he was coverring up a deer which he had killed not long before. \u00a0\u00a0 about 12 oClock Sergt Ordway killed a cabberree or antelope, a Specie of our goats. \u00a0\u00a0 one of the party killed another which was mired in the mud. \u00a0\u00a0 about one oC. we packed up our goods and loaded the officers perogue. \u00a0\u00a0 about 3 oClock P. M. we set off and proceeded on. \u00a0\u00a0 passed high broken hills & round knobs on each Side of the River and narrow bottoms. \u00a0\u00a0 passed a Small willow Island near the N. S. \u00a0\u00a0 we halted a fiew minutes at a bottom on N. S. and killed 2 buffaloe, 3 Deer and one buffaloe calf. \u00a0\u00a0 Camped on the S. S. at a bottom covered with timber. \u00a0\u00a0 came 7 miles to day.

\n

\nThursday May 16th \u00a0\u00a0 A heavy dew fell last night, but a pleasant Clear morning. \u00a0\u00a0 All hands were employed, in opening the Goods, to get them thoroughly dried before we packet them up again,\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 Some of the Men after they had finished opening the Goods, went a hunting.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 they wounded a large Panther, in the bottom which we were encamped in, as he was covering up a deer which he had killed a short time before.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 About 2 o'Clock P. M. One of our Serjeants killed a Caberree or Antelope, which is a specie of the Goat kind, and another of the party killed another of the same \nkind, that was fast mired in the Mud,\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 About One oClock P. M. we packed up all our Goods, and loaded the Officers pettyauger, At 3 o'Clock P. M we set off, and passed high broken hills, & high Nobs which lay on each side of the River, and narrow bottoms, and a small Island having Willows growing on it lying on the North side of the River, We halted a few minutes at a bottom on the North side of the River, when some of our party went out and killed, 2 buffalo, One buffalo Calf, and three deer, which were brought to our Pettyaugers \u00a0\u00a0 We proceeded on, and Encamped in a bottom cover'd with timber; lying on the South side of the River, having come 7 Miles this day.\u2014\n\n\n\n

1. Both captains' journals say they camped on the starboard, or north, side, in Phillips County, Montana. Atlas maps 38, 50, 58 clearly show the camp on the larboard, or south side of the Missouri, in Garfield County. Ordway and Whitehouse place the camp on the \"S.\" (south) side. In any case, the site is now under Fort Peck Reservoir. MRC map 67. (back)

2. The mountain lion, or cougar, Felis concolor, already known to science. Burroughs, 94\u201395. (back)
\n
3. Also given on Atlas map 38, in Clark's hand. (back)
\n
4. \n Atlas map 38 calls it a \"naked point,\" as does an excised portion of Clark's course. (back)
\n
5. In Garfield County, Montana, on a site now under Fort Peck Reservoir; see Lewis's entry and note for further discussion of this campsite. (back)
