\n June 22, 1804\n

\n
\n \n

[Clark] [1]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n22nd June Friday \u00a0\u00a0 after a Violent gust of wind accompanied with rain from the West, which commenced at Day brake, and lasted about one hour, we Set out under a gentle Breeze from the N W. and proceeded on \u00a0\u00a0 S. 14\u00b0 W. 2\u00bd ms. to pt. on L. S. \u00a0\u00a0 Ord[way]\n [2] Killed a goose, S 25\u00b0 W 3 Ms. to a pt. on S. S. \u00a0\u00a0 psd. Snags and Swift water on the S. S.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 S. 66\u00b0 W: \u00bd a me. on S pt. \u00a0\u00a0 N 60 W 4\u00bd me. to pt. L. S. \u00a0\u00a0 passed \u2329the Lower pt. of\u232a a large Isd. on the S. S.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 (Ferenthiers Thermometr at 3 oClock P, M, 87 d which is 11 d above Summr heat) [3] and one [island] on the L. S. opposit against which there is a handsom Prarie of high Bottom & up Land, Capt Lewis went out in this Prarie & walked Several miles, Come to opposit the mouth of a large Creek on the S. S. Called River of the Fire Prarie\n [4] \u00a0\u00a0 at the mouth of this creek the party on Shore Shields & Collins was camped waiting for our arrival & inform that they Pass'd thro: Some fine Lands, and well watered \u00a0\u00a0 G D. [Drouillard] Killed a fine Bear to day

\n \n
\n
\n \n

[Clark]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n22nd June Friday \u00a0\u00a0 river rose 4 Inchs last night. I was waken'd \u2329at\u232a before day light this morning by the guard prepareing the boat to receve an apparent Storm which threttened violence from the West \u00a0\u00a0 at day light \u2329the\u232a a violent wind accompanied with rain cam from the W. and lasted about one hour, it Cleared away, and we Set out and proceeded on under a gentle breeze from the N. W. \u00a0\u00a0 passed Some verry Swift water Crouded with Snags, pass two large Island opposit each other, and immediately opposit a large & extensive Prarie on the Labd Side, This Prarie is butifull a high bottom for 1\u00bd mile back and rises to the Common leavel of the Countrey about 70 or 80 feet and extends back out of view. Capt. L walked on Shore a few miles this after noon \u00a0\u00a0 (at 3 oClock P M. Ferents Thermometer Stood at 87\u00b0: = to 11 d Summer heat) \u00a0\u00a0 we came to on the L. Side opposit the mouth of a large Creek Called the River of the Fire Prarie, at the mouth of this Creek the Party on Shore were waiting our arrival, they informed that the Lands thro: which they passed was fine & well watered

[Ordway]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n\nFriday June 22d 1804. \u00a0\u00a0 we Set out at 7 oC after a hard Shower of rain & high wind from N. E. Thunder and lightning &.C\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 the day fair \u00a0\u00a0 proceeded on 2\u00bd miles \u00a0\u00a0 one of the men [5] killed a goose. \u00a0\u00a0 we passed a handsome prarie on the South Side & a Creek Called little fire Creek. [6] \u00a0\u00a0 passed a Creek on the N. Side called Big fire Creek where our hunters were waiting for us. \u00a0\u00a0 we Camped opst. on South Side

[Floyd]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Friday June 22d \u00a0\u00a0 Set out at 7 oclock after a verry hard Storm thunder \u2329wind from the N. E.\u232a and Rain wind [WC: from the West, proceeded on under a gentle Breeze from the N. W. \u00a0\u00a0 passd] \u2329from the N. E. past\u232a a Creek on the South Side Calleded the Littel Fire Creek \u00a0\u00a0 it Comes in opset the middel of a Small Isd on the South Side \u00a0\u00a0 Strong water \u00a0\u00a0 Came 9 miles \u00a0\u00a0 encamped on the Southe Side at a Prarie \u00a0\u00a0 this Prarie is Called Fire \u00a0\u00a0 on the N. Side Comes in a Creek Calleded the Fishing Creek \u00a0\u00a0 the Creek is about 50 yards wide and High Land\n\n

\n \n
\n
\n \n

[Gass]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Friday 22nd. \u00a0\u00a0 It rained hard from four to seven in the morning, when we continued our voyage. About 12, one of our men went out and killed a large bear. We encamped at a handsome prairie on the south side opposite a large creek, called the Fire-prairie, and which is 60 yards wide.\n

\n \n
\n
\n \n

[Whitehouse]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nFriday 22nd \u00a0\u00a0 the Rain came on Rapidly in the \u2329night\u232a morning \u00a0\u00a0 Interupd our Starting at the usal hour. \u00a0\u00a0 the day Cleard Up at 7 Oclock \u00a0\u00a0 the two latter days was the hotist that has been Seen Or felt a long time. \u00a0\u00a0 the water was Strong with the heat of the day which made the times disagreeble to the party. \u00a0\u00a0 G. Drewyer Killd a large Male Bare weighd Neer 5 hundred Wt. \u00a0\u00a0 \nOur hunters Came in which had been Absent from the 19th Inst. the[y] had part of One deer girkd with them \u00a0\u00a0 their names is J. Sheilds & Collins \u00a0\u00a0 Incampd at the fire perarie \u00a0\u00a0 Roed 12 Miles\u2014

\n

\nFriday June 22nd \u00a0\u00a0 This morning we were detained from starting by a heavy Rain which continued till 7 oClock A. M \u00a0\u00a0 The weather proved excessive hot, as it was the two succeeding days, & being by far the warmest weather that we met with for a long time.

\n

\nThe current running very strong against us, and having to tow the boat it can hardly be imagined the fataigue that we underwent, We came too and Encamped at a place called the fire Priari, shortly after our hunters came to us, George Drewyer one of our hunters who had been absent from the 19th instant joined us having a large he bear with him which he had killed which weigh'd near five hundred weight, and part of a deer the flesh of which he \nhad jerked.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 We Towed 12 Miles this day.\u2014\n\n\n

1. \n Biddle's note at the head of this set of the Field Notes (document 23) reads, \"22d to 26.\" (back)
\n
2. Osgood interprets letters interlined above the word \"Killed\" as \"Ord\" for \"Ordway.\" The sergeant himself says that \"one of the men\" killed the goose. Osgood (FN), 61. (back)
\n
3. \"Summer heat\" was an arbitrary average summer temperature commonly marked on thermometers at the time, in this case seventy-six degrees. (back)
\n
4. Near the Jackson-Lafayette county line, Missouri; the exact location is related to the River of the Fire Prairie, which is itself problematical. Both Clark's journals indicate that this stream was on the starboard, or north, side of the Missouri, with the camp on the opposite side. However, Fire Creek on later maps is on the south side of the river, in Jackson County, and is probably the Little Fire Creek referred to by Ordway and Floyd. The only sizable stream in the vicinity on the north side is Fishing River, a few miles upstream. Clark's 1810 map of the West (Atlas map 123) shows Fire Prarie River on the north side of the Missouri and Fire Prarie Creek on the south side, their mouths nearly opposite each other. in Clark's postexpedition list of rivers and creeks, he says that Fire Prarie Creek comes in from the southwest. Coues suggests that the mouth of Fishing River had moved upstream some time after 1804. This would also simplify matters, in that the island on which the expedition was forced by wind to spend most of June 23, 1804, could be Fishing River Island, which by the 1890s was downstream from the mouth of Fishing River, instead of upstream from it, as the expedition's island would have to be. It is possible, however, that this island, described by Biddle as separated from the north bank by a narrow, timber-choked channel, later became part of the mainland. To add to the confusion, Nicollet shows Fire Creek as \"Clear water Creek\" and indicates a \"Fire Prairie River\" on the south bank in present Jackson County, and mouthing above Fishing River, which is not readily identifiable with a major stream in that vicinity today. Coues (HLC), 1:30 and n. 63; Nicollet (MMR), 365; MRC map 13. (back)
\n
5. Perhaps Ordway himself; see Clark's entry for this day. (back)
\n
6. See Clark's entry for this day for a discussion of the difficulties in identifying the two creeks mentioned in this entry and in locating the camp for this day. (back)
