\n June 25, 1804\n

\n
\n \n

[Clark] [1]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Monday June 25th \u00a0\u00a0 a heavy fog Detaind us about an hour \u00a0\u00a0 Set out passed the Isd on a course from the last point S 49\u00b0 W, 3 Ms to a point on the S. S. \u00a0\u00a0 S 55\u00b0 W \u00bd Me. S. S.\u00a0\u00a0 a Coal-Bank on the opposit or L. S Side, this bank appears to Contain great quantity of excellente Coal, [2] the wind from the N: W \u00a0\u00a0 a Small Creek Called Coal or (Chabonea) [3] N 50\u00b0 W to the Pt, L. S. 3\u00bd Miles \u00a0\u00a0 Hard water & logs, Bank falling in, Passed a Small Creek L. S. Called \n Labeenie\n \n [4] \u2329Lat. 38 37' 5\"\u232a \u00a0\u00a0 a Prarie is Situated on the S. S. a Short Distance from the river, which contains great quantities of wild apples of the Size of the Common apple, [5] the French Say is well flavered when ripe, which is the time the leaves begin to fall N 70\u00b0 W \u00bd me. along the right Side of a Willow Isd. Situated on the L. Side S. 80\u00b0 W \u00bd me. L. S. \u00a0\u00a0 S 55\u00b0 W. \u00bd me. to Pt. of Smal Isd. L. S. \u00a0\u00a0 S 15\u00b0 W \u00bd me. L. S.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 S. 2\u00b0 E 2 me. pt on Lbd S. (here I will only remark that the Deer in the Morning & evening are feeding in great numbers on the banks of the River, they feed on young willow, and amuse themselves running on the open beeches or points[)] \u00a0\u00a0 We have hard water this afternoon round the heads of Small Islds. on the L. Side below a Small High Prarie S. 48\u00b0 W. 2 Ms. pt. S. S. \u00a0\u00a0 passd. a small Isd. on which we Camped [6] The party on Shore did not join us to day, or have we Seen or her [heard] of them \u00a0\u00a0 river falling fast about 8 Inches in 24 hours, the Hills on the L. S. this evening higher than usial about 160 or 180 feet. \u00a0\u00a0 the lands appear of a Simalier to those passed

\n \n

\n \n

[Clark]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n25th, June Monday \u00a0\u00a0 a thick fog detained us untile 8 oClock, passed a Island, at 3 miles passed a Coal-mine, or Bank of Stone Coal, on the South Side, this bank appears to Contain great quantity of fine Coal, the river being high prevented our Seeeing that contained in the Cliffs of the best quallity, a Small Creek mouth's \u2329in\u232a below This bank Call'd after the bank \u2329Chabonea\u232a [NB: Charbon] [7] Creek \u00a0\u00a0 the Wind from the N. W. \u00a0\u00a0 passed a Small Creek on the L. Side at 12 oClock, Called Bennet's Creek \u00a0\u00a0 The Praries Come within a Short distance of the river on each Side which Contains in addition to Plumbs [8] Raspberries & vast quantities of wild \u2329crab\u232a apples, \u2329which is of a [blank] [9] and wild flowers\u232a \u00a0\u00a0 great numbs. of Deer are seen feeding on the young willows & earbage in the Banks \u2329op\u232a and on the Sand bars in the river. \u00a0\u00a0 our party on Shores did not join us this evening \u00a0\u00a0 we Camped on an Island Situated on the S. Side, opposit some hills higher than Common, Say 160 or 180 feet above the Bottom. The river is Still falling \u00a0\u00a0 last night it fell 8 Inches

[Ordway]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n\nMonday June 25th 1804. \u00a0\u00a0 a foggy morning. \u00a0\u00a0 it Detained us about an\nhour. \u00a0\u00a0 we Set out, passed a Coal Bank on the North Side which appears to\nhave Great quantity of Coal in it. \u00a0\u00a0 we passed a Small Creek Called Coal\n [10] or (Chaboned) on the Same Side. \u00a0\u00a0 we passed a Creek on the South Side called\n\n Labeenie\n . [11] \u00a0\u00a0 we Saw a great nomber of Deer feeding on the Sand Beachs \u00a0\u00a0 they feed on young willow [12] & are verry numerious. \u00a0\u00a0 we passed Some handsom high praries on the South Side of the River. \u00a0\u00a0 passed Some high hills on the South Side. Some rocks, &.C. Signs of Springs on the N. Side of Sd.\nhills. R. Fields killed a Deer. \u00a0\u00a0 we Came 11\u00bd Miles & Camped on an \u2329the\u232a Isd. lower point of an Island Near the North side of the River. Capt Lewis\nkilled a Rabit. [13] \u00a0\u00a0 R. Fields kille a Deer this evening. \u00a0\u00a0 our flanking party did not git to us this evening,

\n \n

[Floyd]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nMonday June 25th \u00a0\u00a0 we Set out at 8 oclock after the Fogue was Gon, [WC: \u00a0\u00a0 pass a Coal mine on the South Side above a Small Island, a Small Creek below which takes its name from the bank of Coal, and large Creek at about one Mile higher up the River on the Same Side Called (un batteur La benne River)] \u00a0\u00a0 [X: passed several small Islands on the South Side. Some hard water, & camped on a small Island near the North Side \u00a0\u00a0 Capt Lewis killed a Rabit, R. Fields a Deer this eving \u00a0\u00a0 our Flanking party did not join us this evening \u00a0\u00a0 (my hand is painfull)]\n\n\n\n

\n \n

[Gass]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Monday 25th. \u00a0\u00a0 The morning was foggy and at seven o'clock we pursued our voyage. The river here is narrow with high land on the south side. We passed a creek on the south side called Labenile, [14] and encamped on an island.\n\n\n

[Whitehouse]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nMonday 25 \u00a0\u00a0 Got on our way at hard Scrable Perarie \u00a0\u00a0 passd two Creeks the One Calld. la beane [15] and the other Rowling Creek\u2014 [16] \u00a0\u00a0 S. W. S[ide] \u00a0\u00a0 a litle above the latter two wolves appeard On Shore \u00a0\u00a0 A man from on board of the white Peiroug went ashore Shot One of them \u00a0\u00a0 On the E side is high land and well timberd \u00a0\u00a0 the hills puts in neer the River\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 Roed 14 Miles\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 Incampd on a small Island \u00a0\u00a0 the hunters\u2014 [17]\n

\n

\nMonday June 25th \u00a0\u00a0 This morning early we left hard scrabble priari, and passed two creeks in about one Mile distance from where we started; the first called Labeane and the other rowling Creek laying on the So. side their courses being to their heads South west by South, a small distance above the latter creek, we saw two Wolves, on the shore; one of our Men went a shore from on board the pettiauger, and shot one of them. \u00a0\u00a0 The land on the North side of the River lays high & is well Timber'd. \u00a0\u00a0 Several hills running near the River, the current still running strong. \u00a0\u00a0 in the Evening we came too, and encamped on a small Island called hunters Island, the distance we rowed this day being 14 Miles.\u2014\n\n

\n
1. This latter part of this entry and most of the next is written over a note by Lewis. See his entry for June 12, 1804, above. (back)
\n
2. Several coal beds, ranging from six inches to two and one-half feet thick, are known from the eastern part of Jackson County, Missouri. One, the rush Creek mine east of Kansas City, produced coal for several years but was abandoned in 1904. Hinds, 211\u201314. (back)
\n
3. Perhaps later Sleepy Branch, Jackson County. MRM map 40. (back)
\n
4. The \"Bennet's Creek\" of the Codex A entry, perhaps named for Fran\u00e7ois M. Benoit, later referred to by Clark as \" Bennet\" (see below, July 14, 1804), or a member of his family. Biddle gives it as \"La Benite,\" the name it bears in Nicollet. La Benite Park perpetuates that name. It is evidently the later Sugar Creek in Jackson County. Coues (HLC), 1:32 and n. 69; Osgood (FN), 62 n. 8; Nicollet (MMR), 368; MRC map 14; MRM map 41. (back)
\n
5. Probably Pyrus ioensis (Wood) Bailey, wild crab, which is the most common crab apple in Missouri. P. coronaria L., wild crab, is also known in the area but is much less common. Steyermark, 799. (back)
\n
6. They evidently went around the later Liberty bend, now cut off by the Missouri, and camped opposite the modern community of Sugar Creek, Jackson County, a suburb of Kansas City. MRC map 14. (back)
\n
7. \n Biddle apparently crossed out \"Charbonea\" when he sustituted \"Charbon.\" (back)
\n
8. Probably Prunus americana Marsh., wild plum. Steyermark, 860. The captains gave the word plums, like currants, a broad application. (back)
\n
9. A space of more than half a line was left here for a later insertion that was never made. The entire phrase, placed in brackets in the original, was later crossed out. (back)
\n
10. Perhaps Sleepy Branch, Jackson County, Missouri. (back)
\n
11. Evidently Sugar Creek, Jackson County, and perhaps named for Fran\u00e7ois M. Benoit (see Clark's entry for this day). Only Floyd (or a writer in his journal) adds the words \"um batteur,\"\nprobably meaning \"a bateau.\" (back)
\n
12. An unknown willow, Salix sp. (back)
\n
13. Only Ordway and Floyd (or a writer in his journal) report Lewis getting an eastern\ncottontail, Sylvilagus floridanus, on this day. (back)
\n
14. One of expedition members' several variations of the creek perhaps named for Fran\u00e7ois M.Benoit; present Sugar Creek, Jackson County, Missouri. (back)
\n
15. Evidently Sugar Creek, Jackson County, Missouri, and perhaps named for Fran\u00e7ois M. Benoit (see Clark's entry for this day). (back)
\n
16. Not mentioned by the other journal keepers. Perhaps it is a much distorted version of Coal Creek, or of the French name which Clark gives as \"Chabonea,\" and which Nicholas Biddle gives as \"Charbon\" in his edition of the journals. That stream is perhaps present Sleepy Branch, Jackson County, Missouri. However, \"Rolling Creek\" may be meant. (back)
\n
17. Given as \"Hunters Island\" in the fair copy, another place-name used only by Whitehouse. The island was evidently opposite Sugar Creek, Jackson County. (back)
