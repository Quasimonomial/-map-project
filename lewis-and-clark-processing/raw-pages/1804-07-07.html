\n July 7, 1804\n

\n
\n \n

[Clark] [1]

\n \n
\u00a0

\n7th of July Satturday 1804 \u00a0\u00a0 Set out early \u00a0\u00a0 passed Some verry Swift water on the L. S. which Compelled us to Draw up by the Cord. \u00a0\u00a0 a verry warm morning, passed a butifull Prarie on the right Side which extends back, those Praries has much the appearance from the river of farms, Divided by narrow Strips of woods \u00a0\u00a0 those Strips of timber grows along the runs which rise on the hill & pass to the river \u00a0\u00a0 a Cleft above, one man sick (\n Frasure\n ) \u00a0\u00a0 Struck with the Sun, Saw a large rat on the Side of the bank, [2] Killed a wolf on the Bank [3] \u00a0\u00a0 passed (2) a verry narrow part of the river, all confined within 200 yards, a yellow bank above, passed a Small willow Island on the S. point, (in Low water those Small Willow Islands are joined to the Sand bars makeing out from the Points) \u00a0\u00a0 a pond on the S. S near the prarie we passed yesterday in which G D. Saw Several young Swans \u00a0\u00a0 we Came to and Camped on the L. S. [4] and two men Sent out last evening with the horses did not Join us this evening agreeable to orders\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 a hard wind with Some rain from the N, E at \u2329Dark\u232a 7 oClock which lasted half an hour, with thunder & lightning. \u00a0\u00a0 river fall a little

[Clark]

\n July the 7th Satturday 1804
\u00a0
\n \n

\nSet out early \u00a0\u00a0 passed Some Swift water, which obliged us to draw up by roapes, a Sand bare at the point opposit a butifull Prarie on the S. Side Calld. (1) St. Michul, [5] those Praries on the river has verry much the appearence of \u2329old\u232a farms from the river Divided by narrow Strips of wood land, which wood land is Situatd. on the runs leading to the river. \u00a0\u00a0 passed a \u2329Clift\u232a Bluff of yellow Clay above the Prarie. [6] \u00a0\u00a0 Saw a large rat on the bank. Killed a Wolf. \u00a0\u00a0 at 4 oClock pass a Verry narrow part of the river \u00a0\u00a0 water Confd. in a bead not more than 200 yards wide at this place \u00a0\u00a0 the Current runs against the L. Side. \u00a0\u00a0 no Sand to Confine the Current on the S. S. passed a Small sand Island above the Small Islds. Situated at the points, in low water form a part of the Sand bars makeing out from those points

\n

\nIncamped on the S. S. \u00a0\u00a0 at 7 oClock a Violent Ghust of wind from the N. E. with Some rain, which lasted half an hour (G D. informs me that he Saw in a Pond on the S. S. which we passed yesterday; a number of young Swans\u2014,[)] \u00a0\u00a0 one man verry Sick, Struck with the Sun, Capt. Lewis bled him & gave Niter which has revived him much [7]\n

[Ordway]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n\nSaturday July 7th 1804. \u00a0\u00a0 we Set our eairly \u00a0\u00a0 passed Swirt waters on the South Side, verry warm morning, passed a beautiful prarie on the North Side which extends back, those praries called St. Michel has much the appearance from the river of farms Divided by narrow Strips of woods \u00a0\u00a0 those Strips of timber grows along the runs which rise on the hills, & pass to the River, I went on Shore with the Horses in the afternoon In the North Side \u00a0\u00a0 crossed a Creek 2 miles up in the evening \u00a0\u00a0 followed down to the mouth, and Camped it being too late to find the boat, the Musquitoes troubled me So that I Could not Sleep, as this Creek is without name & my Describeing it to my Capt. he named it Ordway Creek. [8] \u00a0\u00a0 Some of the men in the Boat killed a wolf to day \u00a0\u00a0 they Camped [9] on the South Side of the Missouris. \u00a0\u00a0 one man taken Sick (Frasier).\n\n\n

[Gass]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\n Saturday 7th. \u00a0\u00a0 At an early hour we proceeded on our voyage; passed a high handsome prairie on the north side, and killed a wolf and a large woodrat [11] on the bank. The principal difference between it and the common rat \nis, its having hair on its tail.\n\n\n

[Whitehouse]

\n \n
\u00a0
\n \n

\nSaterdy 7 \u00a0\u00a0 Got under way about Sun Rise \u00a0\u00a0 Six Miles from whare we Started Came to the most beautifull prarie On the E. S. Whare Nature formd Some battryes And Read Outs [12] \u00a0\u00a0 the hills putts in Neer the River \u00a0\u00a0 A Quarter of mile \u2329from the River\u232a to the N. E of Sd. prarie a rock on the Bank of the River about 320 feet from the Surface of the watter high to the top thereoff. \u00a0\u00a0 after passing Sd. place towards the Evening a man Espyd. a wolf lying a Sleep \u00a0\u00a0 with the Noise of the Oars Roeing he awoke Stood to know what was a comeing \u00a0\u00a0 Captn. Lewis shot at him Wounded the Animal, Colter likeways, Killd him \u00a0\u00a0 it was thought he was mad when the first \u2329Ball\u232a Bawl Struck him he Snapd. at his hind part \u00a0\u00a0 Roed. 15 Miles. \u00a0\u00a0 Incampd.\u2014

\n

\nSaturday July 7th \u00a0\u00a0 We left the Priari at sun rise, and proceeded on Six Miles, when we passed a most beautiful Priari, lying on the No. East side of the River, where Nature had formed some batterys and Redoubts, by Hills, which put in near to the River.\u2014

\n

\nOn the bank of the River, about one quarter of a Mile North east of the Priari is a rock \u2329which lies on the bank of the River,\u232a and is 320 feet high from the surface of the Water to the top of it, we left this rock and towards evening, one of our men espied a Wolf laying a sleep on the shore, as we approached towards him, the noise of our Oars awoke him, he stood there to see what was coming, when Captain Lewis shot at him, and struck him with a Ball, the Wolf then acted as if mad snapping continually at his hind parts. \u00a0\u00a0 The Captain order'd one Colter to fire at him, which he did, and killed him.\u2014 \u00a0\u00a0 In the Evening we encamped on the bank of the River, having rowed 15 Miles this day.\u2014\n\n

1. \n Biddle's notation at the head of this sheet of the Field Notes (document 29) reads \"July 7th to 9th.\" (back)
\n
2. Probably the eastern wood rat, first mentioned on May 31, 1804. (back)
\n
3. \n Whitehouse says Lewis wounded the animal and Colter killed it. (back)
\n
4. A camp on the larboard side today would place them in Doniphan County, Kansas. The various river shifts in the area may have placed the actual site in northwest Buchanan County, Missouri, a little upstream from St. Joseph. MRC map 18. (back)
\n
5. At the present site of St. Joseph, Missouri. MRC map 17. (back)
\n
6. Thick accumulations of loess\u2014a pale, yellowish brown loam of sandy, claylike silt\u2014 were deposited by wind during the so-called Wisconsin glaciation in the Pleistocene Epoch and stand in steep bluffs, sixty or more feet above the floodplain in some places, near this area. (back)
\n
7. Bleeding was the standard remedy of the times for nearly everything. The \"niter\" was potassium nitrate (saltpeter), used to increase the flow of perspiration and urine and to reduce fevers. Cutright (LCPN), 64; Chuinard, 154, 156. (back)
\n
8. \n Clark does not mention the creek in his entries for the day, nor does he show it on any of his maps. It may be Mace Creek, north of the Andrew-Buchanan county line, Missouri,\nwhere the party met Ordway the next day; see Clark's entry of July 8. (back)
\n
9. The main party camped a little upstream of St. Joseph, Buchanan County, Missouri. (back)
\n
10. \n Clark and Ordway have the party on the opposite shore; either way, they were a little upstream of St. Joseph, Buchanan County, Missouri. (back)
\n
11. No other enlisted man takes note of the animal, probably the eastern wood rat, Neotoma floridana.\n (back)
\n
12. \"Redoubts,\" as in the faircopy. (back)
